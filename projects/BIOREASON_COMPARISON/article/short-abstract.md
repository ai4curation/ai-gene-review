Assigning functions to gene products is a foundational task of molecular biology, accomplished through literature-based expert curation and computational prediction. Most annotation databases deploy HMM- and family-based pipelines. Recently, protein language models and other AI methods have triggered an explosion of new prediction tools. Reviewing these presents challenges.

The Critical Assessment of Functional Annotation (CAFA) series tracks aggregate progress against Gene Ontology database snapshots, but its scores often reward annotations curators later reject. A second, acute problem is that modern agentic and foundation-model predictors no longer emit only GO terms: BioReason-Pro, for example, outputs a free-text functional summary and a chain-of-thought reasoning trace alongside its term list, which CAFA cannot grade. de Crécy-Lagard et al. manually reviewed 453 predictions from a top deep learning method on uncharacterised *E. coli* proteins: only 3/453 were genuinely novel and correct, the remainder falling into systematic error classes. But performing this level of extensive manual review of a broad range of annotations is challenging.

Here we demonstrate an agentic review system (AIGR) that synthesizes multiple lines of evidence and performs biological reasoning to evaluate sequence-based predictions. AIGR complements CAFA-style benchmarks by grading narrative and reasoning content in qualitative terms, providing human-readable explanations, and assigning failed predictions to a failure mode taxonomy. Applied to a large-scale evaluation of BioReason-Pro and a replication of the de Crécy-Lagard taxonomy, we find that recent reasoning and embedding-based predictors either make systematic errors or, when correct, recapitulate baseline HMM-based approaches.
