# Divergence in the M20 Gene Family: Yeast CPS1 vs. Mammalian PM20D1

## Overview of the M20 Peptidase Family

The M20 peptidase family belongs to clan MH and comprises co-catalytic metallopeptidases that utilize two zinc ions per protein monomer for catalysis. This family contains exopeptidases including carboxypeptidases, dipeptidases, and specialized aminopeptidases, with the active site residues arranged in a conserved pattern of His/Asp, Asp, Asp, Glu, Glu, Glu/Asp, His for metal ligation and catalysis. The family is subdivided into multiple subfamilies—**M20A, M20B, M20C, M20D, and M20F**—each with distinct substrate specificities and biological functions.[1][2]

Crystal structures of M20 peptidases reveal a characteristic two-domain architecture: an N-terminal catalytic domain and a C-terminal dimerization domain that links monomers in homodimeric assemblies. The dimerization domain has been identified as a "small-molecule binding domain" (SMBD) belonging to the ACT-like superfamily, providing evolutionary insights into this enzyme family.[3][2]

## Yeast Carboxypeptidase S (CPS1/M20.002)

### Classification and Structure

Yeast carboxypeptidase S (carboxypeptidase yscS), encoded by the *CPS1* gene in *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*, is classified as **M20.002** within **subfamily M20A** of the M20 peptidase family. The enzyme is also known as Gly-Xaa carboxypeptidase, reflecting its substrate preference for releasing C-terminal amino acids from peptides with glycine as the penultimate residue. The protein consists of 576 amino acids with multiple functional domains including N-glycosylation sites.[4][2][5][6]

### Unique Membrane-Associated Biosynthesis

A distinctive feature of yeast CPS1 is its biosynthesis as a **type II integral membrane protein**, with its precursor form (pCps1) containing a transmembrane domain spanning amino acids 20–40. This membrane-bound form transits the secretory pathway, receiving N-linked oligosaccharide modifications at two or three sites, producing 78 and 81 kDa transmembrane precursor forms. The enzyme is delivered to the vacuole (the yeast equivalent of mammalian lysosomes) via the **multivesicular body (MVB) pathway**, where ubiquitination by the E3 ligase Rsp5 on Lys8 serves as a sorting signal.[7][8][9]

Upon arrival at the vacuole, CPS1 is processed by the vacuolar proteases Pep4 (proteinase A) and Prb1 (proteinase B) to release a soluble, mature active form into the vacuolar lumen. This proteolytic maturation pathway distinguishes CPS1 from the serine carboxypeptidase Y (CPY/Prc1), which uses a different sorting mechanism via the Vps10 receptor.[10][11][12][8]

### Substrate Specificity and Function

CPS1 exhibits partially overlapping substrate specificity with carboxypeptidase Y, contributing approximately 60% of the enzymatic activity required to hydrolyze the synthetic dipeptide Cbz-Gly-Leu. As a zinc-dependent metallocarboxypeptidase, the enzyme is necessary for utilizing certain peptides as sole nitrogen sources and plays roles in recycling amino acids from intracellularly generated peptides. Expression of the *CPS1* gene is regulated by nutrient availability, with increased expression during nitrogen starvation conditions.[13][14][15][10]

## Mammalian PM20D1 (M20.011)

### Classification and Discovery

Mammalian PM20D1 (peptidase M20 domain containing 1) is classified as **M20.011** within **subfamily M20A**. Despite sharing the M20A subfamily classification with yeast CPS1, PM20D1 was discovered in 2016 as a novel secreted enzyme enriched in brown adipose tissue (BAT) and thermogenic adipocytes. The human PM20D1 protein shares 71% identity and 86% similarity with its mouse ortholog.[16][2][17][18]

### Distinct Secretory Nature and Function

Unlike yeast CPS1, which is synthesized as a membrane-bound vacuolar protein, **PM20D1 is a classically secreted enzyme** that circulates in blood plasma and associates tightly with lipoproteins, particularly low-density and high-density lipoproteins. This extracellular localization is fundamental to its biological function in regulating systemic energy metabolism.[19][20]

PM20D1 catalyzes a **bidirectional reaction**—both the biosynthesis and hydrolysis of N-fatty acyl amino acids (NAAs) from free fatty acids and amino acids. This enzymatic activity is fundamentally different from the carboxypeptidase activity of yeast CPS1. PM20D1 requires conserved residues (H125, D127, and H465 in mouse) predicted to coordinate divalent cations, and mutations in these sites abolish both synthase and hydrolase activities.[21][18][16]

### Metabolic and Thermogenic Roles

The N-acyl amino acids produced by PM20D1 function as **UCP1-independent mitochondrial uncouplers**, directly binding mitochondria to stimulate respiration and thermogenesis. This represents a novel thermogenic pathway distinct from classical UCP1-mediated uncoupling in brown adipose tissue. Key functions include:[21][16][19]

- **Energy expenditure regulation**: Mice with elevated circulating PM20D1 exhibit augmented respiration, reduced adiposity, and improved glucose homeostasis[16][21]
- **Metabolic homeostasis**: PM20D1 knockout mice display bidirectional dysregulation of NAA levels, glucose intolerance, and decreased insulin sensitivity[22][21]
- **Cold tolerance**: Natural genetic variants affecting PM20D1 expression (such as those between BALB/c and C57BL/6 mice) significantly impact thermogenic capacity[23]

Human genetic polymorphisms near the *PM20D1* locus are associated with body mass index and Alzheimer's disease risk, underscoring its clinical relevance.[24][19]

## Evolutionary Perspective on M20 Family Divergence

The M20 peptidase family illustrates remarkable functional divergence from a common ancestral enzyme framework. While maintaining the conserved co-catalytic zinc-binding mechanism characteristic of clan MH metallopeptidases, family members have evolved dramatically different:

**Substrate specificities**: The five mammalian M20 peptidases (ACY1, CNDP1, CNDP2, PM20D1, PM20D2) each regulate distinct regions of biochemical space. ACY1 hydrolyzes N-acetyl amino acids, CNDP1 regulates carnosine, CNDP2 catabolizes threonyl dipeptides, and PM20D1 controls N-fatty acyl amino acids—demonstrating that individual enzymes within the family, despite sequence homology, exhibit control over non-overlapping metabolic pathways.[25]

**Subcellular and tissue localization**: The acquisition of a membrane anchor by yeast CPS1 versus the secreted nature of PM20D1 represents a fundamental evolutionary divergence affecting protein trafficking, substrate accessibility, and biological context. PM20D1's circulation in blood and association with lipoproteins enables systemic regulation of metabolism, while CPS1's vacuolar localization restricts its function to intracellular protein degradation.[20]

**Functional neofunctionalization**: PM20D1's bidirectional synthase/hydrolase activity on lipid-amino acid conjugates represents a departure from classical peptidase activity. This "enzymatic convergence despite divergence in primary sequence" is exemplified by the finding that FAAH, a serine hydrolase sharing only 11% identity with PM20D1, also exhibits N-acyl amino acid hydrolase activity—suggesting this enzymatic function has evolved independently multiple times.[26]

**Absence of direct orthology**: Importantly, PM20D1 appears to lack a direct yeast ortholog. While yeast contains M20A subfamily members like CPS1 (Gly-Xaa carboxypeptidase), the specialized function of PM20D1 in N-fatty acyl amino acid metabolism represents a mammalian-specific innovation potentially related to the evolution of thermogenic adipose tissue. This parallels observations in plants, where M20D subfamily members (auxin amidohydrolases) underwent horizontal gene transfer and evolutionary exaptation into auxin regulatory pathways.[27][28][29][23]

## Cooperative Enzymatic Regulation: Lessons from PM20D1

Recent studies reveal that PM20D1 operates within a complex enzymatic network. The discovery that FAAH serves as a second intracellular N-acyl amino acid synthase/hydrolase demonstrates **enzymatic division of labor** between extracellular (PM20D1) and intracellular (FAAH) pathways for regulating these bioactive lipids. Dual blockade of PM20D1 and FAAH reveals non-additive, cooperative interactions that could not be predicted from individual enzyme properties—underscoring the importance of systems-level analysis in understanding metabolic pathway evolution.[22][26]

The M20 peptidase family thus provides a compelling example of how a conserved enzymatic scaffold can be redeployed across evolution to serve fundamentally different biological purposes—from vacuolar protein turnover in unicellular organisms to systemic metabolic regulation in mammals.
