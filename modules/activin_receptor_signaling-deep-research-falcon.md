# Activin Receptor Signaling Pathway Module: A Concise Review

## 1. Executive Summary

The activin receptor signaling pathway is a conserved branch of the transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β) superfamily that transduces extracellular activin ligand signals through membrane-bound serine/threonine kinase receptors to intracellular SMAD2/3-SMAD4 transcriptional programs. The system comprises three obligatory steps: (i) high-affinity engagement of activin dimers with type II receptors (ACVR2A/ACVR2B), (ii) recruitment and trans-phosphorylation of the type I receptor ACVR1B (ALK4), and (iii) phosphorylation of receptor-regulated SMADs (SMAD2 and SMAD3), which complex with SMAD4 and translocate to the nucleus to regulate target gene transcription (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 4-5, fox2024transforminggrowthfactor pages 4-5). This pathway governs growth, differentiation, reproductive physiology, immune regulation, and tissue homeostasis across metazoans, and its dysregulation is implicated in fibrosis, cancer, muscle wasting, and cardiovascular disease. Recent work has revealed additional layers of complexity, including competition among type I receptors for shared type II receptors, the formation of non-signaling complexes with wild-type ACVR1, lysosomal trafficking-based degradation as a regulatory mechanism, and non-canonical signal transduction through MAPK and MRTF-A pathways.

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### What the system includes

The activin receptor signaling module, as defined here, encompasses the ligand–receptor–SMAD cascade initiated by activin family ligands (principally activin A, a homodimer of INHBA β-subunits) binding to activin type II receptors (ACVR2A and ACVR2B), which recruit and activate the type I receptor ACVR1B (ALK4), leading to phosphorylation of SMAD2 and SMAD3, their association with the common mediator SMAD4, nuclear translocation, and transcriptional regulation of target genes (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 4-5, szilagyi2022competitionbetweentype pages 1-2, fox2024transforminggrowthfactor pages 4-5). Negative regulators integral to the system include the extracellular antagonist follistatin, inhibin heterodimers (containing INHA), the inhibitory SMAD7, and the membrane pseudoreceptor BAMBI (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 5-7, stepurko2023molecularinsightsinto pages 31-33).

### Neighboring but distinct pathways

Several closely related signaling cascades should be distinguished from the core activin module:

- **TGF-β signaling proper** uses TGF-β1/2/3 ligands, TGFBR2 as the type II receptor, and TGFBR1 (ALK5) as the type I receptor, converging on the same SMAD2/3–SMAD4 axis but employing distinct receptor complexes (fox2024transforminggrowthfactor pages 4-5).
- **BMP signaling** engages BMP type I receptors (ALK2/ACVR1, ALK3/BMPR1A, ALK6/BMPR1B) and activates the SMAD1/5/8 branch rather than SMAD2/3, despite sharing the type II receptors ACVR2A/ACVR2B with activin (szilagyi2022competitionbetweentype pages 1-2, ganjoo2022bonemorphogeneticproteins pages 3-5).
- **Nodal signaling** also activates SMAD2/3 through ALK4/ALK7 but requires the EGF-CFC co-receptors (e.g., Cripto/TDGF1) and operates primarily during embryonic axis specification (tindall2024thetgfβsuperfamily pages 2-3, fox2024transforminggrowthfactor pages 4-5).

The promiscuity of ACVR2A/ACVR2B as shared type II receptors for activins, BMPs, myostatin, and GDFs is a recurrent source of confusion in the literature. While activin signaling and BMP signaling share these receptors, the downstream outcome—SMAD2/3 versus SMAD1/5/8 activation—is determined by which type I receptor is recruited (szilagyi2022competitionbetweentype pages 1-2).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### Step 1: Ligand–receptor engagement

Activin ligands are disulfide-linked homodimers or heterodimers of inhibin β-subunits (βA and βB). Activin A (βA:βA) binds directly to the extracellular domains of type II receptors ACVR2A or ACVR2B at the outer convex surface of the β-subunit dimer with high affinity—activin A exhibits approximately six-fold higher affinity for type II receptors than activin B (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 4-5). Type II activin receptors are constitutively active serine/threonine kinases that do not require ligand binding for kinase activity but require ligand to recruit type I receptors (huang2024investigatingsmadsignaling pages 13-16, stepurko2023molecularinsightsinto pages 53-55).

### Step 2: Type I receptor activation

The ligand-bound type II receptor recruits ACVR1B (ALK4) at the interface between the two β dimeric chains. This assembly forms a heterotetrameric complex of two type II and two type I receptors (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 4-5). The constitutively active type II receptor kinase phosphorylates the GS domain (a conserved glycine-serine-rich motif, TTSGSGSG) of the type I receptor, relieving autoinhibition and activating the type I kinase. Phosphorylation of any five serine/threonine residues within the GS domain is sufficient for activation (stepurko2023molecularinsightsinto pages 53-55).

### Step 3: SMAD2/3 phosphorylation and nuclear translocation

The activated type I receptor phosphorylates SMAD2 and SMAD3 at their C-terminal SSXS motif (fox2024transforminggrowthfactor pages 4-5, huang2024investigatingsmadsignaling pages 13-16). Phosphorylated SMAD2/3 forms a trimeric complex with SMAD4 (two R-SMAD subunits and one SMAD4 subunit), which translocates to the nucleus and binds to Smad Binding Elements (SBEs) in the promoters of target genes to regulate transcription (fox2024transforminggrowthfactor pages 4-5). SMAD3 can directly bind DNA, whereas SMAD2 (which contains an additional exon 3 insert in its MH1 domain) requires partner transcription factors for DNA engagement (fox2024transforminggrowthfactor pages 4-5, huang2024investigatingsmadsignaling pages 13-16).

## 4. Major Molecular Players and Active Assemblies

### Ligands

Activin A is the canonical ligand for this module. Additional family members include activin B (βB:βB), activin AB (βA:βB), and activin C/E heterodimers, which show varying receptor affinities and tissue distributions (tindall2024thetgfβsuperfamily pages 2-3, tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 4-5). Notably, activin AC heterodimer can inhibit activin A signaling (stepurko2023molecularinsightsinto pages 31-33). Several other TGF-β superfamily ligands—including myostatin (GDF8), GDF11, and Nodal—also signal through overlapping receptor complexes and the SMAD2/3 branch, complicating pathway-specific attribution (ganjoo2022bonemorphogeneticproteins pages 3-5, tindall2024thetgfβsuperfamily pages 2-3).

### Receptors

ACVR2A and ACVR2B serve as the primary type II receptors. Structural studies demonstrate that ACVR2A shares a conserved mode of growth factor recognition with BMPR2, explaining the promiscuity of these receptors across multiple TGF-β superfamily ligands. ACVR1B (ALK4) is the principal type I receptor for canonical activin signaling, while ACVR1C (ALK7) serves as a context-dependent alternative type I receptor in selected tissues (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 4-5, ganjoo2022bonemorphogeneticproteins pages 3-5). Critically, type I activin receptors (ALK4) and BMP type I receptors (ALK2/3/6) compete for binding to the shared type II receptor ACVR2A, providing a mechanism for fine-tuning the balance between SMAD2/3 and SMAD1/5/8 signaling (szilagyi2022competitionbetweentype pages 1-2).

### Non-signaling complexes and regulatory mechanisms

Wild-type ACVR1 (ALK2), despite its historical name as an "activin receptor," does not transduce activin A signals. Instead, activin A binds ACVR1 to form stoichiometrically identical but functionally distinct non-signaling complexes (NSCs) with type II receptors (hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 1-2, srinivasan2024howactivina pages 8-9). These NSCs undergo rapid endocytosis and lysosomal degradation, effectively sequestering activin A, depleting ACVR1, and reducing type II receptor availability at the cell surface (hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 2-3, hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 5-7, hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 3-5). This lysosomal trafficking mechanism coordinately downregulates both activin availability and BMP signaling capacity through ACVR1, representing a recently discovered regulatory layer (hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 7-10). This property is specific to activin A; activin B, AB, and AC do not form lysosomal trafficking complexes with ACVR1 (hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 5-7).

### Non-canonical signaling

Beyond the canonical SMAD2/3–SMAD4 pathway, activin receptors activate SMAD-independent signaling routes. Activin A activates p38 MAPK to regulate pro-endocrine and erythroid genes independently of SMAD signaling (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 26-27). MEKK1 transduces activin signals in keratinocytes to promote actin stress fiber formation and migration (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 26-27). Activin A also engages the MRTF-A (myocardin-related transcription factor A) pathway to regulate α-smooth muscle actin expression, operating independently of canonical TGF-β signaling components such as TGFBR1, SMAD2, or SMAD4 (soomro2023atherapeutictarget pages 7-10). Additionally, PI3K-AKT signaling has been implicated in activin A's roles in ovarian biology (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 26-27).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### Evolutionary origins and conservation

TGF-β superfamily signaling is deeply conserved across metazoans. Early metazoans such as sponges and placozoans possessed a minimal complement of two type I and one type II receptor genes (jatzlau2026recurrentevolutionof pages 10-10). With the emergence of bilateral symmetry, the TGF-β ligand repertoire expanded alongside receptor signaling refinement. Vertebrate activin receptor repertoires were markedly expanded by two rounds of whole-genome duplication, with additional lineage-specific duplications occurring in teleost fishes, *Xenopus laevis*, carps, and salmonids (jatzlau2026recurrentevolutionof pages 10-10). A recent study identified twelve independent ligand-binding domain (LBD) multiplications across ACVR1, BMPR2, and TGFBR2 receptor classes in diverse vertebrate lineages including fish, amphibians, birds, and mammals, demonstrating recurrent structural plasticity superimposed on strong conservation of the receptor core architecture (jatzlau2026recurrentevolutionof pages 10-10).

In the lamprey (*Lethenteron reissneri*), a jawless vertebrate representative with over 350 million years of evolutionary history, TGF-β subfamily members are present and functionally active, with the TGF-β2 ortholog playing a central role in innate immune regulation. The activin/inhibin β-subunit family and downstream SMAD effectors are conserved from jawless vertebrates through mammals. In zebrafish, three activin/inhibin β-subunits (βAa/inhbaa, βAb/inhbab, and βB/inhbb) have been characterized, with CRISPR/Cas9 knockout studies revealing essential roles in folliculogenesis, ovarian homeostasis, and post-hatching survival. In teleost fish, the ACVR2A–ALK4–SMAD2 axis has been shown to play a predominant role in growth regulation, consistent with deep conservation of the core signaling module across vertebrates (tindall2024thetgfβsuperfamily pages 2-3).

### Cell-type and tissue variation

The activin signaling module exhibits substantial context-dependent variation. In cardiac tissue, INHBA/ACVR2A signaling preferentially promotes SMAD3 activation while inhibiting SMAD2, facilitating cardiac recovery and scar clearance after injury. Conversely, MSTNB/ACVR2B signaling promotes SMAD2 activation while inhibiting SMAD3, reducing myocardial cell proliferation (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 17-19). In the urinary bladder, urothelial cells express the ACVR2B/ACVR1B receptor complex, while activin A-positive interstitial cells regulate urothelial function via paracrine signaling through a basement membrane barrier (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 4-5). In skeletal muscle, simultaneous knockout of TGFBR1 and ACVR1B induces synergistic hypertrophy through increased AKT and p70S6K phosphorylation, whereas single receptor knockout does not produce this effect (szilagyi2022competitionbetweentype pages 1-2). In reproductive tissues, activin A directs fetal germ cell differentiation through direct action on gonocytes, promoting a differentiated transcript profile including increased expression of DNMT3L, NANOS2, and PIWIL4 (soomro2023atherapeutictarget pages 7-10).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### Obligatory ordering

The signaling cascade follows a strict sequential order: (i) ligand binding to type II receptors must precede type I receptor recruitment, as the type II receptor provides the high-affinity binding site; (ii) type II receptor-mediated phosphorylation of the type I receptor GS domain is prerequisite for type I kinase activation; (iii) SMAD2/3 phosphorylation must occur before SMAD4 complex formation and nuclear translocation (fox2024transforminggrowthfactor pages 4-5, huang2024investigatingsmadsignaling pages 13-16, stepurko2023molecularinsightsinto pages 53-55).

### Compartment-specific constraints

The lysosomal trafficking of activin A–ACVR1 non-signaling complexes is temporally regulated: cells recover BMP responsiveness within approximately 2 hours through new protein synthesis-mediated receptor replenishment (hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 3-5). Kinase activity of ACVR1 is required for lysosomal trafficking—kinase-dead or R206H mutant ACVR1 fail to internalize activin A–receptor complexes (hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 10-14).

### Mutual exclusivity

The competition between activin type I receptors (ALK4) and BMP type I receptors (ALK2/3/6) for binding to shared type II receptors (ACVR2A) creates a mutually exclusive allocation of signaling capacity: overexpression of a competing type I receptor reduces signaling through the opposing SMAD branch (szilagyi2022competitionbetweentype pages 1-2). This provides a cell-intrinsic mechanism for balancing SMAD2/3 versus SMAD1/5/8 output based on relative receptor expression levels.

### Disease-associated failure modes

OpenTargets analysis reveals strong disease associations for all core pathway components: SMAD4 is most strongly associated with pancreatic adenocarcinoma (score 0.69) and colorectal adenocarcinoma (0.68); ACVR2A with colorectal (0.61) and pancreatic (0.53) adenocarcinoma; ACVR1B with colorectal (0.54) and pancreatic (0.47) adenocarcinoma (OpenTargets Search: -ACVR1B,ACVR2A,SMAD2,SMAD3,SMAD4,INHBA). Loss of SMAD4 function is a hallmark of pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, while frameshift mutations in ACVR2A microsatellite coding repeats are recurrent in microsatellite-instable colorectal cancers (OpenTargets Search: -ACVR1B,ACVR2A,SMAD2,SMAD3,SMAD4,INHBA).

The activin R206H mutation in ACVR1 causes fibrodysplasia ossificans progressiva (FOP), converting the normally non-signaling activin A–ACVR1 complex into an aberrant SMAD1/5 signaling complex that drives heterotopic ossification (srinivasan2024howactivina pages 9-11, srinivasan2024howactivina pages 8-9). Garetosmab, an anti-activin A antibody, achieved approximately 97% relative reduction in new heterotopic ossification lesion activity in the phase 2 LUMINA-1 trial (NCT03188666) in FOP patients, though the primary endpoint did not reach statistical significance (p=0.07) (srinivasan2024howactivina pages 5-6).

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### SMAD2 versus SMAD3 functional divergence

Although SMAD2 and SMAD3 are co-activated downstream of ACVR1B, emerging evidence suggests they have non-redundant and sometimes opposing functions. In cardiac tissue, selective promotion of SMAD3 versus SMAD2 by distinct receptor combinations produces qualitatively different biological outcomes (tang2024activinsandinhibins pages 17-19). Deep learning approaches have been applied to deconvolve SMAD2- versus SMAD3-specific chromatin binding sites, revealing that these closely related transcription factors regulate distinct gene sets, but the lack of SMAD-specific antibodies for ChIP-seq has historically limited the resolution of this question (huang2024investigatingsmadsignaling pages 13-16).

### Non-signaling complexes: mechanism and physiological significance

The discovery that wild-type ACVR1 forms non-signaling complexes with activin A that undergo lysosomal degradation represents a paradigm-shifting regulatory mechanism. However, the molecular basis for why stoichiometrically identical complexes (ACVR1–activin A–type II receptor versus ACVR1B–activin A–type II receptor) produce opposing outcomes (degradation versus signaling) remains unknown, though it likely involves ligand-induced conformational changes in the receptor complex (hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 1-2, hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 7-10). The physiological roles of this lysosomal trafficking mechanism remain largely unexplored (hom2024coordinateregulationof pages 1-2).

### Receptor promiscuity and pathway crosstalk

The shared usage of ACVR2A/ACVR2B by activins, BMPs, myostatin, and GDFs creates extensive pathway crosstalk that confounds the assignment of biological effects to specific ligand–receptor pairings (ganjoo2022bonemorphogeneticproteins pages 3-5, tindall2024thetgfβsuperfamily pages 2-3). Competition between type I receptors for common type II receptors provides a novel balancing mechanism, but the quantitative parameters governing this competition in physiological contexts are poorly defined (szilagyi2022competitionbetweentype pages 1-2).

### Non-canonical pathway contributions

The relative contributions of canonical SMAD2/3 signaling versus non-canonical pathways (p38 MAPK, MRTF-A, PI3K-AKT) to specific activin biological outputs remain incompletely characterized. In renal mesangial cells, activin A supports profibrotic TGF-β1 effects through both canonical SMAD3 and non-canonical MRTF-A pathways, with additive rather than synergistic effects, suggesting distinct parallel mechanisms (soomro2023atherapeutictarget pages 7-10). The extent to which non-canonical signaling varies across cell types and physiological states is an active area of investigation.

### Evolutionary plasticity

Recent identification of recurrent ligand-binding domain multiplications in TGF-β receptors across diverse vertebrate lineages reveals an unexpected dimension of structural plasticity superimposed on the highly conserved receptor core architecture (jatzlau2026recurrentevolutionof pages 10-10). The functional consequences of these domain-level innovations for activin signaling specificity in non-model organisms remain to be determined.
