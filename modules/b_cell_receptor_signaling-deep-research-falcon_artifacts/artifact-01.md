| Cross-linking / clustering model | Multivalent antigen cross-links monomeric BCRs, increasing local receptor density and ITAM availability so SRC-family kinases can phosphorylate CD79A/CD79B and initiate signaling (pqac-00000011, pqac-00000006) | Historically supported by robust signaling with multivalent and membrane-displayed antigens; clustering clearly correlates with signal amplification, microcluster formation, and downstream propagation after engagement (pqac-00000008, pqac-00000010) | Does not satisfactorily explain triggering by monovalent antigens, signal spread to non-engaged BCRs, or situations where clustering seems secondary rather than initiating; clustering alone is no longer sufficient as a universal explanation (pqac-00000011, pqac-00000006) | Still considered important for amplification and efficient triggering by high-valency antigens, but no longer favored as a complete standalone model; now treated as one component of a broader unified framework (pqac-00000008, pqac-00000009) |
| Conformation-induced oligomerization model | Antigen binding induces a structural change in the BCR that exposes oligomerization interfaces or alters transmembrane/cytoplasmic accessibility, promoting signaling-competent receptor assemblies (pqac-00000006, pqac-00000012) | FRET-based studies and structural analyses support antigen-associated conformational rearrangements in IgM- and IgG-BCRs; mechanical studies suggest that force could allosterically regulate receptor structure and signal transmission across the membrane (pqac-00000005, pqac-00000012, pqac-00000010) | Direct causal evidence remains incomplete: conformational changes can occur without signaling, and monovalent soluble antigens can trigger despite being unlikely to impose the same structural perturbations; the exact transmembrane allosteric path is unresolved (pqac-00000005, pqac-00000006) | Viewed as plausible and compatible with structural and biophysical data, especially for membrane-associated antigens, but not established as a universal triggering mechanism; best regarded as a modulator within a multimechanism model (pqac-00000008, pqac-00000012) |
| Dissociation-activation model | Resting BCRs are proposed to exist in autoinhibited oligomers or clusters; antigen binding disperses these assemblies, exposing CD79 ITAMs or relieving steric inhibition so kinases can act (pqac-00000006, pqac-00000011) | Addresses the problem of monovalent antigen triggering and is conceptually consistent with reports that resting BCRs can exist in higher-order assemblies or protein islands rather than as fully isolated monomers (pqac-00000005, pqac-00000007) | Evidence for stable inhibitory oligomers in resting B cells is inconsistent; more recent work suggests predominantly monomeric receptors or loosely organized nanoscale islands, weakening strong versions of the model (pqac-00000005, pqac-00000006) | Remains an influential alternative hypothesis, especially for explaining monovalent ligands, but is not broadly accepted as the dominant mechanism; increasingly incorporated into a dynamic equilibrium view rather than treated as exclusive (pqac-00000008, pqac-00000009) |
| Antigen-footprint / kinetic segregation model | Antigen engagement creates a membrane contact zone that physically excludes large inhibitory phosphatases such as CD45/CD148, shifting the kinase-phosphatase balance toward phosphorylation and signaling; antigen size, rigidity, and geometry determine effectiveness (pqac-00000006, pqac-00000007) | Explains how monovalent antigens can trigger if they generate sufficient steric footprint; integrates well with membrane-bound antigen presentation, spatial organization, and force-sensitive immune synapse biology; compatible with data that mechanical context and antigen geometry shape BCR output (pqac-00000007, pqac-00000010) | Hard to test directly in all systems; footprint requirements likely vary by antigen, isotype, and membrane context; does not exclude additional contributions from conformational changes or clustering (pqac-00000008, pqac-00000010) | Among the strongest current frameworks for initiation, especially for membrane-associated antigen recognition; in 2024-2025 it is often presented as central to a unifying model in which spatial phosphatase exclusion, clustering, and conformational effects all contribute (pqac-00000008, pqac-00000009) |
