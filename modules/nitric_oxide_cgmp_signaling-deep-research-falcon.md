# The Nitric Oxide–cGMP Signaling Pathway Module: A Review-Style Synthesis

## 1. Executive Summary

The nitric oxide (NO)–cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP) signaling pathway is a conserved signal transduction module in which NO produced by nitric oxide synthases (NOS) activates soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC) to synthesize cGMP from GTP. The resulting cGMP signal is transduced primarily by cGMP-dependent protein kinase I (PRKG1/PKG I), which phosphorylates substrates controlling vascular smooth muscle tone, calcium homeostasis, and cellular remodeling. The signal is terminated by phosphodiesterase 5A (PDE5A), which hydrolyzes cGMP to 5′-GMP. This three-step cascade—NO production → sGC activation → cGMP effector/termination—represents one of the best-characterized paracrine signaling modules in mammalian physiology. Its core components are deeply conserved across metazoans, with NO signaling documented from placozoans to vertebrates (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 6-8, locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 1-2). The pathway is of major therapeutic importance: sGC stimulators (riociguat, vericiguat), sGC activators, and PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) target distinct nodes of this module for the treatment of pulmonary hypertension, heart failure, and erectile dysfunction (benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 4-6, benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 1-2).

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### What is included

The NO–sGC–cGMP pathway module, as defined here, encompasses three obligatory steps: (i) enzymatic production of NO by NOS isoforms, particularly endothelial NOS (NOS3/eNOS); (ii) activation of cytosolic sGC (an α1β1 heterodimer encoded by *GUCY1A1* and *GUCY1B1*) through NO binding to its heme prosthetic group, catalyzing GTP → cGMP conversion; and (iii) downstream signal transduction by the cGMP effector kinase PRKG1 and signal termination by PDE5A (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 1-2, mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 4-6, kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 4-6).

### Neighboring pathways that should be distinguished

The natriuretic peptide–particulate guanylate cyclase (pGC) pathway also generates cGMP but employs transmembrane receptor guanylyl cyclases (GC-A activated by ANP/BNP, GC-B by CNP, GC-C by guanylin) rather than the soluble, NO-responsive sGC. Crucially, these two cGMP-generating systems have distinct subcellular localizations and may activate PKG in different intracellular compartments, leading to divergent downstream effects (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8, bajec2025targetingtheno–sgc–cgmp pages 8-10, kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 2-4). NO also exerts cGMP-independent effects through S-nitrosylation, S-glutathionylation, and tyrosine nitration of target proteins (lee2023nitricoxidean pages 2-4). These non-canonical NO actions should be distinguished from the sGC-dependent pathway module described here, even though they may operate in parallel within the same cells (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 1-2).

Additionally, undifferentiated embryonic stem cells lack functional sGC expression, meaning that NO effects in these cells are cGMP-independent; functional NO–sGC–cGMP signaling requires differentiation-dependent sGC expression (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 9-10).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### Step 1: Nitric oxide production

The initiating event is enzymatic synthesis of NO from L-arginine by NOS enzymes. In the canonical vascular setting, NOS3 (eNOS) in endothelial cells is the primary NO source for paracrine signaling to adjacent vascular smooth muscle cells, although NOS1 (nNOS) also contributes in cardiomyocytes and neurons (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8, kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 1-2). NOS activity is calcium/calmodulin-dependent and further regulated by phosphorylation, protein-protein interactions, and subcellular localization. The cGMP-dependent pathway is activated at NO concentrations in the 1–30 nM range (lee2023nitricoxidean pages 2-4).

### Step 2: Soluble guanylate cyclase activation

NO diffuses across cell membranes to reach its intracellular target, soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC). sGC is a heterodimeric enzyme composed of an α1 subunit (GUCY1A1) and a β1 subunit (GUCY1B1) that together form the catalytically active complex (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 2-4). The β1 subunit carries a non-covalently bound ferrous (Fe²⁺) heme prosthetic group that serves as the NO sensor. Upon NO binding to heme iron, sGC undergoes a conformational change that activates its catalytic domain, increasing cGMP synthesis 100- to 200-fold (locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 1-2, kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 2-4).

The maturation and assembly of functional sGC is itself regulated: heme insertion into the sGCβ subunit requires GAPDH and the Hsp90-apo-sGCβ complex through an ATP-dependent process. Once heme is properly inserted, Hsp90 and GAPDH dissociate, allowing sGCβ to pair with sGCα to form the active heterodimer essential for NO responsiveness (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8).

Under oxidative stress, the heme iron can be oxidized to the ferric (Fe³⁺) state, which renders sGC insensitive to NO and may lead to heme loss, producing heme-deficient apo-sGC. This redox-dependent switching between holo-sGC and apo-sGC is pharmacologically exploitable: sGC stimulators target the heme-containing form while sGC activators target the heme-free form (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 2-4, benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 4-6).

In cardiomyocytes, membrane-localized sGC (GC-1) resides in caveolin-enriched microdomains near T-tubules, enabling rapid local cGMP production. Oxidative stress can dislocate GC-1 from these microdomains, reducing cGMP generation capacity (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8).

### Step 3: cGMP effector signaling and termination

**PRKG1 (PKG I):** The primary downstream effector of sGC-derived cGMP is cGMP-dependent protein kinase I (PRKG1), predominantly the PKGIα isoform in the heart and vasculature (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 4-6, kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 4-6). Upon cGMP binding, PKGIα translocates from diffuse cytosolic distribution to the plasma membrane, where it phosphorylates multiple substrates (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8):

- **IRAG1** (encoded by *MRVI1*): PKGIβ forms a trimeric complex with IRAG1 and IP3 receptor type I (IP3R-I) at ER/SR membranes. Phosphorylation of IRAG1 at serine 696 (bovine)/677 (human) inhibits IP3R-mediated Ca²⁺ release, a mechanism critical for cGMP-dependent smooth muscle relaxation (pruschenk2023novelfunctionalfeatures pages 2-4, pruschenk2023novelfunctionalfeatures pages 4-6, pruschenk2023novelfunctionalfeatures pages 1-2).
- **Myosin light chain phosphatase (MLCP):** PKG1 directly activates MLCP, promoting smooth muscle relaxation by dephosphorylating myosin light chains (feo2025influenceofsoluble pages 14-16, mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 4-6).
- **RhoA, RGS2, TRPC channels, and N-type Ca²⁺ channels:** PKG phosphorylation of these targets modulates vasoconstrictor signaling, G-protein-coupled receptor activity, and ion channel function (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 4-6, feo2025influenceofsoluble pages 14-16).

An important modifier of PKG1α function is its oxidation status. Oxidation at cysteine 42 alters PKG1α intramyocyte localization and downstream signaling capacity, coupling myocardial oxidative stress to pathological remodeling (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8).

**PDE5A:** Phosphodiesterase 5A hydrolyzes cGMP to 5′-GMP, serving as the primary negative regulator that limits the duration and spatial spread of NO-derived cGMP signals. PDE5A activity is regulated allosterically by cGMP binding to its N-terminal GAF-A domain. Furthermore, PKG-dependent phosphorylation of PDE5A enhances its catalytic activity, establishing a negative feedback loop that autoregulates cGMP levels (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 4-6, kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 4-6).

Notably, cGMP also modulates other PDEs in a concentration-dependent manner: low cGMP concentrations (0.1–10 nmol/L) inhibit PDE3A (enhancing contractility), while higher levels (100 nmol/L–1 µmol/L) activate PDE2A (dampening β-adrenergic signaling) (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### Evolutionary conservation

NO signaling is deeply conserved across metazoans and was suggested to be present in the earliest life forms on Earth (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 6-8). Guanylyl cyclases and cGMP emerged in primitive early life forms including protostome and deuterostome invertebrates and developed into hormone/messenger receptor cascades. NO-cGMP-mediated signaling has been documented in placozoans, cnidarians (Hydra), mollusks, crustaceans, and chordates (locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 16-18, prelic2023modulationofthe pages 2-3, locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 15-16).

### NOS gene expansion

A pivotal evolutionary transition is the expansion of NOS genes. Most invertebrates, including Drosophila, carry a single NOS gene (dNOS), while vertebrates possess three paralogous NOS genes (NOS1/nNOS, NOS2/iNOS, NOS3/eNOS) arising from whole-genome duplication events (locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 5-7, jeong2024functionandregulation pages 1-2, locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 1-2). The canonical NO-sGC signaling pathway mediated by soluble guanylate cyclase is conserved between vertebrates and invertebrates, but some intracellular mediators appear to be lineage-specific (jeong2024functionandregulation pages 1-2). In cephalochordates like amphioxus, three NOS genes (NosA, NosB, NosC) originated from independent cephalochordate-specific duplications rather than the vertebrate genome-duplication events (locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 5-7).

The vertebrate-specific NOS3 (eNOS) isoform is specialized for vascular endothelial signaling. The "eNOS-like" vascular specialization is therefore not universal across metazoans—many invertebrates use their single NOS gene in contexts analogous to neuronal, immune, and vascular functions simultaneously (jeong2024functionandregulation pages 1-2, locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 1-2).

### Tissue and cell-type variation

The pathway operates in distinct functional contexts depending on cell type:
- **Vascular smooth muscle:** The canonical relaxation pathway with prominent expression of sGC (GUCY1A1/GUCY1B1), PRKG1, and PDE5A.
- **Cardiomyocytes:** sGC localizes to caveolin-enriched microdomains near T-tubules; PKG1α and PDE5A regulate contractility, remodeling, and survival signaling (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8).
- **Platelets:** sGC activity in platelets controls activation and thrombosis; loss of sGC in platelets contributes to atherosclerotic plaque formation through reduced angiopoietin-1 release (pruschenk2023novelfunctionalfeatures pages 2-4, pruschenk2023novelfunctionalfeatures pages 1-2).
- **Neurons:** NO-cGMP signaling regulates synaptic plasticity and neurotransmission; NOS expression in olfactory sensory neurons is broadly conserved among non-insect animals (prelic2023modulationofthe pages 2-3).
- **Stem cells:** Undifferentiated ES cells lack functional sGC, so NO acts through cGMP-independent mechanisms; differentiation upregulates sGC expression enabling canonical pathway engagement (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 9-10).

The paracrine nature of NO and its membrane permeability mean that different NOS isoforms can compensate for each other. NOS-deficient animals manifest multiple cardiovascular defects, indicating the pathway's critical developmental importance, but single-NOS knockout models often show modest phenotypes due to compensation (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 6-8).

### Functional conservation across aquatic chordates

Conserved NO functions across aquatic chordates include regulation of germinal cell maturation, anterior head structure development, larval settlement behavior, immune modulation, and sensory processing. These roles are documented from tunicates and cephalochordates to teleost fishes and amphibians (locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 13-15, locascio2023nitricoxidefunction pages 5-7).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### Obligatory ordering

The pathway has a strict sequential order: NO must be produced before sGC can be activated, and sGC must generate cGMP before PRKG1 can phosphorylate its targets. PDE5A requires substrate cGMP to exert its hydrolytic function. PDE5 inhibitors, for instance, are ineffective when upstream NO-sGC activity is insufficient to generate the cGMP they are designed to protect from degradation (benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 4-6, benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 1-2).

### Redox-dependent switching

The heme oxidation state of sGC represents a critical constraint. Under normal reducing conditions, ferrous heme binds NO to activate cGMP synthesis. Oxidative stress converts heme to the ferric state or causes heme loss, rendering sGC NO-insensitive. This redox switch has direct therapeutic implications: sGC stimulators require heme-containing holo-sGC, whereas sGC activators bypass this requirement by targeting the heme-free enzyme (benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 4-6, kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 2-4).

### Compartmentalization constraints

Subcellular localization of sGC and its corresponding PDEs creates intracellular nanodomains of cGMP signaling. The NO-sGC pathway and the natriuretic peptide-pGC pathway generate cGMP in different intracellular compartments, potentially activating PKG at distinct sites with different substrate access (bajec2025targetingtheno–sgc–cgmp pages 8-10, mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8). Redistribution of sGC away from caveolin-enriched domains under oxidative stress impairs localized cGMP generation (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8).

### Genetic constraints

Rare variants in *GUCY1A1* and *GUCY1B1* that impair sGC function are associated with increased risk of hypertension at exome-wide significance in large-scale human genetics studies, confirming the non-redundant physiological importance of this pathway in blood pressure regulation (kots2024regulationandpharmacology pages 6-8). Activating PRKG1 variants have been linked to thoracic aortic aneurysm and dissection, highlighting that both loss- and gain-of-function mutations in pathway components cause disease.

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

Several important uncertainties remain in the field:

1. **Nanodomain specificity:** While compartmentalized cGMP signaling is well accepted, no analog of the A-kinase anchoring proteins (AKAPs) that organize cAMP signaling has been identified for the cGMP/PKG system. Whether localized cGMP signaling relies on similar scaffold proteins or on distinct physical mechanisms remains an open question (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8).

2. **Redox regulation of PKG1α:** The functional consequences of PKG1α oxidation at cysteine 42 are increasingly recognized as important in cardiac pathology, but the precise conditions under which oxidized versus reduced PKG1α drives distinct signaling outcomes—and whether this is therapeutically exploitable—remain under active investigation (mishra2025cardiaccgmpregulation pages 7-8).

3. **Mechanistic distinction between sGC stimulators and PDE5 inhibitors:** Although the dual mechanism of sGC stimulators (NO-independent stimulation plus NO sensitization) theoretically provides advantages over PDE5 inhibitors under conditions of NO depletion, extensive research is still needed to clarify the clinical significance of these mechanistic differences across disease states (benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 10-11, benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 4-6).

4. **sGC activator development:** Clinical development of sGC activators has been hampered by systemic hypotension (as seen with cinaciguat). Inhaled delivery strategies (e.g., mosliciguat) represent attempts to achieve local activation while minimizing systemic effects, but optimal clinical applications remain undefined (benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 4-6, benza2024thenitricoxide–soluble pages 10-11).

5. **Cross-species extrapolation:** While the canonical NO-sGC pathway is conserved, some intracellular mediators of NO signaling appear to be vertebrate-specific or invertebrate-specific. Care is needed when extrapolating findings from model organisms like *Drosophila* (which has a single NOS gene and no strict eNOS ortholog) to mammalian vascular biology (jeong2024functionandregulation pages 1-2).

6. **IRAG1 mechanism:** Although IRAG1 is established as a critical PKGIβ substrate linking cGMP to IP3R-mediated Ca²⁺ release inhibition, the precise molecular mechanism by which PKG-mediated IRAG1 phosphorylation alters IP3R function remains incompletely resolved (pruschenk2023novelfunctionalfeatures pages 16-17).

7. **Compensatory signaling in NOS deficiency:** Proteomic studies in triple-NOS knockout mice reveal that complete loss of NO signaling triggers adaptive rewiring including enhanced MAPK3/MAPK1 (ERK1/2) signaling, which may represent a maladaptive compensatory mechanism promoting pathological cardiomyopathy. The therapeutic implications of this rewiring remain to be explored.
