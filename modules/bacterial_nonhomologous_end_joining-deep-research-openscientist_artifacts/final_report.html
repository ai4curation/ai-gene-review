Bacterial Non-Homologous End Joining: The Ku/LigD Module

A review-style synthesis of the compact prokaryotic system for Ku/LigD-mediated repair of DNA double-strand breaks

1. Executive Summary

Bacterial non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) is the minimal, self-contained pathway by which many prokaryotes reseal DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs) without a homologous template. In stark contrast to the multi-protein eukaryotic apparatus (Ku70/Ku80, DNA-PKcs, Artemis, XRCC4/Ligase IV, XLF, PAXX, and others), the bacterial system is built from just two dedicated proteins: a homodimeric Ku DNA-end-binding factor and a multifunctional DNA ligase, LigD. Together these two players carry out every activity the reaction requires — end recognition, end protection, end bridging (synapsis), end processing (fill-in, resection, and nucleotide "healing"), and ATP-dependent sealing (PMID: 17506672; PMID: 12215643).

The best-supported mechanistic model is straightforward and sequential: Ku homodimers thread onto and slide along broken duplex ends, protect them from nucleolytic degradation, bridge the two ends into a synaptic complex, and recruit LigD via a species-variable Ku C-terminus. LigD then uses three autonomous catalytic domains — an archaeo-eukaryotic-primase (AEP)-like polymerase (POL), a manganese-dependent 3'-phosphoesterase (PE), and an ATP-dependent ligase (LIG) — to remodel incompatible ends into a ligatable configuration and seal the backbone (PMID: 16023671; PMID: 16446439). The pathway is template-free and intrinsically error-prone, leaving molecular scars — single-nucleotide (frequently ribonucleotide) insertions, templated fill-ins, and short deletions — at the junctions (PMID: 15778718).

Three broad conclusions emerge from this investigation. First, bacterial NHEJ is physiologically specialized for the non-replicating state — stationary phase, dormancy, and spores — precisely the conditions where a sister chromatid is unavailable and homologous recombination (HR) cannot operate (PMID: 17360246; PMID: 17496093). Second, the module is assembled from ancient, redeployed protein folds — a Ku core shared with eukaryotes and a polymerase from the AEP primase clan — and is distributed patchily across bacteria through gene loss and horizontal transfer, even being pirated by mycobacteriophages (PMID: 11483577; PMID: 16949369). Third, the "minimal" two-component system is flanked by backup and alternative routes — LigC/LigC1 ligases, the PrimC primase, and a faithful, Ku/LigD-independent joining of cohesive 3'-overhangs — so that end structure and physiological context, not a single fixed enzyme set, dictate outcome (PMID: 18281464; PMID: 24957619).

2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

2.1 What is included

The system reviewed here is the species-neutral Ku/LigD module for DSB repair by NHEJ in bacteria. Its defining features are:

  • Ku — a prokaryotic, homodimeric DNA-end-binding protein that recognizes broken double-stranded DNA ends in a sequence-independent manner, protects and aligns them, and recruits the ligase.
  • LigD — a dedicated, modular NHEJ enzyme that provides end-processing (polymerase and nuclease/phosphoesterase) and ATP-dependent ligation activities within a single polypeptide.
  • The functional coupling between them: Ku-bound, synapsed ends provide the substrate context for LigD-mediated processing and sealing (PMID: 16023671; PMID: 20018881).

2.2 What is excluded and what is commonly confused

Several neighboring processes are frequently discussed alongside bacterial NHEJ but are mechanistically distinct and lie outside this scope:

Process Why it is separate
Homologous recombination (HR) — RecA/RecBCD/AdnAB Template-dependent, error-free, requires a homologous donor; dominant in replicating cells. HR and NHEJ are parallel, partly redundant DSB routes; loss of one is compensated by the other (PMID: 21219454; PMID: 24658131).
Single-strand annealing (SSA) A third, RecA-independent DSB route in mycobacteria that uses RecBCD; distinct machinery and logic (PMID: 21219454).
Mismatch repair, nucleotide excision repair, base excision repair Repair single-strand lesions, not DSBs. Note that some NHEJ proteins moonlight in BER (Ku AP-lyase activity; LigD/LigC/PrimC in BER), which can blur assays but does not change the DSB-repair definition (PMID: 25355514; PMID: 33918798).
Eukaryotic NHEJ Shares a Ku core and functional logic but requires many additional factors (DNA-PKcs, XRCC4-LigIV, XLF, Artemis). The bacterial system is a two-protein reduction, not a scaled-down copy (PMID: 17506672).

2.3 Competing definitions

The literature is largely consistent in defining bacterial NHEJ as the Ku + LigD two-component system (PMID: 17938628). The main definitional ambiguity concerns how expansive "the pathway" is. A strict definition limits it to Ku + LigD. A broader, more accurate definition recognizes that in organisms such as Mycobacterium smegmatis, NHEJ is a network in which the LigD POL domain can partner with backup ligases (LigC1) or stand-alone POL-domain paralogs (PolD1/PolD2), and in which accessory factors (a Sir2-like deacetylase) modulate efficiency (PMID: 24957619; PMID: 23198659; PMID: 21637345). This review adopts the two-protein core as the boundary while explicitly cataloguing the accessory and backup layer.

3. Mechanistic Overview

3.1 The canonical sequence of events

The best current model runs as follows:

                                ▼

                                ▼

3.2 Obligatory, conditional, and accessory steps

  • Obligatory: DNA-end recognition/protection by Ku and phosphodiester sealing by an ATP-dependent ligase (normally LigD LIG) are required for repair of blunt and 5'-overhang breaks in vivo; deleting ku or ligD abolishes NHEJ and sensitizes non-replicating cells to ionizing radiation (PMID: 21219454; PMID: 12215643).
  • Conditional: End processing (POL fill-in, PE healing) is invoked only when ends are not directly ligatable. Blunt and 5'-overhang breaks depend stringently on the POL domain; cohesive 3'-overhangs can bypass Ku/LigD entirely (PMID: 18281464).
  • Accessory: Backup ligases (LigC1), stand-alone POL paralogs (PolD1/PolD2), and modulators (Sir2-like deacetylase) contribute redundancy and regulation but are individually dispensable under standard conditions (PMID: 24957619; PMID: 23198659; PMID: 21637345).

4. Major Molecular Players and Active Assemblies

4.1 Key Finding 1 — Bacterial NHEJ is a minimal, autonomous two-component system (Ku + LigD)

Bacterial NHEJ collapses the eukaryotic multi-factor apparatus into just two proteins that between them possess all the break-recognition, end-processing, and ligation activities required. This was first predicted bioinformatically by Aravind & Koonin (2001), who noted that prokaryotic Ku homologs form predicted operons with genes for an ATP-dependent DNA ligase and/or an archaeo-eukaryotic-type primase — the signature of a self-contained DSB-repair cassette (PMID: 11483577). The authoritative microbial review of Pitcher, Brissett & Doherty (2007) states the point directly: "the bacterial NHEJ complex is a two-component system that, despite its relative simplicity, possesses all of the break-recognition, end-processing, and ligation activities required to facilitate the complex task of DSB repair" (PMID: 17506672). Genetics confirm the functional minimalism: ablating ku or ligD abolishes NHEJ and renders non-replicating mycobacteria hypersensitive to ionizing radiation (PMID: 21219454; PMID: 24957619).

4.2 Key Finding 2 — LigD is a modular enzyme with three autonomous catalytic domains

LigD is the "Swiss-army knife" of the system. Domain dissection of M. tuberculosis LigD demonstrated that its polymerase (primase), nuclease/phosphoesterase, and ligase functions reside in physically separable, individually active domains (PMID: 16023671). Weller et al.'s and later structural work established the modular architecture directly: "LigD consists of an ATP-dependent ligase domain fused to a polymerase domain (Pol) and a phosphoesterase module" (PMID: 16446439). The three modules are:

Domain Fold / family Activity Notes
POL (polymerase) AEP / archaeal-primase-like Templated & non-templated (ribo)nucleotide addition; gap fill; end bridging Two-metal mechanism, Mn-dependent, prefers rNTPs; the direct catalyst of mutagenic NHEJ in vivo
PE (3'-phosphoesterase) Novel bifunctional phosphoesterase family Mn-dependent 3'-ribonuclease + 3'-phosphatase ("end healing") Resects 3'-ribonucleotide tracts; requires penultimate 2'-OH for the ribonuclease step
LIG (ligase) ATP-dependent DNA ligase Nick sealing Requires active-site lysine for adenylation

The crystal structure of the Pseudomonas/Mycobacterium LigD POL domain revealed the archaeal-primase-like fold and two-metal chemistry, and — importantly — showed that "a mutation of Mycobacterium LigD that uniquely ablates the polymerase activity results in increased fidelity of blunt-end double-strand break repair in vivo by virtue of eliminating nucleotide insertions at the recombination junctions," pinning the source of NHEJ mutagenesis on the POL domain (PMID: 16446439). The PE domain's Mn-dependent 3'-ribonuclease/3'-phosphatase resection chemistry was mapped in a series of Pseudomonas studies (PMID: 15897197; PMID: 16540477; PMID: 16046407).

4.3 Key Finding 3 — Bacterial Ku shares a conserved core with eukaryotic Ku but has a unique, multipurpose C-terminus

Aravind & Koonin dissected Ku into a eukaryote/prokaryote-conserved core ring plus lineage-specific extensions — "the Ku core that is conserved in eukaryotes and prokaryotes" (PMID: 11483577). Bacterial Ku lacks the eukaryotic von Willebrand A (vWA) domain and functions as a homodimer (versus the Ku70/Ku80 heterodimer of eukaryotes). The bacterial-specific C-terminus is the functional hotspot: dissection in Bacillus subtilis showed that "the minimal C-terminus is required for the Ku-LigD interaction, whereas the basic extension controls the threading and DNA bridging abilities of Ku" (PMID: 26961308). In M. tuberculosis, the Ku C-terminus is a "multipurpose arm" that binds DNA and LigD and stimulates ligation, with the extended C-terminus limiting DNA binding and the Ku core also contributing to ligase stimulation and direct LigD binding (PMID: 36250639). Recent cryo-EM work resolved apo and DNA-bound mycobacterial Ku homodimers and showed that Ku forms extended proteo-filaments upon binding DNA, driving DNA synapsis; the C-terminus regulates DNA loading and subsequent LigD recruitment (PMID: 41298423; PMID: 41521670).

4.4 Key Finding 7 — The Ku-recruits-ligase core mechanism is strongly supported experimentally

The founding experimental demonstration is Weller et al. (2002, Science), which established that bacterial Ku homologs retain the end-binding biochemistry of eukaryotic Ku, that "bacterial Ku specifically recruits DNA ligase to DNA ends and stimulates DNA ligation. Loss of these proteins leads to hypersensitivity to ionizing radiation in Bacillus subtilis" (PMID: 12215643). This Ku-recruits-and-stimulates-ligase mechanism has since been reproduced across Mycobacterium, Pseudomonas, and Bacillus by genetics, in-vitro reconstitution, and structural biology (PMID: 15778718; PMID: 20018881; PMID: 23691176; PMID: 36250639), and is synthesized in the definitive Shuman & Glickman review, which confirms that "mycobacteria and other genera have an NHEJ system that depends on a dedicated DNA ligase, LigD, and the DNA-end-binding protein Ku" (PMID: 17938628).

5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

5.1 Key Finding 4 — Dedicated to non-replicating/dormant states, and intrinsically mutagenic

Bacterial NHEJ is not a general-purpose DSB pathway; it is the repair option of last resort in cells that cannot use HR. In M. smegmatis, deletion of ku/ligD sensitizes cells to ionizing radiation and desiccation specifically in stationary phase, not in log phase — because in replicating cells a sister chromatid supports error-free HR: "NHEJ-mutant strains (Deltaku, DeltaligD, Deltaku/ligD) were significantly more sensitive to ionizing radiation (IR) during stationary phase than wild-type M. smegmatis. However, there was little difference in IR sensitivity between NHEJ-mutant and wild-type strains in logarithmic phase" (PMID: 17360246; PMID: 17496093). NHEJ is the sole DSB pathway operating during M. tuberculosis dormancy (PMID: 41521670).

The price of template-free joining is fidelity. Repair of blunt and 5'-overhang DSBs is roughly 50% mutagenic, with molecular signatures of templated fill-in, non-templated single-nucleotide additions (often ribonucleotides), and nucleolytic resection: "NHEJ of blunt-end and complementary 5'-overhang DSBs is highly mutagenic" (PMID: 15778718). At chromosomal I-SceI breaks, NHEJ produces predominantly single-nucleotide insertions and rapidly mutates the break site in the absence of HR (PMID: 17496093).

5.2 Key Finding 5 — End structure dictates pathway choice; backup ligases and a faithful alternative route exist

The system is best understood as end-structure-tuned. Aniukwu et al. (2008) showed that repair of blunt and 5'-overhang breaks depends stringently on Ku and the LigD POL domain, whereas 3'-overhang breaks are handled by "a novel end-joining pathway specific to breaks with 3' overhangs that is Ku- and LigD-independent and perfectly faithful" — most plausibly relying on the essential NAD⁺-dependent replicative ligase LigA (PMID: 18281464). Redundancy extends to ligases: LigC1 supports a LigD-ligase-independent backup route that still requires the LigD POL domain — "we define a LigD ligase-independent NHEJ pathway in Mycobacterium smegmatis that requires the ATP-dependent DNA ligase LigC1 and the POL domain of LigD" (PMID: 24957619); LigC also provides backup for blunt-end repair (PMID: 15778718); and LigC plus the AEP-primase PrimC contribute additional backup activities under oxidative stress (PMID: 33918798).

Pathway-choice summary:

End structure Primary route Ku required? LigD required? Fidelity
Blunt Ku + LigD (POL-dependent) Yes Yes (POL critical) Mutagenic
5'-overhang Ku + LigD (POL fill-in) Yes Yes Mutagenic
Cohesive 3'-overhang LigA-dependent, Ku/LigD-independent No No Faithful
Blunt (backup) LigC / LigC1 + LigD POL Yes POL only Variable

5.3 Key Finding 6 — Ancient, redeployed folds; patchy distribution via loss and horizontal transfer

The Ku/LigD module is a mosaic of old parts. The prokaryotic Ku core is homologous to the eukaryotic Ku core, placing the fold before the eukaryote/prokaryote divergence; eukaryotic Ku later acquired a vWA domain and heterodimerized (PMID: 11483577). The LigD polymerase belongs to the archaeo-eukaryotic primase (AEP) superfamily, adopting "a fold similar to that of archaeal DNA primase" (PMID: 16446439; PMID: 17174332). The two genes frequently sit together in predicted operons — "genes for prokaryotic Ku homologs form predicted operons with genes coding for an ATP-dependent DNA ligase and/or archeal-eukaryotic-type DNA primase" (PMID: 11483577). The module is absent from many bacteria (e.g., E. coli), giving a patchy phyletic distribution consistent with gene loss and horizontal transfer (PMID: 17506672). Strikingly, mycobacteriophages Corndog and Omega carry their own Ku and hijack the host LigD to circularize their genomes via short 4-base cos ends, demonstrating the module's mobility and modularity (PMID: 16949369).

Because the LigD polymerase is the representative bacterial member of the AEP clan and Ku's core is the representative shared fold, these two domains — rather than the lineage-specific C-terminal extensions or accessory paralogs — are the best proxies for understanding the ancestral NHEJ role.

6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

Ordering constraints. The reaction is strongly ordered: Ku must load and protect ends before recruiting LigD (Ku is upstream and rate-limiting), and end processing precedes ligation — the PE domain heals 3' termini and the POL domain fills/realigns before the LIG domain can seal (PMID: 16023671; PMID: 17947582). In M. tuberculosis, Ku is the rate-limiting factor for the whole pathway (PMID: 41521670).

Physiological / compartment constraints. NHEJ is functionally confined to the non-replicating state. In log phase, HR outcompetes and largely replaces it; the two pathways are partly redundant so that inactivating NHEJ is compensated by elevated HR, and vice versa (PMID: 21219454; PMID: 24658131). This physiological gating is why NHEJ mutants show phenotypes only in stationary phase, spores, and dormancy (PMID: 17360246; PMID: 31948638).

Substrate constraints. End chemistry governs which route is used and whether processing is needed. Faithful, direct joining is possible for compatible cohesive ends; blunt/5'-overhang ends require mutagenic POL activity; the PE 3'-ribonuclease step even requires a specific 2'-OH configuration on the penultimate ribose (PMID: 18281464; PMID: 16540477).

Evidence ruling out otherwise-plausible paths. The Ku/LigD-independent, perfectly faithful 3'-overhang route (PMID: 18281464) rules out the assumption that all bacterial end joining flows through Ku/LigD. The POL-ablation experiment (PMID: 16446439) rules out the LIG or PE domains as the principal source of insertional mutagenesis. Stand-alone POL paralogs PolD1/PolD2, although biochemically LigD-POL-like, were shown not to substitute for LigD POL in fill-in during NHEJ under the conditions tested, ruling them out as the primary NHEJ polymerases (PMID: 23198659).

Failure modes. Loss of Ku or LigD → radiation/desiccation hypersensitivity of dormant cells; loss of both HR and NHEJ (Δku ΔligD ΔrecA) cripples intracellular survival of M. tuberculosis, an effect rescuable by neutralizing host ROS/RNS (PMID: 24658131). Because NHEJ is mutagenic, its "success" can itself be a failure mode at the sequence level, fixing insertions/deletions at the repaired locus (PMID: 17496093).

7. Controversies and Open Questions

  1. What drives synapsis in vivo — Ku filaments or the LigD polymerase? Recent cryo-EM shows Ku itself oligomerizes into proteo-filaments that bridge ends (PMID: 41298423; PMID: 41521670), whereas an earlier crystal structure captured the LigD POL domain mediating end synapsis as a proposed bridging intermediate (PMID: 17947582). Whether these are sequential stages, redundant mechanisms, or context-dependent alternatives is unresolved.

  2. What is the source of repair-associated deletions? The POL domain explains insertions, and the PE/nuclease activity can generate deletions in reconstituted E. coli systems (PMID: 20530153), but the in-vivo origin of terminal deletions at junctions is not fully assigned.

  3. How general is the mycobacterial/Pseudomonas paradigm? Nearly all mechanistic detail comes from Mycobacterium, Pseudomonas, and Bacillus. Given the module's patchy distribution and species-variable Ku C-termini and LigD domain arrangements (some organisms split LigD activities across separate proteins), extrapolating a single model across all NHEJ-competent bacteria risks overgeneralization (PMID: 17506672).

  4. Are second Ku–LigD contact sites functionally important? Beyond the primary POL–Ku interaction, evidence for a second Ku-dependent LIG-domain interaction has been reported (PMID: 20530153; PMID: 36250639); their relative in-vivo weight is unclear.

  5. Role of accessory modulators. A Sir2-like NAD⁺-dependent deacetylase interacts with Ku and LigD and modestly promotes NHEJ (PMID: 21637345); the regulatory logic (e.g., acetylation control of end binding) is not established.

Well-supported vs. indirect. Strongly supported: the two-component composition, LigD's three-domain modular chemistry, Ku's end-binding/recruitment role, POL as the mutagenesis catalyst, and the stationary-phase specialization — all backed by convergent genetics, biochemistry, and structures. More indirect / model-dependent: the exact in-vivo synaptic architecture, the LigA identity of the faithful 3'-overhang route (inferred), and the physiological importance of backup ligases outside stress conditions.

8. Limitations and Knowledge Gaps

  • Organism bias. The evidence base is dominated by a handful of Actinobacteria and Bacillus/Pseudomonas; broad phyla with Ku/LigD homologs remain experimentally unexplored.
  • In-vitro vs. in-vivo gap. Much end-processing chemistry (PE ribonuclease specificity, POL ribonucleotide preference) is characterized on defined primer-templates that may not reflect chromosomal DSB contexts.
  • Reconstitution artifacts. Heterologous reconstitution in E. coli (which lacks native NHEJ) is powerful but can over- or under-represent activities (e.g., nuclease-driven deletions) relative to the native host.
  • Structural snapshots. The new cryo-EM Ku-filament and older POL-synapsis structures are static; the temporal choreography of loading → synapsis → processing → ligation is inferred, not directly observed as a trajectory.

9. Proposed Follow-up Experiments / Actions

  1. Time-resolved / single-molecule synapsis assays (e.g., smFRET or live-cell imaging as in PMID: 41521670) comparing Ku-only, LigD-only, and Ku+LigD conditions to determine whether Ku filaments and LigD-POL bridging are sequential or alternative synaptic mechanisms.
  2. Cross-phylum functional survey. Reconstitute Ku/LigD pairs from underexplored phyla in a common heterologous host to test how far the mycobacterial paradigm generalizes and to catalogue split-LigD architectures.
  3. Deletion-source mapping. Use domain-specific catalytic mutants (PE-dead, POL-dead, nuclease-dead) at a defined chromosomal I-SceI break to assign the enzymatic origin of junction deletions in vivo.
  4. Genetic test of the faithful 3'-overhang route. Directly test LigA (and other candidate ligases) dependence for Ku/LigD-independent cohesive-end joining to confirm the inferred LigA route (PMID: 18281464).
  5. Regulatory dissection of accessory factors. Define whether the Sir2-like deacetylase regulates Ku/LigD by acetylation, and whether backup ligases (LigC/LigC1) and PrimC are induced under specific stresses (PMID: 21637345; PMID: 33918798).
  6. Therapeutic targeting. Given NHEJ's essentiality in M. tuberculosis dormancy, pursue Ku-synapsis or LigD-domain inhibitors as adjuncts that block persistence-phase DSB repair (PMID: 41298423).
