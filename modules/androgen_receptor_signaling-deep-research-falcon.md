# Androgen Receptor Signaling Pathway Module: A Mechanistic Review

## 1. Executive Summary

The androgen receptor (AR) signaling pathway is a canonical steroid hormone signaling axis in which the lipophilic androgens testosterone and 5α-dihydrotestosterone (DHT) activate a cytoplasmic nuclear receptor that, upon ligand engagement, translocates to the nucleus and drives transcriptional programs governing male sexual development, reproductive physiology, metabolism, and—when dysregulated—disease states including prostate cancer. This review treats the AR signaling module as a three-step system: (i) a chaperone-mediated maturation cycle that maintains apo-AR in a ligand-competent conformation; (ii) a ligand-dependent activation step encompassing conformational change, nuclear import, dimerization, and chromatin engagement; and (iii) a transcriptional output phase governed by the exchange of corepressors for coactivators at androgen response elements (AREs). The molecular boundaries of this module are defined by the AR protein itself together with the HSP90-based chaperone machinery, the importin-dependent nuclear transport system, and the coregulator complexes (principally NCOA1/SRC-1, NCOA2/SRC-2, and NCOR1) that shape gene-selective transcriptional outcomes. The system is evolutionarily ancient, with AR emerging approximately 400 million years ago in jawed vertebrates, and its core functional domains—particularly the DNA-binding domain (DBD) and ligand-binding domain (LBD)—remaining highly conserved from cartilaginous fish to mammals (falconieri2025theevolutionof pages 1-2, meszaros2022theevolutionand pages 8-10).

---

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### 2.1 What Is Included

The AR signaling pathway module, as defined here, encompasses the linear sequence of molecular events from the maintenance of unliganded AR in a chaperone-bound, ligand-competent state in the cytoplasm through to the productive engagement of RNA polymerase II at androgen-responsive genes in the nucleus. The core components include:

- The **AR protein** itself (NR3C4), a 920-amino acid modular transcription factor comprising an N-terminal domain (NTD, aa 1–559), a DNA-binding domain (DBD, aa 559–625), a hinge region (aa 625–676), and a ligand-binding domain (LBD, aa 676–920) (chen2024nterminaldomainof pages 3-5, wiacek2026anabolic–androgenicsteroidsrevisited pages 7-9).
- The **HSP90 chaperone system**, including HSP70, HSP40, HOP/STIP1, p23, and the immunophilin co-chaperones FKBP51 (FKBP5) and FKBP52 (FKBP4) (ratajczak2022heatshockproteins pages 8-10).
- The **nuclear import machinery**, specifically importin α (KPNA1), importin β (KPNB1), and Ran GTPase (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 2-3).
- The **coactivator and corepressor complexes**, primarily the steroid receptor coactivator (SRC) family members NCOA1 (SRC-1), NCOA2 (SRC-2/TIF2), and NCOA3 (SRC-3), the corepressors NCOR1 and NCOR2/SMRT, and the associated histone-modifying enzymes p300/CBP (bawa2026nuclearreceptorcoregulator pages 2-3, jafari2022nuclearreceptorcoregulators pages 3-5, leach2022cellularspecificityof pages 11-12).

### 2.2 What Should Be Treated Separately

Several pathways and processes are frequently conflated with the AR signaling module but are mechanistically distinct:

- **Steroidogenesis and androgen biosynthesis**: The synthesis of testosterone and DHT from cholesterol precursors (involving CYP17A1, SRD5A1/2, 3β-HSD, and HSD17B enzymes) is upstream of AR activation and constitutes a separate biosynthetic pathway.
- **Non-genomic/rapid AR signaling**: Membrane-associated or cytoplasmic AR actions that activate kinase cascades (e.g., Src, PI3K/Akt, MAPK) on a timescale of seconds to minutes operate through mechanisms distinct from the canonical genomic pathway described here. These non-genomic effects remain less well characterized and are a subject of active investigation.
- **Glucocorticoid receptor (GR) signaling**: Although GR shares a nearly identical chaperone cycle and considerable structural homology with AR, cross-talk between the two receptors—particularly in the context of castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC) where GR can compensate for AR loss—should be analyzed as a parallel system rather than part of the AR module.
- **AR splice variant signaling**: Constitutively active AR variants such as AR-V7, which lack the LBD entirely, bypass the chaperone cycle and ligand-dependent activation steps. These variants represent an alternative route to AR-driven transcription with distinct coregulator recruitment patterns (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 9-10).

### 2.3 Competing Definitions

The literature sometimes defines "AR signaling" broadly to include all androgen-mediated effects in cells—genomic, non-genomic, and paracrine—while other treatments restrict the term to the canonical nuclear receptor transcription factor cycle. This review adopts the narrower definition, treating the chaperone cycle → ligand-dependent activation → ARE-directed transcription as the core module (leach2022cellularspecificityof pages 1-2).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

The canonical sequence of events in AR signaling proceeds through three obligatory phases, each causally linked to the next.

### 3.1 Phase I: The Androgen Receptor Chaperone Cycle

In the absence of ligand, AR is maintained in the cytoplasm in a dynamic complex with the HSP90 chaperone machinery. This cycle ensures that the LBD adopts a conformation competent for high-affinity androgen binding. The sequential steps are:

1. **Client recognition by HSP70/HSP40**: HSP70 (HSPA1A/HSPA8), assisted by its J-domain co-chaperone HSP40, initially recognizes and binds nascent or partially folded AR, preventing aggregation and preparing the client for transfer to HSP90 (ratajczak2022heatshockproteins pages 8-10).

2. **HSP70-to-HSP90 transfer via HOP/STIP1**: The co-chaperone HOP (STIP1) serves as a molecular bridge, simultaneously binding HSP70 and HSP90 through its TPR domains and maintaining HSP90 in an open conformation ready to accept the AR client (ratajczak2022heatshockproteins pages 8-10).

3. **Maturation in the HSP90 complex**: Once AR is loaded onto HSP90, the co-chaperone p23 stabilizes the closed, ATP-bound HSP90 dimer, promoting the final maturation of AR's LBD into a ligand-binding-competent state. Immunophilin co-chaperones FKBP51 and FKBP52 associate with the complex through their TPR domains and regulate steroid receptor conformation, transport, and transcriptional activity (ratajczak2022heatshockproteins pages 8-10, agam2024theroleof pages 5-7).

4. **FKBP5 as a co-chaperone and feedback node**: FKBP51 (encoded by FKBP5) occupies a particularly important position in the AR chaperone cycle. It functions within an HSP90-p23 super-chaperone complex that promotes androgen-dependent transcription activation and cell growth. Critically, FKBP5 transcription is itself activated by AR, establishing a positive feedback loop: androgen stimulation → AR activation → FKBP5 induction → enhanced AR maturation and signaling (agam2024theroleof pages 5-7). FKBP52 (FKBP4) functions as a complementary immunophilin, with evidence suggesting it plays a particularly important role in AR signaling potentiation, and FKBP51 and FKBP52 together regulate AR dimerization and proliferative responses in prostate cancer cells (agam2024theroleof pages 15-15).

5. **Cytoplasmic retention by SGTA**: The co-chaperone SGTA, which interacts with both HSP70 and HSP90, can retain AR in the cytoplasm and reduce its ligand sensitivity, serving as a negative regulatory node within the chaperone cycle (ratajczak2022heatshockproteins pages 8-10).

### 3.2 Phase II: Androgen Receptor Activation

Upon androgen binding, the AR undergoes a series of conformational and spatial transitions:

1. **Ligand binding and conformational change**: Testosterone or DHT enters the hydrophobic ligand-binding pocket of the LBD (comprising 11 α-helices), triggering repositioning of helix 12 to seal the pocket and create the activation function-2 (AF-2) surface. This conformational change disrupts the chaperone complex and enables the intramolecular N/C interaction, in which the ²³FQNLF²⁷ motif of the NTD engages the AF-2 groove of the LBD (chen2024nterminaldomainof pages 3-5, zakari2024unveilingthelatest pages 4-5).

2. **NLS exposure and chaperone release**: Androgen binding promotes dissociation of HSP90 and IPO7 from the AR complex. Protein kinase A (PKA)-mediated phosphorylation of HSP90 facilitates this release, exposing the nuclear localization signal (NLS) in the hinge region (aa 613–633) (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 2-3, yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 12-13).

3. **Nuclear translocation**: The exposed NLS is recognized by importin α (KPNA1), which forms a complex with importin β (KPNB1). This trimeric complex traverses the nuclear pore, and Ran-GTP in the nucleus triggers release of AR from the importin complex (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 2-3).

4. **Dimerization and DNA binding**: In the nucleus, the intramolecular N/C interaction is supplanted by intermolecular N/C contacts between two AR monomers, facilitating homodimerization. AR dimerization occurs through both DBD-DBD and LBD-LBD interfaces, with each monomer's zinc finger recognizing one half-site of the palindromic ARE (canonical inverted repeat 5′-AGAACA-3′). Selective AREs featuring direct repeats (5′-AGAACAnnnAGAACA-3′) confer AR-specific gene regulation distinct from other steroid receptors (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 2-3, chen2024nterminaldomainof pages 3-5).

### 3.3 Phase III: Androgen Transcriptional Output

Once AR dimers are stably bound to AREs at promoters and enhancers, the receptor orchestrates transcription through a ligand-dependent coregulator exchange:

1. **Corepressor-to-coactivator switch**: In the unliganded or antagonist-bound state, AR associates with corepressor complexes containing NCOR1 and NCOR2/SMRT, which interact with the LBD through CoRNR box motifs (extended helical LXXI/HIXXXI/L peptides). These corepressors recruit HDAC3-containing complexes that maintain a repressive chromatin state. Agonist binding repositions helix 12 of the LBD, which sterically disfavors CoRNR box binding while creating a surface that selectively accommodates the shorter LXXLL motifs (NR boxes) found in coactivators (bawa2026nuclearreceptorcoregulator pages 2-3, jafari2022nuclearreceptorcoregulators pages 3-5).

2. **SRC/NCOA family coactivator recruitment**: The SRC family members NCOA1 (SRC-1), NCOA2 (SRC-2/TIF2), and NCOA3 (SRC-3) bind to the agonist-occupied AR through their LXXLL motifs, interacting with both the AF-1 region of the NTD and the AF-2 surface of the LBD. These coactivators possess intrinsic histone acetyltransferase (HAT) activity and additionally contain two activation domains: AD1 recruits p300/CBP, while AD2 recruits arginine methyltransferases CARM1 and PRMT1 (bawa2026nuclearreceptorcoregulator pages 2-3, leach2022cellularspecificityof pages 11-12). Loss of NTD-cofactor interactions has a stronger impact on transcriptional activity than loss of LBD-cofactor interactions, underscoring the functional primacy of the NTD in AR-driven gene expression (yavuz2024phaseseparationmediated pages 4-6).

3. **Chromatin remodeling and enhancer activation**: Recruited p300/CBP acetylates histones (particularly H3K27), creating marks recognized by the bromodomain protein BRD4. BRD4 in turn facilitates enhancer-promoter interactions and bridges to the Mediator complex (MED1) and RNA polymerase II machinery to drive productive transcription (yavuz2024phaseseparationmediated pages 8-9).

4. **NCOR1 as a context-dependent transcriptional repressor**: NCOR1 represses AR-targeting genes and functionally inhibits coactivator action. In normal prostate epithelium, NCOR1 expression contributes to the restraint of androgen-responsive proliferative programs. Notably, NCOR1 expression declines during prostate cancer progression, with reduced levels in tumors compared to normal tissue and further reduction in metastases, suggesting that loss of NCOR1-mediated repression is one mechanism by which AR-driven transcription becomes deregulated in cancer (leach2022cellularspecificityof pages 11-12).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### 5.1 Evolutionary Origins and Conservation

The AR gene emerged early in vertebrate evolution, approximately 400 million years ago, likely through duplication of the progesterone receptor (PR) gene, which itself evolved from an ancestral estrogen receptor (ER) (falconieri2025theevolutionof pages 1-2). AR is present in all jawed vertebrates (Gnathostomata), including Chondrichthyes (cartilaginous fish), indicating it arose before the divergence of cartilaginous fish from other jawed vertebrates. Functionally active eukaryotic linear motifs (ELMs) present in human AR can be traced back to Chondrichthyes, including motifs in the LBD required for androgen binding (falconieri2025theevolutionof pages 1-2).

Phylogenetic analysis of 536 vertebrate species spanning Chondrichthyes, Osteichthyes, Amphibia, Reptilia, Avia, and Mammalia reveals that the DBD is the most highly conserved domain (near-complete conservation across classes), while the LBD shows substantial conservation. The NTD, by contrast, shows greater evolutionary divergence, particularly in non-mammalian vertebrates, which completely lack the polymorphic polyQ and polyG repeat regions found in mammals (meszaros2022theevolutionand pages 8-10, falconieri2025theevolutionof pages 2-3). Importantly, secondary and tertiary protein structures of AR orthologs remain conserved across vertebrate classes, and treatment with physiological androgen concentrations triggers transactivation in fish AR orthologs expressed in mammalian cells, confirming preserved function across >450 million years of vertebrate evolution (falconieri2025theevolutionof pages 5-6).

A cryptic phosphorylation site in the LBD functions as a suppressive regulatory signal conserved from fish to humans, and a similar phospho-site is found in the LBD of other steroid receptors and their ancestors, suggesting deep ancestral origins for this regulatory mechanism (falconieri2025theevolutionof pages 13-13). In teleost fishes, AR gene duplication serves as an evolutionary driver generating diversity in sexual characteristics (falconieri2025theevolutionof pages 15-16). The AR-specific FxxLF motif, critical for the N/C interaction, appears conserved in mammals and is present in amphibians, though it shows variation in birds (evolving to GxFxxFF in Avia) (falconieri2025theevolutionof pages 7-9).

### 5.2 Domain-Specific Conservation

The chaperone machinery components (HSP90, HSP70, p23, immunophilins) are among the most conserved protein families across eukaryotes, with the HSP90-steroid receptor interaction representing an ancient regulatory mechanism that predates the emergence of steroid receptors themselves. The coregulator proteins—particularly the SRC/NCOA family and the NCOR1/2 complexes—are shared across all nuclear receptor signaling systems in vertebrates, indicating their ancestral role in modulating ligand-dependent transcription.

### 5.3 Cell-Type and Tissue-Specific Variation

AR signaling is not a monolithic pathway but varies dramatically across cell types and tissues:

- **Epithelial vs. stromal compartments**: In prostate, AR is expressed in luminal epithelial cells, fibromuscular stromal cells, and blood vessel endothelial cells, but not in basal cells. Stromal AR drives expression of paracrine growth factors (IGF, FGF, EGF) that maintain epithelial glandular homeostasis, while epithelial AR directly controls genes involved in differentiation and secretory function (ratajczak2022heatshockproteins pages 3-5, leach2022cellularspecificityof pages 1-2).

- **AR cistrome differences**: The AR binding landscape (cistrome) differs substantially between fibroblasts and epithelial cells. Fibroblast-specific AR sites show minimal FOXA1 motif enrichment but prominent AP-1 enrichment, and AR in fibroblasts does not target epithelial genes like PSA or KLK2 (leach2022cellularspecificityof pages 7-8). In benign versus malignant epithelium, only 16% of AR binding sites overlap, with cancer-specific sites associated with proliferative and metabolic gene programs (leach2022cellularspecificityof pages 2-4).

- **Coregulator expression context**: Coregulator ratios vary between prostate epithelial cells and fibroblasts and even among fibroblasts from different tissue origins. NCOR1 expression is specific to epithelial cells with minimal stromal expression, while factors such as Filamin A link AR signaling to cell movement and invasiveness specifically in fibroblasts (leach2022cellularspecificityof pages 12-13).

- **Disease progression**: Distinct AR binding patterns emerge at each prostate cancer stage—normal, localized, metastatic, and castrate-resistant—reflecting progressive reprogramming of the AR transcriptome driven by changes in pioneer factor activity, coregulator availability, and AR itself (leach2022cellularspecificityof pages 2-4).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### 6.1 Obligatory Ordering

The canonical pathway imposes strict ordering constraints:

1. **Chaperone cycle must precede ligand binding**: HSP90-mediated maturation of the LBD is required for AR to achieve ligand-binding competence. Disruption of HSP90 function (e.g., by geldanamycin or ganetespib) leads to AR degradation via the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway rather than simply preventing ligand binding.

2. **Ligand binding must precede nuclear translocation** (for full-length AR): The NLS in the hinge region is masked in the apo state, and its exposure requires ligand-induced conformational changes and HSP90 dissociation (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 2-3). This ordering constraint is bypassed by constitutively active splice variants like AR-V7 that lack the LBD.

3. **Dimerization is required for ARE binding**: While monomeric AR can associate transiently with chromatin, stable engagement at palindromic AREs requires homodimerization through DBD-DBD and LBD-LBD interfaces (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 2-3).

### 6.2 Mutually Exclusive States

- **N/C interaction vs. cofactor recruitment**: When AR is in its monomeric, DNA-unbound state, the FQNLF motif in the NTD occupies the AF-2 groove of the LBD through intramolecular N/C interaction. Upon stable DNA binding, this intramolecular contact is released, freeing the AF-2 groove for cofactor (SRC/NCOA) recruitment through their LXXLL motifs (yavuz2024phaseseparationmediated pages 4-6). This represents a mutually exclusive binary state.

- **Coactivator vs. corepressor binding**: The position of helix 12 in the LBD determines whether LXXLL-containing coactivators or CoRNR-box-containing corepressors can bind, and these interactions are structurally mutually exclusive (bawa2026nuclearreceptorcoregulator pages 2-3, jafari2022nuclearreceptorcoregulators pages 3-5).

### 6.3 Post-Translational Regulatory Layers

Multiple PTMs modulate the pathway at various points:

- **Phosphorylation**: CDK9-mediated phosphorylation at serine 81 is a key modification promoting AR transcriptional activity in response to androgen (lumahan2024regulatingandrogenreceptor pages 4-5). PKA-mediated phosphorylation of HSP90 is essential for nuclear translocation of AR (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 12-13).

- **SUMOylation**: Covalent conjugation of SUMO proteins to AR at K386 and K520 selectively represses transcription of target genes involved in cell proliferation. The SUMO pathway exerts pervasive influence on AR chromatin interactions, augmenting pioneer-like AR binding to inaccessible chromatin while diminishing interaction with accessible regions, thereby reshaping the transcriptional output (lumahan2024regulatingandrogenreceptor pages 13-14, launonen2024centralroleof pages 1-1).

- **Ubiquitination**: AR is subject to proteasomal degradation through ubiquitin ligases including MDM2, providing a mechanism for signal termination and protein turnover.

### 6.4 Phase Separation and Transcriptional Condensates

ARs possess intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) rich in charged amino acids that facilitate liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) in the nucleus. DNA binding is required to generate membrane-less condensates where AR, MED1, and other cofactors concentrate at transcription sites. These condensates increase local availability of transcriptional machinery and can exclude repressive factors such as HDACs, potentially creating microenvironments favorable for productive transcription (yavuz2024phaseseparationmediated pages 8-9). The role of polyQ repeat length in the NTD in modulating condensate formation and transcriptional activity remains an area of active investigation (meszaros2022theevolutionand pages 1-2).

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### 7.1 Structural Discrepancies in NTD-LBD Interactions

A significant controversy exists between live-cell fluorescence microscopy studies, which show loss of NTD-LBD interactions in DNA-bound ARs, and cryo-EM structural analysis, which shows the NTD wrapping around the LBDs at the core of the AR dimer complex. This discrepancy may reflect the difference between static structural snapshots and the dynamic conformational ensemble that AR adopts in living cells (yavuz2024phaseseparationmediated pages 4-6).

### 7.2 Antiandrogen Mechanisms

Although antiandrogens are classically thought to function by recruiting corepressors, ARs treated with antagonists fail to form stable DNA-binding foci, behaving similarly to DBD mutants. This raises the question of whether antagonists truly promote corepressor recruitment at chromatin or instead prevent stable chromatin engagement altogether (yavuz2024phaseseparationmediated pages 4-6).

### 7.3 LBD Point Mutations and Coregulator Interactions

Multiple LBD point mutations (L702H, W742C/L, H875Y, T878A) that arise during therapeutic resistance alter ligand specificity and can enable AR activation through non-androgen ligands. However, the impact of these mutations on AR-coregulator interactions has not been systematically investigated, leaving a significant gap in understanding treatment resistance mechanisms (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 9-10).

### 7.4 AR Splice Variants and Coregulator Selectivity

AR-V7 and other constitutively active splice variants lack the LBD and therefore bypass both the chaperone cycle and ligand-dependent activation. Importantly, AR-V7 has been shown to recruit corepressors such as NCOR1 and NCOR2 differently from full-length AR, and its cistrome is distinct, suggesting mechanistic complexity that remains incompletely understood. The full-length AR and AR-V7 may coexist in the same cells, and how their activities are integrated remains an open question (yavuz2025coregulatorsdetermineandrogen pages 9-10).

### 7.5 Phase Separation: Mechanism or Epiphenomenon?

While AR foci formation via LLPS has been documented and biomolecular condensates have been proposed to organize transcription, it remains debated whether phase separation is a mechanistic requirement for AR-driven transcription or an emergent property of high local protein concentration at active loci (yavuz2024phaseseparationmediated pages 8-9, meszaros2022theevolutionand pages 1-2).

### 7.6 Full-Length AR Structure

No near-atomic resolution structure of full-length AR has been determined, and the intrinsically disordered nature of the NTD (comprising over half of the protein) presents a formidable barrier to comprehensive structural characterization. This limits understanding of how the NTD coordinates with the structured domains to control transcriptional activity (meszaros2022theevolutionand pages 1-2).

### 7.7 Non-Genomic Signaling Integration

How cytoplasmic/membrane-associated rapid AR signaling (e.g., through Src, PI3K/Akt, MAPK cascades) integrates with or modulates the canonical genomic pathway described here remains poorly defined. The relative contribution of non-genomic signaling to physiological androgen responses in different tissues is an unresolved question.
