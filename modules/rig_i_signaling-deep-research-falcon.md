# The RIG-I Signaling Pathway Module: A Review-Style Synthesis

## 1. Executive Summary

The RIG-I signaling pathway is a cytoplasmic innate immune surveillance system that detects viral RNA and triggers type I and type III interferon (IFN) responses. The pathway proceeds through three obligatory steps: (1) viral RNA sensing by the receptor RIG-I (DDX58), which recognizes short double-stranded RNA bearing 5′-triphosphate or 5′-diphosphate termini; (2) assembly of the MAVS signalosome, in which activated RIG-I nucleates prion-like aggregation of the adaptor MAVS on mitochondrial, peroxisomal, and MAM membranes, recruiting TRAF family ubiquitin adaptors; and (3) TBK1/IKKε-mediated phosphorylation and activation of the transcription factors IRF3 and IRF7, which translocate to the nucleus and drive IFN-β transcription. This module shares downstream effectors (TBK1, IRF3) with the cGAS-STING and TLR3-TRIF pathways but is distinguished by its sensor, adaptor, and subcellular organization. The pathway is conserved across vertebrates but has been lost in certain avian lineages (chickens, turkeys, quail), where MDA5 provides compensatory RNA sensing. Recent advances have clarified the structural basis of RIG-I autorepression, the role of post-translational modifications in MAVS aggregation control, and the metabolic integration of MAVS signaling at distinct organellar platforms.

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### Scope of the system

The RIG-I signaling pathway module, as defined here, encompasses three core mechanistic steps: (i) cytoplasmic viral RNA recognition by RIG-I, (ii) MAVS signalosome assembly on organellar membranes, and (iii) TBK1/IKKε-dependent activation of IRF3/IRF7 transcription factors leading to type I IFN induction (li2022regulationofantiviral pages 2-5, chan2022cytoplasmicrnasensors pages 1-2, solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 1-2).

### Neighboring pathways often confused with this module

Several innate immune pathways converge on TBK1 and IRF3 but are mechanistically distinct from the RIG-I module. MDA5 (IFIH1) is a parallel RIG-I-like receptor (RLR) that also signals through MAVS but recognizes long dsRNA through cooperative filament formation rather than 5′-end-selective binding (chan2022cytoplasmicrnasensors pages 2-4, huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 3-4). TLR3 detects dsRNA in endosomes and signals through TRIF rather than MAVS (li2022regulationofantiviral pages 2-5, magor2022evolutionofrna pages 1-2). The cGAS-STING pathway senses cytosolic DNA and signals through the second messenger cGAMP and the ER/Golgi-resident adaptor STING (venkatraman2024ikkɛinducessting pages 2-4, venkatraman2024ikkɛinducessting pages 4-6). All four pathways converge on TBK1-IRF3, which can lead to conflation. The NF-κB arm activated downstream of MAVS through TRAF6 and canonical IKK complexes is an adjacent output that should be treated as a parallel branch rather than the defining output of the RIG-I module (chan2022cytoplasmicrnasensors pages 1-2, wu2023mitochondrialantiviralsignaling pages 2-3).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### Step 1: Viral RNA Sensing by RIG-I

RIG-I exists in the cytoplasm in an autoinhibited conformation in which the N-terminal tandem CARD domains (CARD1 and CARD2) are sequestered against the helicase insertion domain (Hel2i), rendering them unavailable for downstream signaling (sikorska2024recentdevelopmentsin pages 1-3, li2022regulationofantiviral pages 2-5). The C-terminal domain (CTD) serves as the primary RNA sensor, containing a positively charged binding cleft that recognizes 5′-triphosphate (5′ppp) or 5′-diphosphate (5′pp) blunt-ended dsRNA with extremely high affinity (~0.05 nM), compared to ~1.5 mM for cellular 5′-monophosphate RNA (solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 10-12). This >30,000-fold discrimination is central to self-versus-non-self recognition.

Upon viral RNA binding, the CTD and helicase domains reorganize into a ring-like structure around the dsRNA, causing conformational changes that disrupt the CARD2:Hel2i autoinhibitory interface and eject the CARDs into solution (sikorska2024recentdevelopmentsin pages 3-6, sikorska2024recentdevelopmentsin pages 1-3). Critically, RNA binding rather than ATP binding is the primary driver of CARD ejection, although ATP hydrolysis provides an additional proofreading layer by accelerating RIG-I dissociation from non-pathogenic RNAs, thereby minimizing off-target activation (sikorska2024recentdevelopmentsin pages 3-6, solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 12-14). Conserved residues including I875 and H830 in the CTD sterically block recognition of endogenous 5′-monophosphate and capped RNAs, preventing autoinflammatory responses to mitochondrial RNA (solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 12-14).

The exposed CARDs form tetrameric assemblies stabilized by K63-linked polyubiquitin chains, adopting a lock-washer conformation that serves as the nucleation seed for MAVS activation (solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 10-12, solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 12-14). Two E3 ubiquitin ligases are critical for this process: TRIM25, which catalyzes K63-linked ubiquitination of the RIG-I CARD (classically at K172), and RIPLET (RNF135), which ubiquitinates both the CTD and CARD regions (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 7-9, małkowska2022factorsaffectingrigilike pages 2-4). RIPLET shows preferential activity toward RIG-I dimers on longer dsRNA, suggesting that RNA length influences signaling efficiency (solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 12-14). The relative contributions of TRIM25 and RIPLET remain an active area of investigation, with evidence for hierarchical, cell-type-dependent, and context-dependent functions (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 7-9).

RIG-I recognizes a minimum dsRNA length of ~10 nucleotides and preferentially signals from short dsRNA (<500 bp), in contrast to MDA5 which favors long dsRNA (>2 kb) (sikorska2024recentdevelopmentsin pages 3-6, chan2022cytoplasmicrnasensors pages 2-4). Notably, RIG-I can also recognize certain metabolite-capped RNAs (NAD+, FAD, dephosphoCoA caps) that retain diphosphate linkages at the 5′ end, representing a novel class of immunostimulatory ligands (solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 10-12).

### Step 2: MAVS Signalosome Assembly

MAVS (also known as IPS-1, VISA, or Cardif) is a 540-amino acid adaptor protein anchored to the outer mitochondrial membrane, peroxisomes, and mitochondria-associated ER membranes (MAMs) via its C-terminal transmembrane domain (jiang2025frommitochondriato pages 1-2, he2023mavsintegratesglucose pages 1-2). Upon interaction with activated RIG-I CARD tetramers, the N-terminal CARD of MAVS undergoes a conformational switch that propagates as prion-like, protease-resistant filaments along the organellar membrane (jiang2025frommitochondriato pages 1-2, wu2023mitochondrialantiviralsignaling pages 2-3). This self-templating aggregation is a hallmark of MAVS activation and is essential for signal amplification (li2023wdr77inhibitsprionlike pages 1-2).

The aggregated MAVS polymer serves as a scaffold that recruits multiple TRAF family members—TRAF2, TRAF3, TRAF5, and TRAF6—through distinct TRAF-binding motifs (shi2025pwwp3adisruptsthe pages 1-2, chan2022cytoplasmicrnasensors pages 1-2). VISA/MAVS recruits TBK1 via TRAFs to mitochondria, inducing IRF3 phosphorylation and type I interferon production (shi2025pwwp3adisruptsthe pages 1-2). TRAF6 recruitment supports both NF-κB activation and TBK1 engagement, while TRAF3 is classically associated with the IFN-biased arm of signaling (wu2023mitochondrialantiviralsignaling pages 2-3, li2022regulationofantiviral pages 2-5). TBK1 phosphorylates MAVS itself at a consensus pLxIS motif, enabling recruitment of IRF3, which is subsequently phosphorylated by TBK1 (shi2025pwwp3adisruptsthe pages 1-2).

MAVS signalosome assembly is tightly regulated by post-translational modifications. K63-linked ubiquitination enhances MAVS signaling by promoting TRAF and TBK1/IKKε complex recruitment (jiang2025frommitochondriato pages 5-6). S-palmitoylation, catalyzed by ZDHHC7 at Cys508 or ZDHHC12 at Cys79, acts as a checkpoint for MAVS aggregation on the mitochondrial outer membrane—palmitoylation does not affect MAVS stability or mitochondrial localization under resting conditions but is essential for proper aggregation upon viral stimulation (liu2024mavscys508palmitoylation pages 3-4, wang2024palmitoylationactsas pages 6-8, wang2024palmitoylationactsas pages 10-12). Arginine methylation by PRMT9 at Arg41 and Arg43 inhibits spontaneous MAVS aggregation in resting cells; upon viral infection, PRMT9 dissociates from mitochondria, permitting aggregation (jiang2025frommitochondriato pages 5-6). Additional regulatory modifications include O-GlcNAcylation (which promotes K63-linked ubiquitination), SUMOylation, and acetylation (SIRT3 deacetylates MAVS at K7 to promote aggregation) (jiang2025frommitochondriato pages 5-6, wang2024palmitoylationactsas pages 10-12).

Negative regulators of MAVS aggregation include WDR77, which binds the MAVS proline-rich region and directly inhibits prion-like filament formation (li2023wdr77inhibitsprionlike pages 1-2), and PWWP3A, which competes with TRAF6 for MAVS binding, impeding TBK1 recruitment—PWWP3A is degraded by the E3 ligase PJA2 after viral infection, relieving this inhibition (shi2025pwwp3adisruptsthe pages 1-2).

Importantly, MAVS at different subcellular locations drives distinct signaling outputs. Peroxisomal MAVS interacts with glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) and recruits TRAF6 and IRF1 to induce type III IFN expression and pentose phosphate pathway activation (he2023mavsintegratesglucose pages 1-2, huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 23-27). MAM-tethered MAVS interacts with glutamine-fructose-6-phosphate transaminase and recruits TRAF6 and TRAF2 to induce type I IFN expression and hexosamine biosynthesis pathway activation (he2023mavsintegratesglucose pages 1-2, huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 11-12). This compartmental specialization represents an emerging dimension of RIG-I-MAVS biology.

### Step 3: TBK1-IRF Activation

TBK1 (TANK-binding kinase 1) is the principal kinase downstream of MAVS that drives IRF3 phosphorylation and type I IFN induction. TBK1 is recruited to the MAVS signalosome through ubiquitin-dependent mechanisms involving NEMO, TANK, and TRAF-mediated scaffolds. TRIM26-mediated auto-ubiquitination bridges TBK1-NEMO interactions to recruit TBK1 to the MAVS signalosome (wu2025nemofamilyof pages 5-7). Once concentrated at the signalosome, TBK1 undergoes trans-autophosphorylation at Ser172 in its kinase activation loop, which is essential for catalytic activity (venkatraman2024ikkɛinducessting pages 2-4).

Activated TBK1 phosphorylates IRF3 at multiple serine residues within the C-terminal regulatory domain—Ser396, Ser398, Ser402, Ser404, and Ser405 (site 2)—relieving autoinhibition and permitting nuclear translocation with the coactivator CBP (wang2024themultipleroles pages 13-13). CBP subsequently phosphorylates IRF3 at Ser385 or Ser386 in the regulatory region, enabling IRF3 dimerization as homodimers or heterodimers with IRF7 (wang2024themultipleroles pages 13-13). The activated IRF3 dimer, complexed with CBP or p300, forms a holocomplex that binds ISRE (interferon-stimulated response element) sequences in IFN-β and IFN-α gene promoters, initiating transcription (wang2024themultipleroles pages 13-13).

IKKε (IKBKE), the TBK1 paralog, shares functional overlap and can support antiviral signaling, particularly NF-κB and MAPK activation. However, TBK1 is generally dominant for IRF3 phosphorylation, and IKKε appears to play a more prominent role in non-IFN immune outputs (venkatraman2024ikkɛinducessting pages 2-4, venkatraman2024ikkɛinducessting pages 4-6). The secreted IFN-β then signals through the IFNAR-JAK-STAT pathway in an autocrine and paracrine manner to induce interferon-stimulated genes (ISGs) in infected and neighboring cells (chan2022cytoplasmicrnasensors pages 1-2).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### Conservation across vertebrates

RIG-I is broadly conserved across vertebrate lineages, found in mammals, birds (especially ducks and other waterfowl), reptiles, amphibians, and teleost fish (magor2022evolutionofrna pages 8-9, magor2022evolutionofrna pages 1-2). The RLR family (RIG-I, MDA5, LGP2) belongs to the DExD/H-box RNA helicase superfamily, suggesting an ancient origin within or before the vertebrate radiation. MAVS, while showing relatively weak primary sequence conservation (e.g., only 28% identity between duck and human MAVS), retains conserved functional architecture—an N-terminal CARD, central signaling regions, and C-terminal transmembrane anchor—and its activation mechanism through prion-like aggregation appears to be a conserved feature (magor2022evolutionofrna pages 8-9).

### Lineage-specific losses and compensatory mechanisms

A striking evolutionary variation is the loss of RIG-I in galliform birds, including chickens (*Gallus gallus*), turkeys, and quail (magor2022evolutionofrna pages 9-10). In these species, MDA5 appears to functionally compensate for RIG-I loss, with chicken MDA5 acquiring properties distinct from mammalian MDA5, including preferential responsiveness to short dsRNA (magor2022evolutionofrna pages 9-10). LGP2 shows unique positive selection sites exclusively in the three RIG-I-lacking avian species, suggesting co-evolution to compensate for the missing sensor (magor2022evolutionofrna pages 9-10). Notably, duck RIG-I can function when expressed in chicken cells, confirming that the downstream pathway remains competent (magor2022evolutionofrna pages 9-10).

Duck RIG-I shares only 53% identity with human RIG-I but conserves key structural domains (magor2022evolutionofrna pages 8-9). However, it lacks the critical K172 residue used for TRIM25-mediated ubiquitination in mammals, instead utilizing alternative ubiquitin attachment sites—indicating that the E3 ligase-receptor interface has diversified across avian and mammalian lineages (magor2022evolutionofrna pages 8-9).

### Cell-type and compartmental variation

Within mammals, RIG-I pathway output varies by cell type. In insulin-sensitive muscle cells and adipocytes, RLRs can be sequestered to the plasma membrane, inhibiting IFN responses (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 9-11). The subcellular distribution of MAVS across mitochondria, peroxisomes, and MAMs creates compartment-specific signaling outputs: peroxisomal MAVS drives type III IFN and pentose phosphate pathway activation, while MAM-associated MAVS drives type I IFN and hexosamine biosynthesis (he2023mavsintegratesglucose pages 1-2, huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 11-12). This metabolic integration suggests that MAVS functions not only as an immune adaptor but as a metabolic switch coupling antiviral defense to glucose utilization (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 11-12).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### Obligatory sequence of events

The pathway follows a strict sequential logic: (1) RNA recognition by the CTD must precede (2) CARD exposure and oligomerization, which must precede (3) MAVS nucleation, which must precede (4) TRAF/TBK1 recruitment and IRF3 phosphorylation. Each step depends on the prior one, and disruption at any point—such as HCV NS3/4A protease cleavage of MAVS from mitochondria—abolishes downstream signaling (shi2025pwwp3adisruptsthe pages 1-2, li2023wdr77inhibitsprionlike pages 1-2).

### Self-discrimination constraints

RIG-I must discriminate between abundant cellular RNA and rare viral RNA. Multiple mechanisms enforce this selectivity: (i) the CTD selectively binds 5′ppp/5′pp blunt-ended dsRNA while rejecting 5′-monophosphate and capped RNAs through steric exclusion by I875 (solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 12-14); (ii) ATPase-driven translocation minimizes RIG-I dwell time on non-specific RNA substrates (sikorska2024recentdevelopmentsin pages 3-6, solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 12-14); (iii) K63-linked ubiquitination by TRIM25/RIPLET preferentially stabilizes RIG-I complexes on authentic viral ligands (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 7-9). Failure of these proofreading mechanisms—such as the I875A mutation—leads to recognition of endogenous mitochondrial RNA and autoinflammatory signaling (solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 12-14).

### Negative feedback and homeostatic control

Multiple negative feedback mechanisms prevent hyperactivation: MAVS aggregation is restrained by PRMT9-mediated arginine methylation in resting cells (jiang2025frommitochondriato pages 5-6); autophagic degradation of ubiquitinated RIG-I/MDA5 via p62/SQSTM-1 and NDP52 provides post-activation shutdown (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 11-12); K27-linked ubiquitination by MARCH8/RNF34 targets MAVS for autophagic degradation (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 11-12); and the ISG product LRRC25, upregulated by IFN signaling, promotes p62-mediated degradation of ISGylated RIG-I (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 11-12).

### Viral evasion strategies

Viruses have evolved numerous strategies to disrupt this pathway. HCV NS3/4A protease cleaves MAVS from the mitochondrial membrane (shi2025pwwp3adisruptsthe pages 1-2). EBV deubiquitinase BPLF1 removes K63- and K48-linked ubiquitin chains from TBK1, suppressing IRF3 activation. Influenza and SARS-CoV-2 NS proteins target multiple nodes in the pathway (li2022regulationofantiviral pages 2-5). These evasion strategies validate the biological importance of each pathway step.

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### Role of ATP in RIG-I activation
Contradicting conclusions have been reached regarding whether ATP hydrolysis is required for CARD ejection from the autoinhibited state. Recent NMR studies support a model in which RNA binding, rather than ATP, is the primary driver of CARD release, with ATP providing proofreading rather than activation per se (sikorska2024recentdevelopmentsin pages 3-6, sikorska2024recentdevelopmentsin pages 1-3). However, earlier models attributed a more central role to ATP-dependent conformational changes.

### TRIM25 versus RIPLET hierarchy
The relative contributions of TRIM25 and RIPLET to RIG-I activation remain contentious. Some studies indicate RIPLET is required for endogenous RIG-I-dependent responses while TRIM25's contribution is more context-dependent (burkart2022kineticcharacterizationand pages 130-132). Others demonstrate that both are essential but operate at different stages or on different RIG-I assemblies (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 7-9). Resolution of this hierarchy likely requires more systematic comparisons across cell types and viral contexts.

### MAVS aggregation regulation
While prion-like MAVS aggregation is well established, the molecular checkpoint that permits or prevents this switch remains incompletely understood. The recent discovery of palmitoylation as a critical checkpoint—with two independent studies identifying different palmitoylation sites (C508 via ZDHHC7 and C79 via ZDHHC12)—raises questions about whether these represent redundant or context-specific mechanisms (liu2024mavscys508palmitoylation pages 3-4, wang2024palmitoylationactsas pages 6-8, wang2024palmitoylationactsas pages 10-12).

### Compartment-specific MAVS functions
The finding that peroxisomal MAVS drives type III IFN while MAM-associated MAVS drives type I IFN is paradigm-shifting but requires validation across cell types and in vivo models (he2023mavsintegratesglucose pages 1-2, huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 11-12). How cells regulate the relative abundance of MAVS at these compartments and whether this distribution changes during infection or metabolic stress remains largely unknown.

### Self-RNA recognition and autoimmunity
The discovery that RIG-I can recognize metabolite-capped RNAs, endogenous Pol III transcripts, and mitochondrial RNA under certain conditions raises fundamental questions about the boundary between antiviral defense and autoinflammatory disease (solotchi2024proofreadingmechanismsof pages 12-14). The SLE-associated MAVS C79F mutation, which impairs palmitoylation, directly links MAVS PTM regulation to human autoimmune disease (wang2024palmitoylationactsas pages 6-8).

### Metabolic integration
The coupling of MAVS signaling to glucose metabolism—including the shift from glycolysis to PPP and HBP—represents an emerging and incompletely understood dimension. Whether and how these metabolic changes feed back to modulate antiviral immunity in vivo, particularly in metabolic disease contexts such as type 2 diabetes, remains an important open question (huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 11-12, huizen2025therigilikereceptor pages 14-15).
