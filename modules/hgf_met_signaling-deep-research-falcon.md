# The HGF–MET Signaling Pathway Module: A Mechanistic Review

## 1. Executive Summary

The hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) / MET receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK) signaling axis constitutes a tightly regulated module that converts extracellular paracrine cues into intracellular programs controlling motility, invasive growth, survival, and epithelial morphogenesis. Mature HGF engages the MET receptor's extracellular SEMA domain, inducing receptor dimerization and sequential trans-autophosphorylation of kinase-activation-loop tyrosines (Y1234/Y1235), followed by phosphorylation of C-terminal docking tyrosines (Y1349/Y1356) (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-2, raj2022molecularmechanism(s)of pages 3-5, linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9). These phosphotyrosines nucleate a multisite docking platform centered on the adaptors GRB2 and GAB1, which recruit effectors including PI3K p85, SHP2, SOS1, CRK, and STAT3 to activate the RAS–MAPK, PI3K–AKT–mTOR, and JAK–STAT cascades (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, perezbaena2023theroleof pages 2-4). Signal termination is achieved principally through CBL-mediated ubiquitination at the juxtamembrane Y1003 residue, which routes activated MET to lysosomal degradation (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9, ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-2). Dysregulation of this pathway—through MET amplification, activating mutations, or exon 14 skipping—drives oncogenesis across multiple tumor types and has become a clinically actionable therapeutic target (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-3).

---

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### 2.1 What the System Includes

The HGF–MET signaling module encompasses the ligand (HGF), the receptor (MET, also known as c-MET or HGFR), the immediate docking adaptors that assemble on the activated receptor's intracellular tail (principally GAB1 and GRB2), and the first-tier effector enzymes recruited to these adaptors (PI3K p85, SHP2, SOS1, CRK, STAT3). This module also includes the negative regulatory apparatus: the CBL E3 ubiquitin ligase system and the endocytic trafficking machinery that control receptor turnover (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9, gallo2024themetoncogenea pages 13-15). RON (MST1R), the only other member of the MET RTK family, shares structural features with MET and its ligand MSP (macrophage-stimulating protein) is homologous to HGF, but RON signaling is typically treated as a parallel rather than overlapping system.

### 2.2 What Should Be Treated Separately

Several neighboring processes are frequently conflated with the core HGF–MET module but are mechanistically distinct:

- **EGFR and other RTK crosstalk**: MET forms extensive interaction networks with other RTKs (EGFR, HER2, AXL, PDGFR), integrins, CD44, and plexins (gallo2024themetoncogene pages 1-3). These interactions modulate MET signaling output and contribute to therapeutic resistance, but represent trans-regulatory inputs rather than core components of the pathway.
- **Downstream RAS–MAPK and PI3K–AKT cascades**: While MET feeds into these canonical signaling highways, these cascades are shared by dozens of RTKs and should be regarded as general effector pathways rather than MET-specific modules.
- **Endosomal signaling**: MET continues to signal from endosomes after internalization, and sorting nexins (SNX1/2) regulate the spatiotemporal dynamics of this process. However, endosomal signaling represents a distinct regulatory layer on top of the core activation mechanism (gallo2024themetoncogenea pages 13-15).
- **Semaphorin–plexin signaling**: Although MET evolved from plexin-like ancestors and shares SEMA domain architecture, modern MET binds HGF rather than semaphorins, and the two systems operate through different effector logics (alves2019originandevolution pages 6-7, alves2019originandevolution pages 1-2).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### 3.1 HGF–MET Ligation

HGF is secreted as an inactive single-chain precursor (pro-HGF) that must be cleaved by extracellular serine proteases to generate the mature, active heterodimer consisting of an α-chain and a β-chain linked by disulfide bonds (linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9, linossi2021stateofthe pages 9-11). The α-chain, containing an N-terminal domain and four kringle domains, provides the primary high-affinity binding interface with MET, while the β-chain (a serine protease homology domain lacking catalytic activity) contributes to receptor engagement at a second, lower-affinity site (raj2022molecularmechanism(s)of pages 3-5, linossi2021stateofthe pages 4-6). Heparan sulfate proteoglycans serve as co-factors that facilitate HGF dimerization and enhance its local concentration at the cell surface, though the precise mechanism remains under investigation (linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9).

MET is a disulfide-linked heterodimer consisting of a 50-kDa α-chain and a 145-kDa β-chain (raj2022molecularmechanism(s)of pages 3-5). The extracellular region is organized into SEMA, PSI (plexin–semaphorin–integrin), and four IPT (immunoglobulin–plexin–transcription factor) domains, followed by a single-pass transmembrane helix containing a GxxxG dimerization motif (Gly933–Gly937), a juxtamembrane regulatory segment, a tyrosine kinase domain, and a C-terminal docking tail (alghabkari2024aberrantmetreceptor pages 2-4, linossi2021stateofthe pages 9-11). HGF binds the SEMA domain at two sites with different affinities; the combined α- and β-chain interactions are required for productive receptor activation (raj2022molecularmechanism(s)of pages 3-5, linossi2021stateofthe pages 4-6).

A prevailing model posits a 2:2 HGF:MET heterodimeric complex in which an HGF dimer bridges two MET extracellular domains (linossi2021stateofthe pages 3-4, linossi2021stateofthe pages 4-6). Recent cryo-EM studies have revealed an asymmetric 2:2 MET:HGF complex architecture, though high-resolution structures of the full complex in a membrane-embedded context remain incomplete (gallo2024themetoncogene pages 1-3, linossi2021stateofthe pages 4-6).

### 3.2 MET Docking-Platform Assembly

Ligand-induced dimerization brings intracellular kinase domains into proximity, enabling trans-autophosphorylation. The activation is sequential: Y1235 in the activation loop is phosphorylated first due to its greater solvent exposure, destabilizing the autoinhibited conformation; Y1234 phosphorylation follows and together they stabilize the fully active kinase state (linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9). The catalytically active kinase then phosphorylates C-terminal residues Y1349 and Y1356, creating the SH2-recognition motif Y1349VHVX₃Y1356VNV that serves as the core docking platform (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-2, raj2022molecularmechanism(s)of pages 3-5).

**GRB2** binds to phosphorylated MET via its SH2 domain, with pY1356 being the principal GRB2-binding site (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, perezbaena2023theroleof pages 10-12). GRB2's N- and C-terminal SH3 domains simultaneously engage proline-rich motifs on downstream effectors: the nSH3 domain recruits the guanine nucleotide exchange factor SOS1 to activate RAS, while the cSH3 domain can bind GAB1 proline-rich sequences (breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 3-5, breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 1-3). Remarkably, recent structural work has shown that GRB2 can self-assemble into two-dimensional meshwork-like multimers and phase-separated condensates through intermolecular SH2/cSH3 interfaces, with Gab1 peptides serving as bivalent cross-linkers that promote this assembly (breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 3-5, breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 22-24, breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 5-8, breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 1-3). The SH2 phosphotyrosine-binding site remains accessible within these multimeric assemblies, suggesting that GRB2 condensation may amplify signal transduction at activated receptors (breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 1-3, breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 8-10).

**GAB1** (GRB2-associated binder-1) is the principal multisite docking adaptor downstream of MET and is recruited through two complementary mechanisms (perezbaena2023theroleof pages 2-4, perezbaena2023theroleof pages 12-13, ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 10-11):

1. **Direct recruitment**: GAB1 possesses a unique MET-binding domain (MBD, amino acids 450–532) that directly contacts the activated MET kinase domain—a feature that distinguishes MET signaling from most other RTK pathways (perezbaena2023theroleof pages 1-2, ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 10-11).
2. **Indirect recruitment via GRB2**: GAB1's proline-rich region contains both canonical (PX₃RX₂KPX₇PLD) and atypical (PXXXR) GRB2-binding motifs that engage GRB2's C-terminal SH3 domain, providing an alternative recruitment route (perezbaena2023theroleof pages 2-4). Importantly, GAB1 recruitment requires a functional GRB2-binding site at Y1356 on MET (perezbaena2023theroleof pages 10-12), though GRB2-independent recruitment that depends on the C-terminal lobe and structural integrity of the MET kinase domain has also been demonstrated (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 10-11, alghabkari2024aberrantmetreceptor pages 13-14).

Once recruited and phosphorylated, GAB1 serves as a signaling platform with 16 phosphotyrosine sites and a PH domain for membrane localization (perezbaena2023theroleof pages 1-2, perezbaena2023theroleof pages 4-5). GAB1 recruits:
- **PI3K p85** via SH2-domain recognition of GAB1 phosphotyrosines, activating the PI3K–AKT survival pathway; ERK-mediated phosphorylation of GAB1 at T477 enhances this interaction (perezbaena2023theroleof pages 2-4, perezbaena2023theroleof pages 5-7).
- **SHP2** via binding to phosphotyrosines Y627 and Y659, which form a bisphosphoryl tyrosine-based activation motif (BTAM), driving RAS–MAPK–ERK signaling (perezbaena2023theroleof pages 2-4, perezbaena2023theroleof pages 12-13, perezbaena2023theroleof pages 4-5).
- **CRK** and other SH2-containing proteins that contribute to cytoskeletal remodeling and migration (perezbaena2023theroleof pages 1-2, perezbaena2023theroleof pages 4-5).

### 3.3 Motility and Growth Output

The activated MET docking platform drives three major downstream cascades:

**RAS–MAPK pathway**: GRB2 recruits SOS1, a guanine nucleotide exchange factor that activates RAS–GTP, initiating the RAF–MEK–ERK kinase cascade. This cascade regulates nuclear transcription factors (Elk1, Ets1, c-Myc) that control proliferation, differentiation, motility, and invasion (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, kim2024pancreaticcancertreatment pages 4-6).

**PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway**: PI3K p85 recruitment (directly or via GAB1) activates PI3K, generating PIP₃ which activates AKT. AKT promotes cell survival by inhibiting pro-apoptotic factors (BAD, FOXO) and activating effectors involved in metabolism, protein synthesis, and cell-cycle progression (mTOR, GSK3β, MDM2) (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, gallo2024themetoncogenea pages 1-2).

**JAK–STAT pathway**: MET activates STAT3 indirectly through JAK or SRC-family kinases. Phosphorylated STAT3 dimerizes and translocates to the nucleus to regulate genes governing survival, differentiation, invasion, and morphogenesis; STAT3 and NF-κB are critical for HGF-dependent tubule formation and branching morphogenesis (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-3, ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, gallo2024themetoncogenea pages 1-2).

Additional effectors include PLCγ, c-Src, and Wnt pathway intermediates, whose activation contributes to the full spectrum of MET biological outputs: proliferation, motility, invasiveness, angiogenesis, and anti-apoptotic signaling—collectively termed the "invasive growth" program (gallo2024themetoncogenea pages 1-2, raj2022molecularmechanism(s)of pages 1-3).

### 4.1 Negative Regulation and Signal Termination

Signal termination is primarily achieved through CBL-mediated ubiquitination. Upon HGF-induced activation, phosphorylation of Y1003 in the MET juxtamembrane domain recruits the E3 ubiquitin ligase CBL via its TKB (tyrosine kinase binding) domain, which contains a conserved DpYR motif (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-2, ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 10-11). CBL-mediated polyubiquitination targets MET for endocytosis via the endophilin–CIN85–CBL complex, followed by endosomal sorting and lysosomal degradation (linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9, linossi2021stateofthe pages 16-18, gallo2024themetoncogenea pages 13-15).

A second negative regulatory mechanism involves phosphorylation of S985 in the juxtamembrane domain by protein kinase C (PKC), which inhibits MET kinase activity and reduces phosphorylation of downstream tyrosines Y1234, Y1235, Y1349, and Y1356 (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9). S985 phosphorylation is bidirectionally regulated by PKC and protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A), serving as a rheostat for signaling intensity (linossi2021stateofthe pages 16-18). Additionally, the juxtamembrane domain contains a caspase cleavage site (ESVD1002) that can inactivate MET under apoptotic stress (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-2).

The critical importance of these negative regulatory mechanisms is illustrated by MET exon 14 skipping mutations (METΔex14), found in approximately 3–4% of non-small cell lung cancers. Loss of the juxtamembrane domain eliminates the Y1003 CBL-binding site, impairing ubiquitination and lysosomal degradation and prolonging oncogenic signaling—an alteration sufficient to transform cells (linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9, ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-2, ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 10-11, ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-3).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### 5.1 Evolutionary Origin and Conservation

Phylogenetic analyses reveal that MET RTKs evolved through the acquisition of an intracellular tyrosine kinase domain by a plexin-like ancestor in the lineage leading to Bilateria, making MET a relatively recent evolutionary innovation (alves2019originandevolution pages 6-7, alves2019originandevolution pages 1-2). The extracellular SEMA domain is shared between MET, plexins, and semaphorins, reflecting their common ancestry. However, while plexins bind semaphorins through their SEMA domains, MET binds the structurally unrelated growth factor HGF—a fundamental functional divergence (alves2019originandevolution pages 1-2).

Key evolutionary observations include:
- **Bilaterian origin**: MET RTKs are found in Chordata, Lophotrochozoa, and Echinodermata, but are notably absent from Ecdysozoa (insects, nematodes), indicating lineage-specific loss in this major clade (alves2019originandevolution pages 6-7).
- **Met-LP RTKs**: A novel class of MET-like RTKs retaining a complete plexin extracellular domain (Met-LP RTK) was identified in Lophotrochozoa and Echinodermata, representing independent acquisition of tyrosine kinase domains by plexin-like precursors (alves2019originandevolution pages 6-7).
- **Plexin–semaphorin ancestry**: Plexins and semaphorins themselves emerged over 600 million years ago in a common ancestor of choanoflagellates and Metazoa, predating the emergence of MET RTKs (alves2019originandevolution pages 6-7).
- **RON (MST1R)**: The MET family's only other member in vertebrates, RON arose through gene duplication and divergence. Both MET and RON share downstream signaling logic (RAS–MAPK, PI3K–AKT) and contribute to pro-tumor metabolism through MYC-dependent glycolysis and CTNNB1-dependent cholesterol biosynthesis.

### 5.2 Cell-Type and Tissue Variation

HGF–MET signaling operates primarily in a paracrine mode: HGF is produced by mesenchymal cells (fibroblasts, stellate cells) while MET is expressed predominantly on epithelial cells—a spatial separation that restricts pathway activation to contexts of tissue injury, development, or stromal–epithelial communication (raj2022molecularmechanism(s)of pages 3-5, gallo2024themetoncogenea pages 1-2). Physiological roles include:
- **Liver regeneration**: HGF is a primary mitogen for hepatocytes following partial hepatectomy or injury.
- **Embryonic development**: MET knockout is embryonic lethal, with defects in placenta, liver, limb, diaphragm, and cardiac development; GAB1-deficient embryos display similar phenotypes, underscoring GAB1's essential role in MET-dependent morphogenesis (perezbaena2023theroleof pages 1-2, alghabkari2024aberrantmetreceptor pages 13-14).
- **Wound healing and tissue repair**: HGF promotes re-epithelialization and regeneration in skin, kidney, and lung epithelia (gallo2024themetoncogenea pages 1-2).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### 6.1 Obligatory Ordering

Several steps in MET activation must occur in a strict sequence. HGF must be proteolytically activated before it can productively engage MET; receptor dimerization must precede trans-autophosphorylation; Y1234/Y1235 phosphorylation must precede full catalytic activation; and C-terminal Y1349/Y1356 phosphorylation is required before the docking platform can assemble (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-2, linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9, linossi2021stateofthe pages 9-11). Notably, the C-terminal tail may stabilize the activation loop in an inactive state until Y1349 and Y1356 become phosphorylated and disengage from the kinase C-lobe, providing an additional autoinhibitory mechanism (linossi2021stateofthe pages 9-11).

### 6.2 Mutual Exclusivity and Context-Dependence

- **CBL-mediated degradation vs. sustained signaling**: Y1003 phosphorylation and CBL recruitment are temporally coupled to receptor activation—CBL ubiquitination occurs after signaling has been initiated, creating a built-in timer for signal termination. Loss of Y1003 (as in METΔex14) decouples activation from degradation (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-2).
- **S985 vs. kinase activation**: PKC-mediated S985 phosphorylation acts as a negative regulator that is mutually antagonistic with full kinase activation; PP2A reverses this phosphorylation to restore responsiveness (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, linossi2021stateofthe pages 16-18).
- **GAB1 PH domain requirement**: GAB1 localization to the plasma membrane via its PH domain is required for epithelial morphogenesis downstream of MET, linking pathway output to membrane lipid composition (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 10-11, alghabkari2024aberrantmetreceptor pages 13-14).
- **Juxtamembrane domain integrity**: Complete loss of the juxtamembrane domain's first 39 amino acids abolishes kinase activation potential, while partial loss may inhibit activation, and complete deletion leads to constitutive, unregulated activation—demonstrating that this region serves as both a positive and negative regulator (ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4).

### 6.3 Failure Modes with Clinical Consequences

Oncogenic dysregulation of MET occurs through multiple mechanisms: gene amplification (METamp), exon 14 skipping mutations (METΔex14, ~3–4% of NSCLC), activating point mutations in the kinase domain, gene fusions, and protein overexpression (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-3, ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 3-4, ji2025targetingcmetalterations pages 1-3). METΔex14 mutations result in prolonged pathway activation driving upregulation of invasion-associated genes (SERPINE1, MMP1, NRP1) and growth/survival genes (ELK3, ETV1, TMEM158) (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-3). FDA-approved selective MET inhibitors including capmatinib and tepotinib have demonstrated clinical efficacy in METΔex14-positive NSCLC, with objective response rates of ~40–68% depending on treatment line (ghosh2025mechanisticinsightsinto pages 2-3).

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

Despite decades of research, several fundamental questions remain unresolved:

1. **Full-length HGF–MET complex structure**: The structure of full-length HGF remains unknown, and high-resolution structures of the complete MET extracellular domain in apo or HGF-bound states are lacking. Current models derive from studies of fragments rather than intact molecules (linossi2021stateofthe pages 9-11, linossi2021stateofthe pages 4-6, linossi2021stateofthe pages 1-3).

2. **HGF α-chain binding site controversy**: While the SEMA domain (blades 5–7) is the accepted primary binding site for HGF's α-chain, an alternative primary binding site in the IPT3–4 domains of the MET stalk has been proposed but remains unresolved (linossi2021stateofthe pages 4-6).

3. **Role of heparan sulfate**: How heparan sulfate proteoglycans mechanistically facilitate HGF dimerization and enhance ligand affinity remains unknown despite its demonstrated biological importance (linossi2021stateofthe pages 8-9).

4. **Transmembrane signal transduction**: How conformational changes in the MET extracellular domain are translated through the transmembrane helix to activate the intracellular kinase domain is largely uncharacterized. The assembly of intracellular domains upon ligand binding remains poorly understood (linossi2021stateofthe pages 9-11, linossi2021stateofthe pages 3-4).

5. **Furin cleavage significance**: MET is cleaved by furin at Arg307–Ser308, positioned near the putative HGF α-chain binding site. The functional significance of this processing for ligand binding and receptor activation is unknown (linossi2021stateofthe pages 3-4).

6. **GRB2 phase separation in vivo**: While GRB2 can form meshwork-like condensates that concentrate signaling components in vitro, the physiological relevance of these structures at activated MET receptors in living cells remains to be established (breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 3-5, breithaupt2023selfassemblyofgrb2 pages 22-24).

7. **Endosomal vs. surface signaling**: Whether MET signals primarily from the plasma membrane or continues to signal—or signals differently—from endosomal compartments after internalization remains an area of active investigation, with implications for understanding how negative regulators like SNX1/2 shape signaling output (gallo2024themetoncogenea pages 13-15).

8. **Co-receptor mechanisms**: MET forms co-complexes with numerous cell-surface proteins including RTKs, integrins, CD44, plexins, and GPCRs (gallo2024themetoncogene pages 1-3). Whether these represent direct interactions and how they converge on or diverge from canonical MET homodimer activation is structurally undefined (linossi2021stateofthe pages 1-3).
