L-Aspartate de novo and Selected Salvage Routes to NAD⁺

A scope-limited review of the routes that converge on nicotinate mononucleotide (NaMN) and complete NAD⁺ through deamido-NAD, plus the topologically distinct NAMPT→NMN direct-completion route.

1. Executive summary

Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD⁺) is assembled from a small pyridine mononucleotide (NaMN or NMN) by a two-reaction "completion" chemistry — adenylylation of the mononucleotide to a dinucleotide, then installation of the amide nitrogen. The system reviewed here is the set of routes that (i) build or recover the deamidated mononucleotide nicotinate mononucleotide (NaMN), and (ii) carry NaMN through deamido-NAD (nicotinate adenine dinucleotide, NaAD) to NAD⁺. NaMN is made by four routings of three chemistries: de novo from L-aspartate (NadB → NadA → NadC); by Preiss–Handler phosphoribosylation of nicotinate (PncB) either directly or after nicotinamide deamidation by PncA; or by deamidation of NMN (PncC). All three converge on a single obligatory node: NaMN must be adenylylated (NadD, or a bifunctional NMNAT) to deamido-NAD and then amidated by NAD synthetase (NadE/NADSYN) to NAD⁺.

Standing apart from this deamidated arm is one amidated route that is included here because it shares the same terminal adenylyltransferase family but uses a different completion topology: NAMPT phosphoribosylates nicotinamide to NMN, and NMN is adenylylated directly to NAD⁺ by NMNAT, with no NAD synthetase step. The central organizing principle of the entire system is therefore when the amide nitrogen is installed: late (deamidated arm, NadE-dependent) versus early (NAMPT arm, NadE-independent).

The review deliberately excludes the L-tryptophan/kynurenine de novo route (owned by a separate module), nicotinate degradation, transhydrogenases, NAD kinase/NADP formation, and NAD-consuming reactions.

The amidated arm (bottom, red) is topologically separate: NAMPT makes NMN already bearing the amide, and NMNAT adenylylates it directly to NAD⁺ with no NAD synthetase step. The organizing variable is the timing of amide-nitrogen installation — late (deamidated arm) versus early (amidated arm).

2. Definition and biological boundaries

2.1 What is inside the boundary

The system comprises exactly these reactions:

A. NaMN production 1. L-aspartate de novo: L-aspartate → iminoaspartate (NadB, L-aspartate oxidase) → quinolinate (NadA, quinolinate synthase) → NaMN (NadC, quinolinate phosphoribosyltransferase, decarboxylating). 2. Direct Preiss–Handler entry: nicotinate → NaMN (PncB, nicotinate phosphoribosyltransferase). 3. Nicotinamide deamidation then Preiss–Handler: nicotinamide → nicotinate (PncA, nicotinamidase) → NaMN (PncB). 4. NMN deamidation: NMN → NaMN (PncC, NMN deamidase).

B. Deamidated completion (shared) 5. NaMN → deamido-NAD (NadD bacterial family, or bifunctional NMNAT). 6. deamido-NAD → NAD⁺ (NadE/NADSYN, ammonia- or glutamine-dependent).

C. Amidated direct completion (separate topology) 7. nicotinamide → NMN (NAMPT). 8. NMN → NAD⁺ directly (NMNAT).

2.2 What is outside the boundary (and often confused with it)

  • L-tryptophan / kynurenine de novo route. In fungi and animals, quinolinate — the same intermediate produced by NadA — is generated instead from tryptophan via kynurenine (TDO/IDO, KMO, kynureninase KYNU, HAAO). This produces the identical NaMN branch point but by entirely different upstream chemistry and is treated in the separate kynurenine_nad_de_novo module. It is important not to double-count quinolinate → NaMN → NAD⁺: NadC and the completion enzymes are shared, but the aspartate and tryptophan arms are parallel, mutually alternative sources of quinolinate. Human Congenital NAD Deficiency Disorder maps to the tryptophan arm (KYNU, HAAO) and to the shared completion enzyme NADSYN1 (33942433 35491967).
  • Nicotinate degradation (the nic catabolic cluster) — consumes, rather than salvages, nicotinate.
  • Pyridine-nucleotide transhydrogenases, NAD kinase (NAD⁺ → NADP⁺), and NAD-consuming enzymes (sirtuins, PARPs, ADP-ribosyl cyclases, bacterial DNA ligase). These define the demand side and the NADP pool, not NAD⁺ synthesis, and are excluded.
  • Nicotinamide riboside (NR) salvage via nicotinamide riboside kinases (NRK1/2) feeds NMN and is adjacent, but is not itemized in this scope's provisional outline; it is mentioned only where it clarifies the NMN node.

2.3 Competing definitions in the literature

Two framing conventions coexist. The microbial literature (Osterman, Rodionov, Raffaelli and colleagues) treats NAD homeostasis as a pyridine nucleotide cycle: NAD is consumed to nicotinamide/NMN and recycled through PncA/PncB/PncC back to NaMN (7592458 21953451). The mammalian literature emphasizes the NAMPT-centred salvage loop (nicotinamide → NMN → NAD⁺) as the dominant flux (35903330). These are not contradictory: they describe organisms that install the amide nitrogen at opposite ends of the pathway. A recurring source of confusion is the word "salvage," which is used for both the deamidated (PncA/PncB/PncC) and amidated (NAMPT/NRK) routes even though they complete NAD⁺ by different topologies.

3. Mechanistic overview

3.1 The two completion topologies

The unifying insight is that NAD⁺ completion is a mononucleotide → dinucleotide adenylylation followed (or not) by an amidation:

  • Deamidated arm. NaMN (carries a carboxylate at the pyridine 3-position) is adenylylated to deamido-NAD (NaAD), which still carries the carboxylate. A dedicated NAD synthetase (NadE) then converts the carboxylate to the amide using ATP activation and an ammonia donor. Two chemical steps of nitrogen handling are required overall (the pyridine ring enters already deamidated).
  • Amidated arm. NMN already carries the amide. A single adenylylation by NMNAT yields NAD⁺ directly. No NadE step exists or is needed.

Consequently, the oxidation/deamidation state of the mononucleotide dictates the number of downstream steps: anything at the NaMN state is committed to the two-step NadD + NadE completion; anything at the NMN state can be completed in one step. This is the single most important constraint in the system (Section 6).

3.2 Obligatory, conditional, and accessory steps

  • Obligatory for any organism using this system: the adenylylation step (NadD or NMNAT) and, for the deamidated arm, the amidation step (NadE). NaMN → deamido-NAD → NAD⁺ has no known bypass.
  • Conditional (route-selecting): which NaMN source operates depends on genotype and nutrient supply. De novo NadABC is used when no exogenous pyridine is available; Preiss–Handler (PncB) is used when nicotinate is available; PncA precedes PncB when the supplied vitamer is nicotinamide; PncC operates where NMN accumulates from NAD turnover.
  • Accessory / recycling: PncA, PncB, and PncC are dispensable in a minimal-medium de novo organism but essential for pyridine-nucleotide cycling and for growth on specific vitamers. Regulatory proteins (NiaR, NrtR, Rex) tune expression but are not catalytic.

3.3 Step-by-step

  1. L-aspartate oxidation (NadB). An FAD-dependent desaturating dehydrogenase converts L-aspartate to iminoaspartate, reducing FAD; the flavin is reoxidized by fumarate anaerobically and by molecular O₂ aerobically, the latter releasing H₂O₂ as an adventitious by-product (eliminating NadB turnover cut endogenous H₂O₂ by ~25–30 % in an E. coli scavenger-null strain) (20149100). NadB is homologous to the flavoprotein subunit of succinate dehydrogenase/fumarate reductase — a deeply conserved respiratory-flavoprotein lineage. This redox flexibility lets the de novo arm's first step operate across oxygen tensions, complementing the O₂-sensitivity of the downstream [4Fe-4S] NadA.
  2. Quinolinate synthase (NadA). Condenses iminoaspartate with dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) and cyclizes/dehydrates to quinolinate (31390192). NadA carries a [4Fe-4S]²⁺ cluster ligated by only three conserved cysteines, leaving a unique non-cysteinyl fourth iron that binds substrate/intermediate and is central to catalysis (18959769 31390192). The cluster makes NadA oxygen-sensitive, a physiologically important liability.
  3. Quinolinate phosphoribosyltransferase (NadC). Transfers the phosphoribosyl group from PRPP to quinolinate and decarboxylates, giving NaMN + CO₂. This is a type-II PRTase and is the point at which the aspartate and tryptophan de novo arms merge.
  4. Preiss–Handler / deamidation feeders. PncA (nicotinamidase) hydrolyzes nicotinamide → nicotinate + NH₃; PncB (nicotinate PRTase) uses PRPP and ATP to make NaMN (ATP hydrolysis raises the effective equilibrium toward NaMN); PncC deamidates NMN → NaMN directly.
  5. NaMN adenylylation (NadD or NMNAT). ATP + NaMN → NaAD + PPi.
  6. Amidation (NadE/NADSYN). NaAD is adenylylated on its carboxylate (forming an NaAD-adenylate), then attacked by ammonia (ammonia-dependent NadE) or by ammonia liberated in-situ from glutamine (glutamine-dependent NADSYN), giving NAD⁺ + AMP + PPi.
  7. NAMPT arm. NAMPT + PRPP convert nicotinamide → NMN; NMNAT adenylylates NMN → NAD⁺ directly.

Active-assembly highlights.

  • NadA/NadB coupling. The two de novo enzymes form a functional pair; cross-species reconstitution (B. subtilis NadA with E. coli NadB) works, indicating a conserved interaction interface for iminoaspartate channelling (18959769). Iminoaspartate is unstable, so hand-off limits its escape.
  • NadD as a fold archetype. NadD, NMNAT, and phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase share the nucleotidyltransferase α/β phosphodiesterase superfamily with the (T/H)xGH motif; bacterial NadD and human NMNAT are structurally similar yet pharmacologically separable, which is why NadD is an antibacterial target (19716475 20578699).
  • Glutamine-dependent NadE as a channeling machine. The synthetase and glutaminase active sites are separated by a ~40 Å ammonia tunnel with three constriction points that gate transfer and enforce kinetic synergism between the two active sites (22280445 31911602).

5. Evolutionary and cell-biological variation

5.1 Across major lineages

  • Bacteria (and plants, and many archaea). Predominantly use the L-aspartate de novo arm (NadB/NadA/NadC) plus NadD (nicotinate-specific) and NadE. The amide nitrogen is installed late. Deamidated salvage (PncA/PncB/PncC) recycles pyridines back to NaMN. Comparative-genomic reconstructions across hundreds of bacteria show extensive lineage-specific presence/absence of these genes and multiple transcriptional strategies (NiaR in Firmicutes/Thermotogales; NrtR/Nudix regulators broadly; Rex for redox balance) (18276644 18276643 22210771).
  • Fungi and animals. Lack NadB/NadA; make quinolinate from tryptophan (outside this scope) and rely heavily on amidated NAMPT salvage, with NMN completed directly by NMNAT — the amide nitrogen is installed early. Humans have both an ammonia-dependent (NADSYN2-like) and glutamine-dependent (NADSYN1) NAD synthetase for the deamido-NAD produced by their tryptophan/ Preiss–Handler flux (12547821).
  • Pathogen reductive loss. Shigella/EIEC illustrate pathoadaptive loss of the de novo arm: silencing of nadA/nadB yields a nicotinic-acid–requiring (Nic⁻) phenotype, apparently because quinolinate attenuates invasion (24120364). This is a clean natural experiment showing the de novo arm is dispensable when salvage substrate is available.
  • Mobile/large genetic elements. Some gut "jumbo" phages encode complete NAD synthesis gene sets, an extreme case of horizontal acquisition of this module (39707494).

5.2 Enzyme-family variation that achieves the same outcome

  • Adenylyltransferase families: bacterial NadD (nicotinate-selective, deamidated arm only) versus eukaryotic NMNAT (dual NaMN/NMN specificity, serves both arms). These are the "alternative adenylyltransferase families" of the brief.
  • NAD synthetase nitrogen donors: ammonia-dependent (synthetase core only) versus glutamine-dependent (added CN-hydrolase glutaminase domain + ammonia tunnel). The glutamine dependence is a modular add-on: mutating the glutaminase catalytic Cys reverts NADSYN1 to ammonia-only behaviour (12547821).

5.3 Cell-type, compartment, and physiological state (eukaryotes)

The three human NMNAT isoforms are compartmentalized — NMNAT1 (nucleus), NMNAT2 (Golgi/cytosol), NMNAT3 (mitochondria) — with distinct kinetics, metal preferences, and substrate-binding order, arguing for compartment- specific NAD pools rather than one freely mixing cellular pool (16118205 17402747 29748257). NMNAT2 is a labile axon-survival factor whose loss triggers Wallerian-type axon degeneration (a major reason this branch is biomedically prominent). NAMPT is rate-limiting and stress-regulated, coupling NAD⁺ supply to metabolic and inflammatory state (35903330).

5.4 Deepest plausible origin and what is ancient vs. derived

NAD⁺ itself is among the very small set of coenzymes assigned to the core metabolism of the last universal common ancestor (LUCA) — alongside ADP and pyridoxal phosphate — and all three have been shown to be catalytically active in their metabolic reactions in the absence of enzymes, implying a prebiotic contribution to abiogenesis (39292780). The pressure to synthesize and regenerate NAD is therefore essentially as old as cellular life, and the completion chemistry — mononucleotide → dinucleotide adenylylation followed by amidation — is best read as the ancient, conserved core of the system.

Reading the parts by apparent age:

  • Ancient / conserved: (i) the adenylylation step, catalyzed by the nucleotidyltransferase α/β-phosphodiesterase superfamily that also produced phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase — a fold shared by NadD and NMNAT and therefore predating the bacterial/eukaryotic split; (ii) NAD synthetase, an ancestral member of the ATP-pyrophosphatase (N-type amidotransferase) clan; (iii) the L-aspartate de novo arm — NadB belongs to the deeply conserved succinate-dehydrogenase/fumarate-reductase flavoprotein family and works anaerobically (fumarate) as readily as aerobically (20149100), while NadA's [4Fe-4S] chemistry is a hallmark of ancient, anaerobe-compatible metallo-enzymes. The aspartate route's anaerobic compatibility fits an early, low-oxygen biosphere.
  • Later elaborations / replacements / losses: (i) the glutaminase domain + ammonia tunnel appended to NAD synthetase is a modular add-on to an ammonia-dependent core (mutating the glutaminase Cys reverts human NADSYN1 to ammonia-only; 12547821), and phylogenetics identify a poorly synergistic glutamine-dependent enzyme (T. maritima) as closer to the ancestral state, with tight coupling being derived (19647806); (ii) the amidated NAMPT→NMN→NMNAT topology, dominant in animals, is a lineage where the de novo aspartate arm was lost and replaced by tryptophan-derived quinolinate plus heavy amidated salvage; (iii) reductive losses of de novo genes (Shigella nadA/nadB; obligate host-associated bacteria) recur wherever pyridine vitamers are reliably available.

Best representatives for the ancestral role. Because these families expanded, the least specialized members are the most informative: for the adenylyltransferase fold, bacterial NadD (single-domain, nicotinate-specific) better reflects the ancestral adenylylation than the compartment-specialized, dual-specificity eukaryotic NMNAT isoforms; for the amidation step, a poorly-coupled glutamine-dependent NadE such as T. maritima's, or a plain ammonia-dependent NadE, better represents the ancestral synthetase than the highly synergistic human/mycobacterial enzymes (19647806).

6. Constraints, dependencies, and failure modes

6.1 Ordering constraints (hard)

  1. Amide-timing determines topology. A nucleotide at the NaMN state can only reach NAD⁺ via NadD → NadE (two nitrogen-handling operations). A nucleotide at the NMN state reaches NAD⁺ via NMNAT alone. There is no productive "NaMN → NAD⁺ direct amidated adenylylation" and no "NMN → deamido-NAD → NAD⁺" within the completion step — the only way to move NMN into the deamidated arm is to deamidate it first (PncC). This mutual exclusivity is the backbone constraint of the system.
  2. Adenylylation precedes amidation. NadE acts on deamido-NAD, not on NaMN; the ribose-adenylyl dinucleotide must exist before the ring amide can be installed. Order is fixed.
  3. NadB precedes NadA precedes NadC. Iminoaspartate (NadB product) is the NadA substrate; quinolinate (NadA product) is the NadC substrate. The instability of iminoaspartate enforces tight kinetic/physical coupling of NadB–NadA.
  4. PncA precedes PncB when the entering vitamer is nicotinamide (must be deamidated to nicotinate before phosphoribosylation by PncB).

6.2 Substrate/compartment specificity

  • Bacterial NadD is nicotinate-selective: it will not efficiently complete amidated NMN, so bacteria relying on NadD must deamidate (PncC) any NMN they wish to reuse (11704676 7592458). Eukaryotic NMNAT is permissive and therefore removes this constraint, which is part of why the amidated arm dominates in animals.
  • O₂ sensitivity of NadA restricts robust de novo flux under aerobiosis unless the cluster is protected/repaired; this is one plausible selective pressure favouring salvage in some aerobes.

6.3 Failure modes

  • Loss of completion enzymes is lethal/near-lethal. NadD and NadE are essential in most bacteria (validated antibacterial targets; 19716475 33372865). In humans, biallelic NADSYN1 loss-of-function causes Congenital NAD Deficiency Disorder (VACTERL-like vertebral, cardiac, renal, limb malformations; NAD pools partially rescuable by nicotinamide supplementation), demonstrating that the amidation node is developmentally indispensable (33942433 35491967 36649848 42153968).
  • Loss of a NaMN source is conditionally lethal. nadA/nadB silencing is tolerated only when nicotinate/nicotinamide is supplied (Nic⁻ phenotype; 24120364).
  • NMN toxicity/regulatory coupling. Accumulated NMN can inhibit essential enzymes (e.g., DNA ligase in Salmonella); PncC recycling relieves this, linking the deamidated arm to genome maintenance (7592458).

6.4 Evidence that rules out otherwise-plausible paths

  • There is no enzyme that amidates NaMN directly to NMN in this system; the amide is installed either at the free base (NAMPT uses nicotinamide) or at the dinucleotide (NadE on NaAD) — never on the deamidated mononucleotide. The strict NaMN→NaAD→NAD⁺ order is supported by the substrate specificity of NadE (acts on NaAD) and of NadD (acts on NaMN) (16784754 11704676).

7. Controversies and open questions

  1. NadA catalytic mechanism and cluster ligation. The role of the unique non-cysteinyl iron and the identity of on-pathway intermediates remain partly unsettled; a crystallographically trapped species has been argued to be an intermediate but its assignment is debated (31390192). How the O₂-sensitive cluster is protected/repaired in aerobes is unresolved.
  2. Regulation of the glutaminase↔synthetase coupling in NadE. Kinetic synergism and ammonia-tunnel gating are documented, but the degree of "leak" (uncoupled glutamine hydrolysis) varies by organism, and an ancestral, poorly-synergistic glutamine-dependent enzyme (T. maritima) has been proposed — implying the tight coupling seen in M. tuberculosis/human is a derived optimization (19647806 22280445 31911602). Whether the ancestral NadE was ammonia- or glutamine-dependent is not fully settled.
  3. One NAD pool or many (eukaryotes). Compartmentalized NMNAT isoforms argue for distinct nuclear/Golgi/mitochondrial pools, but the extent of inter-compartment NAD/NMN transport is still being mapped (16118205 29748257).
  4. Moonlighting activities. NAMPT and PncB/NAPT catalyze a facultative ATPase / adenosine-5′-tetraphosphate (Ap4) metabolism of uncertain physiological weight (28416276); the PncC-associated COG1058 domain is an ADP-ribose pyrophosphatase whose functional link to NAD recycling is proposed but not fully defined (23776507).
  5. Organism-mixing caution. Much mechanism is stitched from different organisms (B. subtilis NadA, S. aureus/B. anthracis NadD, M. tuberculosis NadE, human NMNAT/NADSYN, S. typhimurium PncC). Substrate specificity and essentiality are not safely transferable across these without checking — a recurring interpretive risk in this literature.

Limitations of this review

Mechanistic detail is unevenly distributed across model organisms, and several claims are inferred from comparative genomics rather than direct biochemistry. Structural snapshots of NadA intermediates and of the NadE ammonia tunnel are powerful but come from single organisms. The amidated (NAMPT/NMNAT) arm is included for topological contrast but its upstream tryptophan-derived quinolinate flux is deliberately not treated here. Quantitative flux partitioning among the four NaMN sources in any single organism under defined conditions remains an open experimental target.
