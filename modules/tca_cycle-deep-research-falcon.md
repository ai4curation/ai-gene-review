# The Oxidative Tricarboxylic Acid Cycle and Central-Carbon Entry Nodes: A Taxon-Neutral Review

## 1. Executive Summary

The oxidative tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle is among the most deeply conserved metabolic modules in biology, serving as the central hub for the complete oxidation of acetyl-CoA-derived carbon through eight enzymatic steps that regenerate oxaloacetate while producing CO₂, NADH, FADH₂, and substrate-level phosphorylation equivalents (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2, vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 3-5). The cycle does not operate in isolation: its sustained function depends on feeder reactions—principally the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (PDHc) that generates acetyl-CoA, and pyruvate carboxylase that replenishes oxaloacetate—and is flanked by ancillary pathways such as the methylcitrate cycle for propionate detoxification (wijaya2021characterizationandtargeting pages 24-27, arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 3-4). While the textbook eight-step sequence is broadly conserved, substantial enzymatic variation exists at several nodes. These include redox-cofactor variants of isocitrate dehydrogenase (NAD⁺- versus NADP⁺-dependent), nucleotide-specificity variants of succinyl-CoA ligase (ADP- versus GDP-forming), and electron-acceptor variants of malate oxidation (NAD-dependent malate dehydrogenase versus membrane-bound malate:quinone oxidoreductase versus malic enzyme) (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2, vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 5-6, koendjbiharie2021thepeppyruvateoxaloacetatenode pages 11-12, katayama2022malicenzymenot pages 1-2). Evolutionary evidence now supports both prebiotic origins of individual TCA-cycle reactions and a likely post-last-universal-common-ancestor elaboration of the full closed cycle (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 2-3, nogal2023theprotometabolicnature pages 12-14). Beyond its canonical energetic role, the TCA cycle is increasingly recognized as a signaling hub, with intermediates such as succinate, fumarate, and 2-hydroxyglutarate functioning as oncometabolites and immunomodulators when dysregulated (sarkar2025tcacyclederivedoncometabolites pages 2-4, arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 12-13).

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### 2.1 Scope of the System

The oxidative TCA cycle, in its strictest definition, encompasses the eight consecutive enzymatic reactions that convert oxaloacetate plus acetyl-CoA through citrate, isocitrate, 2-oxoglutarate, succinyl-CoA, succinate, fumarate, and malate back to oxaloacetate (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2, vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 3-5). Most TCA cycle enzymes are soluble matrix proteins in mitochondria (or cytoplasmic in bacteria), with the important exception of succinate dehydrogenase (Complex II), which is embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane and simultaneously participates in the respiratory electron transport chain (vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 3-5).

Two classes of reactions closely coupled to, but not intrinsic parts of, the cyclic sequence should be recognized as boundary reactions. The first class comprises **acetyl-CoA-generating feeder reactions**, most prominently the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (PDHc), which irreversibly converts pyruvate to acetyl-CoA in a multienzyme oxidative decarboxylation (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 3-4, szabo2024mitochondrialalphaketoacid pages 1-6). The second class comprises **anaplerotic reactions** that replenish cycle intermediates withdrawn for biosynthesis, principally pyruvate carboxylase (pyruvate → oxaloacetate) and, in many bacteria and plants, phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase (koendjbiharie2021thepeppyruvateoxaloacetatenode pages 11-12, arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 4-6).

### 2.2 What Is Not the TCA Cycle

Several neighboring pathways are frequently conflated with the core cycle. The **glyoxylate shunt** (isocitrate lyase and malate synthase), while sharing intermediates with the TCA cycle, bypasses the two decarboxylation steps and permits net carbon assimilation from acetyl-CoA; it is not a variant of the oxidative cycle but rather an alternative routing (huang2023themethylcitratecycle pages 5-7). The **electron transport chain** (Complexes I, III, IV, and ATP synthase), though functionally coupled to the TCA cycle via NADH and quinol, constitutes a separate system; only Complex II (succinate dehydrogenase) is shared. The **reductive TCA cycle** (rTCA), running in the reverse direction for CO₂ fixation, employs several different enzymes (e.g., ATP-citrate lyase rather than citrate synthase) and should be treated as a distinct pathway despite sharing many intermediates (vazquezsalazar2025evolutionaryroutesto pages 10-12, vazquezsalazar2025evolutionaryroutesto pages 8-10).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### 3.1 The Canonical Eight-Step Sequence

The cycle begins when **citrate synthase** catalyzes the Claisen condensation of acetyl-CoA with oxaloacetate to form citrate, releasing CoA-SH (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2). **Aconitase** (aconitate hydratase) then isomerizes citrate to isocitrate through a cis-aconitate intermediate, employing an iron-sulfur cluster essential for catalysis (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2, vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 3-5). The first oxidative decarboxylation is catalyzed by **isocitrate dehydrogenase**, which exists as NAD⁺-dependent (IDH3 in mammals) and NADP⁺-dependent (IDH1/IDH2) isoforms, generating 2-oxoglutarate (α-ketoglutarate) (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2, vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 5-6).

The second oxidative decarboxylation is performed by the **2-oxoglutarate dehydrogenase complex** (OGDHc), a multienzyme assembly homologous to PDHc, comprising E1 (oxoglutarate dehydrogenase, ThDP-dependent), E2 (dihydrolipoyllysine-residue succinyltransferase), and E3 (dihydrolipoyl dehydrogenase, shared with PDHc), producing succinyl-CoA and NADH (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2, szabo2024mitochondrialalphaketoacid pages 1-6, liu2022structuresandcomparison pages 1-3). **Succinyl-CoA ligase** then converts succinyl-CoA to succinate with concomitant substrate-level phosphorylation, generating either ATP (ADP-forming isoform) or GTP (GDP-forming isoform) depending on subunit composition and tissue context (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2, vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 5-6).

**Succinate dehydrogenase** (SDH/Complex II), a membrane-embedded flavoenzyme with iron-sulfur clusters, oxidizes succinate to fumarate while transferring electrons to the quinone pool, directly coupling TCA oxidation to the respiratory chain (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2, vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 3-5). **Fumarate hydratase** (fumarase) catalyzes the stereospecific hydration of fumarate to L-malate. Finally, **malate dehydrogenase** (MDH) oxidizes L-malate to oxaloacetate using NAD⁺, regenerating both the cycle substrate and NADH (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2).

### 3.2 Feeder and Anaplerotic Entry Nodes

The **pyruvate dehydrogenase complex** (PDHc) operates as the principal gateway linking glycolysis to the TCA cycle. This megadalton-scale assembly (reaching ~10 MDa) comprises three catalytic components—E1 (pyruvate dehydrogenase), E2 (dihydrolipoyl acetyltransferase), and E3 (dihydrolipoyl dehydrogenase)—organized around an E2 core scaffold (szabo2024mitochondrialalphaketoacid pages 1-6, wang2025dynamicsofthe pages 1-3). Recent cryo-electron tomography has revealed the in situ architecture of intact mammalian PDHc, showing an icosahedral E2 core (60-mer) decorated with a heterogeneous and dynamic complement of peripheral E1 (average ~21 heterotetramers, range 1–46) and E3 (average ~4 dimers, up to 12) subunits (wang2025dynamicsofthe pages 3-3, wang2024architectureofintact pages 5-9). Flexible lipoyl domains serve as "swinging arms" that channel substrates between E1, E2, and E3 active sites (wang2025dynamicsofthe pages 3-3, liu2022structuresandcomparison pages 1-3). PDHc activity is tightly regulated: in mammals, four pyruvate dehydrogenase kinases (PDK1–4) phosphorylate and inactivate the complex when NADH, ATP, and acetyl-CoA are abundant, while pyruvate dehydrogenase phosphatases (PDPs) reactivate it in the presence of NAD⁺, ADP, and pyruvate (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 10-11, szabo2024mitochondrialalphaketoacid pages 19-22).

**Pyruvate carboxylase** is the major anaplerotic enzyme in many organisms, carboxylating pyruvate to oxaloacetate using ATP and biotin as cofactors, thereby replenishing cycle intermediates that have been siphoned off for biosynthesis of amino acids, nucleotides, or heme (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 3-4, arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 4-6). In bacteria and plants, **phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase** provides an alternative or complementary anaplerotic route at the PEP-pyruvate-oxaloacetate (PPO) node (koendjbiharie2021thepeppyruvateoxaloacetatenode pages 11-12).

### 3.3 Regulation of Core Cycle Flux

Three enzymes serve as primary regulatory nodes within the cycle: citrate synthase, isocitrate dehydrogenase, and oxoglutarate dehydrogenase (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 10-11). All three are inhibited by elevated NADH levels, which signal saturation of the electron transport chain. High ATP/ADP ratios inhibit IDH, slowing the cycle when cellular energy is sufficient (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 10-11). In plants, redox regulation via thioredoxins and extensive post-translational modifications—including phosphorylation, acetylation, and succinylation—provide additional layers of control, allowing metabolic flux to be rapidly adjusted to meet changing demands during photosynthesis and respiration (zheng2025regulationofplant pages 9-10, zheng2025regulationofplant pages 8-9).

## 4. Major Molecular Players and Active Assemblies

### 4.1 α-Keto Acid Dehydrogenase Complexes

The PDHc and OGDHc belong to the α-keto acid dehydrogenase complex family, which also includes the branched-chain α-keto acid dehydrogenase complex (BCKDHc). All share the same three-component catalytic logic (E1 decarboxylase, E2 acyltransferase, E3 dihydrolipoyl dehydrogenase) and architectural principles, with E2 forming the scaffold and lipoyl domains providing the substrate-channeling mechanism (szabo2024mitochondrialalphaketoacid pages 1-6, szabo2024mitochondrialalphaketoacid pages 6-9). However, the core geometries differ: Gram-negative bacterial PDHc has a 24-meric cubic E2 core, whereas eukaryotic PDHc uses a 60-meric icosahedral core; OGDHc universally uses the 24-meric cubic arrangement (szabo2024mitochondrialalphaketoacid pages 16-19, liu2022structuresandcomparison pages 1-3). The native mammalian OGDHc structure has been resolved at 3.3 Å by cryo-EM, revealing that E2 trimers assemble into a cubic core with E1 and E3 subunits distributed heterogeneously around the surface (liu2022structuresandcomparison pages 1-3). Despite similar monomeric E2 folds, distinct interdomain and intertrimer interactions prevent heterologous assembly between PDHc and OGDHc components (liu2022structuresandcomparison pages 1-3).

### 4.2 Succinate Dehydrogenase (Complex II)

SDH is unique among TCA enzymes in being membrane-integral and serving dual roles in both the cycle and the electron transport chain. It comprises four subunits: SDHA (flavoprotein with covalently bound FAD), SDHB (iron-sulfur protein), and SDHC/SDHD (membrane anchor subunits containing heme and the quinone-binding site) (vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 5-6, vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 3-5). SDH mutations are among the most clinically consequential TCA enzyme defects, causing hereditary paraganglioma-pheochromocytoma syndromes through succinate accumulation (wang2023theconnectionbetween pages 5-6).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### 5.1 Prebiotic Origins and Deep Ancestry

The TCA cycle occupies a central position in theories of life's chemical origins. Under conditions simulating prebiotic environments—warm iron-rich water at ~70°C—pyruvate and glyoxylate can non-enzymatically produce 9 of 11 TCA intermediates and 5 universal metabolic precursors (nogal2023theprotometabolicnature pages 12-14). Additional experimental work has demonstrated that iron-catalyzed, mineral-mediated reactions can accomplish many individual cycle steps without enzymes, supporting the hypothesis that the cycle chemistry preceded enzymatic catalysis (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 2-3, arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 15-16).

The **reductive TCA cycle** has been proposed as a prebiotic predecessor to the modern oxidative cycle, operating in reverse to fix CO₂. However, the rTCA shows a patchy phylogenetic distribution, occurring mainly in certain Proteobacteria, Chlorobi, and Aquificales, and is largely absent from Archaea, suggesting it emerged after the bacterial-archaeal divergence rather than being universal to the last common ancestor (vazquezsalazar2025evolutionaryroutesto pages 10-12, vazquezsalazar2025evolutionaryroutesto pages 8-10). Martin and colleagues argue that the **acetyl-CoA pathway** (Wood-Ljungdahl pathway), rather than the rTCA cycle, represents the most ancient CO₂ fixation route, predating both the rTCA and the oxidative cycle (martin2020olderthangenes pages 13-14, martin2020olderthangenes pages 3-5). The oxidative TCA cycle itself likely became fully functional only after Earth's atmosphere became sufficiently oxygenated to support molecular oxygen as a terminal electron acceptor (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 2-3).

**Malate oxidation** exhibits particularly rich variation. In canonical eukaryotic and many bacterial systems, NAD-dependent MDH performs this step. However, malate:quinone oxidoreductase (MQO), present in approximately 27% of sequenced bacterial genomes, uses quinone rather than NAD⁺ as electron acceptor, rendering the reaction physiologically irreversible and directly coupling it to the respiratory chain (koendjbiharie2021thepeppyruvateoxaloacetatenode pages 11-12). In *Corynebacterium glutamicum*, MQO is the main contributor to TCA cycle malate oxidation, whereas in *Escherichia coli* the NAD-dependent MDH predominates (koendjbiharie2021thepeppyruvateoxaloacetatenode pages 11-12). In organisms lacking MDH entirely, such as *Helicobacter pylori*, MQO assumes the sole malate oxidation role (koendjbiharie2021thepeppyruvateoxaloacetatenode pages 11-12). Strikingly, in cyanobacteria such as *Synechocystis* sp. PCC 6803, neither MDH nor MQO is the primary malate oxidizer. Instead, NADP⁺-dependent **malic enzyme** serves this function, generating NADPH rather than NADH and effectively rewiring the final step of the cycle toward NADPH-centered respiratory metabolism (katayama2022malicenzymenot pages 1-2).

**The succinyl-CoA to succinate step** also shows important variation. In anaerobic acetate-oxidizing bacteria such as *Desulfurella acetivorans*, a **succinyl-CoA:acetate CoA-transferase** replaces the canonical succinyl-CoA ligase, simultaneously converting succinyl-CoA to succinate while activating acetate to acetyl-CoA (pettinato2022succinylcoaacetatecoatransferasefunctioning pages 7-9, pettinato2022succinylcoaacetatecoatransferasefunctioning pages 3-4). This dual-function enzyme saves approximately one ATP equivalent compared to separate acetate activation, a critical advantage for energy-limited strict anaerobes (pettinato2022succinylcoaacetatecoatransferasefunctioning pages 1-3). Phylogenetic analysis indicates that this CoA-transferase variant has convergently evolved multiple times across different bacterial lineages (pettinato2022succinylcoaacetatecoatransferasefunctioning pages 9-11).

### 5.3 The Methylcitrate Cycle as a TCA-Adjacent Module

The **methylcitrate cycle** (MCC) is a dedicated detoxification pathway for propionyl-CoA, the toxic product of odd-chain fatty acid β-oxidation, branched-chain amino acid catabolism, and cholesterol metabolism (wijaya2021characterizationandtargeting pages 24-27). The cycle consists of three core enzymes: **2-methylcitrate synthase** (PrpC), which condenses propionyl-CoA with oxaloacetate to form 2-methylcitrate; **2-methylcitrate dehydratase** (PrpD), which dehydrates 2-methylcitrate to 2-methyl-cis-aconitate; and **2-methylisocitrate lyase** (PrpB), which cleaves the resulting 2-methylisocitrate into pyruvate and succinate (wijaya2021characterizationandtargeting pages 24-27, wijaya2021characterizationandtargeting pages 27-30). The cycle products feed directly into central metabolism: pyruvate can re-enter via PDHc, and succinate enters the TCA cycle at step 6 (wijaya2021characterizationandtargeting pages 24-27).

PrpC and citrate synthase share common evolutionary ancestry, and in some bacteria PrpC displays partial citrate synthase promiscuity—a feature that has been exploited in "underground metabolism" to rescue *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* strains lacking citrate synthase (huang2023themethylcitratecycle pages 4-5). Similarly, 2-methylisocitrate lyase (PrpB) and isocitrate lyase evolved from a common ancestral enzyme in fungi, with specific amino acid substitutions (F455L and T457S) converting isocitrate lyase specificity toward 2-methylisocitrate (huang2023themethylcitratecycle pages 5-7). Intermediates of the MCC, particularly propionyl-CoA, 2-methylcitrate, and 2-methylisocitrate, are toxic when they accumulate due to pathway impairment, inhibiting TCA cycle enzymes including citrate synthase, aconitase, isocitrate dehydrogenase, pyruvate dehydrogenase, and succinyl-CoA synthetase (huang2023themethylcitratecycle pages 7-9).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### 6.1 Obligatory Ordering and Thermodynamic Constraints

The eight reactions of the oxidative TCA cycle must proceed in their canonical order for the pathway to function as a cycle. Citrate synthase commits carbon by forming the C6 tricarboxylate citrate; subsequent reactions cannot proceed without this initial condensation. The two irreversible oxidative decarboxylations (IDH and OGDH steps) release CO₂ and are thermodynamically committed under physiological conditions, making the upper arc of the cycle effectively unidirectional (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 10-11). The MDH reaction (malate → oxaloacetate) is thermodynamically unfavorable under standard conditions (ΔG°' ≈ +29 kJ/mol), but is driven forward by the highly favorable citrate synthase reaction that consumes its product oxaloacetate, illustrating how coupling between adjacent steps maintains directional flux (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 1-2).

### 6.2 Compartment Specificity

In eukaryotes, all TCA cycle enzymes are localized to the mitochondrial matrix except SDH, which spans the inner mitochondrial membrane (vesnina2026citricacidcycle pages 3-5). This compartmentalization requires specific transporters—the mitochondrial pyruvate carrier (MPC) for pyruvate import, and various dicarboxylate/tricarboxylate carriers for metabolite exchange (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 3-4). In bacteria, the cycle operates in the cytoplasm, with SDH integrated into the plasma membrane. Plant cells possess additional complexity, with PDHc existing in both mitochondria (feeding the TCA cycle) and plastids (supporting fatty acid biosynthesis), and recent work showing that PDHc can translocate to the nucleus during ethylene signaling to provide acetyl-CoA for histone acetylation (zheng2025regulationofplant pages 8-9).

### 6.3 Oncometabolite-Mediated Failure

Mutations in SDH, fumarate hydratase (FH), and isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH1/2) produce pathological accumulation of TCA intermediates that function as **oncometabolites** (sarkar2025tcacyclederivedoncometabolites pages 2-4, sarkar2025tcacyclederivedoncometabolites pages 1-2). IDH1/2 mutations generate a neomorphic product, D-2-hydroxyglutarate (D-2HG), which competitively inhibits α-ketoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases including TET DNA demethylases and Jumonji-domain histone demethylases, leading to DNA and histone hypermethylation, blocked cellular differentiation, and promotion of leukemogenesis and gliomagenesis (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 12-13, sarkar2025tcacyclederivedoncometabolites pages 4-6). SDH mutations cause succinate accumulation, which inhibits prolyl hydroxylases (PHDs) and stabilizes hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) proteins under normoxic conditions, creating a pseudohypoxic state that drives angiogenesis, epithelial-mesenchymal transition, and metabolic reprogramming (wang2023theconnectionbetween pages 5-6, wu2024thetricarboxylicacid pages 11-12). FH mutations lead to fumarate accumulation that additionally modifies proteins through succination of cysteine residues, inactivating redox enzymes and DNA repair proteins (sarkar2025tcacyclederivedoncometabolites pages 6-7). These discoveries have led to the development of mutant IDH inhibitors (ivosidenib, enasidenib) as targeted cancer therapeutics (sarkar2025tcacyclederivedoncometabolites pages 2-4).

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### 7.1 Directionality and Ancestral State

A persistent debate concerns whether the oxidative or reductive direction of the TCA cycle is more ancient. The reductive TCA cycle is often invoked as a primordial CO₂ fixation pathway, and non-enzymatic chemistry supports individual rTCA reactions (arnold2023regulationandfunction pages 2-3, nogal2023theprotometabolicnature pages 12-14). However, the rTCA cycle shows a patchy phylogenetic distribution and requires specific enzymes (notably ATP-citrate lyase) absent from many lineages, suggesting it may be a later elaboration rather than universal (vazquezsalazar2025evolutionaryroutesto pages 10-12, vazquezsalazar2025evolutionaryroutesto pages 8-10). The acetyl-CoA pathway has been argued to represent a more fundamental ancestral route (martin2020olderthangenes pages 13-14, martin2020olderthangenes pages 3-5). Whether the full closed oxidative cycle existed before the Great Oxidation Event or assembled piecemeal from partial "horseshoe" arcs remains unresolved (vazquezsalazar2025evolutionaryroutesto pages 8-10).

### 7.2 Enzyme Substitution at the Malate Node

The remarkable diversity of malate oxidation mechanisms—MDH, MQO, and malic enzyme—raises the question of whether the "textbook" NAD-dependent MDH step is truly canonical or simply the best-studied variant. The demonstration that cyanobacteria primarily use malic enzyme for TCA-derived malate oxidation challenges the assumption that MDH is universally the operative enzyme (katayama2022malicenzymenot pages 1-2). The authors of that study emphasize that organism-by-organism experimental verification is necessary to identify which enzyme actually carries TCA flux at this step, cautioning against assumptions based on genome annotation alone (katayama2022malicenzymenot pages 1-2).

### 7.3 Dynamic Stoichiometry of Multienzyme Complexes

Recent structural studies have revealed that the stoichiometry of PDHc and OGDHc peripheral subunits is not fixed but highly variable even within a single cellular population (wang2025dynamicsofthe pages 3-3, wang2024architectureofintact pages 5-9). The functional significance of this heterogeneity—whether it reflects regulatory fine-tuning of catalytic activity, assembly intermediates, or stochastic variation—remains an active area of investigation. This suggests an additional regulatory mechanism whereby the number of assembled E1 and E3 subunits may be coordinated to match metabolic demands (wang2025dynamicsofthe pages 3-3).

### 7.4 Non-Canonical Signaling Roles of TCA Intermediates

Beyond oncometabolites, TCA cycle intermediates are increasingly recognized for signaling roles in normal physiology. Succinate acts as an extracellular signal through the SUCNR1 (GPR91) receptor, α-ketoglutarate modulates chromatin state through dioxygenase cofactor activity, and itaconate (derived from cis-aconitate by aconitate decarboxylase) is a potent immunomodulator in activated macrophages (sarkar2025tcacyclederivedoncometabolites pages 2-4, lian2024tricarboxylicacidcycle pages 4-5). The boundaries between metabolic and signaling functions of TCA intermediates are not yet clearly delineated, and how cells balance these dual roles remains poorly understood.

### 7.5 Incomplete TCA Cycles

Many organisms—particularly obligate anaerobes and certain parasites—operate incomplete or branched TCA cycles, using only portions of the pathway for biosynthetic purposes rather than complete oxidation. How these partial cycles evolved from or were derived from a complete ancestral cycle, and whether they represent secondary losses or reflect primary modularity, is debated (vazquezsalazar2025evolutionaryroutesto pages 10-12).
