# The Intrinsic Apoptotic Signaling Pathway: BCL2-Family Mitochondrial Decision, Apoptosome Assembly, and Caspase-9 Activation

## 1. Executive Summary

The intrinsic (mitochondrial) apoptotic pathway is a cell-autonomous death program in which intracellular stress signals converge on the BCL2 protein family to regulate mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP). MOMP releases cytochrome c and other intermembrane space proteins into the cytosol, triggering assembly of the APAF1 apoptosome and activation of initiator caspase-9, which in turn activates effector caspases-3 and -7 to execute cell demolition (mustafa2024apoptosisacomprehensive pages 7-9, janssens2024decodingimmunogeniccell pages 7-7). This pathway is widely considered to have MOMP as its "point of no return," although recent work on minority MOMP, sublethal apoptotic stress, and caspase-independent death has substantially complicated this model (schofield2023regulatorsmountup pages 2-3, victorelli2023apoptoticstresscauses pages 8-9). While often presented as conserved across metazoans, phylogenetic evidence now demonstrates that caspase-9 is deuterostome-specific, that apoptosome architecture varies across lineages, and that the requirement for cytochrome c as an apoptosome trigger is not universal (horkan2024evolutionofapoptotic pages 1-2, krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 1-1). This review synthesizes current understanding of the pathway's boundaries, core mechanism, molecular variation, evolutionary history, physical constraints, and unresolved controversies.

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### 2.1 What Is Included

The intrinsic apoptotic pathway, as defined in mammalian cells, encompasses three obligatory modules: (i) the BCL2-family mitochondrial decision, in which the balance between pro- and anti-apoptotic BCL2 family members determines whether BAX and/or BAK oligomerize to permeabilize the outer mitochondrial membrane; (ii) cytochrome c–dependent apoptosome assembly, in which released cytochrome c binds APAF1 to form a heptameric scaffold; and (iii) caspase-9 activation and the downstream effector caspase cascade (mustafa2024apoptosisacomprehensive pages 7-9, mustafa2024apoptosisacomprehensive pages 9-11, borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 1-3).

### 2.2 Boundaries with Neighboring Pathways

Several processes are frequently confused with or entangled in the intrinsic pathway but should be treated as distinct:

- **Extrinsic (death receptor) pathway.** While caspase-8-mediated cleavage of BID to tBID links the extrinsic pathway to MOMP in type II cells, the death receptor signaling cascade itself—involving FAS, TRAIL receptors, FADD, and caspase-8 activation—is a separate module (janssens2024decodingimmunogeniccell pages 7-7, kataoka2022biologicalpropertiesof pages 1-2).
- **Mitochondrial permeability transition (MPT).** The mitochondrial permeability transition pore (mPTP) involves inner membrane permeabilization via cyclophilin D and is mechanistically distinct from BAX/BAK-mediated MOMP, although both can contribute to cell death (victorelli2023apoptoticstresscauses pages 4-5).
- **Necroptosis and pyroptosis.** While BCL2 family proteins regulate necroptotic cell death and MOMP-derived DAMPs can trigger inflammasome activation, these are distinct regulated cell death modalities (bock2023whencelldeath pages 4-5).
- **Non-apoptotic BCL2 functions.** BCL2 family members regulate ER calcium homeostasis, autophagy (via Beclin-1 interactions), mitochondrial dynamics, and mitophagy, which are important biological functions but operate outside the canonical apoptotic signaling cascade (lalier2022bcl2familymembers pages 14-16, vogler2025thebcl2family pages 3-5).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### 3.1 Step 1: The BCL2-Family Mitochondrial Decision

The BCL2 protein family governs the central commitment step of intrinsic apoptosis. The family comprises three functional subgroups unified by shared BCL2 homology (BH) domains: (i) anti-apoptotic members (BCL2, BCL-XL, MCL-1, BCL-W, A1/BFL-1, BCL-B), which contain BH1–BH4 domains forming a hydrophobic groove that sequesters pro-apoptotic BH3 domains; (ii) pro-apoptotic effectors (BAX, BAK, BOK), which also contain BH1–BH4 and form the pores responsible for MOMP; and (iii) BH3-only proteins, which act either as direct activators (BIM, tBID, PUMA) or sensitizers (BAD, NOXA, BIK, HRK, BMF) (saddam2024emergingbiomarkersand pages 8-9, vogler2025thebcl2family pages 3-5).

**Activation models.** Three non-mutually exclusive models describe how BAX/BAK become activated:

1. *Direct activation*: activator BH3-only proteins (BIM, tBID, PUMA) bind directly to the activation groove (helices α2–α5) or a rear trigger site on BAX/BAK, inducing conformational changes that expose the BH3 domain and promote oligomerization (saddam2024emergingbiomarkersand pages 6-8, moldoveanu2023apoptoticmitochondrialporation pages 8-10, vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 8-11).
2. *Indirect activation (displacement model)*: sensitizer BH3-only proteins neutralize anti-apoptotic BCL2 members, releasing sequestered BAX/BAK or BH3-only activators (saddam2024emergingbiomarkersand pages 6-8).
3. *Membrane-mediated permissive model*: BAX/BAK can spontaneously activate through interaction of helix α9 with the mitochondrial membrane lipid bilayer, with anti-apoptotic proteins serving as the primary restraint (saddam2024emergingbiomarkersand pages 6-8).

**Pore formation.** In healthy cells, BAX resides in the cytosol with its C-terminal α9 helix docked in the hydrophobic groove, while BAK is constitutively anchored at the outer mitochondrial membrane, restrained by anti-apoptotic proteins and VDAC2 (vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 5-8, moldoveanu2023apoptoticmitochondrialporation pages 8-10). Upon activation, both effectors undergo conformational changes: the α9 helix is released, the latch region (helices α6–α8) separates from the core (helices α2–α5), and the exposed BH3 domain engages the groove of a neighboring monomer to form BH3-in-groove dimers (vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 5-8, vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 8-11, vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 11-14). These dimers further oligomerize via α9:α9 interactions to form toroidal protein-lipid pores that grow progressively larger—initially allowing passage of cytochrome c (~12 kDa), then expanding to release larger cargo such as the SMAC homodimer (~50 kDa) (vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 8-11, vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 11-14). Cardiolipin and other mitochondrial lipids play important roles in promoting oligomer assembly (vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 11-14).

**BOK as an atypical effector.** BOK shares the multi-BH domain architecture of BAX and BAK but differs fundamentally in regulation. BOK localizes primarily to the ER and Golgi rather than mitochondria, is constitutively active, and is controlled through proteasomal degradation via the gp78 E3 ligase and ERAD pathway rather than by canonical BCL2 family interactions (moldoveanu2023apoptoticmitochondrialporation pages 1-3, moldoveanu2023apoptoticmitochondrialporation pages 8-10, vogler2025thebcl2family pages 3-5). BOK can independently permeabilize mitochondria, particularly under proteasome inhibition or ER stress, and forms oligomers with patterns distinct from BAX (moldoveanu2023apoptoticmitochondrialporation pages 8-10, vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 1-5).

### 3.2 Step 2: Cytochrome c Release and Apoptosome Assembly

Following MOMP, cytochrome c is released from the mitochondrial intermembrane space into the cytosol alongside SMAC/DIABLO, AIF, and other factors (mustafa2024apoptosisacomprehensive pages 7-9, mustafa2024apoptosisacomprehensive pages 1-2). Cytosolic cytochrome c binds to the WD40 repeat domains of APAF1, inducing conformational changes that enable APAF1 oligomerization through its nucleotide-binding oligomerization domain (NOD) in a dATP/ATP-dependent manner (borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 1-3, borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 22-24, borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 3-5).

The resulting apoptosome is a ~1.1 MDa heptameric wheel-like structure comprising seven APAF1 protomers, each bound to one cytochrome c molecule (sever2023activationofcaspase9 pages 1-2). Importantly, recent in-cell studies using cryo-electron tomography have revealed that rather than forming discrete wheel-shaped particles, APAF1 assembles into large, transient, cloud-like meshworks—membrane-less compartments of organelle-like dimensions that represent the functional apoptosome in living cells (borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 5-7, borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 3-5). These assemblies contain cytochrome c and recruit caspase-9, but their pleiomorphic ultrastructure suggests that the canonical heptameric wheel observed in vitro may represent a minimal structural unit within larger, more dynamic cellular assemblies (borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 5-7).

### 3.3 Step 3: Caspase-9 Activation and Effector Cascade

Procaspase-9 is recruited to the apoptosome through CARD–CARD (caspase recruitment domain) interactions between its prodomain and the APAF1 CARD hub (borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 1-3, borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 22-24). Recruitment to the apoptosome increases the local concentration of caspase-9 by over 7,000-fold compared to cellular levels (sever2023activationofcaspase9 pages 11-11). Recent NMR and biochemical studies have provided critical mechanistic insights: caspase-9 protease domains remain largely monomeric when tethered to the apoptosome in the absence of substrate, with a weak intrinsic dimerization affinity (K_D ~15 mM) (sever2023activationofcaspase9 pages 1-2, sever2023activationofcaspase9 pages 11-11). However, substrate binding triggers rapid and extensive dimerization, representing a substrate-dependent activation mechanism that challenges simpler induced-proximity models (sever2023activationofcaspase9 pages 1-2). This elegant mechanism ensures that caspase-9 is primed for activation but only becomes fully active when substrate (procaspase-3/7) is available, providing an additional layer of regulation.

Active caspase-9 then cleaves and activates the executioner caspases-3 and -7, which execute the apoptotic program through proteolysis of hundreds of cellular substrates, leading to DNA fragmentation, chromatin condensation, membrane blebbing, and formation of apoptotic bodies (mustafa2024apoptosisacomprehensive pages 7-9, mustafa2024apoptosisacomprehensive pages 9-11). This cascade is counterregulated by XIAP, which inhibits caspases downstream of MOMP; SMAC/DIABLO, released alongside cytochrome c, antagonizes XIAP to permit efficient caspase activation (janssens2024decodingimmunogeniccell pages 7-7, mustafa2024apoptosisacomprehensive pages 9-11).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### 5.1 Evolutionary Divergence

Recent phylogenetic analyses have fundamentally revised the traditional view that intrinsic apoptosis is a conserved pathway across metazoans. Key findings include:

**Caspase-9 is deuterostome-specific.** Contrary to longstanding assumptions, caspase-9 is restricted to deuterostomes (vertebrates, cephalochordates, echinoderms) and is not found in ecdysozoans or lophotrochozoans. The initiator caspases of *Drosophila* (Dronc) and *C. elegans* (CED-3) are orthologous to vertebrate caspase-2, not caspase-9 (horkan2024evolutionofapoptotic pages 1-2, krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 2-3).

**Apoptosome architecture varies.** The mammalian apoptosome is heptameric (seven APAF1 protomers), the *Drosophila* Dark apoptosome is octameric, and the *C. elegans* CED-4 complex is tetrameric (krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 5-6). These differences in stoichiometry reflect deep evolutionary divergence in platform architecture.

**BCL2 family repertoire differs dramatically.** Mammals possess a complex repertoire with multiple anti-apoptotic, effector, and BH3-only proteins. *Drosophila* has only two BCL2 family members (Buffy and Debcl), while *C. elegans* has essentially one anti-apoptotic homolog (CED-9) (krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 8-8, krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 1-2). The BCL2 family itself has deep metazoan origins, with five conserved monophyletic clades (Bok, Bak, Bax as pro-apoptotic; Bcl-2/W/XL and Bcl-B/Mcl-1/Bfl-1 as anti-apoptotic) present in both bilaterian and non-bilaterian sequences (krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 9-10).

**Cytochrome c and MOMP are not universally required.** The requirement for cytochrome c release as an apoptosome trigger is a mammalian feature. In ecdysozoans (*C. elegans*, *Drosophila*), apoptosis initiation does not depend on MOMP or cytochrome c release (krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 8-9, krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 2-3).

### 5.2 Cell-Type and Tissue Variation

Within mammals, the intrinsic pathway shows significant cell-type-specific variation. Different tissues rely on distinct anti-apoptotic proteins for survival: lymphocytes depend heavily on BCL2, platelets on BCL-XL, and many solid tissue cell types on MCL-1 (croce2025thebcl2protein pages 4-5). The BAX/BCL2 ratio varies across tissues and developmental stages and serves as a determinant of apoptotic priming (xu2025bakandbax pages 2-4). Cytochrome c post-translational modifications provide tissue-specific regulation: phosphorylation at Tyr48 inhibits apoptosis in detoxification organs, while Lys53 acetylation inhibits both oxidase activity and apoptotic function, potentially contributing to apoptosis evasion in cancer (zhou2024diversefunctionsof pages 6-7, zhou2024diversefunctionsof pages 4-5, zhou2024diversefunctionsof pages 5-6).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### 6.1 Obligatory Ordering

The pathway follows a strict temporal sequence. BH3-only protein activation must precede BAX/BAK oligomerization; MOMP must occur before cytochrome c is available for apoptosome assembly; and APAF1 oligomerization must precede caspase-9 recruitment and activation (mustafa2024apoptosisacomprehensive pages 7-9, borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 1-3). The requirement for dATP/ATP in apoptosome assembly adds a metabolic checkpoint—severely ATP-depleted cells may not form functional apoptosomes (borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 22-24).

### 6.2 MOMP as a Commitment Point—And Its Exceptions

Widespread MOMP has traditionally been considered irreversible because it simultaneously depletes mitochondrial function, releases pro-apoptotic factors, and triggers the caspase cascade (killarney2024sublethalengagementof pages 1-3). However, several important exceptions have emerged:

**Minority MOMP (miMOMP).** When only a subset of mitochondria undergo MOMP, cells can survive. This sublethal apoptotic stress releases sufficient mtDNA through BAX/BAK macropores to activate cGAS–STING signaling without committing the cell to death (victorelli2023apoptoticstresscauses pages 8-9, victorelli2023apoptoticstresscauses pages 1-2). In senescent cells, miMOMP drives the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) through cGAS–STING–NF-κB signaling, contributing to chronic inflammation and age-related tissue dysfunction (victorelli2023apoptoticstresscauses pages 8-9, victorelli2023apoptoticstresscauses pages 6-6). Inhibition of miMOMP in aged mice decreases inflammatory markers and improves healthspan (victorelli2023apoptoticstresscauses pages 1-2).

**Sublethal MOMP in cancer.** Cancer cells can survive incomplete engagement of the apoptotic machinery, which paradoxically promotes genomic instability via limited caspase activation and CAD-mediated DNA damage, epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition, metastasis, and multidrug resistance (schofield2023regulatorsmountup pages 2-3, killarney2024sublethalengagementof pages 21-22).

**Caspase-independent death.** Even when caspases are inhibited, extensive MOMP can cause cell death through mitochondrial dysfunction and energy depletion (killarney2024sublethalengagementof pages 1-3). The heme-regulated eIF2α kinase HRI pathway (HRI–eIF2α–ATF4) represents a caspase-independent signaling axis activated by cytochrome c release (killarney2024sublethalengagementof pages 21-22).

### 6.3 VDAC2 as a Differential Regulator

VDAC2 illustrates how a single protein can exert opposing effects on the two canonical effectors: it restrains BAK in inactive complexes at the OMM, preventing oligomerization, while simultaneously promoting BAX recruitment to mitochondria (vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 5-8). This differential regulation contributes to cell-type-specific apoptotic thresholds.

### 6.4 Post-Translational Regulation of Cytochrome c

Cytochrome c function is extensively tuned by post-translational modifications. Phosphorylation at Tyr48 and Ser47 inhibits both respiration and apoptosis (zhou2024diversefunctionsof pages 4-5, zhou2024diversefunctionsof pages 5-6). Nitration of Tyr46, Tyr48, and Tyr74 impairs apoptotic signaling by preventing apoptosome assembly and blocking caspase-9 activation (zhou2024diversefunctionsof pages 13-14). Conversely, Tyr74 nitration boosts peroxidase activity (zhou2024diversefunctionsof pages 5-6). These modifications create a molecular rheostat that integrates metabolic state with apoptotic susceptibility (zhou2024diversefunctionsof pages 1-2).

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### 7.1 Direct vs. Indirect Activation: Settled or Still Debated?

While both direct and indirect activation models are supported by extensive experimental evidence, their relative contributions in vivo remain context-dependent and actively debated. The membrane-mediated permissive model adds a third dimension, suggesting BAX/BAK can spontaneously activate through membrane interactions when anti-apoptotic restraint is removed (saddam2024emergingbiomarkersand pages 6-8, vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 8-11). The extent to which each mechanism dominates in different physiological and pathological contexts remains unresolved.

### 7.2 The Nature of the Apoptosome In Vivo

The classical view of the apoptosome as a discrete heptameric wheel has been challenged by in-cell observations showing large, pleiomorphic, cloud-like APAF1 assemblies that function as transient membrane-less compartments (borgeaud2025largetransientassemblies pages 5-7). Whether the heptameric wheel represents a fundamental building block within these larger structures, or whether alternative stoichiometries contribute to function, is an important open question.

### 7.3 Caspase-9 Activation Mechanism

The substrate-dependent dimerization model proposed by Sever et al. (2023) challenges simpler induced-proximity models of caspase-9 activation, demonstrating that caspase-9 molecules remain monomeric on the apoptosome until substrate is present (sever2023activationofcaspase9 pages 1-2, sever2023activationofcaspase9 pages 11-11). This model has important implications for understanding how the apoptosome functions as a regulated holoenzyme rather than a simple concentration platform.

### 7.4 Minority MOMP and Its Biological Significance

The discovery that sublethal MOMP can drive inflammation, genomic instability, and senescence has opened an entirely new dimension of BCL2 family biology (victorelli2023apoptoticstresscauses pages 8-9, schofield2023regulatorsmountup pages 2-3). Key unresolved questions include: what determines whether MOMP is complete or partial? How do cells calibrate the fraction of mitochondria that undergo permeabilization? Is minority MOMP a regulated physiological process or an imperfect execution of the death program?

### 7.5 Evolutionary Divergence and Model Organism Extrapolation

The demonstration that caspase-9 is deuterostome-specific, and that *C. elegans* and *Drosophila* apoptosis does not depend on cytochrome c release or MOMP, urges caution in extrapolating findings across species (horkan2024evolutionofapoptotic pages 1-2, krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 2-3, krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 1-1). Lophotrochozoans show complex evolutionary histories with multiple gains, losses, and diversifications of apoptotic components, further emphasizing that no single model organism captures the full diversity of intrinsic apoptotic signaling (horkan2024evolutionofapoptotic pages 1-2, krasovec2024intrinsicapoptosisis pages 8-9).

### 7.6 Non-Apoptotic Functions of BCL2 Family Proteins

BCL2 family members regulate ER calcium homeostasis, autophagy, mitochondrial dynamics, and mitophagy (lalier2022bcl2familymembers pages 14-16, vogler2025thebcl2family pages 3-5). The relative importance of these non-canonical functions versus apoptotic regulation, particularly for MCL-1 and BCL-XL in tissue maintenance, remains a critical open question with therapeutic implications (croce2025thebcl2protein pages 4-5).

### 7.7 Pore Structure and Mechanism of Membrane Permeabilization

Despite decades of research, the precise mechanism by which BAX/BAK oligomers permeabilize lipid bilayers remains elusive. BAX forms larger pores more slowly than BAK, and pore growth dynamics differ between the two effectors—cells expressing predominantly BAK release mtDNA faster (bock2023whencelldeath pages 5-6). Whether the pore is a purely proteinaceous ring, a protein-lipid toroidal structure, or a lipid-only pore stabilized by protein at the edge is still debated (vandenabeele2023poreformingproteinsas pages 11-14).
