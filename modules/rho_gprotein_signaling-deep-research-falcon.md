# The Rho Protein Signal Transduction Pathway Module: A Concise Review

## 1. Executive Summary

The Rho GTPase signal transduction pathway constitutes a core regulatory module in eukaryotic cell biology, governing actomyosin organization, cell adhesion, polarity, migration, and cytokinesis. At its center, Rho-family GTPases—principally RhoA—function as molecular switches that cycle between an inactive GDP-bound state and an active GTP-bound state. This cycling is controlled by three classes of regulatory proteins: guanine nucleotide exchange factors (GEFs) that catalyze activation, GTPase-activating proteins (GAPs) that accelerate inactivation, and guanine nucleotide dissociation inhibitors (GDIs) that maintain cytosolic pools of inactive GTPases (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 2-5, jung2020dysregulationofrho pages 1-3). The pathway is ancient, conserved across all eukaryotes, and has been extensively elaborated in metazoans through expansion of GEF, GAP, and effector families. This review focuses on the minimal signaling module defined by ARHGEF1 (a representative RhoGEF), ARHGAP1 (a representative RhoGAP), and the effector kinase ROCK1, analyzing how these components cooperate to translate extracellular signals into cytoskeletal output.

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### Scope of the system

The Rho protein signal transduction pathway, as defined here, encompasses three obligatory steps: (1) **Rho nucleotide exchange**, in which a GEF (exemplified by ARHGEF1/p115RhoGEF) catalyzes GDP-to-GTP exchange on RhoA; (2) **Rho GAP reset**, in which a GAP (exemplified by ARHGAP1/p50RhoGAP) accelerates GTP hydrolysis to return RhoA to its inactive state; and (3) **ROCK cytoskeletal output**, in which GTP-bound RhoA engages the effector kinase ROCK1 (or ROCK2) to drive actomyosin contractility, stress fiber formation, and focal adhesion assembly (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 2-5, kim2022mitoticproteinkinasedriven pages 7-8, cheng2025rhorockpathwayas pages 1-3).

The human genome encodes approximately 20 Rho GTPases, more than 70 RhoGEFs of the Dbl family, and a comparably large complement of RhoGAPs (ohashi2024rolesofthe pages 1-3, jung2020dysregulationofrho pages 1-3). While the broader Rho signaling network encompasses Rac1-driven lamellipodia, Cdc42-driven filopodia, and numerous alternative effectors (e.g., PAK kinases, formins, mDia), the present review treats these as neighboring but distinct modules that share upstream regulatory logic. The Ras–Raf–MAPK, PI3K–AKT, Wnt–PCP, and Hippo–YAP pathways frequently crosstalk with Rho signaling but should be considered separately unless their intersection with the RhoA–ROCK axis is directly relevant (bement2024patterningofthe pages 1-6, lin2025guaninenucleotideexchange pages 7-8).

### What is excluded

Rac1- and Cdc42-specific signaling outputs—such as WAVE/WASP-driven actin polymerization—are mechanistically distinct from the RhoA–ROCK axis and are not addressed here in depth. Similarly, atypical Rho GTPases (e.g., RhoH, RhoBTB, Rnd subfamily) that lack canonical GTPase cycling are outside the scope of this module (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 1-2).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### Step 1: Rho nucleotide exchange (activation)

The initiating event of the module is the activation of RhoA by a GEF. ARHGEF1 (also known as p115RhoGEF or Lsc) serves as a representative canonical RhoGEF. It contains an N-terminal RH (regulator of G-protein signaling homology) domain, a central DH (Dbl homology) catalytic domain, and an adjacent PH (pleckstrin homology) domain (omble2022gpcrsthatrhoar pages 5-7, omble2022gpcrsthatrhoar pages 13-15). Mechanistically, the DH domain binds GDP-bound RhoA with high affinity and destabilizes the GDP-binding site: the N-terminal extension (αN1/αN2) of the DH domain interacts with the Switch I region of RhoA while the PH domain engages Switch II, collectively weakening the nucleotide–GTPase interaction and promoting formation of a nucleotide-free intermediate (lin2025guaninenucleotideexchange pages 3-5, hairston2025rhosmallgtpase pages 15-17). Because intracellular GTP concentrations are approximately tenfold higher than GDP, GTP is preferentially loaded onto the nucleotide-free GTPase, producing the active RhoA-GTP conformation (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 2-5).

ARHGEF1 is activated primarily downstream of G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), where Gα12/13 subunits directly bind the RH domain, relieving autoinhibition of GEF activity (omble2022gpcrsthatrhoar pages 5-7, omble2022gpcrsthatrhoar pages 13-15, omble2022gpcrsthatrhoar pages 3-5). In vascular smooth muscle cells, ARHGEF1 can also be activated by Jak2-mediated phosphorylation at Tyr738, linking cytokine signaling to RhoA-dependent vasoconstriction (lin2025guaninenucleotideexchange pages 16-17). A landmark 2025 study published in *Nature* demonstrated that platelet-derived thromboxane A2 (TXA2) activates ARHGEF1 in T cells via the TP receptor–Gα12/13 axis, establishing an immunosuppressive ARHGEF1→RhoA→ROCK→PTEN pathway that limits TCR-driven kinase signaling (yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 8-9, yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 1-2, yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 5-5).

### Step 2: Rho GAP reset (inactivation)

GTP-loaded RhoA is returned to the inactive state by GAPs, which supply a catalytic arginine residue ("arginine finger") to the GTPase active site, dramatically accelerating the intrinsic but extremely slow GTP hydrolysis rate (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 2-5, jung2020dysregulationofrho pages 1-3). ARHGAP1 (p50RhoGAP/Cdc42GAP) is the prototypical Rho-family GAP. Structural studies have revealed a sophisticated autoinhibition mechanism: ARHGAP1 possesses an N-terminal BCH (BNIP-2 and Cdc42GAP Homology) domain that forms an intertwined dimeric structure (chichili2021structuralbasisfor pages 1-2). The BCH domain sequesters prenylated RhoA through a lipid-binding pocket and a RhoA-binding loop, while simultaneously maintaining the adjacent GAP domain in an inactive conformation via the β5-strand (chichili2021structuralbasisfor pages 10-10, chichili2021structuralbasisfor pages 7-8, chichili2021structuralbasisfor pages 5-6). Destabilization of the β5-strand releases the GAP domain from autoinhibition, exposing the catalytic arginine and allowing efficient RhoA-GTP hydrolysis (chichili2021structuralbasisfor pages 7-8). This regulatory architecture ensures that GAP activity is tightly controlled in space and time.

### Step 3: ROCK cytoskeletal output

While RhoA is GTP-bound, it engages downstream effectors. The principal effectors in the actomyosin contractility arm are the Rho-associated coiled-coil containing kinases ROCK1 and ROCK2. GTP-bound RhoA binds the C-terminal Rho-binding domain (RBD) of ROCK, inducing a conformational change that relieves the autoinhibitory fold and activates the N-terminal kinase domain (kim2022mitoticproteinkinasedriven pages 7-8, cheng2025rhorockpathwayas pages 1-3, hairston2025rhosmallgtpase pages 11-12).

Activated ROCK phosphorylates multiple substrates that converge on actomyosin contractility through three parallel arms:

1. **Direct MLC phosphorylation**: ROCK1 phosphorylates myosin regulatory light chain (MLC) at Thr18 and Ser19, directly activating myosin II ATPase activity and promoting actomyosin contraction (kim2022mitoticproteinkinasedriven pages 7-8).
2. **MYPT1 inhibition (calcium sensitization)**: ROCK phosphorylates the MYPT1 regulatory subunit of myosin light chain phosphatase (MLCP) at inhibitory sites (Thr696 and Thr853), preventing MLCP from dephosphorylating MLC and thus sustaining contractile force independently of intracellular calcium changes (kim2022mitoticproteinkinasedriven pages 7-8, cheng2025rhorockpathwayas pages 1-3, randar2025rhoarhokinasesignalingin pages 1-2).
3. **LIMK–cofilin axis**: ROCK activates LIM kinase (LIMK1 at Thr508; LIMK2 at Thr505), which phosphorylates and inactivates cofilin, an actin-depolymerizing protein. This stabilizes actin filaments and promotes stress fiber formation (kim2022mitoticproteinkinasedriven pages 7-8, wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 5-7, chetty2022rhofamilygtpase pages 14-15).

These three arms act synergistically: MLC phosphorylation activates myosin, MYPT1 inhibition sustains that activation, and cofilin inactivation stabilizes the actin filaments on which myosin exerts force.

### The role of GDI in the cycle

RhoGDIs (three mammalian members, with ARHGDIA/RhoGDI1 being the most broadly expressed) extract geranylgeranylated Rho GTPases from membranes by shielding their prenyl groups in a hydrophobic pocket, inhibiting nucleotide exchange and GTP hydrolysis, and protecting inactive GTPases from degradation (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 2-5, jung2020dysregulationofrho pages 1-3, bement2024patterningofthe pages 19-22). GDI-mediated membrane extraction is essential for maintaining the cytosolic pool of inactive Rho GTPases available for rapid reactivation at specific membrane sites, and represents a fourth regulatory layer that intersects all three steps of the module.

## 4. Major Molecular Players and Active Assemblies

### ARHGEF1 (p115RhoGEF)

ARHGEF1 belongs to the Lbc (leukemia-associated Rho GEF/Lsc/p115) subfamily of Dbl-family RhoGEFs. It is expressed in peripheral blood leukocytes, vascular smooth muscle, kidney, and pancreas (omble2022gpcrsthatrhoar pages 3-5). Its activation downstream of Gα12/13-coupled GPCRs—including CXCR4, LPA receptors, S1P2R, and the TP receptor—positions it as a critical integrator of GPCR-to-cytoskeleton signaling (omble2022gpcrsthatrhoar pages 5-7). ARHGEF1-deficient mice show reduced leukocyte recruitment and attenuated atherosclerosis, underscoring its role in vascular inflammation (lin2025guaninenucleotideexchange pages 16-17). The recent identification of ARHGEF1 as the key mediator of TXA2-dependent T cell immunosuppression in metastasis—the mechanistic basis for aspirin's anti-metastatic activity—represents a major new functional dimension for this GEF (yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 8-9, yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 1-2, yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 2-3).

### ARHGAP1 (p50RhoGAP)

ARHGAP1 is the founding member of the RhoGAP family and displays broad specificity for RhoA, Rac1, and Cdc42 in vitro, though its *in vivo* GAP activity is shaped by the BCH domain autoinhibition mechanism (chichili2021structuralbasisfor pages 1-2). The crystal structure of the BCH domain from *Schizosaccharomyces pombe* p50RhoGAP reveals that it adopts a Sec14-like fold with a dimeric architecture, harboring both a lipid-binding pocket for prenylated RhoA and a protein–protein interaction surface that controls GAP domain accessibility (chichili2021structuralbasisfor pages 5-6). Proximity interaction network studies have confirmed that ARHGAP1 shows broad interaction patterns with multiple Rho GTPases, consistent with a general housekeeping role in RhoA inactivation (chichili2021structuralbasisfor pages 10-10).

### ROCK1 and ROCK2: functional divergence

ROCK1 and ROCK2 share 92% identity in their kinase domains and are expressed ubiquitously during embryogenesis, with tissue-specific differences emerging in the adult (randar2025rhoarhokinasesignalingin pages 1-2, benarroch2023whatisthe pages 1-3). In the brain, ROCK2 predominates in neurons while ROCK1 is enriched in glia (benarroch2023whatisthe pages 1-3). Conditional knockout studies in endothelial cells have demonstrated important isoform-specific roles: ROCK2 is the dominant isoform for FAK and LIMK phosphorylation, junction reformation, and angiogenesis, while both isoforms are required for full MLC phosphorylation (lange2025endothelialrhokinase pages 16-19, lange2025endothelialrhokinase pages 13-16). In cardiovascular models, ROCK1 and ROCK2 can have opposing effects—cardiomyocyte-specific ROCK1 deletion worsened cardiac dysfunction under pressure overload, while ROCK2 deletion was protective (cheng2025rhorockpathwayas pages 3-5). Complete endothelial loss of both ROCK1 and ROCK2 is lethal in mice due to multi-organ hemorrhage, whereas single-isoform deletions are viable, demonstrating partial redundancy with isoform-specific specialization (lange2025endothelialrhokinase pages 16-19, lange2025endothelialrhokinase pages 19-23).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### Evolutionary conservation

Rho GTPases are a subfamily of the Ras superfamily found in all eukaryotic cells, with the first member discovered in 1985 in *Aplysia* abdominal ganglia (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 1-2). The Rho-Rac family represents one of four major small GTPase families alongside Arf-Sar, Rab-Ran, and Ras-Rap, and these families are conserved across the entire eukaryote domain (forbes2021interactomeandevolutionary pages 1-2). Comparative genomic studies of Dictyostelid social amoebas—spanning approximately 0.5 billion years of evolution—demonstrate that while GTPases themselves show extensive species- or lineage-specific gain and loss, the GEFs and GAPs are generally better conserved across all examined genomes (forbes2021interactomeandevolutionary pages 2-3, forbes2021interactomeandevolutionary pages 1-2). This suggests that the regulatory machinery has been under stronger purifying selection than the switches themselves.

In metazoans, the Rho GTPase family has expanded to over 20 members in humans, most of which are ubiquitously expressed, with exceptions such as RhoH (hematopoietic-specific), Rac2 (primarily immune cells), and certain RhoBTB and Rnd family members (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 1-2). The Dbl-family RhoGEF repertoire has expanded to more than 70 members in the human genome, enabling the coupling of diverse upstream signals to a limited set of Rho GTPase switches (ohashi2024rolesofthe pages 1-3). Plants possess a distinct Rho-related family called ROP (Rho of Plants) GTPases, which share the core GTP/GDP cycling logic but have diverged substantially in effector engagement and upstream regulation.

### Cell-type and tissue variation

The system shows marked cell-type specificity. In vascular smooth muscle cells, the RhoA–ROCK axis mediates calcium sensitization and sustained contraction via MYPT1 phosphorylation, contributing to vascular tone regulation and hypertension (randar2025rhoarhokinasesignalingin pages 1-2). In endothelial cells, ROCK controls junctional integrity, barrier function, and angiogenesis (lange2025endothelialrhokinase pages 16-19, lange2025endothelialrhokinase pages 19-23). In neurons, RhoA–ROCK signaling regulates axonal growth, dendritic remodeling, and synaptic plasticity, while also contributing to neuroinflammation through glial activation (benarroch2023whatisthe pages 1-3). In platelets, RhoA drives shape change and thrombus formation (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 5-7). In immune cells, the module has a newly recognized role in T cell immunosuppression through the TXA2–ARHGEF1–RhoA–ROCK–PTEN axis (yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 8-9, yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 5-5).

### Alternative routes to similar outcomes

The same cytoskeletal output—actomyosin contractility—can be achieved through alternative molecular routes. Myosin light chain kinase (MLCK) directly phosphorylates MLC in a calcium/calmodulin-dependent manner, providing a ROCK-independent path to myosin activation. Other RhoA effectors such as mDia formins cooperate with ROCK to assemble actomyosin bundles (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 5-7). Multiple RhoGEFs beyond ARHGEF1 can activate RhoA in a context-dependent manner, including GEF-H1/ARHGEF2 (microtubule-regulated), Ect2 (cytokinesis-specific), and the Lbc-type GEFs Arhgef11/Arhgef12 (duman2022racmaninoffandrhovel pages 7-9, lin2025guaninenucleotideexchange pages 7-8).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### Ordering constraints

The three steps must occur in a defined logical order: GEF-mediated activation must precede effector engagement, and GAP-mediated inactivation must follow. However, recent work emphasizes that activation and inactivation are not sequential but rather temporally coupled—simultaneous GEF and GAP activity is required to maintain dynamic activity zones and enable traveling waves of Rho activity during cytokinesis and cell migration (bement2024patterningofthe pages 6-8). The "GTPase Flux Hypothesis" proposes that continuous cycling through active and inactive states is the functionally relevant parameter, not merely the steady-state level of GTP-bound RhoA (bement2024patterningofthe pages 6-8).

### Membrane dependence

RhoA activation is obligatorily membrane-associated. The C-terminal CAAX motif of RhoA is geranylgeranylated, and this lipid modification is required for membrane insertion, effector engagement, and full signaling output. GDI-mediated extraction from membranes effectively silences RhoA signaling in the cytosol (wang2022rhogtpasefamily pages 2-5, bement2024patterningofthe pages 19-22). The BCH domain of ARHGAP1 specifically recognizes the prenyl group of RhoA through its lipid-binding pocket, ensuring that the GAP acts preferentially on membrane-associated GTPase (chichili2021structuralbasisfor pages 10-10).

### Spatiotemporal patterning

Rho GTPase signaling generates spatially organized patterns—stable clusters, traveling waves, and oscillatory pulses—through positive and negative feedback circuits involving GEFs, GAPs, and effectors (bement2024patterningofthe pages 14-17, bement2024patterningofthe pages 1-6). During cytokinesis, ECT2 (a Rho GEF) and RGA-3/4 (a Rho GAP) create complementary waves at the cell equator, where positive feedback through Rho-GTP allosteric activation of its own GEF is coupled with F-actin-dependent negative feedback via GAP recruitment (bement2024patterningofthe pages 14-17). In cell migration, Rac activity at the leading edge is coupled to Rho activity at the rear through GEF-mediated crosstalk, with Lbc-type GEFs Arhgef11 and Arhgef12 recruited to protrusions by active Rac, subsequently activating Rho for retraction (duman2022racmaninoffandrhovel pages 7-9).

### Failure modes

Loss of ROCK1/2 in endothelial cells causes catastrophic failure of vascular integrity—combined endothelial deletion is lethal within one week due to multi-organ hemorrhage, demonstrating that the module is essential for tissue homeostasis (lange2025endothelialrhokinase pages 16-19). Conversely, constitutive activation of RhoA–ROCK signaling drives pathological contractility, fibrosis, hypertension, and cancer metastasis (randar2025rhoarhokinasesignalingin pages 1-2, cheng2025rhorockpathwayas pages 5-7). Context-dependent failure modes are notable: in cardiac tissue, ROCK1 loss is deleterious under pressure overload but protective in other settings, while ROCK2 loss shows the opposite pattern (cheng2025rhorockpathwayas pages 3-5).

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### Isoform specificity versus redundancy

A major unresolved question is the degree to which ROCK1 and ROCK2 are functionally interchangeable. While they share 92% kinase domain identity, emerging data reveal isoform-specific substrates (ROCK2 preferentially phosphorylates FAK and LIMK in endothelial cells) and context-dependent compensatory mechanisms (ROCK2 deletion triggers ROCK1 upregulation in some tissues) (lange2025endothelialrhokinase pages 16-19, lange2025endothelialrhokinase pages 19-23, cheng2025rhorockpathwayas pages 3-5). Selective ROCK2 deletion alone can increase fibrosis through compensatory ROCK1 upregulation, illustrating that pan-ROCK inhibition and isoform-selective inhibition may have fundamentally different therapeutic consequences (cheng2025rhorockpathwayas pages 3-5).

### Feedback complexity

Static signaling diagrams cannot predict the actual spatiotemporal patterns of Rho GTPase activity. The same molecular network produces pulsed contractions in *C. elegans* embryos but traveling waves during cytokinesis in other species. Manipulating the GAP-to-GEF ratio produces opposite effects in frog oocytes versus starfish oocytes (bement2024patterningofthe pages 17-19). Different phosphoinositide-dependent feedback relationships can coexist in the same cell, producing mixed-mode oscillations (bement2024patterningofthe pages 14-17). These findings challenge reductionist models and call for more quantitative, systems-level approaches.

### GEF and GAP specificity

With more than 70 Dbl-family RhoGEFs and a comparably large number of RhoGAPs in the human genome, the rules governing which GEF–GAP pair operates in a given cellular context remain poorly defined (ohashi2024rolesofthe pages 1-3). Only a small fraction of possible GEF–GTPase and GAP–GTPase interactions have been experimentally validated, and functional redundancy among GEFs complicates genetic analysis (forbes2021interactomeandevolutionary pages 2-3).

### ARHGEF1 as an immunomodulatory target

The discovery that ARHGEF1 mediates TXA2-dependent T cell immunosuppression and is required for aspirin's anti-metastatic effects raises the possibility that selective ARHGEF1 inhibition could enhance anti-tumor immunity (yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 8-9, yang2025aspirinpreventsmetastasis pages 2-3). However, given ARHGEF1's roles in vascular smooth muscle contractility, leukocyte recruitment, and other processes, the therapeutic window for targeting this GEF remains to be defined.
