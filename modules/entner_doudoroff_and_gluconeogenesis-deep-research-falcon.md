# The Entner-Doudoroff Glycolysis and Gluconeogenesis Module: A Review

## 1. Executive Summary

The Entner-Doudoroff (ED) pathway represents a widespread alternative to the canonical Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas (EMP) glycolytic route for hexose-phosphate catabolism. First described in *Pseudomonas saccharophila* by Entner and Doudoroff in 1952, this pathway is now recognized as the primary glycolytic strategy in a remarkable diversity of bacteria—including an estimated 90% of marine bacterial isolates—as well as in many archaea, cyanobacteria, algae, and even plants (borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 3-6, krevet2020enzymaticsynthesisof pages 1-2). The ED module defined here encompasses three functionally distinct tiers: (i) an upper oxidative branch converting glucose 6-phosphate to 6-phosphogluconate, (ii) a committed ED branch that dehydrates and cleaves 6-phosphogluconate to pyruvate and glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate via the signature intermediary 2-keto-3-deoxy-6-phosphogluconate (KDPG), and (iii) gluconeogenic bypass reactions that regenerate hexose phosphates when carbon must flow from pyruvate or triose phosphates back toward glucose 6-phosphate. These three tiers are connected through the shared lower EMP trunk—the reversible reactions from glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate to pyruvate—which serves both catabolic and anabolic directions.

The following schematic diagram illustrates the overall architecture of the ED glycolysis and gluconeogenesis module, highlighting the catabolic (blue), ED-committed (green), and gluconeogenic (red) flow directions, as well as the EDEMP cycling connection (dashed):

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### 2.1 What Is Included

The ED glycolysis and gluconeogenesis module, as defined here, encompasses:

1. **Glucose phosphorylation**: Conversion of glucose to glucose 6-phosphate by glucokinase (Glk, EC 2.7.1.2) (kopp2020enzymologyofalternative pages 1-3).
2. **Oxidative upper branch** (shared with the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway): Glucose 6-phosphate dehydrogenase (Zwf, EC 1.1.1.49) and 6-phosphogluconolactonase (Pgl, EC 3.1.1.31) producing 6-phosphogluconate (kopp2020enzymologyofalternative pages 1-3, hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3).
3. **ED committed branch**: 6-Phosphogluconate dehydratase (Edd, EC 4.2.1.12) and KDPG aldolase (Eda, EC 4.1.2.14), yielding pyruvate and glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 1-2, hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3).
4. **Shared lower EMP trunk**: The six-step sequence from triose-phosphate isomerase through pyruvate kinase that converts glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate to pyruvate with substrate-level phosphorylation (santanamolina2025chimericoriginsand pages 3-4, hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3).
5. **Gluconeogenic bypasses**: Phosphoenolpyruvate synthase (PpsA), PEP carboxykinase (PckA), fructose-bisphosphate aldolase, fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (Fbp/GlpX), and glucose-6-phosphate isomerase (Pgi) that regenerate hexose phosphates from C₃ precursors (zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 1-2, zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 9-11, siebers2004reconstructionofthe pages 4-5).

### 2.2 What Should Be Treated Separately

Several neighboring processes are frequently discussed alongside the ED pathway but are not part of this module:

- **The oxidative pentose phosphate pathway (oxPPP)**: While the Zwf and Pgl reactions are shared entry points, the oxidative decarboxylation step catalyzed by 6-phosphogluconate dehydrogenase (Gnd, EC 1.1.1.44) is *not* part of the ED branch—it diverts 6-phosphogluconate toward ribulose 5-phosphate rather than KDPG (bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 1-2, hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3).
- **The non-oxidative PPP**: Transketolase and transaldolase reactions that interconvert sugar phosphates are downstream consumers of ED/PPP intermediates but are mechanistically distinct.
- **Periplasmic glucose oxidation**: In many pseudomonads, glucose is first oxidized to gluconate or 2-ketogluconate in the periplasm before phosphorylation, bypassing intracellular glucokinase entirely (wilkes2019acyclicmetabolic pages 18-20, weimer2020industrialbiotechnologyof pages 1-3).
- **The TCA cycle and anaplerotic reactions**: Although pyruvate from the ED pathway feeds directly into acetyl-CoA generation and the TCA cycle, these downstream pathways are outside the module boundary.
- **Modified (non-phosphorylative and semi-phosphorylative) ED pathways in archaea**: These variants share the aldol-cleavage logic but employ different enzyme families and operate on non-phosphorylated intermediates (sutter2016keyenzymesof pages 39-45, siebers2004reconstructionofthe pages 1-2). They are best treated as evolutionary relatives rather than components of the same module.

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### 3.1 The Catabolic Direction

In the classical phosphorylative ED pathway, glucose entering the cell is phosphorylated to glucose 6-phosphate by glucokinase (kopp2020enzymologyofalternative pages 1-3). This hexose phosphate is then oxidized by glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (Zwf), producing NADPH and 6-phosphoglucono-δ-lactone, which is hydrolyzed by 6-phosphogluconolactonase (Pgl) to 6-phosphogluconate (kopp2020enzymologyofalternative pages 1-3, hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3).

The committed ED branch then diverges from the pentose phosphate pathway: 6-phosphogluconate dehydratase (Edd) catalyzes an irreversible dehydration of 6-phosphogluconate to KDPG (bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 1-2, krevet2020enzymaticsynthesisof pages 1-2). KDPG aldolase (Eda) then cleaves this six-carbon intermediate into pyruvate (C₃) and glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (GAP, C₃) in a retro-aldol reaction (bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 1-2). This two-enzyme committed branch is the hallmark of the ED pathway.

The GAP product enters the shared lower EMP trunk, proceeding through glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH), phosphoglycerate kinase (PGK), phosphoglycerate mutase (PGM), enolase (ENO), and pyruvate kinase (PK) to generate a second molecule of pyruvate with concomitant production of ATP and NADH (santanamolina2025chimericoriginsand pages 3-4, hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3). The net yield of the classical ED pathway is therefore one ATP, one NADPH, and one NADH per glucose—in contrast to the two ATP and two NADH produced by the EMP pathway (borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 6-8, hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3).

### 3.2 The EDEMP Cycle

A critical insight from studies in *Pseudomonas putida* and related pseudomonads is that the ED pathway does not operate as a simple linear route but instead functions as part of a cyclic metabolic architecture termed the **EDEMP cycle** (dvorak2024syntheticallyprimedadaptationof pages 2-3, weimer2020industrialbiotechnologyof pages 1-3, dvorak2024syntheticallyprimedadaptationof pages 5-6). In organisms that lack phosphofructokinase (Pfk), such as *P. putida*, GAP produced by the ED branch can be recycled: triose-phosphate isomerase converts GAP to dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP), fructose-bisphosphate aldolase condenses GAP + DHAP into fructose-1,6-bisphosphate, and fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase plus glucose-6-phosphate isomerase regenerate glucose 6-phosphate, which re-enters the ED pathway (wilkes2019acyclicmetabolic pages 18-20, dvorak2024syntheticallyprimedadaptationof pages 2-3). This cycling enables carbon distribution between ED, EMP, and PPP nodes and is essential for supplying precursors for biosynthesis via the non-oxidative PPP branch.

In *P. protegens* Pf-5, metabolic flux analysis confirmed that up to 95% of consumed glucose is routed through the ED pathway, with subsequent cyclic flux from the ED through the upper EMP pathway providing carbon to the non-oxidative pentose phosphate pathway (wilkes2019acyclicmetabolic pages 18-20).

### 3.3 Gluconeogenic Bypass Reactions

When cells must synthesize hexose phosphates from C₃ or C₄ precursors (e.g., during growth on amino acids, organic acids, or fatty acids), three irreversible glycolytic steps must be bypassed:

**Pyruvate to phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)**: Two main enzymatic routes accomplish this bypass:
- *Phosphoenolpyruvate synthase (PpsA, EC 2.7.9.2)*: Directly converts pyruvate + ATP → PEP + AMP + Pi (zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 1-2, siebers2004reconstructionofthe pages 4-5).
- *PEP carboxykinase (PckA)*: Converts oxaloacetate + GTP (or ATP, depending on the lineage variant) → PEP + CO₂ + GDP/ADP, coupling gluconeogenesis to the TCA cycle via oxaloacetate (zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 1-2, zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 9-11).

Studies in *Brucella abortus* demonstrated that pyruvate phosphate dikinase (PpdK, functionally equivalent to PpsA) is essential for intracellular virulence, while the fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase double mutant (Δ*fbp*-Δ*glpX*) showed only modest growth reduction, suggesting redundancy or alternative routes at the FBP → F6P step (zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 1-2, zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 9-11).

**Fructose 1,6-bisphosphate to fructose 6-phosphate**: Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (Fbp, EC 3.1.3.11, or GlpX) catalyzes the irreversible hydrolysis of FBP to F6P, bypassing the phosphofructokinase step (zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 1-2, siebers2004reconstructionofthe pages 4-5).

**Fructose 6-phosphate to glucose 6-phosphate**: Glucose-6-phosphate isomerase (Pgi) catalyzes this reversible interconversion, completing hexose-phosphate regeneration (dvorak2024syntheticallyprimedadaptationof pages 2-3, dvorak2024syntheticallyprimedadaptationof pages 5-6).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### 5.1 Distribution Across Bacterial Lineages

The ED pathway is estimated to be utilized by approximately 27% of heterotrophic prokaryotic microorganisms for sugar and sugar acid degradation (krevet2020enzymaticsynthesisof pages 1-2). Initially recognized primarily in Gram-negative bacteria, ED pathway enzymes (Edd and Eda) have subsequently been identified in numerous Gram-positive bacteria, some archaea, and a few single-celled eukaryotes (borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 3-6). The pathway is the dominant glycolytic strategy in marine bacteria: 90% of 25 marine bacterial isolates tested rely on the ED pathway, with notable exceptions in the Vibrionaceae family (borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 3-6). In contrast, terrestrial bacterial isolates more commonly employ the EMP pathway (borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 3-6).

Key organisms utilizing the ED pathway as a primary or essential glycolytic route include *Pseudomonas* spp. (lacking phosphofructokinase), *Zymomonas mobilis* (the canonical anaerobic ED organism), *Caulobacter crescentus*, and many human pathogens including *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Helicobacter pylori*, and *Salmonella* species (krevet2020enzymaticsynthesisof pages 1-2, kopp2020enzymologyofalternative pages 1-3). In *Escherichia coli*, the ED pathway is present but primarily inactive during glucose growth, becoming induced during growth on gluconate or under carbon starvation (hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3, murray2005multipleregulatorscontrol pages 8-9).

The **semi-phosphorylative ED pathway** (spED) has been characterized in the haloarchaeon *Haloferax volcanii*, where glucose dehydrogenase (GDH) oxidizes glucose to gluconate, gluconate dehydratase (GAD) dehydrates gluconate to 2-keto-3-deoxygluconate (KDG), KDG kinase phosphorylates KDG to KDPG, and KDPG aldolase cleaves KDPG to pyruvate and GAP (sutter2016keyenzymesof pages 19-23, sutter2016keyenzymesof pages 39-45). The **non-phosphorylative ED pathway** (npED) in *Sulfolobus solfataricus* omits the phosphorylation step entirely before aldol cleavage, yielding pyruvate and glyceraldehyde rather than GAP (siebers2004reconstructionofthe pages 1-2, kopp2020enzymologyofalternative pages 3-4). Remarkably, the hyperthermophilic crenarchaeon *Thermoproteus tenax* employs both ED pathway variants alongside a modified EMP pathway, an arrangement unique among studied organisms (siebers2004reconstructionofthe pages 1-2).

### 5.3 ED Pathway in Cyanobacteria and Phototrophs

The ED pathway was established as the fourth glycolytic pathway in *Synechocystis* sp. PCC 6803 in 2016 (bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 1-2). However, the prevalence of the complete ED pathway in cyanobacteria remains controversial: while the EDD/ilvD dehydratase homolog is found in approximately 92% of cyanobacterial genomes, the complete ED pathway (requiring EDA) occurs in only ~47% of cyanobacteria, and only four cyanobacteria possess native EDD enzymes with single (non-dual) function (bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 3-5, bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 2-3).

### 5.4 Chimeric Origins in Eukaryotic Central Carbon Metabolism

Recent phylogenomic analysis by Santana-Molina et al. (2025) revealed that eukaryotic central carbon metabolism has chimeric origins, with contributions from Asgardarchaeota operating predominantly in glycolysis (including ADP-dependent glucokinase, phosphoglycerate mutases, and enolase) and Alphaproteobacteria contributing primarily to TCA cycle enzymes. Notably, the ED pathway enzymes Edd and Eda in photosynthetic eukaryotes appear to derive from cyanobacterial endosymbiotic gene transfer, alongside several PPP and EMP enzymes (santanamolina2025chimericoriginsand pages 3-4, santanamolina2025chimericoriginsand pages 4-5).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### 6.1 Obligatory Sequence

The ED committed branch has a strict sequential dependency: Edd must act before Eda, as KDPG is the obligate substrate for the aldolase. Upstream, the Zwf → Pgl → Edd sequence is also obligatory in organisms using the classical phosphorylative route. The lower EMP trunk reactions are largely reversible (except pyruvate kinase), establishing the biochemical basis for the gluconeogenic bypass requirement (bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 1-2, hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3).

### 6.2 Energetic Trade-offs

The ED pathway produces only one net ATP per glucose (versus two from EMP), a consequence of generating one pyruvate directly from the aldolase step without substrate-level phosphorylation (borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 6-8, hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3). However, this energetic disadvantage is offset by lower protein synthesis cost—the ED pathway requires substantially less enzymatic protein than the EMP pathway—and by the generation of NADPH, which confers advantages in oxidative stress tolerance (borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 6-8, borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 3-6).

### 6.3 Regulatory Architecture

In *E. coli*, Eda expression is controlled by at least three promoters (P1, P2, P4) responding to different signals: GntR represses P1 and is derepressed by gluconate; KdgR represses P2 and is derepressed by hexuronates; PhoB acts as an unusual repressor at P4 under phosphate-replete conditions (murray2005multipleregulatorscontrol pages 8-9, murray2005multipleregulatorscontrol pages 5-6, murray2005multipleregulatorscontrol pages 1-1). Additionally, the global regulator CsrA indirectly activates Eda synthesis, linking ED pathway expression to carbon starvation responses (murray2005multipleregulatorscontrol pages 9-9). In *Pseudomonas* and *Pectobacterium*, HexR serves as a global regulator controlling the zwf-eda operon, with KDPG or related intermediates potentially serving as effector molecules (wang2021pectobacteriumatrosepticumkdpg pages 7-9).

### 6.4 Gluconeogenic Essentiality

Organisms relying exclusively on the ED pathway for glycolysis face a particular dependency on gluconeogenic bypass enzymes when growing on non-sugar carbon sources. In *Brucella abortus*, deletion of pyruvate phosphate dikinase (ppdK) or malic enzyme (mae) severely impaired intracellular growth and virulence, whereas fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase deletions had more modest effects, suggesting that the pyruvate-to-PEP conversion is the critical gluconeogenic bottleneck in this organism (zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 1-2, zunigaripa2014brucellaabortusdepends pages 9-11). In *Thermoproteus tenax*, the pyruvate phosphate dikinase (PPDK) is bidirectional, serving both anabolic and catabolic functions depending on substrate and cosubstrate ratios, and a type IV archaeal fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase from the inositol-monophosphatase family replaces the canonical bacterial enzyme (siebers2004reconstructionofthe pages 4-5).

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### 7.1 EDD/ilvD Dual Function in Cyanobacteria

A major unresolved question concerns whether the dihydroxy-acid dehydratase (IlvD), an enzyme in branched-chain amino acid biosynthesis, also functions as a bona fide 6-phosphogluconate dehydratase (EDD) in cyanobacteria. Depending on whether IlvD is counted as a functional EDD, the prevalence of the complete ED pathway in cyanobacteria ranges from ~1% (if only dedicated EDD enzymes are counted) to ~47% (if IlvD is assumed to have dual function) (bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 3-5, bachhar2022entnerdoudoroffpathwayin pages 2-3). Biochemical characterization of IlvD's dehydratase specificity is an important pending task.

### 7.2 Metabolite Channeling in Glycolysis

Studies using ¹³C-pulse labeling in *E. coli* Δ*pfkA* mutants revealed unsequential labeling dynamics in glycolysis intermediates, potentially suggesting metabolite channeling—the direct transfer of intermediates between enzymes without full equilibration in the cytosol (hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3). If confirmed, channeling would have significant implications for metabolic modeling and synthetic biology applications.

### 7.3 Evolutionary Priority of ED vs. EMP

Whether the ED pathway is more ancient than the EMP pathway remains debated. The presence of modified ED variants across both Bacteria and Archaea, combined with phylogenomic evidence that different components of glycolysis trace to different ancestral lineages (Asgardarchaeota vs. Alphaproteobacteria vs. Cyanobacteria), suggests that modern central carbon metabolism is a chimeric assembly rather than a monophyletic inheritance (santanamolina2025chimericoriginsand pages 3-4, santanamolina2025chimericoriginsand pages 4-5). The lower enzymatic complexity of the ED pathway and its broader thermodynamic favorability have been cited as arguments for its antiquity, but rigorous phylogenetic reconstruction of the individual enzyme families does not yet conclusively resolve the question.

### 7.4 Functional Degeneracy and Redundancy

The coexistence of ED and EMP pathways in the same organism (as in *E. coli* and *T. tenax*) raises questions about the selective pressures maintaining both routes. Von Borzyskowski et al. (2020) argued that this "functional degeneracy"—where different molecular solutions accomplish the same metabolic outcome—is not exceptional but rather the standard in microbial central carbon metabolism, driven by advantages in different environmental contexts including oxidative stress tolerance, protein economy, and carbon source availability (borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 6-8, borzyskowski2020biochemicalunityrevisited pages 3-6).

### 7.5 Biotechnological Applications

The ED pathway has attracted growing interest in metabolic engineering. *Zymomonas mobilis* serves as a natural ethanologen owing to its anaerobic ED pathway, and engineering ED pathway activity into *E. coli* can alleviate catabolite repression and improve NADPH availability without carbon loss as CO₂ (hollinshead2016examiningescherichiacoli pages 1-3). The EDEMP cycle architecture of *P. putida* has become a target for rational engineering of substrate utilization, including recent work on adapting this organism to non-native substrates such as D-xylose through combined metabolic engineering and adaptive laboratory evolution (dvorak2024syntheticallyprimedadaptationof pages 2-3, weimer2020industrialbiotechnologyof pages 1-3).
