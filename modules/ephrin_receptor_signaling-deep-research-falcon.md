# Ephrin Receptor Signaling Pathway Module: A Comprehensive Review

## 1. Executive Summary

The ephrin-Eph receptor signaling system constitutes the largest family of receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) in vertebrates, comprising 14 Eph receptors (9 EphA and 5 EphB in mammals) and 8 ephrin ligands (5 ephrin-A and 3 ephrin-B) (arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 1-3, pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 1-3). This signaling module is uniquely distinguished from other RTK systems by two features: (i) the requirement for membrane-tethered ligands, which mandates direct cell–cell contact for signal initiation, and (ii) the capacity for bidirectional signaling, whereby both the receptor-expressing ("forward") and the ligand-expressing ("reverse") cell receive instructive signals (shi2026theroleof pages 2-4, guo2024ephsincancer pages 5-7). The pathway operates through a defined sequence—ephrin–Eph engagement at cell contacts, receptor oligomerization and kinase activation via juxtamembrane tyrosine phosphorylation, and output through Rho-family GTPases and SH2-domain adaptor networks to pattern adhesion, repulsion, migration, and vascular organization (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 3-4, arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 1-3). Evolutionary analyses indicate that complete Eph-ephrin signaling dates to the last common ancestor of all extant metazoans, with precursor domains identifiable in choanoflagellates (krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 1-2, krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 2-4). Despite decades of study, the field continues to grapple with important unresolved questions, particularly the paradoxical dual roles of Eph receptors as both tumor suppressors and oncogenes, the functional significance of ligand-independent signaling, and the emerging role of biomolecular condensate formation in signal transduction (guo2024ephsincancer pages 5-7, guo2024ephsincancer pages 10-11, pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 23-26).

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### What the system includes

The ephrin-Eph receptor signaling pathway module encompasses three obligatory steps: (1) cell–cell ephrin–Eph engagement, (2) Eph receptor kinase activation, and (3) adhesion and guidance output. At its core, the system begins when membrane-tethered ephrin ligands on one cell bind Eph receptor tyrosine kinases on an apposing cell, promoting receptor dimerization, higher-order clustering, and transphosphorylation of conserved juxtamembrane tyrosines (JX1 and JX2), which relieve autoinhibition of the kinase domain (shi2026theroleof pages 2-4, arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 1-3). The phosphorylated receptor then recruits SH2-domain-containing adaptor proteins (Nck, CrkII, Grb7/Grb10, Src-family kinases) and activates downstream Rho-family GTPases (RhoA, Rac1, Cdc42), ultimately regulating the actin cytoskeleton to drive cell adhesion, repulsion, migration, or boundary formation (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 3-4, shi2026theroleof pages 2-4).

Ephrin-A ligands are GPI-anchored and lack an intrinsic cytoplasmic domain, whereas ephrin-B ligands are single-pass transmembrane proteins with cytoplasmic tails that support bona fide reverse signaling through tyrosine phosphorylation and PDZ-domain interactions (chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4, shi2026theroleof pages 2-4). EphA receptors preferentially bind ephrin-A ligands and EphB receptors preferentially bind ephrin-B ligands, although cross-class interactions are documented (chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4, guo2024ephsincancer pages 5-7).

### Neighboring pathways to distinguish

Several signaling systems frequently intersect with Eph-ephrin signaling but should be treated as distinct:

- **Rho GTPase signaling per se**: While RhoA/Rac1/Cdc42 are key effectors of Eph signaling, the Rho GTPase network itself is a general cytoskeletal regulatory module shared by many receptor systems (arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 1-3).
- **EGFR and VEGFR signaling**: EPHA2 physically associates with EGFR and VEGFR2, and crosstalk between these RTKs modulates Eph receptor phosphorylation state and downstream outcomes, but these represent lateral pathway interactions rather than core Eph-ephrin components (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 7-9, chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4).
- **Metalloprotease-mediated shedding and γ-secretase processing**: ADAM10/ADAM17-dependent cleavage of ephrin or Eph ectodomains terminates cell–cell adhesion and enables cell separation, but this proteolytic step represents a regulatory mechanism rather than a signaling step per se (chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4).
- **Wnt, integrin, and cadherin pathways**: These interact with Eph-ephrin signaling at the level of adhesion regulation but constitute independent systems (chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### Step 1: Cell–cell ephrin–Eph engagement

Signaling initiation requires physical cell–cell contact because both ligand and receptor are membrane-anchored (arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 1-3, pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 1-3). When ephrin ligands on one cell surface engage Eph receptors on the opposing cell, the ligand-binding domain (LBD) and cysteine-rich domain (CRD) of the receptor facilitate initial dimerization (arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 1-3). This contact triggers further receptor oligomerization into large clusters ranging from hundreds of nanometers to micrometers, stabilized by multiple LBD–ephrin and symmetric receptor–receptor interfaces (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 1-3). The size and geometry of these clusters are functionally significant: larger clusters produce stronger, more persistent signaling, and cluster size determines which downstream proteins are recruited (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 4-6). Recent evidence suggests that ephrin-induced EPHA2 clustering may trigger phase separation of plasma membrane lipids, creating signaling microdomains that concentrate specific effectors while excluding others (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 4-6).

An exception to the contact-dependent paradigm has been documented in cancer, where EPHA2 on extracellular vesicles can signal to ephrin-A1-expressing tumor cells without direct cell–cell contact (chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4).

### Step 2: Eph receptor kinase activation

Upon ligand-induced clustering, Eph receptors undergo transphosphorylation on two conserved juxtamembrane tyrosine residues (JX1 and JX2). In the unliganded state, these unphosphorylated tyrosines adopt an autoinhibitory conformation that blocks substrate access to the kinase domain. Phosphorylation of JX1 and JX2 releases this inhibition, enabling full kinase activity (chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4, shi2026theroleof pages 2-4, arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 1-3). The phosphorylated tyrosine motifs then serve as docking sites for SH2-domain-containing signaling proteins (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 3-4, arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 1-3). Additional phosphorylation events occur on the kinase activation loop and SAM domain (e.g., Y928 in EphB1, Y921 in EphA2), promoting engagement of adaptors such as Grb7 and Grb10 (arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 3-4).

Non-canonical (ligand-independent) activation represents an important alternative mode. EPHA2 can be phosphorylated on S897 by AKT in the absence of ephrin ligand, triggering pro-migratory signaling distinct from the canonical tyrosine-kinase-dependent pathway (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 7-9, guo2024ephsincancer pages 10-11). This non-canonical pathway is particularly relevant in cancer, where EPHA2 is frequently overexpressed but poorly tyrosine-phosphorylated due to loss of cell–cell contacts (guo2024ephsincancer pages 10-11).

### Step 3: Adhesion and guidance output

Downstream signaling bifurcates into forward and reverse arms:

**Forward signaling** occurs in the Eph receptor-expressing cell. Activated Eph receptors recruit SH2/SH3/SAM/PDZ-domain adaptors and engage effector pathways including RAS/ERK, PI3K/AKT/mTOR, and Rho-family GTPases (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 3-4). The Vav–RhoA–ROCK–myosin II cascade is the primary mechanism for generating actomyosin contractility: activated RhoA stimulates ROCK, which phosphorylates myosin light chains (MLC) and inhibits MLC phosphatase, increasing cortical tension (shi2026theroleof pages 4-6, shi2026theroleof pages 2-4). Additionally, RhoA activates formin-family proteins to promote actin polymerization (shi2026theroleof pages 2-4). The intensity of forward signaling determines cellular outcomes: weak signaling promotes adhesion, while strong signaling disrupts junctions and drives cell separation through actin contraction and microtubule destabilization (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 3-4, arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 3-4).

**Reverse signaling** occurs in the ephrin-expressing cell. Ephrin-B ligands support reverse signaling through phosphorylation of cytoplasmic tyrosine residues, which recruit SH2- and PDZ-domain-containing proteins that modulate Rho GTPase activity and cytoskeletal remodeling (shi2026theroleof pages 2-4, shi2026theroleof pages 4-6). Ephrin-A reverse signaling, though less well characterized, can occur through associated membrane proteins (guo2024ephsincancer pages 5-7).

### Key exemplar molecules

**EFNB2 (ephrin-B2)** is a transmembrane ephrin-B ligand that serves as a definitive arterial endothelial marker. Its proangiogenic activity depends on its cytoplasmic domain, and it is essential for coronary artery development, blood vessel wall assembly, and endothelial migration (zhu2024recentadvancesof pages 3-4, zhu2024recentadvancesof pages 1-3). EFNB2 deletion in vascular smooth muscle cells causes hypotension in male mice, and its upregulation in failing hearts mediates cardiac fibrosis via Stat3 and TGF-β/Smad3 signaling (zhu2024recentadvancesof pages 3-4, zhu2024recentadvancesof pages 4-5). In the EFNB2–EPHB4 axis, ephrin-B2 on arterial endothelium signals bidirectionally with EPHB4 on venous endothelium to establish arterial–venous boundaries (zhu2024recentadvancesof pages 1-3, guo2024ephsincancer pages 11-13).

**EPHA2** exemplifies the complexity of Eph receptor biology. In canonical mode, ephrin-A ligand binding triggers clustering, tyrosine phosphorylation, and activation of tumor-suppressive pathways including inhibition of AKT and mTORC1 (arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 3-4, pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 7-9). In non-canonical mode, S897 phosphorylation by AKT (downstream of EGFR or other RTKs) promotes cell migration and invasion independently of ephrin ligands (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 7-9, guo2024ephsincancer pages 10-11). This duality makes EPHA2 simultaneously a candidate tumor suppressor and an oncogenic driver depending on the cellular context (guo2024ephsincancer pages 5-7, guo2024ephsincancer pages 10-11).

**EPHB4** is the primary receptor for ephrin-B2 and a key venous endothelial marker. EPHB4 deletion in vascular smooth muscle cells reduces blood pressure and vessel contractility, while its expression on endothelial cells mediates cardiomyogenesis (zhu2024recentadvancesof pages 3-4). Phosphorylation of EPHB4 at Y987 protects it from ubiquitination and degradation, and EPHB4 can associate with the insulin receptor to promote its lysosomal degradation, linking Eph signaling to metabolic regulation (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 7-9).

**RhoA** functions as the central cytoskeletal switch in Eph-mediated cell sorting and boundary formation. Activated downstream of Eph receptors via Vav-family GEFs, RhoA drives the ROCK–myosin II pathway to generate cortical tension and actomyosin contractility that physically separates cell populations at tissue boundaries (shi2026theroleof pages 4-6, shi2026theroleof pages 2-4, shi2026theroleof pages 9-11).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### Evolutionary origin

The deepest plausible evolutionary origin of Eph-ephrin signaling extends to the last common ancestor of all extant metazoans—and possibly predates animals entirely. Krishnan et al. (2019) identified complete Eph receptors and ephrin ligands in sponges and ctenophores for the first time, demonstrating that this signaling axis was operational before the divergence of these basal animal lineages (krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 1-2, krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 9-12). Truncated Eph-like receptors and ephrin-like domains have been identified in choanoflagellates (unicellular relatives of animals), although no single choanoflagellate species possesses both functional receptor and ligand (krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 2-4, krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 4-7).

The Eph receptor LBD appears to have evolved from an ancient galactose-binding (discoidin-domain-like) protein, while the ephrin receptor-binding domain (RBD) derived from a monodomain cupredoxin that lost its copper-binding capacity through neofunctionalization (krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 2-4, krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 12-13). The coupling of the Eph-LBD to an intracellular tyrosine kinase domain occurred through domain shuffling events along the metazoan stem lineage (krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 12-13).

### Lineage-specific elaboration

Within metazoans, the EphA/EphB receptor subtype distinction arose near the origin of vertebrates; invertebrate bilaterians possess Eph receptors that cluster basally to both subtypes (krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 7-8). Similarly, ephrin-A/B diversification predated vertebrate emergence, with ephrin-B apparently being more evolutionarily ancient (krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 7-8). Sponges show four distinct Eph receptor clusters and possess seven divergent ephrins with unique features including both transmembrane and GPI-anchoring motifs on the same protein (krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 12-13, krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 7-8). Cnidarians underwent large lineage-specific ephrin expansions, distinct from the bilaterian pattern (krishnan2019thefirstidentification pages 7-8). Vertebrate genomes show the most extensive family expansion, with nine EphA and five EphB receptors in mammals (cortes2023structureandevolution pages 2-4).

*Drosophila* possesses a single Eph receptor and a single ephrin, while *C. elegans* has a conserved but simplified system; these invertebrate models have been important for studying core guidance functions (cortes2023structureandevolution pages 2-4).

### Tissue and developmental variation

The nervous system shows the highest expression levels of Eph receptors and ephrins, where they primarily control axon guidance, synapse formation, and boundary sharpening between neural compartments such as rhombomeres (chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4, shi2026theroleof pages 9-11, arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 3-4). In vascular development, ephrin-B2 marks arteries and EPHB4 marks veins; their bidirectional signaling establishes arterial–venous identity and organizes the capillary bed (zhu2024recentadvancesof pages 1-3, guo2024ephsincancer pages 11-13). During gastrulation, ephrin-B2 and EphA4 are expressed in mesoderm while EPHB4 and ephrin-B1/B3 mark ectoderm, mediating germ layer segregation in *Xenopus* (shi2026theroleof pages 4-6). In postnatal tissues, Eph-ephrin expression is generally low and functions primarily in tissue homeostasis and plasticity (arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 3-4).

A critical finding is that cis interactions (co-expression of Eph and ephrin on the same cell surface) attenuate Eph receptor functions and inhibit forward signaling, whereas trans interactions (across cell–cell contacts) activate bidirectional signaling (zhu2024recentadvancesof pages 1-3). This cis/trans regulatory distinction adds a layer of spatial control to the pathway.

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### Ordering constraints

The pathway follows an obligate sequence: ephrin–Eph cell contact must precede kinase activation, which must precede adhesion/guidance output. Ephrin binding is the prerequisite for canonical receptor oligomerization and JX1/JX2 phosphorylation (chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4, shi2026theroleof pages 2-4). However, ligand-independent activation (through receptor overexpression, clustering at high density, or serine phosphorylation by heterologous kinases) represents a conditional bypass that is particularly relevant in pathological contexts (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 7-9, guo2024ephsincancer pages 10-11).

### Mutual exclusivity and dose-dependence

The signaling outcome depends critically on signal intensity: weak Eph activation promotes cell adhesion and spreading, while strong activation causes cytoskeletal collapse, cell rounding, and repulsion (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 3-4, arora2023ephreceptorsin pages 3-4). This dose-dependent switching behavior means that the same receptor can produce opposite cellular outcomes depending on ephrin concentration and clustering geometry.

### Compartment and cell-type specificity

Forward signaling is restricted to Eph receptor-expressing cells, while reverse signaling occurs exclusively in ephrin-expressing cells—these are mutually exclusive in the same cell for a given receptor–ligand pair in trans (shi2026theroleof pages 2-4, guo2024ephsincancer pages 5-7). The EFNB2–EPHB4 axis is specifically constrained to arterial–venous interfaces during vascular development (zhu2024recentadvancesof pages 1-3). Metalloprotease-mediated ectodomain shedding is required for the conversion of adhesion contacts into repulsion; without cleavage, cells remain tethered (chatzikalil2024theclinicalrelevance pages 2-4).

### Kinase-dead receptors as modulators

Two family members, EphB6 and EphA10, are catalytically dead pseudokinases that cannot autonomously phosphorylate substrates (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 23-26). Nevertheless, EphB6 juxtamembrane tyrosines can be phosphorylated by other kinases (e.g., EPHB4), allowing recruitment of SH2-domain effectors including Abl, Src, and Vav3, indicating that kinase-dead family members function as signal-modulating scaffolds rather than inert proteins.

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### The tumor suppressor–oncogene paradox

Perhaps the most persistent controversy concerns Eph receptors' dual roles in cancer. Eph receptors are frequently overexpressed in tumors yet are often poorly tyrosine-phosphorylated, suggesting that their canonical forward signaling may actually restrain tumor progression (guo2024ephsincancer pages 5-7, guo2024ephsincancer pages 7-10). Mutations impairing kinase function of EphB2, EphA3, and EphA5 are common in cancers, consistent with a tumor-suppressive role for forward signaling (guo2024ephsincancer pages 7-10). Conversely, EPHA2 non-canonical signaling through S897 phosphorylation actively promotes cancer cell migration and invasion (guo2024ephsincancer pages 10-11). The resolution likely involves context: ligand-dependent tyrosine phosphorylation can dephosphorylate pro-tumorigenic serine residues, creating a molecular switch where ligand availability determines whether EPHA2 is suppressive or oncogenic (guo2024ephsincancer pages 10-11).

### Ligand-dependent versus ligand-independent signaling

While canonical signaling requires ephrin binding, Eph receptors clearly signal in the absence of ligand through receptor overexpression-driven clustering, phosphorylation by heterologous kinases, unconventional ligands, and cis interactions (guo2024ephsincancer pages 5-7, pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 23-26). The relative physiological importance of these non-canonical modes versus experimental artifacts of overexpression systems remains debated (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 23-26).

### Phase separation and condensate biology

Emerging evidence suggests that ephrin-induced Eph receptor clustering may generate biomolecular condensates through liquid–liquid phase separation, creating non-equilibrium signaling platforms with threshold-dependent responses (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 4-6, pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 11-13). However, this concept is at an early stage and requires substantial further validation to determine whether phase separation is a mechanistically essential feature of Eph signaling or a correlate of large-scale clustering (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 11-13).

### Heterotypic receptor platforms

Whether different Eph receptor subtypes co-assemble into heterotypic signaling platforms that generate novel functions distinct from homomeric assemblies remains an open question. EPHA2 and EGFR form complexes driven by PIP₂ interactions at the plasma membrane, suggesting that heterotypic RTK assemblies may be an underappreciated aspect of Eph signaling (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 23-26).

### Incompletely characterized phosphorylation sites

The functional significance of many Eph receptor phosphorylation sites—including extracellular tyrosine phosphosites, non-conserved sites, and serine/threonine phosphosites—remains poorly understood (pasquale2024ephreceptorsignaling pages 11-13). Additionally, the roles of glycosylation, alternative splicing, and genetic mutations in modulating Eph receptor function are emerging research areas requiring further investigation.
