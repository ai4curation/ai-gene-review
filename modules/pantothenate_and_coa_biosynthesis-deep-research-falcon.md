# Pantothenate and Coenzyme A Biosynthesis: A Review Centered on the *Pseudomonas putida* KT2440 Module

## 1. Executive Summary

Coenzyme A (CoA) is a universal acyl-group carrier participating in over 100 catabolic and anabolic reactions. Its biosynthesis proceeds through a well-conserved, linear series of enzymatic steps beginning with the assembly of pantothenate (vitamin B5) from pantoate and β-alanine, followed by five sequential conversions of pantothenate to CoA (daugherty2002completereconstitutionof pages 1-2, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 1-2). In *Pseudomonas putida* KT2440, this module (KEGG ppu00770) encompasses the core pantothenate-to-CoA trunk, the pantoate branch from 3-methyl-2-oxobutanoate (α-ketoisovalerate), and several boundary nodes including branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) precursor enzymes, pyrimidine-derived β-alanine supply, acyl carrier protein (ACP) phosphopantetheine handling, and nucleotide hydrolase side reactions. This review synthesizes the mechanistic logic, evolutionary variation, regulatory architecture, and unresolved questions surrounding this essential metabolic module, with particular attention to its instantiation in *P. putida* KT2440.

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### 2.1 What the system includes

The core pantothenate and CoA biosynthesis module comprises two connected segments:

**Pantothenate biosynthesis (de novo):** The conversion of 3-methyl-2-oxobutanoate to pantoate via ketopantoate hydroxymethyltransferase (PanB) and 2-dehydropantoate reductase (PanE), followed by ATP-dependent condensation of pantoate with β-alanine by pantothenate synthetase (PanC) (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 1-2).

**CoA biosynthesis from pantothenate:** The five-step universal pathway: (1) phosphorylation of pantothenate by pantothenate kinase (CoaA/CoaX); (2) CTP-dependent conjugation with cysteine by phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthetase (CoaB domain of CoaBC); (3) decarboxylation by phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylase (CoaC domain of CoaBC); (4) adenylylation by phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase (CoaD/PPAT); and (5) final phosphorylation by dephospho-CoA kinase (CoaE/DPCK) (daugherty2002completereconstitutionof pages 1-2, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 6-8, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 9-10).

### 2.2 Boundary and context nodes

Several neighboring pathways are tracked in the KEGG ppu00770 module but should be treated as precursor supply or downstream sinks rather than core pantothenate/CoA biosynthesis:

- **BCAA precursor supply:** The valine biosynthesis enzymes (acetolactate synthase IlvBN, ketol-acid reductoisomerase IlvC, dihydroxy-acid dehydratase IlvD, and aminotransferase IlvE) generate the 3-methyl-2-oxobutanoate substrate for PanB (franco2017bacterialbranchedchainamino pages 5-8, franco2017bacterialbranchedchainamino pages 51-52).
- **β-Alanine supply:** In *E. coli*, β-alanine is produced primarily by aspartate-1-decarboxylase (PanD); the pyrimidine degradation route (dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase, dihydropyrimidinase PydB, and β-ureidopropionase) can supply β-alanine in some organisms but has been shown not to operate in *E. coli* (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 4-6, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4).
- **ACP phosphopantetheine handling:** Phosphopantetheinyltransferases (PP_1183/AcpS-type) and ACP phosphodiesterase (PP_0922/AcpH-type) connect the CoA pool to fatty acid and secondary metabolite biosynthesis but are downstream users, not biosynthetic enzymes (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 12-13, beld2014thephosphopantetheinyltransferases pages 29-30, beld2014thephosphopantetheinyltransferases pages 26-27).
- **MazG NTP pyrophosphohydrolase:** A nucleotide "house-cleaning" enzyme that hydrolyzes damaged nucleoside triphosphates; its inclusion in the KEGG module reflects shared cofactor/nucleotide context rather than a direct role in CoA synthesis (fan2018oxidationofdctp pages 1-1).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

### 3.1 The pantoate branch

The first committed step of pantothenate biosynthesis is catalyzed by PanB (ketopantoate hydroxymethyltransferase, EC 2.1.2.11), which transfers a hydroxymethyl group from N⁵,N¹⁰-methylene-tetrahydrofolate to 3-methyl-2-oxobutanoate (α-ketoisovalerate), yielding 2-dehydropantoate (ketopantoate) (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 1-2, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4). In *E. coli*, PanB is a 28-kDa polypeptide that assembles into a decameric complex with pentameric ring structures. A catalytic magnesium ion is essential, and the enzyme exhibits end-product inhibition by ketopantoate, providing intrinsic feedback control at the entry point of the pathway (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 1-2). PanB overexpression alone increases CoA production by 40–50% in *E. coli*, indicating that pantoate supply is rate-limiting under standard conditions (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4).

The ketopantoate intermediate is then reduced to D-pantoate by 2-dehydropantoate reductase (PanE, EC 1.1.1.169), using NADPH as reductant (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4). In *P. putida* KT2440, KEGG assigns multiple candidate loci (PP_2325, PP_2998, PP_4452) for this reductase activity, reflecting a well-documented pattern of enzymatic redundancy at this step. The BCAA enzyme IlvC (ketol-acid reductoisomerase) can substitute for PanE both *in vitro* and *in vivo*: *panE* mutants do not require exogenous pantoate when IlvC is abundantly expressed but become auxotrophic when IlvC levels are low (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4). This functional overlap between dedicated PanE-type reductases and promiscuous keto-acid reductases complicates genome annotation, as demonstrated by work in *Lactococcus lactis* where a gene annotated as *panE* actually encodes a D-2-hydroxyacid dehydrogenase with no ketopantoate reductase activity (chambellon2009thed2hydroxyacid pages 5-6, chambellon2009thed2hydroxyacid pages 6-7).

### 3.2 Pantothenate synthetase

PanC (pantothenate synthetase, EC 6.3.2.1) catalyzes the ATP-dependent condensation of pantoate with β-alanine to form pantothenate, releasing AMP and pyrophosphate (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4, bottcher2025coenzymeametabolism pages 1-2). In *P. putida* KT2440, *panC* is encoded at locus PP_4700, typically in an operon with *panB* (PP_4699). Notably, genome-wide mutant screening in *P. putida* KT2440 predicted *panC* disruption would cause pantothenate auxotrophy, but experimental testing of a *panC* knockout showed it did not behave as a simple auxotroph, suggesting either redundant pantothenate synthetase activity or alternative metabolic bypass routes in this organism (molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 12-13, molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 4-6).

### 3.3 The pantothenate-to-CoA trunk

The five-step conversion of pantothenate to CoA is universally conserved, though the enzymes catalyzing each step vary across lineages:

**Step 1: Pantothenate kinase.** This is the first committed step and primary regulatory checkpoint. *P. putida* KT2440 encodes a type III pantothenate kinase (CoaX), a member of the ASKHA (Acetate and Sugar Kinase/Hsc70/Actin) superfamily that is structurally and mechanistically distinct from the type I P-loop kinase (CoaA) of *E. coli* (nicely2007structureofthe pages 1-2). Type III PanKs are characterized by a tight pantothenate-binding pocket that cannot accommodate the β-mercaptoethylamido moiety of CoA, rendering them insensitive to CoA feedback inhibition (nicely2007structureofthe pages 9-10). This has important physiological implications: organisms using CoaX lack the primary metabolic brake on CoA synthesis that type I PanK provides in enterobacteria (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 10-12, bottcher2025coenzymeametabolism pages 2-3).

**Step 2–3: CoaBC/Dfp (bifunctional).** The *dfp* gene (renamed *coaBC*) encodes a bifunctional FMN-containing enzyme in most bacteria (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 8-9, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 9-10). The carboxy-terminal CoaB domain (residues ~191–406) catalyzes CTP-dependent condensation of 4′-phosphopantothenate with cysteine to form 4′-phosphopantothenoylcysteine. The amino-terminal CoaC domain (residues ~2–190) then decarboxylates this intermediate via a thioaldehyde mechanism to yield 4′-phosphopantetheine (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 9-10). In *E. coli*, CoaBC assembles into a homododecamer of 43-kDa subunits. Some lineages, notably enterococci and streptococci, encode CoaB and CoaC as separate genes rather than a single bifunctional protein (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 8-9, bottcher2025coenzymeametabolism pages 1-2).

**Step 4: CoaD (PPAT).** Phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase catalyzes the reversible, Mg²⁺-dependent transfer of AMP from ATP to 4′-phosphopantetheine, forming dephospho-CoA and releasing pyrophosphate (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 9-10). PPAT belongs to the P-loop-containing nucleotide triphosphate hydrolase superfamily and operates through an in-line displacement mechanism (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 10-12). PPAT serves as a secondary regulatory node, subject to feedback inhibition by free CoA (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 12-13, bottcher2025coenzymeametabolism pages 2-3).

**Step 5: CoaE (DPCK).** Dephospho-CoA kinase catalyzes the final phosphorylation, adding an ATP-derived phosphate to the 3′-hydroxyl group of dephospho-CoA to yield CoA (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 9-10). While this enzyme is conserved across bacteria and eukaryotes, archaea employ a structurally unrelated DPCK (arCOG04076 family) that is distantly related to thiamine pyrophosphokinases and preferentially uses GTP rather than ATP as phosphate donor, representing a clear case of convergent evolution (shimosaka2019identificationofdephosphocoenzyme pages 9-10, shimosaka2019identificationofdephosphocoenzyme pages 2-4, shimosaka2019identificationofdephosphocoenzyme pages 1-2).

The distribution of PanK types has profound implications for CoA homeostasis. In organisms using type I PanK (e.g., *E. coli*), CoA acts as a competitive inhibitor with respect to ATP (K_i ~4.3 µM), and the ratio of free CoA to acyl-CoA thioesters modulates pathway flux: when this ratio increases (e.g., from 0.7 to 4.3 upon carbon source shift), pantothenate phosphorylation is selectively inhibited (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 12-13, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 6-8). In contrast, organisms with type II or type III PanK, including *P. putida* and *S. aureus*, lack this feedback brake. In *S. aureus*, this is physiologically meaningful because the bacterium relies on CoA as its major low-molecular-weight thiol, requiring millimolar intracellular CoA concentrations to support its CoA-disulfide reductase redox system (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 10-12, bottcher2025coenzymeametabolism pages 2-3).

### 4.2 The CoaBC bifunctional enzyme

The fusion of phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthetase and decarboxylase activities into a single polypeptide (CoaBC/Dfp) is the predominant arrangement in bacteria (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 8-9, bottcher2025coenzymeametabolism pages 1-2). The decarboxylase domain requires FMN as cofactor and proceeds through a thioaldehyde intermediate (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 9-10). The split organization found in enterococci and streptococci may represent either an ancestral state or a lineage-specific fission event; this remains unresolved.

### 4.3 Phosphopantetheinyltransferases and ACP recycling

CoA serves as the phosphopantetheine donor for post-translational activation of carrier proteins essential in fatty acid biosynthesis, polyketide synthesis, and non-ribosomal peptide synthesis (beld2014thephosphopantetheinyltransferases pages 26-27, beld2014thephosphopantetheinyltransferases pages 24-26, beld2014thephosphopantetheinyltransferases pages 34-36). In *P. putida* KT2440, PP_1183 (AcpS-type phosphopantetheinyltransferase) catalyzes this essential modification, while PP_0922 (ACP phosphodiesterase/AcpH-type) removes the prosthetic group from ACP, releasing 4′-phosphopantetheine that can re-enter the CoA biosynthetic pathway (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 12-13, beld2014thephosphopantetheinyltransferases pages 29-30). The *in vivo* turnover rate of the phosphopantetheine arm exceeds that of the carrier protein itself, suggesting both PPTase and AcpH activities serve regulatory functions in balancing active and inactive carrier protein pools (beld2014thephosphopantetheinyltransferases pages 29-30).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### 5.1 Deep evolutionary conservation

The five-step CoA biosynthesis trunk from pantothenate is universally conserved across all three domains of life, implying an ancient origin predating the last universal common ancestor (LUCA) (daugherty2002completereconstitutionof pages 1-2). However, the specific enzymes catalyzing each step have undergone extensive non-orthologous gene displacement:

- Pantothenate kinase exists in three structurally unrelated forms in bacteria alone (nicely2007structureofthe pages 1-2, nicely2007structureofthe pages 9-10).
- Archaeal DPCK (CoaE equivalent) belongs to a completely different protein superfamily than bacterial/eukaryotic DPCK, employing a Rossmann fold rather than a P-loop fold and preferring GTP over ATP (shimosaka2019identificationofdephosphocoenzyme pages 9-10, shimosaka2019identificationofdephosphocoenzyme pages 2-4).
- In archaea, the order of phosphorylation and condensation steps in pantothenate conversion differs: pantoate kinase (PoK) phosphorylates pantoate first, followed by phosphopantothenate synthetase (PPS), reversing the bacterial/eukaryotic order of pantothenate synthetase followed by pantothenate kinase (shimosaka2019identificationofdephosphocoenzyme pages 2-4).

### 5.2 Pantothenate biosynthesis versus salvage

The de novo pantothenate biosynthetic capability (PanBCDE) is present in most bacteria, plants, and yeast, but is absent from animals and many obligate intracellular parasites, which must obtain pantothenate from dietary or host sources (daugherty2002completereconstitutionof pages 1-2). Among the Apicomplexa, only coccidians (e.g., *Toxoplasma gondii*) retain the ability to synthesize pantothenate; haemosporidians (e.g., *Plasmodium falciparum*) are dependent on salvage.

### 5.3 Variation at the reductase step

The functional redundancy at the ketopantoate reductase step is a widespread phenomenon. In many bacteria, IlvC (the BCAA pathway enzyme ketol-acid reductoisomerase) can moonlight as a ketopantoate reductase, providing a backup route to pantoate when PanE is limiting (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4). In *Corynebacterium glutamicum*, IlvC is reportedly the only enzyme capable of producing pantoate, meaning the dedicated PanE function has been entirely replaced (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4). The multiple PanE candidates in *P. putida* KT2440 (PP_2325, PP_2998, PP_4452) likely reflect this broader pattern of redundant keto-acid reductase specificity rather than multiple dedicated ketopantoate reductases. Annotation at this node should be treated cautiously, as the *L. lactis* case demonstrates that sequence-based PanE annotation can be misleading (chambellon2009thed2hydroxyacid pages 5-6, chambellon2009thed2hydroxyacid pages 6-7).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### 6.1 Obligate ordering

The pathway is strictly ordered: pantothenate must be phosphorylated before cysteine conjugation (CoaB cannot act on unphosphorylated pantothenate), and the 3′-phosphorylation by CoaE must follow the adenylyl transfer by CoaD (daugherty2002completereconstitutionof pages 1-2, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 6-8). The decarboxylation of phosphopantothenoylcysteine by CoaC necessarily follows the synthetase reaction by CoaB. These constraints define an obligatory linear sequence with no verified shortcuts or alternative orderings in any characterized organism.

### 6.2 Substrate channeling and metabolic coordination

When pantothenate kinase is feedback-inhibited or overexpressed such that downstream enzymes cannot keep pace, 4′-phosphopantetheine accumulates and is excreted from the cell, indicating that PPAT (CoaD) represents a bottleneck when upstream flux is deregulated (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 10-12). This excretion phenomenon serves as a "safety valve" but also represents metabolic waste.

### 6.3 Cofactor dependencies

The pathway requires multiple cofactors at different steps: N⁵,N¹⁰-methylene-tetrahydrofolate (PanB), NADPH (PanE/IlvC), ATP (PanC, CoaX, CoaE), CTP (CoaB), FMN (CoaC), and Mg²⁺ (PanB, CoaD) (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 1-2, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 2-4, leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 9-10). Limitation of any of these cofactors can restrict CoA production. When pantothenate is limiting, succinyl-CoA pools become insufficient for protein synthesis (the succinyl-CoA–dependent modification of tRNA), causing growth arrest independently of energy limitation (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 12-13).

### 6.4 Failure modes in *P. putida* KT2440

Genome-wide mutant screening revealed that disruption of genes predicted *in silico* to be essential for pantothenate biosynthesis (PP_4699/*panB*, PP_4700/*panC*) did not consistently produce the expected auxotrophic phenotypes (molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 12-13, molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 4-6). The *panC* knockout from the *Pseudomonas* Reference Culture Collection did not show auxotrophic characteristics, suggesting either that *P. putida* possesses alternative or redundant pantothenate biosynthetic capacity not captured in current metabolic models, or that salvage from trace medium components is sufficient (molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 4-6). This finding highlights an important gap between computational predictions and experimental phenotypes in this organism.

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### 7.1 Identity of the functional ketopantoate reductase(s) in *P. putida*

The assignment of multiple PanE candidates (PP_2325, PP_2998, PP_4452) in *P. putida* KT2440 remains provisional. None of these has been biochemically validated for ketopantoate reductase activity in this organism. Given the documented promiscuity of keto-acid reductases and the frequent misannotation of PanE-like genes (chambellon2009thed2hydroxyacid pages 5-6, chambellon2009thed2hydroxyacid pages 6-7), targeted biochemical characterization is needed to determine which, if any, of these loci encodes the primary ketopantoate reductase versus a moonlighting BCAA-pathway reductase.

### 7.2 Regulation of CoA biosynthesis in type III PanK organisms

In *E. coli* and other organisms with type I PanK, CoA homeostasis is maintained through feedback inhibition at the pantothenate kinase step (leonardi2007biosynthesisofpantothenic pages 6-8). How organisms with feedback-insensitive type III PanK (CoaX), such as *P. putida*, regulate their intracellular CoA pool remains poorly understood. Possible mechanisms include transcriptional regulation of *coaX*, regulation at the PPAT (CoaD) step, or CoA degradation pathways, but these have not been systematically investigated in Pseudomonads.

### 7.3 The PanB side-activity on folate

PanB has been shown to possess a side-activity that cleaves 5,10-methylene-tetrahydrofolate, producing a pterin product that can be recycled by FolK. Overexpression of PanB in *E. coli* leads to accumulation of 6-hydroxymethylpterin and a paradoxical 46% increase in total folate content, suggesting an adaptive interaction between pantothenate and folate biosynthesis that is not fully understood.

### 7.4 The unexpected non-auxotrophy of *P. putida panC* mutants

The failure of *panC* disruption to produce a clear pantothenate auxotroph in *P. putida* KT2440, despite *in silico* predictions of essentiality, raises the possibility of alternative pantothenate biosynthetic routes, salvage mechanisms from environmental pantothenate or pantetheine, or compensatory metabolic rewiring in this metabolically versatile organism (molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 12-13, molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 4-6). This discrepancy between model predictions and experimental observations remains unresolved and may reflect the metabolic flexibility that makes *P. putida* an attractive chassis for biotechnology.

### 7.5 Archaeal-bacterial DPCK non-homology

The discovery that archaeal dephospho-CoA kinase belongs to a completely different protein family (arCOG04076, distantly related to thiamine pyrophosphokinases) than bacterial/eukaryotic DPCK represents a striking case of analogous enzyme evolution for an essential metabolic function (shimosaka2019identificationofdephosphocoenzyme pages 9-10, shimosaka2019identificationofdephosphocoenzyme pages 1-2). Whether this reflects independent evolutionary origins of CoA biosynthesis in the two domains or a late gene replacement event remains debated.

### 7.6 Role of boundary nodes

The inclusion of MazG (NTP pyrophosphohydrolase) in the KEGG module likely reflects its role in nucleotide pool sanitization rather than a direct connection to CoA biosynthesis (fan2018oxidationofdctp pages 1-1). Similarly, the ACP phosphodiesterase (PP_0922) and phosphopantetheinyltransferase (PP_1183) represent major CoA consumption/recycling nodes rather than biosynthetic enzymes, and their regulation in the context of CoA limitation merits further investigation.
