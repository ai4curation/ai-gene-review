# The NLR Signaling Pathway Module: Sensor Activation, Adaptor Assembly, and Inflammatory Output

## 1. Executive Summary

Nucleotide-binding leucine-rich repeat (NLR) receptors constitute a major family of intracellular pattern recognition receptors that detect microbial invasion and endogenous danger signals, transducing these inputs into inflammatory and antimicrobial responses. The mammalian NLR family comprises at least 22 genes, classified into five subfamilies (NLRA, NLRB, NLRC, NLRP, NLRX) based on N-terminal effector domain architecture (chou2023thenlrgene pages 1-3, zhou2023nodlikereceptorsignaling pages 2-4). This review focuses on two paradigmatic NLR signaling modules: (i) the NOD2–RIPK2 NODosome axis, which senses peptidoglycan-derived muramyl dipeptide (MDP) and activates NF-κB and MAPK transcriptional programs, and (ii) the NLRP3–ASC–caspase-1 inflammasome axis, which detects diverse intracellular perturbations and drives proteolytic maturation of IL-1β/IL-18 and gasdermin D–dependent pyroptosis. Though both modules share a conserved three-tiered logic—sensor activation → adaptor assembly → effector output—they employ distinct domain interactions (CARD–CARD versus PYD–PYD), recruit different adaptors (RIPK2 versus ASC), and generate fundamentally different outputs (transcriptional activation versus inflammatory protease cascades). Recent cryo-EM studies have provided atomic-resolution views of inactive NLRP3 cages, NEK7-licensed conformational transitions, and active inflammasome disc assemblies, while emerging work on post-translational regulation (S-acylation, ubiquitination) has refined our understanding of pathway gating. Key unresolved questions include the identity of the unifying NLRP3 activation signal, species-specific differences in NEK7 requirement, and the mechanistic basis of non-canonical and alternative inflammasome pathways.

## 2. Definition and Biological Boundaries

### 2.1 What the NLR Signaling Module Encompasses

The NLR signaling pathway module encompasses the intracellular detection of pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) by NLR family sensors, and the subsequent assembly of supramolecular signaling platforms—the NODosome and inflammasomes—that activate downstream inflammatory effectors. The human NLR family contains 22 members sharing a tripartite domain architecture: a variable N-terminal effector domain, a central NACHT domain (composed of NBD, HD1, WHD, and HD2 subdomains responsible for nucleotide binding and ATPase activity), and C-terminal leucine-rich repeats (LRRs) involved in ligand sensing and autoinhibition (chou2023thenlrgene pages 1-3, sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 4-6).

The five NLR subfamilies are distinguished by their N-terminal domains: NLRA (acidic transactivation domain; sole member CIITA), NLRB (BIR domains; NAIP), NLRC (CARD domains; including NOD1/NLRC1, NOD2/NLRC2, NLRC3-5), NLRP (pyrin domains; NLRP1-14), and NLRX (unclassified N-terminal domain; NLRX1) (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 41-45, almeidadasilva2023theroleof pages 3-5). Within this classification, NOD2 (NLRC2) belongs to the NLRC subfamily, bearing two tandem CARDs, while NLRP3 belongs to the NLRP subfamily, bearing a PYD (zhou2023nodlikereceptorsignaling pages 2-4, dixon2023multifacetedrolesand pages 1-2).

### 2.2 Boundaries and Distinctions

Several related processes should be distinguished from the core NLR signaling module:

- **Toll-like receptor (TLR) signaling** occurs at the plasma membrane or endosomal compartments and functions upstream, providing the priming signal (Signal 1) for inflammasome-competent NLRs such as NLRP3 (fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 1-3). TLR signaling per se is not part of the NLR module but is an obligatory input for canonical NLRP3 activation.

- **Non-NLR inflammasomes** such as AIM2 (dsDNA sensor) and pyrin detect distinct ligands through non-NLR sensor proteins but share the downstream ASC–caspase-1 axis (fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 1-3). These are often discussed alongside NLR inflammasomes but are architecturally distinct.

- **The NLRC4/NAIP inflammasome** represents a specialized NLR inflammasome branch that directly detects bacterial flagellin and type III secretion system components through NAIP co-receptor binding and can activate caspase-1 independently of ASC in certain contexts (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 41-45).

- **PANoptosis and innate immune cell death crosstalk**, involving caspase-8, RIPK3, and MLKL, intersect with inflammasome signaling but constitute a broader cell death regulatory network beyond the core NLR module (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 9-11).

- **NLR transcriptional regulators** (CIITA, NLRC5) modulate MHC expression rather than inflammatory signaling and represent a functionally distinct NLR arm (chou2023thenlrgene pages 1-3).

## 3. Mechanistic Overview

The core NLR signaling module operates through a three-step cascade: sensor activation, adaptor assembly, and inflammatory output. This logic is instantiated through two major parallel pathways.

### 3.1 The NOD2–RIPK2 NODosome Pathway

**Step 1: Sensor Activation.** NOD2 resides in the cytoplasm and at cellular membranes, where S-acylation by zDHHC5 at Cys395 and Cys1033 promotes plasma membrane and endosomal localization essential for peptidoglycan sensing (martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 6-7). Small Rho GTPases (Rac1, Cdc42) are required for NOD2 membrane recruitment (li2024nod1andnod2 pages 4-6). Upon binding MDP—which is phosphorylated by the host kinase NAGK to form phospho-MDP as the cellular substrate—NOD2 undergoes conformational changes and ATP-dependent self-oligomerization through its NBD and CARD domains, assembling into a 7–8 member oligomeric structure termed the NODosome (dixon2023multifacetedrolesand pages 2-4).

**Step 2: Adaptor Assembly.** Oligomerized NOD2 exposes its tandem CARDs, which engage the C-terminal CARD of RIPK2 through homotypic CARD–CARD interactions. This promotes RIPK2 polymerization into elongated filament complexes that expose the RIPK2 kinase domain (li2024nod1andnod2 pages 6-7). A critical regulatory checkpoint follows: RIPK2 undergoes K63-linked polyubiquitination by XIAP and linear M1-linked ubiquitination by LUBAC, which are essential for signal propagation. Additional E3 ligases including TRAF6, cIAP1/cIAP2, ITCH, and Pellino3 contribute to RIPK2 ubiquitination (dixon2023multifacetedrolesand pages 2-4, li2024nod1andnod2 pages 6-7, dixon2023multifacetedrolesand pages 10-11).

**Step 3: Inflammatory Signal Relay.** Polyubiquitinated RIPK2 recruits the TAK1–TAB2/TAB3 complex and LUBAC subsequently recruits NEMO, assembling the NEMO/IKK complex (guryanova2022regulationofimmune pages 7-8). TAK1 phosphorylates IKKβ, which in turn phosphorylates IκBα, targeting it for proteasomal degradation and freeing NF-κB for nuclear translocation and transcription of pro-inflammatory cytokines, chemokines, and antimicrobial peptides (dixon2023multifacetedrolesand pages 2-4). In parallel, RIPK2–TAK1 activates MAPK cascades (ERK, JNK, p38) through MKK6 phosphorylation (dixon2023multifacetedrolesand pages 2-4). Additionally, RIPK2 can engage TRAF3 and activate TBK1/IKKε to phosphorylate IRF3, inducing interferon gene expression (li2023mechanisticinsightsinto pages 19-21). Thus, the primary output is transcriptional rather than proteolytic.

### 3.2 The NLRP3–ASC–Caspase-1 Inflammasome Pathway

**Step 1: Sensor Activation (Two-Signal Model).** NLRP3 inflammasome activation follows a canonical biphasic model. Signal 1 (priming) involves TLR ligands, IL-1β, or TNFα stimulation, which activates NF-κB to upregulate expression of NLRP3 and pro-IL-1β. Priming also involves post-translational modifications including ubiquitination, phosphorylation, and sumoylation that maintain NLRP3 in an activation-competent but autoinhibited state (fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 1-3). Signal 2 (activation) is triggered by diverse stimuli including extracellular ATP, bacterial pore-forming toxins (nigericin), crystalline particles (MSU, CPPD), amyloid fibrils, and other perturbagens. The chemical diversity of these stimuli strongly suggests NLRP3 does not directly bind a specific ligand but rather senses a common downstream cellular disturbance, with K⁺ efflux being the most widely supported convergence signal (xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 3-4, walle2024druggingthenlrp3 pages 5-8).

Structurally, inactive NLRP3 forms "double-ring" cage structures through LRR–LRR interactions, with PYDs shielded inside to prevent premature activation (xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 3-4, fu2024mechanisticinsightsfrom pages 8-9). S-acylation at Cys130 and Cys958 by zDHHC enzymes drives NLRP3 localization to the trans-Golgi network (TGN), a prerequisite for inflammasome signaling (martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 4-5, martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 3-4). Upon activation, TGN vesicles carrying NLRP3 traffic along microtubules to the microtubule-organizing center (MTOC), where NEK7, a centrosomal kinase, binds NLRP3 through its C-terminal lobe contacting the NBD, HD2, and LRR domains (fu2024mechanisticinsightsfrom pages 8-9, fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 7-9). NEK7 binding disrupts the LRR–LRR interfaces maintaining the cage, inducing an ~85–90° rigid-body rotation within the NACHT domain that transitions NLRP3 from an ADP-bound closed state to an ATP-bound open, disc-like active conformation (fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 7-9, xu2024rotationandselfassembly pages 1-2). S-acylation at Cys837/838 by zDHHC5 further promotes the NEK7-dependent conformational change (martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 4-5).

**Step 2: Adaptor Assembly.** Cage opening exposes the NLRP3 PYD, which nucleates polymerization of ASC (PYCARD) through homotypic PYD–PYD interactions. ASC polymerizes into large filamentous assemblies that condense into a single perinuclear speck (~1 μm) per cell—the hallmark of inflammasome activation. The ASC CARD domain then recruits pro-caspase-1 through CARD–CARD interactions, nucleating caspase-1 filament formation (fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 1-3, fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 7-9). The active inflammasome disc comprises 10–11 NLRP3–NEK7 subunits forming flower-shaped structures with PYD filaments at the center (fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 7-9).

**Step 3: Inflammatory Protease Output.** Proximity-induced dimerization activates caspase-1, generating the p33/p10 active enzyme species (fu2024mechanisticinsightsfrom pages 8-9). Active caspase-1 cleaves three principal substrates: pro-IL-1β and pro-IL-18 into their mature, secreted forms, and gasdermin D (GSDMD) at its linker region, releasing the cytotoxic N-terminal domain (GSDMD-NT). GSDMD-NT oligomerizes in the plasma membrane to form pores that enable non-conventional secretion of mature IL-1β/IL-18 and, at higher levels, drive pyroptotic cell lysis (fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 1-3, walle2024druggingthenlrp3 pages 42-46).

## 4. Major Molecular Players and Active Assemblies

### 4.1 NOD2 (NLRC2/CARD15)

NOD2 is a CARD-containing NLR with the domain arrangement CARD×2–NACHT–LRR (padoan2023inflammationautoinflammationand pages 10-11, zhou2023nodlikereceptorsignaling pages 2-4). It is expressed primarily in myeloid cells and intestinal Paneth cells (chou2023thenlrgene pages 8-9). NOD2 directly binds MDP derived from both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacterial peptidoglycan. Mutations in the LRR domain abolish MDP detection and NF-κB activation (li2024nod1andnod2 pages 10-12). Beyond MDP sensing, NOD2 participates in ER stress-induced inflammation, autophagy initiation through ATG16L1 recruitment, and viral immune responses (dixon2023multifacetedrolesand pages 2-4, li2023mechanisticinsightsinto pages 19-21).

### 4.2 NLRP3 (Cryopyrin)

NLRP3 belongs to the NLRP subfamily with the architecture PYD–NACHT–LRR, and notably possesses a FISNA (fish-specific NACHT-associated) domain, a feature shared with NLRP12 (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 41-45). NLRP3 is broadly expressed in myeloid cells, microglia, and certain non-immune cells. It functions as an indirect sensor of cellular perturbation rather than a receptor for a specific ligand, responding to membrane damage, ionic flux, organelle stress, and metabolic disturbances that converge on common signals such as K⁺ efflux (xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 3-4, sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 9-11).

### 4.3 RIPK2 (RICK/CARDIAK)

RIPK2 is a CARD-containing serine/threonine kinase that serves as the obligate adaptor downstream of NOD1 and NOD2. Its kinase domain is N-terminal and its CARD is C-terminal, enabling homotypic CARD–CARD engagement with NOD sensors. While RIPK2 kinase activity contributes to signaling, the critical function of RIPK2 in this pathway is as a ubiquitin scaffold: K63-linked ubiquitination by XIAP and linear ubiquitination by LUBAC are essential for downstream TAK1/IKK complex recruitment and NF-κB/MAPK activation (dixon2023multifacetedrolesand pages 2-4, li2024nod1andnod2 pages 6-7).

### 4.4 ASC (PYCARD)

ASC is a bipartite adaptor containing an N-terminal PYD and a C-terminal CARD. It bridges PYD-containing NLR sensors (NLRP3, NLRP1, and others) to CARD-containing pro-caspase-1. ASC polymerizes into large filamentous specks that serve as supramolecular organizing centers for caspase-1 activation. Extracellular ASC specks released from pyroptotic cells can propagate inflammasome activation in neighboring cells, functioning as danger signals (fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 1-3, fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 7-9).

### 4.5 Caspase-1

Caspase-1 (formerly IL-1β-converting enzyme) is an inflammatory cysteine protease containing a CARD prodomain, a large (p20) catalytic subunit, and a small (p10) subunit. It is activated by proximity-induced dimerization on ASC CARD filaments within the inflammasome. Its three principal substrates—pro-IL-1β, pro-IL-18, and GSDMD—collectively mediate the inflammatory and pyroptotic outputs of the canonical inflammasome pathway (fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 1-3, walle2024druggingthenlrp3 pages 42-46).

### 4.6 NEK7

NEK7 (NIMA-related kinase 7) is a centrosomal serine/threonine kinase that functions as a licensing co-factor for NLRP3 activation. Its C-terminal kinase lobe contacts multiple domains of NLRP3 (NBD, HD2, LRR), disrupting the inactive cage and enabling the conformational transition to the active disc (fu2024mechanisticinsightsfrom pages 8-9, fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 7-9). NEK7's dual role in mitosis and inflammasome activation creates a cell-cycle checkpoint: cells in mitosis have limited NEK7 availability for NLRP3 activation, potentially coupling cell division and inflammation (xu2024rotationandselfassembly pages 1-2).

## 5. Evolutionary and Cell-Biological Variation

### 5.1 Evolutionary Origins

NLR-like genes are present across remarkably diverse lineages, from bacteria and archaea through algae, fungi, plants, and animals, suggesting an ancient origin for the NACHT-containing sensor architecture (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 41-45). The NACHT domain belongs to the STAND (signal transduction ATPases with numerous domains) superfamily of P-loop NTPases, which is among the most ancient signaling domain families. In plants, NLR (NB-LRR) proteins function as resistance (R) genes in effector-triggered immunity, operating through a conceptually analogous sensor-activation logic but with different downstream outputs (hypersensitive response rather than inflammasome-mediated cytokine processing). Evolutionary modeling suggests NLR networks evolved from multifunctional ancestors through functional specialization, with ancestral NLRs serving dual sensor-and-helper roles before diversifying into specialized receptor and adaptor functions (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 41-45).

In marine invertebrates, NLR orthologs exist in cnidarians, echinoderms, and molluscs, though their signaling outputs are less well characterized than in vertebrates (guryanova2022innateimmunitymechanisms pages 2-5). Teleost fish possess expanded NLR repertoires with functional inflammasome components, including ASC and caspase-1 orthologs. The FISNA domain, found in mammalian NLRP3 and NLRP12, derives its name from its initial identification in fish NLRs, suggesting these proteins retain an ancient structural feature (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 41-45).

### 5.2 Cell-Type and Context-Dependent Variation

NLRP3 inflammasome activation varies substantially across cell types. In human monocytes, extracellular LPS alone (through TLR4–TRIF–caspase-8) can achieve NLRP3 activation without a separate Signal 2, representing an "alternative" activation pathway distinct from the canonical two-signal model used in murine macrophages (walle2024druggingthenlrp3 pages 5-8). Neutrophils exhibit inflammasome activation with distinct kinetics and outcomes compared to macrophages, including ASC speck transfer between cell types. Inherently reduced ASC expression in hepatocytes restricts caspase-1 processing in the liver, limiting pyroptosis in that compartment.

NOD2 expression is most prominent in myeloid cells and Paneth cells, while NOD1 has broader tissue distribution including epithelial cells (chou2023thenlrgene pages 8-9). This differential expression reflects their distinct roles: NOD2 functions primarily at sites of direct microbial encounter (intestinal crypts, macrophages), while NOD1 provides more ubiquitous surveillance.

### 5.3 Alternative Routes

Beyond canonical NLRP3 activation, non-canonical inflammasome pathways involve caspases 4/5 (human) or caspase-11 (mouse), which directly sense intracellular LPS and cleave GSDMD to form pores, secondarily activating NLRP3 through K⁺ dissipation (walle2024druggingthenlrp3 pages 5-8). The NLRC4/NAIP inflammasome achieves similar caspase-1 activation through a distinct sensor mechanism that does not require ASC for caspase-1 activation, though ASC enhances signaling amplification. NLRP1, CARD8, and NLRP6 represent additional inflammasome-forming NLRs with distinct activation triggers (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 9-11).

## 6. Constraints, Dependencies, and Failure Modes

### 6.1 Obligatory Ordering

Several steps in both NLR pathways must occur in a defined sequence:

- **NOD2 pathway**: MDP binding → NOD2 oligomerization → RIPK2 CARD–CARD recruitment → RIPK2 ubiquitination → TAK1/IKK complex assembly → NF-κB/MAPK activation. RIPK2 ubiquitination by XIAP is an obligatory checkpoint; mutations in XIAP-dependent ubiquitination sites diminish NOD2 signaling (li2024nod1andnod2 pages 6-7).

- **NLRP3 pathway**: Priming (Signal 1) → NLRP3 upregulation and PTM competence → Activation signal (Signal 2) → K⁺ efflux → TGN dispersal → MTOC trafficking → NEK7 licensing → cage opening → PYD exposure → ASC nucleation → caspase-1 activation → substrate cleavage (xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 3-4, fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 1-3, fu2023structuralmechanismsof pages 7-9, fu2024mechanisticinsightsfrom pages 29-33).

### 6.2 Mutual Exclusivity and Compartment Specificity

NEK7 serves dual roles in mitosis (centrosome function) and NLRP3 activation, creating temporal mutual exclusivity between cell division and inflammasome assembly (xu2024rotationandselfassembly pages 1-2). NLRP3 S-acylation by different zDHHC enzymes directs mutually exclusive fates: zDHHC5-mediated S-acylation at Cys837/838 promotes activation, while zDHHC12-mediated modification targets NLRP3 for chaperone-mediated autophagic degradation, functioning as an emergency brake against aberrant inflammation (martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 5-6, martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 4-5).

NOD2 membrane localization through S-acylation is essential for signaling competence; soluble deacylated NOD2 is ubiquitinated and targeted for autophagic degradation via the SQSTM1/p62 cargo receptor, establishing a binary on/off switch between signaling-competent and degradation-committed states (martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 6-7).

### 6.3 Failure Modes and Disease

Loss-of-function NOD2 mutations (particularly three common frameshift and missense variants in the LRR domain) are the strongest genetic risk factors for Crohn's disease, presumably through impaired antimicrobial defense and intestinal immune homeostasis (chou2023thenlrgene pages 8-9). Conversely, gain-of-function NOD2 mutations cause Blau syndrome and early-onset sarcoidosis through constitutive NF-κB activation (chou2023thenlrgene pages 8-9, martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 1-1). Gain-of-function NLRP3 variants cause cryopyrin-associated periodic syndromes (CAPS), characterized by constitutive inflammasome activation and excessive IL-1β production (walle2024druggingthenlrp3 pages 42-46, xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 3-4). XIAP deficiency disrupts NOD2–RIPK2 ubiquitination-dependent signaling, predisposing to inflammatory bowel disease and hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (li2024nod1andnod2 pages 6-7).

## 7. Controversies and Open Questions

### 7.1 The Unifying NLRP3 Activation Signal

Despite decades of study, "a single, unifying mechanism of activation for NLRP3 remains unclear" (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 9-11). K⁺ efflux is the most widely supported convergence point, but NLRP3 activation can occur independently of K⁺ efflux in certain contexts, and the precise molecular mechanism by which low intracellular K⁺ triggers NLRP3 conformational change has been described as "a biological mystery" (dai2023inflammasomestructurebiological pages 2-4). Whether NLRP3 functions as a direct sensor or as a node within a broader innate immune sensing network remains debated (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 9-11).

### 7.2 Species-Specific Differences in NEK7 Requirement

NEK7 is required for NLRP3 inflammasome activation in murine macrophages, but in human macrophages, NEK7 appears to function primarily in priming rather than activation. Signaling through IKKβ can circumvent the NEK7 requirement in human cells (sundaram2024thenlrfamily pages 9-11, xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 1-3). Furthermore, NLRP11, an NLR family member absent in mice, was identified as a required component for NLRP3 activation specifically in human macrophages, highlighting important species differences that complicate extrapolation from mouse models (xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 1-3).

### 7.3 The LRR Domain Dispensability Paradox

Structural studies demonstrate that LRR–LRR interactions maintain the inactive NLRP3 cage, and mutations at the dimerization interface cause auto-activation (xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 3-4). Yet two independent reports show that NLRP3 constructs lacking the LRR domain retain similar function to full-length protein. This contradiction remains unexplained and raises questions about the autoinhibitory model derived from cryo-EM structures (xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 3-4).

### 7.4 Structural Diversity of Inactive NLRP3

Human NLRP3 forms decameric cage structures while mouse NLRP3 forms 12–16-meric assemblies, though whether this reflects genuine species differences or technical variability in sample preparation is unresolved (xu2023thenlrp3inflammasome pages 3-4).

### 7.5 Pyroptosis-Cytokine Uncoupling

Pyroptosis and cytokine secretion, once considered obligately coupled downstream of inflammasome activation, can be uncoupled in a stimulus-, cell-type-, or context-dependent manner. "Hyperactive" cells can release IL-1β through sublytic GSDMD pores without dying, while in other contexts GSDMD-mediated cell death occurs without substantial IL-1β secretion (walle2024druggingthenlrp3 pages 5-8). The mechanistic basis for this uncoupling, particularly the regulation of caspase-1 dynamics that generate different active species with varying substrate preferences, remains an active area of investigation.

### 7.6 Post-Translational Regulation Complexity

The discovery that S-acylation by different zDHHC enzymes can direct the same NLR protein toward either activation or degradation (as shown for NLRP3 with zDHHC5 versus zDHHC12) reveals an underappreciated regulatory layer (martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 5-6, martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 4-5). How the cell coordinates these opposing modifications in space and time, and whether similar regulatory logic applies to other NLR family members, are important unresolved questions (martin2025straighta’sprotein pages 1-1).
