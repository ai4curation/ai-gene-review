## Research report: Rat **Cfd** (UniProt P32038) — Complement factor D (adipsin)

### 1) Target verification and gene/protein identity

**Scope anchoring.** The user-specified target is **Rattus norvegicus Cfd** encoding **Complement factor D (CFD)**, also known as **adipsin**. In the retrieved 2023–2024 literature, *CFD*, *factor D*, and *adipsin* are consistently used as synonyms for the same secreted ~24 kDa serine protease that is the rate-limiting enzyme of the alternative complement pathway (AP). (kong2024roleofcomplement pages 1-2, schubart2023low‐molecularweightinhibitors pages 6-7)

**Accession-level constraint.** None of the retrieved papers explicitly mentions the UniProt accession **P32038**, so accession-level confirmation cannot be demonstrated from the retrieved corpus. However, the conserved biochemical definition of CFD/adipsin (secreted trypsin-like serine protease; AP rate-limiting factor B convertase activator) matches the UniProt-provided description, and no conflicting “Cfd” gene/protein usage was identified in rat within the retrieved materials. (kong2024roleofcomplement pages 1-2, schubart2023low‐molecularweightinhibitors pages 6-7)

### 2) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

#### 2.1 Canonical complement role: alternative pathway C3 convertase formation

**Functional definition.** Complement factor D (CFD) is the **essential serine protease** that drives formation of the alternative pathway C3 convertase by **cleaving complement factor B (FB/CFB)** *only after* factor B binds to C3b (surface) or hydrolyzed C3 (fluid-phase C3(H2O)). This cleavage produces **Ba and Bb** fragments and yields the active convertases **C3bBb** (surface) and **C3(H2O)Bb** (fluid). (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 1-2, kong2024roleofcomplement pages 3-5)

**Rate-limiting step.** Reviews emphasize that factor D activity is rate-limiting for AP C3 convertase generation, such that changes in CFD abundance or activity modulate AP output. (kong2024roleofcomplement pages 3-5, west2024complementinhuman pages 4-6)

#### 2.2 Substrate specificity: why CFD is “safe” in circulation

**Defining biochemical property.** CFD exhibits extreme substrate preference: it cleaves factor B efficiently only in the **C3bB (pro-convertase) complex**, and activity on **free factor B** is negligible. A 2023 enzymology study quantified that **C3bB is ~20,000,000-fold better substrate than free FB**, with measured pseudo–second order apparent efficiencies (kobs/[E]T) of **6.5 × 10^6 M−1 s−1** (C3bB) vs **0.3 M−1 s−1** (free FB). (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 8-10, dani2023quantificationofthe media dfd60c1e)

**Mechanistic model.** The large preference can be understood as a **two-step enhancement**: (i) factor B becomes more cleavage-susceptible in an “open” conformation upon binding C3b (reported ~100-fold), and (ii) factor D itself undergoes **substrate-induced activation** upon binding the C3bB complex (reported additional ~2×10^5-fold), yielding a very large overall effect. (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 10-11)

#### 2.3 CFD maturation: zymogen processing by MASP-3

**Zymogen concept.** CFD is synthesized as **pro-factor D (pro-FD)** and is converted to mature factor D in blood primarily by **MASP-3** (mannan-binding lectin–associated serine protease-3), positioning MASP-3 as an upstream controller of AP readiness. (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 2-3, dani2023quantificationofthe pages 10-11)

**Specificity of MASP-3.** In the same 2023 study, MASP-3 showed essentially no direct cleavage of FB or C3bB under conditions where it efficiently functions as a pro-FD maturase, supporting a model in which MASP-3’s relevant AP role is predominantly **pro-FD processing** rather than direct factor B activation. (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 8-10, dani2023quantificationofthe pages 7-8)

#### 2.4 Residual pro-FD activity (zymogen is not fully inert)

A notable 2023 result is that pro-FD can have measurable catalytic activity toward C3bB. The reported **zymogenicity** (activity difference) was about **800-fold**, i.e., the pro-FD variant cleaved C3bB ~800× more slowly than mature FD. Functionally, supraphysiologic pro-FD concentrations could partially restore AP activity in FD-depleted serum, consistent with the possibility of residual AP function even when pro-FD maturation is impaired. (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 1-2, dani2023quantificationofthe pages 10-11)

### 3) Localization and expression context (where CFD acts)

#### 3.1 Extracellular localization: plasma and tissue interstitial spaces

CFD’s canonical AP function occurs **extracellularly**, particularly in **plasma** and extracellular tissue compartments where C3bB complexes form. A 2023 review summarizes factor D as a circulating ~24 kDa protease with a reported **plasma half-life ~1 hour** and **steady-state concentration ~2 μg/mL (~80 nM)**, with strong dependence on renal handling. (schubart2023low‐molecularweightinhibitors pages 6-7)

#### 3.2 Tissue sources and regulation

**Adipose as a major source.** CFD is widely described as being produced predominantly by **adipocytes**, explaining the historical adipokine name **adipsin**. Additional sources discussed in the literature include immune cells (e.g., monocytes/macrophages) and tissue-specific local production. (nezhadUnknownyeartheroleof pages 23-26, kong2024roleofcomplement pages 1-2)

**Renal control of circulating levels.** Reviews highlight that CFD levels can increase markedly with reduced renal function (e.g., >10-fold increase in renal impairment/end-stage renal failure), which is important when interpreting circulating CFD/adipsin as a biomarker or driver of pathology. (schubart2023low‐molecularweightinhibitors pages 6-7, kong2024roleofcomplement pages 1-2)

**Example of local production: kidney endothelium.** In malignant nephrosclerosis, a 2023 study reports that glomerular endothelial cells express and secrete CFD; CFD knockdown in endothelial cells reduced local complement activation and attenuated Ang II–induced upregulation of endothelial dysfunction markers (ICAM-1, VCAM-1, VWF, endothelin-1), supporting the concept of **tissue-local extracellular CFD function** beyond liver-derived plasma complement alone. (zhang2024adipsinimprovesdiabetic pages 1-2)

### 4) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

#### 4.1 Quantitative enzymology clarifying “zymogen vs active” and specificity (2023)

The 2023 Frontiers in Immunology study provides unusually quantitative measurements of CFD’s unique biology: near-diffusion-limited apparent efficiency on C3bB, extreme discrimination against free FB (20 million-fold), measurable pro-FD activity, and experimental support for MASP-3’s role as the physiological pro-FD maturase in blood. These results sharpen functional annotation for mammalian CFD (including rat Cfd) by turning qualitative pathway diagrams into quantitative constraints relevant to physiology and drug discovery. (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 8-10, dani2023quantificationofthe pages 1-2)

#### 4.2 Therapeutic targeting: factor D as a major drug target (2023–2024)

**Authoritative review perspective.** A high-impact 2024 review in *The Lancet* positions factor D (adipsin) as a key alternative-pathway drug target alongside factor B, and notes that oral small-molecule inhibitors of these proteases are in advanced development for complement-mediated diseases. It also highlights a pharmacologic challenge: very little residual active factor D may be sufficient for factor B activation (reported on the order of **~1–2%** active FD sufficiency), implying the need for deep inhibition to fully suppress the pathway. (west2024complementinhuman pages 4-6)

**Drug discovery landscape.** A 2023 *Immunological Reviews* article summarizes key aspects of factor D biology relevant to inhibitor development (including plasma concentration, half-life, and renal dependence) and reviews progress toward potent selective orally bioavailable inhibitors. (schubart2023low‐molecularweightinhibitors pages 6-7, schubart2023low‐molecularweightinhibitors pages 1-2)

#### 4.3 Noncanonical/tissue roles and biomarkers (2023–2024)

**Tendon repair biomarker/mechanism (2023).** A 2023 human tendon-healing study used quantitative mass spectrometry profiling and reported that CFD was associated with good outcomes in a phase-dependent manner, and proposed mechanisms involving fibroblast migration and collagen I regulation. CFD performance as an inflammatory-stage prognostic marker was reported with **AUC = 0.7**, with cohorts including **40** biopsy patients and **28** microdialysis patients. (chen2023complementfactord pages 4-5)

**Type 2 diabetes biomarker study (2024).** A 2024 study evaluated circulating adipsin as a biomarker of β-cell function in T2D and reported group differences: **3.91 ± 1.51 μg/mL** (T2D with insulin) vs **5.11 ± 1.53 μg/mL** (T2D without insulin; **p < 0.001**) and **4.53 ± 1.15 μg/mL** (normoglycemia). The study also reported statistically significant associations between adipsin and glycemic and C-peptide indices (correlations and regression models). (park2024identificationofadipsin pages 1-2, park2024identificationofadipsin pages 5-7)

**Cardiometabolic disease synthesis (2024).** A 2024 review focusing on cardiovascular and metabolic diseases summarizes evidence linking circulating CFD to cardiometabolic risk, including an association where each **1 SD increase** in plasma CFD corresponded to a **15% higher CVD incidence** (as summarized in the review), and emphasizes that renal dysfunction can strongly raise circulating CFD levels. (kong2024roleofcomplement pages 1-2)

### 5) Current applications and real-world implementations

1. **Functional readout of alternative pathway competence.** Because CFD is rate-limiting and highly substrate-constrained, CFD status is central to interpreting AP activity in serum-based functional assays and to understanding consequences of upstream MASP-3 modulation. (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 8-10, dani2023quantificationofthe pages 10-11)

2. **Biomarker development (translational).** Circulating adipsin/CFD is being evaluated as a biomarker in metabolic disease (e.g., T2D β-cell function) and in tissue repair settings (tendon healing), with reported effect sizes, AUC, and statistically significant group differences in recent human cohorts. (park2024identificationofadipsin pages 1-2, chen2023complementfactord pages 4-5)

3. **Therapeutic targeting (precision complement modulation).** Expert reviews in 2023–2024 emphasize that oral AP inhibitors targeting factor D (and factor B) are a major translational direction for complement-mediated diseases, motivated by the centrality of the factor D–factor B protease node to AP amplification. (west2024complementinhuman pages 4-6, schubart2023low‐molecularweightinhibitors pages 1-2)

### 6) Expert opinions and analysis (authoritative sources)

**Consensus view of CFD as a drug target.** The 2024 *Lancet* review frames complement therapeutics as an expanding, clinically mature area and highlights alternative pathway proteases (factor B and factor D) as high-value targets enabling oral therapy modalities and upstream pathway control. (west2024complementinhuman pages 4-6)

**Drug-discovery challenges and constraints.** The 2023 *Immunological Reviews* paper emphasizes that factor D biology creates unusual constraints for inhibitors: low steady-state concentration, dependence on renal function, and very high catalytic leverage (i.e., small amounts of active FD may sustain AP activity), which in turn shapes potency/selectivity requirements and dosing targets. (schubart2023low‐molecularweightinhibitors pages 6-7)

### Conclusions for rat Cfd (P32038) functional annotation

**Primary molecular function.** Rat CFD (Cfd/adipsin; UniProt P32038) should be annotated as a **secreted trypsin-like serine endopeptidase** of the alternative complement pathway that **cleaves factor B only efficiently when factor B is complexed with C3b/C3(H2O)**, enabling formation of the alternative pathway C3 convertase (C3bBb or C3(H2O)Bb). (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 1-2, kong2024roleofcomplement pages 3-5)

**Substrate specificity.** A key functional hallmark is **multi–order of magnitude preference** for C3b-bound factor B over free factor B (quantified as ~20 million-fold in human biochemical assays), which explains why CFD can circulate without indiscriminately cleaving factor B. (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 8-10, dani2023quantificationofthe media dfd60c1e)

**Localization.** The protein acts **extracellularly** in plasma and tissue microenvironments, with adipose tissue a major source and evidence for additional local tissue production (e.g., endothelium in kidney pathology). (schubart2023low‐molecularweightinhibitors pages 6-7, zhang2024adipsinimprovesdiabetic pages 1-2)

**Regulation and translational relevance.** MASP-3-mediated maturation, renal clearance, and emerging roles in tissue remodeling and metabolism position CFD as both a mechanistic node in innate immunity and an active translational target for oral complement therapeutics and biomarker development. (dani2023quantificationofthe pages 10-11, west2024complementinhuman pages 4-6, park2024identificationofadipsin pages 1-2)
