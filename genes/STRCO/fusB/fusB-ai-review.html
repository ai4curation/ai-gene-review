Gene Description

Elongation factor G 2 (EF-G 2) is a paralog of canonical EF-G (fusA/EF-G1). In bacteria with duplicated EF-G genes, the EF-G2 paralog typically specializes in ribosome recycling (disassembly of post-termination ribosome complexes) rather than canonical translational elongation. EF-G2 retains the structural GTPase fold and GTP-binding capability of the EF-G family but may lack ribosome-dependent GTPase activity, as demonstrated for EF-G2 paralogs in Borrelia, Bacteroides, and Mycobacterium. The PANTHER subfamily classification (PTHR43261:SF1, Ribosome-releasing factor 2) supports a ribosome recycling function. In S. coelicolor, fusB is part of the WblC/WhiB7 regulon and is induced by translation-targeting antibiotics such as lincomycin [PMID:32291305, PMID:37732750], suggesting a role in maintaining translation capacity under antibiotic stress.

0) Target verification and disambiguation (critical)

Verified target: The requested protein is UniProt O87844, annotated as elongation factor G 2 (EF‑G2) encoded by fusB, ordered locus SCO6589, in Streptomyces coelicolor strain M145/A3(2). This assignment is consistent with S. coelicolor primary literature describing fusB as encoding EF‑G and listing fusB (SCO6589) as an EF‑G paralog within an antibiotic-stress regulon. (mukai2023phenomenologicalinterpretationsof pages 2-5, lee2020thewblcwhib7transcription pages 10-12)

Why “fusB” is ambiguous in the wider literature: In many Gram-positive pathogens, “FusB” refers to a small (~25 kDa) two-domain fusidic-acid resistance protein that binds EF‑G and promotes release of fusidic-acid–stalled EF‑G from the ribosome; this FusB resistance protein is not an EF‑G-family TRAFAC GTPase. (starosta2014thebacterialtranslation pages 18-19, wasuna2018repositioningfusidicacid pages 153-157, starosta2014thebacterialtranslation pages 1-2)

Therefore, for S. coelicolor fusB/SCO6589/O87844, the correct interpretation is an EF‑G paralog (EF‑G2), not a fusidic-acid resistance factor of the FusB-family. (mukai2023phenomenologicalinterpretationsof pages 2-5, lee2020thewblcwhib7transcription pages 10-12)

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 EF‑G as a TRAFAC translation-factor GTPase

Elongation factor G (EF‑G) is a conserved translational GTPase (bacterial counterpart of archaeal/eukaryotic EF2/eEF2) that drives ribosomal translocation of tRNA and mRNA during elongation. (fang2024thestructuraland pages 6-8, fang2024thestructuraland pages 1-2)

EF‑G proteins have a five-domain architecture (G/I domain plus domains II–V). The G domain binds and hydrolyzes GTP; domains III–V are EF2-family specific; and EF‑G participates in both elongation and ribosome recycling (with RRF). (fang2024thestructuraland pages 8-11, atkinson2011evolutionofelongation pages 1-2)

1.2 EF‑G in elongation (mechanistic definition)

During elongation, EF‑G promotes codon-by-codon translocation: movement of the ribosome one codon toward the 3′ end of mRNA and shifting peptidyl‑tRNA from the A-site to the P-site. (fang2024thestructuraland pages 1-2)

A mechanistic synthesis (with structural emphasis) indicates that EF‑G uses GTP hydrolysis to coordinate ribosomal motions (e.g., 30S head movements) and to ensure faithful, full-codon translocation before EF‑G release. (fang2024thestructuraland pages 6-8)

1.3 EF‑G in ribosome recycling and rescue pathways

EF‑G also contributes to ribosome recycling by interacting with ribosome recycling factor (RRF) to help dissociate post-termination ribosomal complexes and recycle subunits. (fang2024thestructuraland pages 8-11, atkinson2011evolutionofelongation pages 1-2)

Additionally, EF‑G can function in trans‑translation, helping translocate the tmRNA–SmpB module into the P-site. (fang2024thestructuraland pages 8-11)

1.4 EF‑G paralogs (EF‑G1 vs EF‑G2 concept)

While many bacteria carry a single canonical EF‑G (often fusA in the str operon), paralogs exist. EF‑G is typically encoded in the conserved bacterial str operon (5′‑rpsL‑rpsG‑fusA‑tufA‑3′). (atkinson2011evolutionofelongation pages 1-2)

A fusidic-acid–focused mechanistic source notes that an EF‑G2 paralog exists in some bacteria and that EF‑G participates in both elongation and recycling; EF‑G2 can show divergent activity across species/conditions. (wasuna2018repositioningfusidicacid pages 149-153)

Evolutionary analysis reports that Streptomyces coelicolor possesses its expected str‑encoded EF‑G plus an additional non‑str–encoded EF‑G copy in a distinct clade (strepEFG) near EF‑G2-like groupings, supporting the plausibility of an EF‑G2-like paralog in this genus. (atkinson2011evolutionofelongation pages 7-8)

2) Functional annotation of S. coelicolor fusB (SCO6589; EF‑G2)

2.1 Primary function

The strongest organism-specific evidence supports annotating S. coelicolor fusB (SCO6589) as encoding an EF‑G paralog involved in translation elongation and/or ribosome recycling as part of the core translation-factor repertoire. (lee2020thewblcwhib7transcription pages 10-12)

In S. coelicolor A3(2), fusB is explicitly described as encoding the translation elongation factor EF‑G (with tuf3 encoding EF‑Tu) in the context of antibiotic-stress-induced expression. (mukai2023phenomenologicalinterpretationsof pages 2-5)

Biochemical reaction/mechanism (inferred from EF‑G family): EF‑G catalyzes ribosome-dependent GTP hydrolysis and converts the chemical energy into mechanical work that drives translocation and supports ribosome recycling. (wasuna2018repositioningfusidicacid pages 149-153, fang2024thestructuraland pages 6-8)

2.2 Domains and family consistency

EF‑G proteins’ five-domain architecture provides a coherent explanation for UniProt’s listing of EF‑G family domains and for classification as a TRAFAC class translation-factor GTPase. (fang2024thestructuraland pages 8-11, atkinson2011evolutionofelongation pages 1-2)

By contrast, the FusB resistance proteins are described as small (~25 kDa) two-domain proteins with a zinc-binding fold and are functionally characterized as auxiliary rescue factors rather than core translation GTPases; this mismatch supports the conclusion that S. coelicolor fusB is not the resistance-protein FusB. (wasuna2018repositioningfusidicacid pages 153-157)

3) Cellular localization

EF‑G is a ribosome-associated cytosolic translation factor, binding the 70S ribosome (including contacts with rRNA and ribosomal proteins) during elongation and recycling. This is consistent with structural studies of ribosome•EF‑G complexes, where EF‑G occupies ribosomal functional sites and interfaces with both subunits. (gonzalezlopez2024structuresofthe pages 1-2, gonzalezlopez2024structuresofthe pages 2-3)

Thus, S. coelicolor EF‑G2 (FusB/SCO6589) is most plausibly localized to the cytoplasm, functioning on ribosomes.

4) Pathways and regulatory context in Streptomyces (gene-specific evidence)

4.1 WblC/WhiB7-controlled translation-stress response

In S. coelicolor, the transcription factor WblC/WhiB7 controls intrinsic resistance to translation-targeting antibiotics by activating genes that support translation under antibiotic stress; in this regulon, fusB (SCO6589) is listed as an EF‑G paralog. (lee2020thewblcwhib7transcription pages 10-12)

A 2023 study on lincomycin’s concentration-dependent effects reports that 5 µg/mL lincomycin promoted “high but transient expression” of WblC regulon mRNAs including fusB and tuf3, which the authors identify as encoding EF‑G and EF‑Tu. This links fusB/EF‑G2 to a real-world antibiotic-stress condition and a defined regulatory module in S. coelicolor. (mukai2023phenomenologicalinterpretationsof pages 2-5)

5) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

5.1 2023: Dynamics of EF‑G binding and ribosome rotation

Single-molecule and binding experiments support a model where EF‑G stabilizes the rotated (hybrid) ribosome but can bind both rotated and non-rotated conformations. Quantitative binding values reported include apparent KDs of ~14–16 nM for some pretranslocation complexes, weakening to ~31–66 nM with peptidyl-tRNA, and an apparent KD of ~5 nM when viomycin stabilizes the rotated state. These results refine mechanistic understanding that is relevant for interpreting how EF‑G paralogs might operate under different ribosome states in vivo. (das2023interplaybetweenintersubunit pages 4-7, das2023interplaybetweenintersubunit pages 7-9)

5.2 2024: Near-atomic cryo‑EM structures of EF‑G inhibited by fusidic acid (FA)

High-resolution cryo‑EM structures of the Staphylococcus aureus ribosome bound to EF‑G and inhibited by fusidic acid (FA) and an FA derivative (FA‑cyclopentane) were solved at 2.5 Å (FA) and 2.0 Å (FA‑CP) resolution. FA binds a pocket formed between EF‑G domains I–III and the 23S rRNA sarcin–ricin loop, stabilizing an EF‑G conformation that prevents EF‑G release after GTP hydrolysis. The FA‑CP derivative binds the same pocket with additional contacts, providing structure-based rationale for improved binding/robustness against resistance mutations. (gonzalezlopez2024structuresofthe pages 1-2, gonzalezlopez2024structuresofthe pages 3-4)

Although these structures are not from Streptomyces, they are directly relevant because they define a conserved EF‑G antibiotic-binding site and conformational trapping mechanism that can inform EF‑G family functional annotation and drug-targeting considerations. 6) Current applications and real-world implementations

6.1 Translation factors as antimicrobial drug targets (EF‑G and FA)

EF‑G is a clinically relevant antibiotic target: fusidic acid inhibits translation by binding EF‑G and preventing its release after GTP hydrolysis/translocation. The structural definition of the FA pocket at near-atomic resolution provides a basis for structure-based antibiotic derivative design (e.g., FA‑cyclopentane). (gonzalezlopez2024structuresofthe pages 1-2, gonzalezlopez2024structuresofthe pages 7-9)

6.2 Antibiotic-stress modulation of translation in Streptomyces (biotechnology/ecology relevance)

In S. coelicolor, sub-inhibitory translation-targeting antibiotics can alter ribosome function and secondary metabolism. The observation that lincomycin induces WblC and WblC-regulon translation factors including fusB/EF‑G implies a real-world mechanism by which Streptomyces may maintain translation capacity and reprogram metabolism under antibiotic exposure—relevant both to natural ecology and to industrial manipulation of secondary metabolite production. (mukai2023phenomenologicalinterpretationsof pages 2-5, lee2020thewblcwhib7transcription pages 10-12)

8) Expert interpretation and synthesis (authoritative sources)

Mechanistic consensus: EF‑G’s essential biochemical role is the coupling of GTP hydrolysis to ribosome translocation and recycling; structural/mechanistic reviews emphasize domain architecture and dual roles in elongation and recycling. (fang2024thestructuraland pages 6-8, atkinson2011evolutionofelongation pages 1-2)

Gene-specific synthesis for S. coelicolor: The most direct S. coelicolor evidence supports fusB (SCO6589) as an EF‑G paralog mobilized under translation stress via WblC/WhiB7 regulation; thus EF‑G2 may function as a stress-responsive translation factor**, potentially helping maintain translation under ribosome-targeting antibiotics or during late growth phases when ribosome maintenance becomes limiting. This interpretation is consistent with WblC’s role in maintaining translation rate under antibiotic stress and with lincomycin’s induction of fusB transcripts. (lee2020thewblcwhib7transcription pages 10-12, mukai2023phenomenologicalinterpretationsof pages 2-5)

9) Evidence gaps and limits of current annotation

Despite strong identity disambiguation and regulatory-context evidence, the retrieved corpus did not provide direct SCO6589/EF‑G2 biochemical characterization (e.g., purified-protein GTPase kinetics, direct assays of translocation vs recycling specificity) nor gene knockout/essentiality phenotypes specific to SCO6589. These remain open items for definitive functional specialization beyond “EF‑G paralog.” (mukai2023phenomenologicalinterpretationsof pages 2-5, lee2020thewblcwhib7transcription pages 10-12)

Key Biological Context

fusB is one of two EF-G paralogs in S. coelicolor. In bacteria that have undergone EF-G gene duplication, the two paralogs typically partition the two canonical functions of EF-G:
- EF-G1 (fusA): canonical translational elongation (mRNA-tRNA translocation)
- EF-G2 (fusB): ribosome recycling / disassembly

Evidence from EF-G2 paralogs in other organisms

  1. Borrelia burgdorferi PMID:20132446: Two EF-G paralogs divide labor - EF-G1 for translocation, EF-G2 exclusively for ribosome recycling. EF-G2 does not require GTP hydrolysis for ribosome disassembly when IF-3 is present.

  2. Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron PMID:36472247: EF-G2 supports translocation WITHOUT GTP hydrolysis. Accumulates during carbon starvation. Contains a unique 26-residue region responsible for absence of GTPase activity.

  3. Mycobacterium smegmatis PMID:40569974: EF-G2 lacks ribosome-dependent GTPase activity. Two copies of EF-G2 can bind the 50S subunit, preventing 70S formation. Acts as anti-associator during starvation.

  4. Human mitochondria (GFM2/EF-G2, Q969S9): Mitochondrial ribosome-releasing factor that mediates ribosome disassembly during translation termination. Works with MRRF.

IBA annotation basis

The IBA for GO:0032790 (ribosome disassembly) is based on PANTHER:PTN000754007 and human GFM2 (Q969S9). This is phylogenetically well-supported - the PANTHER subfamily SF1 specifically corresponds to ribosome-releasing factors.

Annotation Assessment Summary

The key insight is that EF-G2 paralogs have functionally diverged from canonical EF-G1. The IEA annotations for "translation elongation factor activity" and "translational elongation" are based on domain-level matches to the broader EF-G family but may not accurately reflect EF-G2's specialized function. The IBA annotation for ribosome disassembly is likely the most accurate functional annotation.

Similarly, GTPase activity is questionable for EF-G2 - multiple studies show EF-G2 paralogs lack ribosome-dependent GTPase activity, though they retain GTP binding capability.

description: >-
  Elongation factor G 2 (EF-G 2) is a paralog of canonical EF-G (fusA/EF-G1). In bacteria
  with duplicated EF-G genes, the EF-G2 paralog typically specializes in ribosome recycling
  (disassembly of post-termination ribosome complexes) rather than canonical translational
  elongation. EF-G2 retains the structural GTPase fold and GTP-binding capability of the
  EF-G family but may lack ribosome-dependent GTPase activity, as demonstrated for EF-G2
  paralogs in Borrelia, Bacteroides, and Mycobacterium. The PANTHER subfamily classification
  (PTHR43261:SF1, Ribosome-releasing factor 2) supports a ribosome recycling function. In
  S. coelicolor, fusB is part of the WblC/WhiB7 regulon and is induced by translation-targeting
  antibiotics such as lincomycin [PMID:32291305, PMID:37732750], suggesting a role in
  maintaining translation capacity under antibiotic stress.
