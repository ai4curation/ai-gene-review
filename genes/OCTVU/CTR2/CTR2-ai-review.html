Gene Description

CTR2 (cephalotocin receptor 2) is a G protein-coupled receptor of the oxytocin/vasopressin receptor superfamily in Octopus vulgaris. It is the second cephalotocin receptor identified in octopus (after CTR1). Functional expression in Xenopus oocytes demonstrated that CTR2 is specifically activated by cephalotocin (CT) via calcium-mediated signaling, but not by octopressin (OP) or vertebrate OT/VP peptides. Unlike CTR1, which is expressed primarily in the nervous system, CTR2 mRNA is mainly distributed in peripheral tissues with highest expression in branchia and vas deferens. Based on expression patterns and vertebrate analogy, CTR2 is proposed to mediate ammonia excretion (via branchia) and production/secretion of steroid hormones (via vas deferens and testis).

Core Functions

CTR2 is a 426-amino-acid G-protein coupled receptor specific for cephalotocin, a nonapeptide of the oxytocin/vasopressin superfamily. It signals via the Gq/11-coupled calcium/inositol phosphate pathway as demonstrated by functional expression in Xenopus oocytes. It is not activated by octopressin or vertebrate OT/VP peptides. The ligand selectivity between CT and OP receptors depends on peptide residues at positions 2-5 (not position 8 as in vertebrates), representing a distinct evolutionary solution to ligand-receptor discrimination. CTR2 has a conserved disulfide bond (Cys119-Cys198) and two N-glycosylation sites (Asn32, Asn35). Unlike CTR1, which is expressed primarily in the nervous system, CTR2 is mainly distributed in peripheral tissues (branchia, vas deferens, testis), suggesting roles in ammonia excretion and steroid hormone regulation rather than neurotransmission. CTR2 expression has also been detected in the gastric ganglion, implicating cephalotocin signaling in digestive regulation and gut-brain communication (PMID:29326594).

Research report: Functional annotation of CTR2 (UniProt Q5WA50) — Cephalotocin receptor 2 in Octopus vulgaris

0) Identity verification (mandatory)

The symbol CTR2 in this report refers specifically to cephalotocin receptor 2 from the common octopus (Octopus vulgaris), which is a rhodopsin/class A (GPCR1) G‑protein coupled receptor in the oxytocin/vasopressin (OT/VP)-type peptide receptor superfamily. It is not the unrelated “CTR2” used in other organisms for copper transporters (e.g., SLC31A2/CTR2 in mammals). Octopus CTR2 was cloned and functionally assayed as a cephalotocin (CT)-selective receptor (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 3-5, kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 1-2).

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Cephalotocin signaling

Cephalotocin (CT) is an invertebrate oxytocin/vasopressin-family nonapeptide in O. vulgaris with sequence CYFRNCPIG‑NH2, closely related to vertebrate OT/VP peptides. CTR2 is one of two CT receptor subtypes described in octopus (CTR‑1 and CTR‑2), alongside a distinct receptor for the related peptide octopressin (minakata2010oxytocinvasopressinandgonadotropin‐releasing pages 2-4).

1.2 What “functional annotation” means here

For a neuropeptide GPCR such as CTR2, the primary function is best defined by:
* Cognate ligand(s) and potency/selectivity
* Downstream signaling (second messenger pathways)
* Cellular localization (membrane receptor) and tissue distribution
* Physiological roles inferred from distribution and functional experiments.

2) Gene/protein overview for O. vulgaris CTR2 (UniProt Q5WA50)

2.1 Molecular class, domains, and defining features

Primary evidence identifies CTR2 as a 426 amino-acid rhodopsin-type GPCR with canonical 7 transmembrane helices and conserved OT/VP receptor-family motifs/residues involved in ligand interaction. The receptor has predicted N-linked glycosylation sites and numerous predicted phosphorylation motifs (PKA/PKC/CK2), enriched in intracellular regions relevant to GPCR regulation and coupling (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 3-5, kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 2-3).

Subcellular localization (inference): As a 7TM GPCR, CTR2 is expected to localize to the plasma membrane of expressing cells, where it detects extracellular peptide ligands and triggers intracellular signaling (supported by its 7TM GPCR architecture and functional membrane signaling assays) (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 3-5, kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 1-2).

3) Molecular function: ligand specificity and signaling

3.1 Cognate ligand: cephalotocin selectivity

In Xenopus oocyte heterologous expression assays, octopus CTR2 was selectively activated by cephalotocin:
* CT EC50 = 67.2 ± 8.9 nM (dose range 1 nM–10 µM) (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 3-5).
* Octopressin did not activate CTR2 even at >10 µM, demonstrating strong selectivity (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 3-5).

This functional pharmacology is also supported visually in the published dose–response figure for CTR2 (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages media ffc6c373).

3.2 Second messenger/signaling pathway

The oocyte assay readout was a Ca2+-mediated inward chloride current, indicating that ligand binding to CTR2 triggers signaling that elevates intracellular calcium, consistent with canonical OT/VP-type GPCR coupling in this assay system (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 1-2). While the specific Gα subtype (e.g., Gq/11 versus other couplings) is not directly assigned in the extracted text for CTR2, the Ca2+-dependent current provides direct functional evidence of Ca2+-linked signaling (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 1-2).

4) Expression and localization in the organism

4.1 Tissue distribution (mRNA)

A key biological distinction between CTR subtypes is tissue distribution:
* CTR2 mRNA is mainly distributed in peripheral tissues, with notable abundance in branchia (gills) and kidney (minakata2010oxytocinvasopressinandgonadotropin‐releasing pages 2-4).
* CTR2 is also reported as abundant in male reproductive tissues such as vas deferens and testis (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 6-7).

A tissue-distribution Southern blot figure supporting this pattern (CTR2 vs CTR1/OPR) was retrieved (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages media a85f2447).

4.2 Digestive system–associated neural tissues (context)

Independent transcriptomic profiling of the O. vulgaris gastric ganglion detected cephalotocin transcript within a mid-level abundance bin (100–1,000 CPM) among surveyed genes, and showed infection-associated differential expression of cephalotocin in that ganglion context (baldascino2017thegastricganglion pages 9-10, baldascino2017thegastricganglion pages 14-15). This supports the broader presence of the cephalotocin system in digestive-tract–associated neural tissue, although this study does not specifically resolve CTR2 (as opposed to CTR1) expression at cellular resolution (baldascino2017thegastricganglion pages 14-15, baldascino2017thegastricganglion pages 9-10).

5) Physiological roles and pathway placement

5.1 Proposed primary physiological roles for CTR2

Based on its strong peripheral expression (especially branchia and kidney), CTR2 is proposed to mediate cephalotocin effects related to:
* Ammonia excretion (branchial role) and/or
* Ionic balance / osmoregulatory control (kidney/branchia association) (minakata2010oxytocinvasopressinandgonadotropin‐releasing pages 2-4).

Based on abundant expression in vas deferens/testis, CTR2 has also been proposed to participate in steroid hormone production/secretion in reproductive tissues (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 6-7).

5.2 Distinction from octopressin receptor physiology

The octopus VP/OT-type system includes both CT and octopressin pathways. Reviews emphasize that octopressin is more broadly expressed in central/digestive ganglia and has pronounced myotropic (smooth muscle) and behavioral effects, whereas cephalotocin appears more restricted and—critically—CTR2 is CT-selective and does not respond to octopressin (odekunle2020comparativeandevolutionary pages 8-9, minakata2010oxytocinvasopressinandgonadotropin‐releasing pages 2-4, kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 3-5).

6) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

6.1 Evidence landscape for 2023–2024

Within the tool-retrieved literature for this run, no 2023–2024 primary studies were retrieved that directly re-characterize octopus CTR2 (e.g., new deorphanization, structure determination, single-cell expression atlas). This suggests that octopus CTR2 remains primarily grounded in earlier cloning/pharmacology and review synthesis (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 3-5, minakata2010oxytocinvasopressinandgonadotropin‐releasing pages 2-4).

6.2 Closest 2023 mechanistic advances relevant to CTR2 inference

A 2023 study in the sea cucumber Apostichopus japonicus characterized a VP/OT-type receptor (AjHOR) and showed activation of cAMP–PKA, possible Ca2+–PKC, and downstream ERK1/2 signaling, linking VP/OT-type receptor activation to conserved intracellular pathways and providing a modern mechanistic reference point for interpreting OT/VP-type receptor signaling across invertebrates (cong2023aputativerole pages 1-2). The same paper notes (as background) that O. vulgaris has three VP/OT-type receptors mediating two ligands, consistent with the established octopus receptor complement (CTR1/CTR2/OPR) (cong2023aputativerole pages 1-2).

7) Current applications and real-world implementations

7.1 Biomedical/biotechnology applications of octopus-derived VP/OT peptides (ligand-centric)

While not a CTR2 study per se, a 2022 experimental pharmacology paper demonstrated that cephalotocin can act as an agonist of human vasopressin receptor subtypes (V1b and V2) and showed antidiuretic effects in rats (reduced urine output, increased urinary osmolality), highlighting translational interest in cephalotocin-like ligands as potential therapeutics (kim2022anoctopusderivedpeptide pages 1-2). This illustrates an application pathway where octopus peptide–receptor pharmacology informs drug discovery.

7.2 Physiology/ecology and aquaculture-adjacent contexts

Expression and function of VP/OT-family neuropeptides are increasingly discussed in cephalopod physiology contexts (e.g., metabolism/mineral homeostasis studies in cephalopods), though specific CTR2-targeted implementations remain limited in the retrieved 2023 record (hatano2023deepoceanwatera was retrieved but did not provide CTR2-specific mechanistic annotation in the extracted evidence).

8) Expert opinions and authoritative synthesis

Authoritative reviews highlight conserved, ancient roles for VP/OT-type signaling across Bilateria (water/salt balance, reproduction, feeding) and emphasize that cephalopods are unusual among invertebrates in having duplicated VP/OT-type peptide signaling components (odekunle2020comparativeandevolutionary pages 1-2, odekunle2020comparativeandevolutionary pages 8-9). In the octopus system specifically, Minakata (2010) synthesizes evidence for peripheral CTR2 enrichment (branchia/kidney) and frames CTR2 as a candidate mediator of excretory/osmoregulatory physiology rather than central neuromodulation (minakata2010oxytocinvasopressinandgonadotropin‐releasing pages 2-4).

10) Practical functional annotation statement (for database/curation use)

CTR2 (UniProt Q5WA50) encodes a cephalotocin-selective oxytocin/vasopressin-family class A GPCR in Octopus vulgaris. It is a predicted plasma membrane 7TM receptor that, when activated by extracellular cephalotocin, triggers Ca2+-linked signaling (as evidenced by Ca2+-mediated chloride currents in Xenopus oocytes). CTR2 shows high peripheral expression (branchia, kidney, reproductive tissues), supporting roles in ammonia excretion/osmoregulation and reproductive endocrine physiology (kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 3-5, minakata2010oxytocinvasopressinandgonadotropin‐releasing pages 2-4, kanda2005novelevolutionarylineages pages 6-7).

  • Tissue-level expression for CTR2 is supported; however, cell-type specificity and protein-level localization (e.g., immunohistochemistry with validated reagents) were not established in the extracted evidence (baldascino2017thegastricganglion pages 9-10).

CTR2 (Cephalotocin Receptor 2) - Deep Research Summary

Gene Identity

  • UniProt: Q5WA50 (Swiss-Prot, reviewed)
  • Gene symbol: CTR2
  • Organism: Octopus vulgaris (Common octopus), NCBI Taxon:6645
  • Protein name: Cephalotocin receptor 2
  • Length: 426 amino acids, 49.4 kDa
  • Accession: EMBL AB112347 (mRNA), AB158494/AB158495 (genomic DNA)
  • Classification: G protein-coupled receptor, rhodopsin-type (class I), vasopressin/oxytocin receptor subfamily

Structural Features

CTR2 is a seven-transmembrane (7TM) G protein-coupled receptor with the canonical topology of the rhodopsin-type GPCR family. Key structural features from UniProt annotation include:

  • Extracellular N-terminus (residues 1-51) with two N-linked glycosylation sites at Asn-32 and Asn-35
  • Seven transmembrane helices: TM1 (52-72), TM2 (85-105), TM3 (121-141), TM4 (166-186), TM5 (209-229), TM6 (289-309), TM7 (320-340)
  • Conserved disulfide bond between Cys-119 (ECL1) and Cys-198 (ECL2), characteristic of rhodopsin-family GPCRs
  • Disordered C-terminal tail (373-426) with compositional bias toward basic/acidic and polar residues
  • Intron between TM6 and TM7: The CTR2 gene shares the conserved intron position between the sixth and seventh transmembrane domains with all vertebrate OT/VP receptor genes (OxyR, V1aR, V1bR, V2R), consistent with common ancestry [PMID:15504101, "OPR, CTR1, and CTR2 genes, similar to all the vertebrate receptors, have an intron between the sixth and seventh TM domains"]

Several regions and residues that are requisite for binding of vertebrate OT/VP receptors with their ligands are highly conserved in CTR2 (and CTR1), but show substitutions compared to the vertebrate consensus. Specifically, five glutamine residues in TM domains 2, 3, 4, and 6 and a lysine residue in TM3 that are essential for OT/VP interaction are conserved in both CTRs PMID:15504101. The sequences Phe-Gln-Val-Leu-Pro-Gln-Leu, Gly-Pro-Asp, Asp-Cys-Trp-Ala and Pro-Trp-Gly found in vertebrate OT/VP receptors (important for ligand binding) are substituted to Phe-Asn-Ile-Leu-Pro-Gln-Leu, Ala-Gly-Asp, Asp-Cys-Trp-Val and Ala-Trp-Val in CTR2 PMID:15504101.

Primary Function

CTR2 is a G protein-coupled receptor that specifically binds cephalotocin (CT), one of two oxytocin/vasopressin superfamily neuropeptides in O. vulgaris.

Ligand Specificity

Functional expression of CTR2 in Xenopus oocytes demonstrated that CTR2 induces calcium-mediated inward chloride current specifically in response to cephalotocin, but not to octopressin or vertebrate OT/VP family peptides [PMID:15504101, "CTR2 and OPR expressed in Xenopus oocytes induced calcium-mediated inward chloride current in a CT- and OP-specific manner respectively"]. This selectivity contrasts with the mechanism in vertebrates, where the difference in polarity of residue 8 determines OT vs VP binding: in octopus, OP and CT share an isoleucine at position 8, and instead the different residues at positions 2-5 are responsible for receptor selectivity [PMID:15504101, "the amino acid residues of OP and CT at positions 2-5, were presumed to play crucial roles in the binding selectivity to their receptors"].

Signaling Pathway

CTR2 couples to Gq/11-type G proteins, activating the inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate-calcium signal transduction cascade, as evidenced by calcium-mediated chloride currents in Xenopus oocytes PMID:15504101. This is consistent with the signaling mode of vertebrate OxyR, V1aR, and V1bR, which also couple to Gq/11 (unlike V2R, which couples to Gs/adenylate cyclase) PMID:15504101.

Cross-Species Pharmacology

Recent pharmacological studies have tested cephalotocin on mammalian receptors. Kim et al. (2022) demonstrated that cephalotocin acts as a selective agonist of human AVP type 1b (V1bR) and type 2 (V2R) receptors, and has antidiuretic activity in rats, reducing urine output similarly to desmopressin PMID:35621979. A separate study found that cephalotocin has anti-stress effects in rats via Avpr1b in hippocampal neurons, reducing immobility in the tail suspension test PMID:36050225.

Biological Role

Proposed Physiological Functions

CTR2 is proposed to mediate at least two major physiological roles based on tissue expression patterns and vertebrate homology:

  1. Ammonia excretion in branchia: By analogy with vasotocin receptors in toadfish branchia that regulate urea excretion [PMID:15504101, "Arg-vasotocin caused prominent transient increases in urea excretion from branchia of toadfish, and Arg-vasotocin receptor mRNA was expressed in the branchia"], and given that ammonia is excreted from octopus branchia, CTR2 may mediate cephalotocin-regulated ammonia excretion.

  2. Steroid hormone production/secretion in reproductive tissues: By analogy with mammalian V1aR in the epididymis, vas deferens, and Leydig cells that regulate testosterone production [PMID:15504101, "These findings, combined with the abundant expression of CTR2 in the vas deferens and testis, support a notion that CT participates in production and/or secretion of the steroid hormones via CTR2"].

Notably, unlike octopressin, cephalotocin does not induce contractile activities on smooth muscles of octopus tissues PMID:15504101, suggesting CTR2 mediates non-contractile physiological functions.

Gastric Ganglion Context

Cephalotocin receptor expression has also been detected in the gastric ganglion of octopus, the largest visceral ganglion implicated in regulation of digestive tract functions. Baldascino et al. (2017) confirmed the presence of cephalotocin and its receptor in this ganglion, and found that parasitic infection altered cephalotocin gene expression, suggesting a role in gut-brain signaling and food intake regulation PMID:29326594.

Expression and Localization

CTR2 mRNA is mainly distributed in peripheral tissues, in contrast to CTR1, which is primarily expressed in the central and peripheral nervous systems PMID:15504101:

  • Highest expression: Branchia (gills) and vas deferens
  • Also detected: Testis and other peripheral tissues
  • Very low expression: Nervous system

This tissue distribution is distinct from both CTR1 (nervous system, pancreas, oviduct, ovary) PMID:14596680 and OPR (widely distributed in both nervous system and peripheral tissues) PMID:15504101.

Evolutionary Context

OT/VP Superfamily in Octopus

O. vulgaris was the first invertebrate shown to possess two OT/VP superfamily peptides PMID:15504101. While most protostomes have only a single OT/VP homolog, the octopus has:
- Cephalotocin (CT): bound by CTR1 and CTR2
- Octopressin (OP): bound by OPR

The OP and CT genes are proposed to have evolved through gene duplication from a common ancestral gene, mirroring the duplication that gave rise to OT and VP in vertebrates [PMID:15504101, "the CT and OP genes, similar to the OT/VP family, evolved through duplication"].

Distinct Evolutionary Lineages for Ligand-Receptor Selectivity

A key finding from Kanda et al. (2005) is that the molecular basis of ligand-receptor selectivity in octopus differs fundamentally from vertebrates. In vertebrates, selectivity depends on the polarity of residue 8 (neutral in OT, basic in VP). In octopus, OP and CT share the same residue (isoleucine) at position 8, and selectivity instead depends on residues at positions 2-5 PMID:15504101. Furthermore, key ligand-binding residues in the receptor TM domains that are conserved in CTR1 and CTR2 are substituted in OPR, suggesting the OP/OPR binding mode differs from all other OT/VP systems [PMID:15504101, "the ligand-receptor selectivity were established through different evolutionary lineages from those of their vertebrate counterparts"].

Broader OT/VP Receptor Evolution in Molluscs

The octopus OT/VP receptor system has been contextualized within broader molluscan evolution. Conopressin receptors in the land snail Theba pisana are related to the octopus system, with conopressin genes expressed in reproductive tissues and the CNS PMID:26752717. The crustacean cardioactive peptide (CCAP) signaling system in the Pacific abalone was shown to be phylogenetically related to the OT/VP receptor family, illuminating the deep evolutionary origins of these neuropeptide signaling systems PMID:37956901.
