Gene Description

FtsI (also known as PBP3/penicillin-binding protein 3) is a class B penicillin-binding protein that functions as a monofunctional D,D-transpeptidase essential for septal peptidoglycan synthesis during bacterial cell division. It contains a single transmembrane helix anchoring a large periplasmic catalytic domain with canonical transpeptidase motifs (SXXK, SXN, KTG). FtsI forms a functional complex with FtsW (the SEDS family glycan polymerase) to constitute the septal peptidoglycan synthase. Critically, FtsI/PBP3 lacks glycosyltransferase activity - this function is provided by FtsW in the complex. The enzyme catalyzes cross-linking of peptidoglycan peptide stems during septation and is a validated target of beta-lactam antibiotics. FtsI activity is regulated by divisome proteins FtsBLQ (inhibitory) and activated by FtsN accumulation.

Proposed New Ontology Terms

peptidoglycan D,D-transpeptidase activity

Definition: Catalysis of the cross-linking of peptidoglycan peptide stems during cell wall synthesis. The reaction involves cleavage of D-Ala-D-Ala and transfer of the peptidyl moiety to an amino group of another peptidoglycan stem peptide, forming a peptide cross-link.

Justification: GO currently lacks a specific term for D,D-transpeptidase activity, which is the primary molecular function of class B penicillin-binding proteins like FtsI/PBP3. The existing term GO:0009002 (serine-type D-Ala-D-Ala carboxypeptidase activity) emphasizes the carboxypeptidase aspect, but transpeptidation is the biologically relevant activity for peptidoglycan cross-linking. A term for L,D-transpeptidase activity exists (GO:0071972) but not for D,D-transpeptidase activity.

Conclusions
Pseudomonas aeruginosa PAO1 ftsI (PA4418) encodes PBP3, the essential septal D,D‚Äëtranspeptidase that operates with FtsW within the divisome. Its C‚Äëterminal transpeptidase domain contains the canonical SXXK/SXN/KTG motifs targeted by Œ≤‚Äëlactams. Regulation by FtsBLQ and activation by FtsN gate septal PG synthesis. Recent 2024 experimental work in P. aeruginosa demonstrates that naturally occurring PBP3 substitutions can increase MICs to several Œ≤‚Äëlactams (notably meropenem, cefepime, ceftazidime, ticarcillin), with phenotype modulation by efflux, and that such substitutions or sub‚ÄëMIC drug exposure impair division and cause filamentation. Clinically used agents with high activity against PBP3, including ceftolozane (in C/T) and cefiderocol, remain critical therapies; surveillance indicates high cefiderocol activity across European P. aeruginosa isolates, though resistance mechanisms often involve siderophore uptake changes. Overall, PBP3 remains a validated antibacterial target, and ftsI mutations constitute an emerging but typically multifactorial contributor to Œ≤‚Äëlactam non‚Äësusceptibility in P. aeruginosa.
