Gene Description

SepJ (also called FraG; locus alr2338) is a large (751-residue) multidomain integral cytoplasmic (inner) membrane protein of the filamentous, heterocyst-forming cyanobacterium Nostoc/Anabaena sp. PCC 7120. It has three well-defined regions: an N-terminal coiled-coil domain, a central proline/serine/threonine-rich linker, and a C-terminal integral-membrane permease domain belonging to the drug/metabolite transporter (DMT / EamA) superfamily. SepJ localizes as a focused spot at the intercellular septa (cell poles) of the filament and forms a ring at the division site during cytokinesis; it assembles into multimeric complexes and binds peptidoglycan through its coiled-coil domain. SepJ is a central component of the septal machinery that joins adjacent cells in the filament: it is required for filament integrity, for the formation of septal junctions and a normal number of septal nanopores, and for the intercellular transfer of small molecules along the filament. Its coiled-coil domain provides a cell-cell anchoring function, while the C-terminal permease domain is implicated in intercellular molecular exchange. Loss of SepJ causes filament fragmentation, arrests heterocyst maturation (heterocyst-specific glycolipids are not made), and abolishes diazotrophic (nitrogen-fixing) growth.

Proposed New Ontology Terms

septal junction

Definition: A proteinaceous cell-cell junction of filamentous cyanobacteria that traverses the septal peptidoglycan through a nanopore and directly connects the cytoplasms of adjacent cells in a filament, mediating and gating the intercellular diffusion of small molecules.

Justification: There is no dedicated GO cellular-component term for the bacterial septal junction. GO:0005918 "septate junction" denotes the arthropod epithelial junction and is not appropriate. SepJ (FraG) is required for septal-junction formation and is a candidate structural component, and would be annotated to this term.

Parent term: cell junction

Core Functions

Acts as a structural, multimer-forming component of the cyanobacterial septal junction machinery at the intercellular septa: its N-terminal coiled-coil domain anchors adjacent cells and binds septal peptidoglycan, while the whole protein is required for filament integrity, septal-junction/nanopore formation and gated intercellular molecular exchange (cell-cell communication) that underpins diazotrophic growth. The C-terminal DMT/EamA permease domain is implicated in intercellular molecular exchange, but no specific transport substrate has been established, so the core molecular function is annotated as a structural (non-catalytic) role rather than a transporter activity.

SepJ (FraG, alr2338) — research notes

UniProt: A0ACD7RSI0 (unreviewed), 751 aa. Gene sepJ = fraG = locus alr2338,
Nostoc/Anabaena sp. PCC 7120 (NCBITaxon:103690). SepJ is one of the best-studied septal
proteins of filamentous, heterocyst-forming cyanobacteria.

Discovery and naming

  • The gene was identified independently and near-simultaneously by two groups. Flores et al.
    named it sepJ for its septal localization PMID:17369306, while Nayar et al. named it fraG from a
    filament-fragmentation screen PMID:17259632. The two
    names refer to the same locus; sepJ is the more widely used designation.
  • Both groups reported the same core mutant phenotype: filament fragmentation, failure to
    synthesize heterocyst-specific glycolipids, and inability to grow diazotrophically
    PMID:17369306 PMID:17259632.

Protein family / domain architecture (EamA / DMT superfamily)

SepJ is a three-domain (multidomain) protein PMID:21299655:
- N-terminal coiled-coil domain (~aa 28–207): strongly conserved; mediates
cell-cell anchoring and peptidoglycan interaction.
- Central linker (~aa 208–401): not conserved in sequence but Pro/Ser/Thr-rich;
dispensable for function in the domain-swap experiments.
- C-terminal integral-membrane permease domain (~aa 412–751): resembles an export
permease PMID:17369306.

The C-terminal permease domain is annotated by InterPro as an EamA domain (IPR000620),
Pfam PF00892 (EamA-like transporter), PANTHER PTHR22911 ("Diverse Metabolite
Transporter Superfamily") — i.e. the DMT / drug-metabolite transporter (EmrE/EamA)
superfamily of small-molecule permeases. This is the InterPro/PANTHER signature that drives
the single existing GOA IEA annotation (GO:0016020 membrane).

Domain-dissection experiments show the two functional modules are separable
PMID:21299655 and
that they carry distinct functions PMID:21299655.

Subcellular localization

SepJ localizes as a focused spot at the intercellular septa (cell poles) and forms a ring
at the division site during cytokinesis PMID:17369306. GFP-fusion topology work indicates the C terminus is
cytoplasmic (SepJ is an integral cytoplasmic/inner-membrane protein), with the long
N-terminal extramembrane section extending toward the periplasm/septum. SepJ, FraC and FraD
are described together as cytoplasmic-membrane proteins located at the cell poles in the
septa PMID:28979840.

Multimerization and peptidoglycan interaction

Native (blue-native) PAGE of Anabaena membranes shows SepJ in high-molecular-weight complexes
PMID:28979840. His-tag
pull-downs show that SepJ and its isolated coiled-coil domain bind peptidoglycan
PMID:28979840. Together this supports SepJ
being a structural component of the septal junctions PMID:28979840.

Role in intercellular communication and diazotrophy

SepJ is needed for the normal number of septal nanopores and for the intercellular
transfer of fluorescent tracers (calcein/FRAP). fraC/fraD mutants mislocalize SepJ and impair
tracer transfer PMID:20487302. The permease domain's similarity to transporters, together with
its septal position, led to the hypothesis that SepJ participates in intercellular movement of
metabolites or regulators PMID:17369306. Note: no specific transport substrate for the SepJ permease domain has
been established, so a catalytic/transporter MF is not asserted as a core function here (the
structural/anchoring role dominates the evidence).

The SepJ-vs-FraCD junction question

There is evidence for at least two types of septal junctions in Anabaena — those related
to SepJ and those related to FraCD PMID:28979840. The recently resolved cryoET SJ structural core is built from SepN (plug) +
FraD (anchor) (PMID:36470860, PMID:42424141), and SepJ is not (yet) placed as a subunit of
that core complex. Whether SepJ forms the same channel as the FraCD/SepN complex or a distinct
but related junction remains unresolved; SepJ is best described as a septal-junction-
associated / architectural protein required for SJ formation, nanopore number and filament
integrity (consistent with modules/septal_junction.yaml, which groups SepJ/FraG among
associated septal proteins rather than as an asserted core subunit).
