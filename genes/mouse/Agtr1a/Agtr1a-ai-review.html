Gene Description

Agtr1a encodes the mouse angiotensin II type 1A receptor, a rhodopsin-family GPCR for angiotensin II. The receptor signals mainly through Gq/phospholipase C and calcium mobilization at the plasma membrane and mediates major renin-angiotensin outputs including vasoconstriction, renal sodium/water handling, aldosterone regulation, and blood-pressure control.

Comprehensive Research Report: Mouse Agtr1a (Angiotensin II Type-1 Receptor A; AT1aR) Functional Annotation

0) Target identity verification (critical disambiguation)

The literature reviewed matches the mouse (Mus musculus) Agtr1a gene encoding the angiotensin II type 1a receptor (AT1aR), a 7‑transmembrane class A (rhodopsin-like) GPCR and the dominant murine AT1 receptor isoform in many tissues. (cui2023theroleof pages 7-13, takayama2023angiotensiniitype pages 1-2)

Rodents express two AT1 receptor isoforms (AT1a/Agtr1a and AT1b/Agtr1b), whereas humans have a single AGTR1 gene; Agtr1a is repeatedly described as the main/major murine AT1 isoform and mouse homolog corresponding to human AGTR1. (takayama2023angiotensiniitype pages 1-2, alhajoj2024assessingadenoviraldelivery pages 39-42)

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Receptor/ligand system

Agtr1a/AT1aR is the principal receptor mediating many effects of the renin–angiotensin system (RAS) effector peptide angiotensin II (Ang II). In vascular tissues, Ang II acting at AT1 receptors produces smooth muscle contraction and increases vascular resistance and blood pressure. (nadasy2024directvasculareffects pages 1-2)

1.2 Canonical (G protein) signaling

AT1 receptors (including AT1aR) are commonly characterized as Gq/11‑coupled GPCRs. Upon Ang II binding, canonical signaling proceeds via PLC activation → PIP2 cleavage → IP3 + DAG → intracellular Ca2+ release + PKC activation, with downstream engagement of kinase pathways and oxidative signaling. (singh2024implicationsofβarrestin pages 1-3)

Downstream consequences in vascular pathobiology include activation of signaling modules (e.g., MAPK/ERK pathways) and transcriptional programs associated with inflammation and remodeling. (nadasy2024directvasculareffects pages 1-2)

1.3 Noncanonical signaling: β‑arrestin recruitment, internalization, and “biased agonism”

AT1 receptor C‑terminal phosphorylation by GRKs recruits β‑arrestin‑1/2, which (i) desensitizes G‑protein signaling, (ii) drives clathrin-dependent internalization, and (iii) can scaffold distinct signaling modules (including MAPK and PI3K pathways) from internalized receptor complexes. (mathieu2024insightsintothe pages 3-4)

“Biased agonism” refers to ligands that preferentially activate one signaling arm (e.g., β‑arrestin) over another (e.g., Gq). In the AT1R field, peptides such as TRV027 (TRV120027) and SII‑AngII are used as β‑arrestin‑biased ligands to probe or exploit these pathways. (mathieu2024insightsintothe pages 3-4, singh2024implicationsofβarrestin pages 1-3)

2) Subcellular and tissue/cellular localization relevant to function

2.1 Plasma membrane receptor in vascular smooth muscle: vasoconstriction and mechanosensing

Recent mechanistic work in mouse resistance vessels supports a central role for AT1aR in myogenic vasoconstriction and mechanosensory control of vascular tone. In these preparations, Gq/11-dependent signaling is required for myogenic constriction, while β‑arrestin‑biased ligands did not reproduce the same vasoconstrictor responses. (cui2023theroleof pages 7-13, cui2023theroleof pages 40-41)

2.2 Vascular endothelium in kidney and brain microvessels

In vivo imaging demonstrates that Ang II can act directly on endothelial cells within kidney and brain microvessels to raise endothelial intracellular Ca2+ and stimulate NO production; these responses are sensitive to the AT1R antagonist losartan (and not blocked by AT2R blockade), implicating endothelial AT1R signaling in vivo. (calderon2024angiotensiniidirectly pages 1-2)

2.3 Renal tubule epithelium: proximal tubule and distal nephron

A 2024 kidney-tubule-specific AT1aR deletion model (Pax8-rtTA/tet-on; Agtr1a-floxed) provides direct evidence that renal tubular AT1aR regulates proximal tubule membrane potential and transport protein expression (e.g., NHE3, Kir4.2) and is linked to systemic blood pressure regulation. (duan2024roleofangiotensin pages 1-3)

2.4 CNS circuits (energy balance and sympathetic regulation)

Agtr1a/AT1aR expression in hypothalamic AgRP neurons supports integrative roles in cardiometabolic regulation; notably, obesity can be associated with a G-protein “signal switch” in a subset of AT1aR-expressing neurons (from Gi-like inhibitory responses to Gq-like excitatory responses), linking AT1aR signaling state to resting metabolic rate adaptation. (balapattabi2023angiotensinat1areceptor pages 1-3)

3) Pathways and mechanistic roles (functional annotation)

3.1 Primary molecular function

Agtr1a encodes a cell-surface receptor whose primary function is to bind Ang II and transduce signals through:
- Gq/11 → PLC → IP3/DAG → Ca2+/PKC (canonical), and
- β‑arrestin recruitment → desensitization/internalization + β‑arrestin–scaffolded signaling (noncanonical). (singh2024implicationsofβarrestin pages 1-3, mathieu2024insightsintothe pages 3-4)

3.2 Vascular function: tone, remodeling, and aneurysm biology

AT1R activation has an acute vasoconstrictor role (via G proteins) and is implicated in chronic vascular inflammation/fibrosis; β‑arrestin signaling can regulate receptor trafficking and longer-term programs in cell growth/survival, motivating pharmacologic strategies that separate these arms. (jara2023distinctmechanismsof pages 1-3)

In an Ang II–driven aneurysm model (ApoE−/− on high-fat diet), Ang II infusion produced aneurysm/dissection outcomes that were preventable by either a standard AT1R blocker or a β‑arrestin‑biased agonist (see statistics below). (jara2023distinctmechanismsof pages 1-3, jara2023distinctmechanismsof pages 14-15)

3.3 Renal tubular function: membrane potential and sodium handling

Kidney-tubule AT1aR deletion reduced expression of NHE3 and Kir4.2 in proximal tubule and altered proximal tubule membrane potential; it also altered distal nephron channel processing (increased cleaved γENaC) and increased amiloride-sensitive Na+ currents in cortical collecting duct, consistent with segment-specific roles of tubular AT1aR in transporter/channel regulation. (duan2024roleofangiotensin pages 1-3)

3.4 Endothelial counter-regulation of vasoconstriction

Direct endothelial AT1R signaling in kidney/brain microvessels evokes Ca2+ and NO responses that may counterbalance vasoconstriction; these responses are attenuated in hypertensive/fibrotic disease models and correlate with microvascular pathology (e.g., endothelial shedding, microthrombi). (calderon2024angiotensiniidirectly pages 1-2)

4) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

4.1 Cell-type–specific genetics as a major methodological advance

A 2024 Hypertension “milestones” review highlights the central role of Agtr1a-floxed mice (developed earlier but now widely deployed) to resolve cell-specific AT1aR functions in proximal tubules versus vascular smooth muscle, including dissociation of blood pressure phenotypes from vascular remodeling outcomes. (Publication date: May 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.1161/hypertensionaha.123.21365) (eguchi2024milestonepaperson pages 3-4)

4.2 β‑arrestin signaling: from receptor biology to therapeutic stratification

A 2024 Hypertension review synthesizes evidence that AT1R β‑arrestin signaling can be protective or maladaptive depending on context, and discusses the need for patient/model stratification when targeting AT1R signaling bias. (Publication date: Jan 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.1161/hypertensionaha.123.19419) (mathieu2024insightsintothe pages 6-8)

A 2024 Cellular Signalling review provides a concise mechanistic picture of how biased ligands reshape receptor trafficking and signaling topology (e.g., prolonged endosomal/cytoplasmic ERK signaling), emphasizing implications for cardiovascular drug discovery. (Publication date: Dec 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cellsig.2024.111410) (singh2024implicationsofβarrestin pages 1-3)

4.3 Direct in vivo endothelial signaling by Ang II

A 2024 JAHA intravital imaging study directly visualized Ang II–evoked endothelial Ca2+ and NO signaling in kidney and brain microvessels, demonstrating that the endothelial response is AT1R-dependent (losartan-sensitive) and becomes blunted in hypertension/fibrosis models. (Publication date: May 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.1161/jaha.123.033998) (calderon2024angiotensiniidirectly pages 1-2)

5) Current applications and real-world implementations

5.1 Pharmacology: ARBs as standard AT1R/AT1aR pathway inhibitors

Clinically used angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) are characterized as neutral/balanced antagonists that block both G‑protein and β‑arrestin components of AT1R signaling. (mathieu2024insightsintothe pages 3-4)

In experimental settings, losartan is commonly used for mechanistic inference; for example, losartan blocked Ang II–evoked endothelial Ca2+ and NO responses in vivo. (calderon2024angiotensiniidirectly pages 1-2)

5.2 Experimental therapeutics: β‑arrestin‑biased ligands (TRV027)

β‑arrestin‑biased ligands are used to attempt to preserve or redirect potentially beneficial AT1R signaling. In cardiovascular translation, TRV027 was evaluated in a Phase IIb BLAST‑AHF trial (reported in review form) with 621 patients randomized to placebo or TRV027 at 1, 5, or 25 mg/hr IV; the drug was well tolerated but did not meet primary endpoints in the overall cohort, with post-hoc signals suggesting benefit in higher systolic BP tertiles. (singh2024implicationsofβarrestin pages 3-5, mathieu2024insightsintothe pages 6-8)

5.3 Genetic models as routine “implementations” for functional annotation

Agtr1a global knockouts, Agtr1a-floxed alleles, and tissue-specific Cre strategies (kidney tubules; vascular smooth muscle; brain regions) now represent standard experimental infrastructure for assigning cell-type contributions to blood pressure control, vascular remodeling, renal transport, and CNS regulation. (eguchi2024milestonepaperson pages 3-4, duan2024roleofangiotensin pages 1-3)

6) Quantitative statistics and data points from recent studies

6.1 Endothelial microvessel signaling (2024)

Acute systemic Ang II increased endothelial intracellular Ca2+ by >4‑fold in kidney and brain microvessels and increased NO reporter signal by >1.5‑fold; both were blocked by losartan. (Calderon et al., May 2024; https://doi.org/10.1161/jaha.123.033998) (calderon2024angiotensiniidirectly pages 1-2)

In Ang II–infused ApoE−/− males on high-fat diet, Ang II produced aneurysm in ≈67% with substantial mortality, and ≈13% mortality occurred from aortic arch dissection without aneurysm; co-infusion with TRV027 or olmesartan prevented Ang II–induced aneurysm-associated mortality (with distinct mechanistic signatures). (Jara et al., Feb 2023; https://doi.org/10.1161/hypertensionaha.122.19232) (jara2023distinctmechanismsof pages 1-3, jara2023distinctmechanismsof pages 14-15)

6.3 Blood pressure phenotype in cell-specific genetic models (2024 review summary)

A Hypertension milestones review reports that a knock-in SM22α VSMC AT1aR deletion reduced baseline blood pressure by 7 mmHg and attenuated Ang II–mediated hypertension, illustrating how cell-targeting strategy can alter inferred contributions of VSMC AT1aR to blood pressure control. (Duan et al., Jan 2024; https://doi.org/10.1161/hypertensionaha.123.21389) (duan2024roleofangiotensin media 76ecb2b3, duan2024roleofangiotensin media b9bb5956, duan2024roleofangiotensin media baf624f1)

7) Expert opinions and authoritative synthesis (2024)

Recent expert reviews in Hypertension emphasize that AT1R/AT1aR signaling cannot be reduced to a single “good vs bad” axis: β‑arrestin signaling can be protective (e.g., improved hemodynamics/endothelial function in some models) or maladaptive (e.g., contributions to aneurysm-related inflammation and vascular smooth muscle migration), implying that therapeutic targeting may require disease- and patient-specific stratification (e.g., baseline SBP or plasma renin activity). Agtr1a curation notes

Evidence synthesis

  • Core receptor identity: Agtr1a encodes the mouse type-1 angiotensin II receptor A, a seven-transmembrane G protein-coupled receptor whose primary molecular function is angiotensin type I receptor activity. UniProt summarizes the function as: "Receptor for angiotensin II, a vasoconstricting peptide, which acts as a key regulator of blood pressure and sodium retention by the kidney." [file:mouse/Agtr1a/Agtr1a-uniprot.txt]

  • Core signaling: UniProt states that activated AGTR1A "couples to G-alpha proteins G(q) (GNAQ, GNA11, GNA14 or GNA15) and thus activates phospholipase C and increases the cytosolic Ca(2+) concentrations." This supports angiotensin-activated GPCR signaling, phospholipase C signaling, and calcium mobilization as direct pathway outputs. [file:mouse/Agtr1a/Agtr1a-uniprot.txt]

  • Mouse kidney-development evidence: Iosipiv and Schroeder report that "cultured murine UB cells express AT1 receptor protein and mRNA" and that ANG II treatment "elicits a dose-related increase in the number of cells that have primary and secondary branches"; the effect is blocked by the AT1 antagonist candesartan. [PMID:12657564 "A role for angiotensin II AT1 receptors in ureteric bud cell branching.", "cultured murine UB cells express AT1 receptor protein and mRNA ... ANG II ... elicits a dose-related increase in the number of cells that have primary and secondary branches"]

  • Mouse renal and blood-pressure physiology: AT1A-deficient mice on altered salt diets show "high urinary volume and low blood pressure despite increased renin and aldosterone levels" and a shifted pressure-natriuresis relationship. This supports non-core organismal annotations for renal output, aldosterone regulation, salt-sensitive blood pressure, and drinking/volume phenotypes. [PMID:12167595 "Effects of AT(1A) receptor deletion on blood pressure and sodium excretion during altered dietary salt intake.", "high urinary volume and low blood pressure despite increased renin and aldosterone levels"]

  • Mouse AT1 receptor redundancy and blood-pressure/kidney phenotypes: Agtr1a/Agtr1b double mutants have "marked hypotension, and abnormal kidney morphology including delayed maturity in glomerular growth, hypoplastic papilla, and renal arterial hypertrophy." This supports AT1 receptor involvement in blood pressure and renal development but does not make broad developmental terms the core molecular function. [PMID:9466969 "Murine double nullizygotes of the angiotensin type 1A and 1B receptor genes duplicate severe abnormal phenotypes of angiotensinogen nullizygotes.", "marked hypotension, and abnormal kidney morphology including delayed maturity in glomerular growth, hypoplastic papilla, and renal arterial hypertrophy"]

  • Brain and drinking behavior: Central angiotensin II experiments show that "the blood pressure increase elicited by centrally administered angiotensin II can be selectively ascribed to the AT(1A) receptor" while drinking depends more on AT1B. This supports keeping brain renin-angiotensin and drinking terms as non-core or qualified phenotypes, not core receptor function. [PMID:10880053 "Divergent functions of angiotensin II receptor isoforms in the brain.", "the blood pressure increase elicited by centrally administered angiotensin II can be selectively ascribed to the AT(1A) receptor"]

  • Trafficking and protein-interaction evidence: ATRAP was isolated using the cytoplasmic domain of murine AT1A and "interacts specifically with the carboxyl-terminal domain of the AT1a receptor"; overexpression inhibits AT1A-mediated PLC activation. A later study reports that ATRAP "significantly promotes downregulation of the AT1R." These support receptor trafficking/regulation annotations but the GO term protein binding remains too vague for curation. [PMID:10358057 "Cloning and characterization of ATRAP, a novel protein that interacts with the angiotensin II type 1 receptor.", "interacts specifically with the carboxyl-terminal domain of the AT1a receptor"] [PMID:15757644 "The novel angiotensin II type 1 receptor (AT1R)-associated protein ATRAP downregulates AT1R and ameliorates cardiomyocyte hypertrophy.", "significantly promotes downregulation of the AT1R"]

  • Curation judgment: terms that describe broad responses to hormones, salt, activity, estrogen, corticosterone, dexamethasone, or generic cell proliferation/inflammation are generally downstream, context-specific phenotypes of angiotensin signaling. They should be kept only as non-core when directly supported by mouse evidence, otherwise marked as over-annotated or removed in favor of the specific receptor/signaling terms.

  • Falcon integration: the Falcon report reinforces the same core model: "Agtr1a encodes a cell-surface receptor whose primary function is to bind Ang II and transduce signals through" canonical Gq/PLC/calcium signaling and beta-arrestin-dependent receptor trafficking. It also adds recent 2023-2024 mouse literature on vascular mechanosensing, renal tubular Agtr1a deletion, and endothelial calcium/NO signaling, which supports keeping broad vascular/renal phenotypes as non-core context rather than expanding the core function. [file:mouse/Agtr1a/Agtr1a-deep-research-falcon.md]
