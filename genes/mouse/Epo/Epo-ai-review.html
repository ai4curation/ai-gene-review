Gene Description

Epo encodes mouse erythropoietin, a secreted glycoprotein cytokine hormone of the EPO/TPO family. The mature ligand is produced mainly by kidney in adult mammals and liver in fetal or neonatal contexts, is induced by hypoxia-regulated HIF signaling, and circulates to erythroid progenitors where it binds EPOR and activates JAK2/STAT and MAPK-linked signaling. The core supported function is endocrine receptor-ligand control of definitive erythropoiesis: survival, proliferation, differentiation, maturation, and hemoglobinization of erythroid cells. Many automated response, neural, immune, cardiac-development, cell-surface, and generic transcription annotations reflect downstream or non-core contexts and should not obscure the primary EPOR ligand function.

Research Report: Functional Annotation of Mouse Epo (UniProt P07321) — Erythropoietin (precursor)

1) Target verification (gene/protein identity and context)

The research target is mouse erythropoietin (Epo), a secreted glycoprotein cytokine hormone required for definitive erythropoiesis; the retrieved mouse developmental genetics and renal physiology literature consistently addresses Epo/EpoR signaling and embryonic phenotypes that align with the UniProt entry description (P07321; erythropoietin precursor) rather than an unrelated “Epo” symbol in another organism or context (lee2001erythropoietin(epo)and pages 4-5, halvorsen2002physiologyoferythropoietin pages 1-2).

2) Key concepts, definitions, and current understanding

2.1 Core molecular function

EPO is the principal endocrine growth factor that promotes erythroid progenitor survival, proliferation, and maturation, supporting red blood cell production (erythropoiesis) (juul2000nonerythropoieticrolesof pages 1-4, halvorsen2002physiologyoferythropoietin pages 1-2). In mouse embryogenesis, EPO is essential for definitive erythropoiesis (fetal liver–derived erythropoiesis), while the initial emergence of primitive erythropoiesis in the yolk sac begins largely independently of EPO (lee2001erythropoietin(epo)and pages 4-5).

2.2 Protein family and structural concepts (functional inference)

EPO is part of the hematopoietin/cytokine signaling system and is described in cytokine-receptor structure reviews as a “long cytokine” with long helices packed in an antiparallel arrangement—consistent with the canonical four-helix-bundle cytokine architecture typical for this class of ligands (bagley2001structuralandfunctional pages 2-4). The erythropoietin receptor (EPOR) is a simple homodimeric cytokine receptor, and ligand binding involves defined receptor “hot spots,” including a key hydrophobic contact residue Phe93 in an EPOR loop implicated in ligand binding (bagley2001structuralandfunctional pages 2-4).

2.3 Cellular and anatomical localization (where EPO acts)

Canonical site of production and secretion: A 2024 review focused on renal interstitial biology summarizes that EPO is mainly secreted by renal interstitial fibroblast-like cells in the kidney cortex—often termed renal EPO-producing (REP) cells—and then enters the circulation to act on erythroid precursor cells in bone marrow that express EPOR (suzuki2024crosstalkbetweenoxygen pages 1-2).

Emerging nuance—tubular EPO production: In a 2024 mouse kidney ischemia–reperfusion injury (IRI) model, prolonged roxadustat exposure (24 h) induced detectable Epo protein production within proximal and distal tubules by immunohistochemistry, whereas short hypoxia/short roxadustat predominantly induced interstitial/peritubular Epo production (yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 1-2, yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 2-6). This finding suggests that under some injury/drug-exposure conditions, renal epithelial compartments may contribute to Epo production (yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 1-2, yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 2-6).

3) Pathways and mechanism of action

3.1 EPOR activation and proximal signaling

EPOR lacks intrinsic kinase activity; upon EPO binding, EPOR dimerization/activation enables signaling through the cytoplasmic tyrosine kinase JAK2 (juul2000nonerythropoieticrolesof pages 1-4). Downstream, signaling involves phosphorylation cascades and activation of transcription factors such as STAT5A/STAT5B (halvorsen2002physiologyoferythropoietin pages 1-2).

3.2 Canonical downstream modules (erythroid biology)

A phosphoproteomics study of EPO-driven erythropoiesis emphasizes canonical EPO/EPOR signaling modules including JAK2/STAT5 and RAS/MEK/ERK (MAPK/ERK) and expands the network to additional EPO-regulated phosphorylation targets involved in adaptor function, cytoskeletal remodeling, and metabolism (held2020phosphoproteomicdiscoveryof pages 1-2). This work supports the view that EPO signaling is not limited to a single transcriptional axis but coordinates multiple cellular programs needed for proliferation, survival, and late-stage erythroid differentiation (held2020phosphoproteomicdiscoveryof pages 1-2).

4) Regulation of Epo expression: hypoxia sensing and HIF biology

A central regulatory principle is that Epo expression is strongly induced by hypoxia through the hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) system in renal EPO-producing cells (suzuki2024crosstalkbetweenoxygen pages 1-2). In the 2024 review focused on REP cells, HIF-2α is described as the major activator of EPO gene expression in REP cells, while upstream prolyl hydroxylase domain (PHD) enzymes—requiring oxygen, Fe2+, and α-ketoglutarate—control HIF-α stability; PHD2 is highlighted as a dominant oxygen sensor controlling EPO via HIF2α hydroxylation (suzuki2024crosstalkbetweenoxygen pages 1-2). The same review emphasizes crosstalk with iron biology: iron availability can modulate HIF2α accumulation and thereby influence EPO induction (suzuki2024crosstalkbetweenoxygen pages 1-2).

5) Mouse genetic evidence (high-confidence functional annotation)

Mouse genetic evidence strongly supports Epo’s essential role in definitive erythropoiesis. Epo and EpoR homozygous-null mice exhibit impaired definitive erythropoiesis with severe anemia and die around embryonic day E13–E13.5, demonstrating an essential requirement for this ligand–receptor system during development (lee2001erythropoietin(epo)and pages 4-5). Expression timing studies show Epo mRNA is detectable in the embryo proper (not yolk sac) beginning around E9.0, aligning with a role in supporting erythroid cell survival/expansion after circulation begins (lee2001erythropoietin(epo)and pages 4-5).

6) Quantitative data and statistics from experimental studies

6.1 Quantitative receptor binding / dose response (mouse models)

A mouse EpoR rescue/transgenic study provides quantitative EPOR binding parameters and receptor abundance:
- EPOR binding-site counts per bone-marrow mononuclear cell were ~67 (wild type), 71 (Tg-B), 27 (Rescued-B), and 83 (Rescued-A) (suzuki2002erythroidspecificexpressionof pages 6-8).
- Ligand-binding affinity (Kd) for recombinant human EPO was similar between rescued and wild type: 201 pM (rescued) vs 270 pM (wild type) (suzuki2002erythroidspecificexpressionof pages 6-8).
- Functional progenitor assays showed genotype-dependent sensitivity: high-EPOR expressors reached a CFU-E response plateau at 0.1 U/mL EPO, whereas low-expressor rescued progenitors required higher EPO concentrations (notably 1–10 U/mL) to increase colony formation (suzuki2002erythroidspecificexpressionof pages 8-9, suzuki2002erythroidspecificexpressionof pages 6-8).

6.2 Quantitative hypoxia/drug induction of renal Epo (2024 mouse study)

In a 2024 mouse kidney IRI model, hypoxia and roxadustat produced large fold-changes in renal Epo protein induction:
- 4 h hypoxia (7% O2) increased interstitial cell–derived Epo production by ~75-fold before IRI.
- 4 h roxadustat increased interstitial cell–derived Epo production by ~6-fold before IRI.
- 24–72 h roxadustat pretreatment increased Epo production during IRI by ~9-fold, and immunohistochemistry localized inducible Epo to proximal and distal tubules with 24 h roxadustat (yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 1-2, yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 2-6).

7) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

7.1 REP cell biology, oxygen–iron crosstalk, and HIF2α-centric control (2024)

A 2024 review synthesizes recent mechanistic understanding that renal interstitial fibroblast-like REP cells are the principal endocrine EPO source in the kidney cortex and that HIF-2α is the major transcriptional activator driving Epo in these cells; upstream PHD enzymes serve as oxygen/iron-dependent regulators (suzuki2024crosstalkbetweenoxygen pages 1-2). This framing aligns kidney EPO regulation with broader systemic iron control, emphasizing network-level regulation rather than single-gene control (suzuki2024crosstalkbetweenoxygen pages 1-2).

7.2 Compartment-specific Epo induction and renoprotection hypothesis (2024)

A 2024 experimental study reports that prolonged HIF-PHD inhibition (roxadustat) can induce tubular Epo expression and associates this with improved kidney function after IRI, whereas short hypoxia or short roxadustat that induces mainly interstitial Epo did not confer the same renoprotection in their model (yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 1-2, yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 2-6). This suggests a hypothesis that the site and timing of Epo production (tubular vs interstitial; peri-injury vs pre-injury) may be relevant for non-erythropoietic tissue outcomes—though this remains model- and context-dependent (yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 1-2).

7.3 Clinical translation trend: HIF-prolyl hydroxylase inhibitors (HIF-PHIs) as endogenous EPO inducers (2024)

A 2024 European Renal Best Practice clinical practice document describes HIF-PH inhibitors as mimicking hypoxia to induce HIF target genes that promote erythropoiesis via endogenous erythropoietin and iron-metabolism effects (stoumpos2024hypoxiainduciblefactorprolyl pages 3-4). The guidance emphasizes that efficacy has been demonstrated in >30 phase 3 clinical trials and includes multiple quantitative endpoints in large randomized programs (stoumpos2024hypoxiainduciblefactorprolyl pages 3-4).

8) Current applications and real-world implementations

8.1 EPO biology in anemia therapeutics

EPO signaling is clinically leveraged through erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) and, increasingly, through oral HIF-PH inhibitors that raise endogenous EPO. A clinical review in perinatology describes recombinant EPO dosing used clinically (e.g., 300–1400 U/kg/week) to increase red cell production and reduce transfusions in certain neonatal contexts (juul2000nonerythropoieticrolesof pages 1-4).

8.2 CKD anemia management: HIF-PH inhibitors versus ESAs (2024 practice guidance)

The 2024 practice document reports multiple large phase 3 programs. Examples of quantitative outcomes include:
- Pooled ANDES/OLYMPUS/ALPS analysis (n=4277) showing hemoglobin increase 1.9 vs 0.2 g/dL, response 80% vs 9%, and rescue therapy 9% vs 31% over 52 weeks (roxadustat vs placebo) (stoumpos2024hypoxiainduciblefactorprolyl pages 3-4).
- ASCEND-ND (n=3872) showing daprodustat non-inferior to darbepoetin with a between-group hemoglobin difference 0.08 g/dL (95% CI 0.03–0.13) (stoumpos2024hypoxiainduciblefactorprolyl pages 3-4).
- In dialysis-dependent CKD trials, multiple HIF-PHIs were non-inferior to ESAs; for example, ROCKIES (n=2133) mean hemoglobin increase 0.77 vs 0.68 g/dL (roxadustat vs epoetin alfa) and ASCEND-D (n=2964) mean hemoglobin increase 0.28 vs 0.10 g/dL (daprodustat vs ESA) (stoumpos2024hypoxiainduciblefactorprolyl pages 7-8).
These data reflect mature real-world translation of the hypoxia/HIF→EPO axis into clinical implementation at scale (stoumpos2024hypoxiainduciblefactorprolyl pages 3-4, stoumpos2024hypoxiainduciblefactorprolyl pages 7-8).

9) Expert synthesis and authoritative interpretation (functional annotation focus)

The most strongly supported functional annotation for mouse Epo (UniProt P07321) is: a secreted, hypoxia-inducible endocrine cytokine produced primarily by renal cortical interstitial REP cells that binds EPOR on erythroid progenitors to activate JAK2-dependent signaling (STAT5 and MAPK/ERK modules) required for survival and terminal differentiation in definitive erythropoiesis (suzuki2024crosstalkbetweenoxygen pages 1-2, lee2001erythropoietin(epo)and pages 4-5, halvorsen2002physiologyoferythropoietin pages 1-2). Mouse genetics (embryonic lethality at ~E13–E13.5 with Epo or Epor loss) provides high-confidence causal evidence that this ligand–receptor pair is essential rather than merely modulatory in vivo (lee2001erythropoietin(epo)and pages 4-5).

A key emerging theme from 2024 work is that “EPO biology” is not only about circulating hormone levels; rather, cell identity and microenvironmental cues (oxygen and iron) shape Epo output, and drug modulation of the HIF/PHD axis can produce compartment-specific patterns (interstitial vs tubular) that may matter for non-erythropoietic outcomes in specific injury models (suzuki2024crosstalkbetweenoxygen pages 1-2, yasuoka2024tubularendogenouserythropoietin pages 1-2).

description: >-
  Epo encodes mouse erythropoietin, a secreted glycoprotein cytokine hormone of the EPO/TPO
  family. The mature ligand is produced mainly by kidney in adult mammals and liver in fetal
  or neonatal contexts, is induced by hypoxia-regulated HIF signaling, and circulates to erythroid
  progenitors where it binds EPOR and activates JAK2/STAT and MAPK-linked signaling. The core
  supported function is endocrine receptor-ligand control of definitive erythropoiesis: survival,
  proliferation, differentiation, maturation, and hemoglobinization of erythroid cells. Many
  automated response, neural, immune, cardiac-development, cell-surface, and generic transcription
  annotations reflect downstream or non-core contexts and should not obscure the primary EPOR
  ligand function.
