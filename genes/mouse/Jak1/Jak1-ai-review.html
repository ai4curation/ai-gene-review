Gene Description

JAK1 is an intracellular receptor-associated non-receptor tyrosine kinase required for many cytokine receptor pathways, including type I/type II interferon and interleukin signaling. It phosphorylates receptor complexes and STAT transcription factors to propagate JAK-STAT signaling.

2) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

2.1 Janus kinases (JAKs) and the JAK–STAT pathway

JAKs (JAK1, JAK2, JAK3, TYK2) are intracellular tyrosine kinases that couple ligand-bound cytokine receptors to transcriptional programs via phosphorylation of receptor tails and STAT transcription factors (signal transducers and activators of transcription). (wei2024januskinasesand pages 1-3, sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 4-5)
Canonical pathway definition: cytokine binding promotes receptor assembly, JAK activation, receptor tyrosine phosphorylation, STAT recruitment via SH2 interactions, STAT phosphorylation, STAT dimerization, and STAT nuclear translocation to regulate gene expression. (wei2024januskinasesand pages 1-3, sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 4-5)

2.2 What “JAK1 function” means biochemically

Primary enzymatic function: JAK1 catalyzes protein tyrosine phosphorylation (ATP-dependent transfer of phosphate to tyrosine residues) on (i) receptor cytoplasmic tails to create docking sites and (ii) STATs to enable their activation and transcriptional function. (wei2024januskinasesand pages 1-3, sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 5-7)
Thus, JAK1’s principal substrate classes in signaling are cytokine receptor intracellular tyrosines and STAT proteins. (wei2024januskinasesand pages 1-3)

2.3 JAK1 domain architecture (definitions)

JAK family proteins contain JAK-homology regions (JH1–JH7) organized into functional modules: an N-terminal receptor-binding module and a C-terminal kinase module. (wei2024januskinasesand pages 1-3, sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 4-5)
Key domains relevant to mouse JAK1 include:
- FERM–SH2 “holodomain”: mediates stable association with receptor intracellular motifs (“box1/box2”). (lv2024thejakstatpathway pages 12-15)
- Pseudokinase domain (JH2/PK): regulatory module that restrains basal kinase activity (autoinhibition). (wei2024januskinasesand pages 1-3, lv2024thejakstatpathway pages 12-15)
- Tyrosine kinase domain (JH1/TK): the catalytically active kinase domain, containing the ATP-binding site targeted by most JAK inhibitors. (taylor2024differentialpropertiesof pages 2-4, sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 4-5)

3) Mechanism, pathways, and cellular localization

3.1 Where JAK1 acts in the cell

JAK1 is primarily cytosolic but functions at the inner face of the plasma membrane by binding cytokine receptor intracellular regions through its FERM–SH2 module, making it effectively receptor-associated during signaling. (sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 5-7, lv2024thejakstatpathway pages 12-15)
Noncanonical localization has been reported: JAK1 has been observed in the nucleus in certain pathological and specialized cellular contexts, suggesting possible additional functions beyond canonical membrane-proximal signaling. (sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 5-7)

3.2 Receptor association and activation mechanism (structural/mechanistic model)

Structural work indicates the JAK1 FERM–SH2 holodomain engages receptor intracellular box1 and box2 motifs as an elongated epitope (with receptor-dependent contributions of box1 vs box2 to binding). (lv2024thejakstatpathway pages 12-15)
The pseudokinase (PK/JH2) and tyrosine kinase (TK/JH1) domains form a tandem regulatory unit in which the PK domain contributes to autoinhibition; deletion of the PK domain increases basal kinase activity but disrupts further activation, consistent with a tuned autoinhibitory/activation switch. (lv2024thejakstatpathway pages 12-15)
Recent structural analyses of full-length mJAK1 provide models for distinct dimeric states, including an autoinhibited state (PDB 8EWY) and an active/nanobody-stabilized state (PDB 7T6F), supporting the idea that cytokine-receptor geometry and JAK dimerization can relieve PK-mediated autoinhibition to enable TK activity. (lv2024thejakstatpathway pages 12-15)
Figure-level structural summary (domain map, receptor binding, and mJAK1 dimer states) is presented in Figure 7 of Lv et al. 2024. (lv2024thejakstatpathway media faa97556)

3.3 Core pathways and cytokine systems that use JAK1

JAK1 is described as broadly expressed and participating in signaling for a wide set of cytokines, including receptors that use the common gamma chain (γc), gp130 (IL-6 family), class II cytokine receptors (e.g., interferons), and IL-10 family receptors, making it one of the most pleiotropic JAKs in cytokine biology. (wei2024januskinasesand pages 1-3)
Representative pathway pairings include JAK1 partnering with other JAKs (JAK2, JAK3, TYK2) to transmit different cytokine signals and activate different STATs, depending on receptor composition. (sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 5-7, taylor2024differentialpropertiesof pages 2-4, sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 4-5)
JAK-associated receptors can also engage additional downstream arms (e.g., PI3K/AKT and MAPK/ERK) alongside STAT signaling. (sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 4-5)

3.4 Negative regulation and signaling control (expert synthesis)

Authoritative reviews emphasize that JAK–STAT signaling is controlled by multiple negative regulators, including SOCS proteins (feedback inhibitors that can promote degradation), protein tyrosine phosphatases, PIAS proteins, adaptor regulators (e.g., LNK/SH2B3), and post-translational modifications (e.g., ubiquitination, SUMOylation). (sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 5-7)

4) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

4.1 Structural biology advances in 2024: full-length mouse JAK1 states

A key 2024 development is the consolidation of full-length mJAK1 structural models that compare autoinhibited and activated conformations and connect receptor-binding geometry to relief of pseudokinase-mediated autoinhibition. These structures provide a mechanistic framework for understanding how receptor engagement and JAK dimerization can switch JAK1 from restrained basal activity to signaling-competent kinase activity. (lv2024thejakstatpathway pages 12-15)

4.2 2024 expert analysis: “selective JAK1 inhibition” is relative

A 2024 pharmacology review stresses that JAK isoform selectivity is relative rather than absolute and is influenced by dose and tissue context, reflecting both conserved ATP-binding site architecture and cooperative JAK biology at receptors. (taylor2024differentialpropertiesof pages 2-4)
This is important for interpreting “JAK1-selective” inhibitors in vivo, where pathway effects arise from both biochemical potency and receptor/JAK pairing context. (taylor2024differentialpropertiesof pages 2-4)

5) Current applications and real-world implementations (JAK1 inhibitors)

5.1 Rationale for JAK1 targeting

Because JAK1 participates in signaling for multiple inflammatory and immune cytokines (including interferons, IL-6 family via gp130, γc cytokines with JAK3, and IL-10 family), it is a major therapeutic target for immune-mediated inflammatory diseases. (wei2024januskinasesand pages 1-3, sarapultsev2023jakstatsignalingin pages 5-7)

5.2 Drug mechanisms and expert pharmacology (2024)

Clinically used JAK inhibitors typically bind the ATP site in the active kinase domain, blocking receptor/STAT phosphorylation and thus cytokine-driven transcriptional programs. (taylor2024differentialpropertiesof pages 2-4)
A 2024 review reports example biochemical potencies (IC50) against JAK1 for several approved inhibitors, including abrocitinib (29.2 nM) and ranges for filgotinib (10–53 nM), illustrating that multiple agents can have strong biochemical activity at JAK1 even if clinical selectivity differs by exposure and biology. (taylor2024differentialpropertiesof pages 2-4)

5.3 Quantitative efficacy/safety statistics from recent (2024) evidence synthesis: atopic dermatitis

A 2024 umbrella review of 16 meta-analyses in atopic dermatitis reported that JAK inhibitors (class-level) improved:
- IGA response vs placebo: RR 2.83 (95% CI 2.25–3.56). (he2024januskinaseinhibitors pages 1-2)
- EASI75 response vs placebo: RR 2.84 (95% CI 2.2–3.67). (he2024januskinaseinhibitors pages 1-2)
- Pruritus (PP-NRS) vs placebo: SMD −0.49 (95% CI −0.67 to −0.32). (he2024januskinaseinhibitors pages 1-2)

For JAK1-selective systemic agents, the umbrella review summarized dose-related efficacy and safety signals:
- Abrocitinib: IGA response RR 3.02 (95% CI 2.26–4.02) overall; abrocitinib 200 mg improved EASI75 RR 3.04 (95% CI 2.22–4.16) vs 100 mg EASI75 RR 2.18 (95% CI 1.78–2.67). (he2024januskinaseinhibitors pages 8-11)
- Upadacitinib: EASI75 effects were reported as strong at both doses (e.g., 15 mg RR 3.48; 30 mg RR 4.14 as summarized from included meta-analyses). (he2024januskinaseinhibitors pages 8-11)
- Safety (abrocitinib 200 mg): increased risks of acne RR 4.34, headache RR 1.76, nausea RR 7.81, with a reported estimate for herpes zoster RR 1.64. (he2024januskinaseinhibitors pages 1-2)
- Safety (upadacitinib): increased risks of acne RR 6.23, nasopharyngitis RR 1.36, and elevated blood creatine phosphokinase (CPK) RR 2.41. (he2024januskinaseinhibitors pages 1-2)
Overall treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs) were increased for JAK inhibitors vs placebo (RR 1.14, 95% CI 1.02–1.28), with higher TEAE risk for systemic vs topical JAK inhibitors in the umbrella review’s synthesis. (he2024januskinaseinhibitors pages 8-11)

6) Expert interpretation and synthesis (authoritative perspectives)

Structural and signaling reviews converge on a model in which JAK1 is an adaptable receptor-bound kinase whose FERM–SH2 module encodes receptor specificity (box1/box2 recognition), while the pseudokinase domain encodes autoinhibitory restraint that can be released by receptor-driven geometry and dimerization, thereby controlling kinase output to STATs. (lv2024thejakstatpathway pages 12-15, lv2024thejakstatpathway media faa97556)
Pharmacology-focused reviews caution that clinical “JAK1 selectivity” should be interpreted functionally (pathway inhibition over dosing intervals and in tissues) rather than as a fixed biochemical property, because selectivity depends on dose, exposure, assay context, and the cooperative nature of JAK pairing at receptors. (taylor2024differentialpropertiesof pages 2-4)

Core function judgment: JAK1 is a receptor-associated, non-receptor protein
tyrosine kinase that phosphorylates receptor tails and STAT proteins in
cytokine signaling. UniProt describes JAK1 as a non-receptor tyrosine kinase
involved in IFN-alpha/beta/gamma pathways, IL-2 and IL-10 receptor signaling,
and direct STAT phosphorylation [UniProt:P52332 "Directly phosphorylates STAT
proteins"]. Mouse conditional knockout resources and cytokine-signaling studies
support a broad immune/cytokine signaling role [PMID:27671227 "Generation of
Janus kinase 1 (JAK1) conditional knockout mice"; PMID:8272873 "Association and
activation of Jak-Tyk kinases"].

Curation stance: non-membrane spanning protein tyrosine kinase activity,
protein tyrosine kinase activity, and tyrosine phosphorylation of STAT proteins
are core. Individual cytokine receptor pathways are valid but context-specific
and generally non-core. Generic binding, broad transferase/kinase labels, metal
ion/nucleotide binding, and vague intracellular signaling terms are marked
over-annotated when a more precise JAK1 activity or pathway term is available.
