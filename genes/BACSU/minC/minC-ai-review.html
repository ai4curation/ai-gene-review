Gene Description

MinC is the septum site-determining protein that functions as the primary effector of the Min system in Bacillus subtilis. It acts as a direct inhibitor of FtsZ polymerization, preventing Z-ring formation at cell poles to ensure accurate midcell division. MinC has a bipartite domain architecture: the N-terminal domain (MinCN) binds to the H9/H10 helix region of FtsZ and inhibits FtsZ filament bundling/stability, while the C-terminal domain (MinCC) mediates dimerization, MinD binding, and targeting to FtsZ via the C-terminal peptide (CTP). Unlike E. coli, B. subtilis lacks MinE and instead uses the DivIVA-MinJ-MinCD system for spatial regulation. DivIVA senses negative membrane curvature at cell poles, recruits MinJ (an integral membrane protein), which in turn bridges to MinCD. This creates a stationary bipolar gradient that concentrates MinC at cell poles, preventing aberrant polar septation while allowing midcell Z-ring assembly. During cytokinesis, DivIVA/MinJ form double rings flanking the nascent septum, spatially separating MinC from the active division site. Loss of minC leads to characteristic minicell formation (small DNA-less polar divisions), cell elongation, and persistent polar FtsZ rings. MinC also contributes to disassembly of completed cytokinetic rings after division. The MinCD complex promotes FtsZ filament fragmentation at membranes through a mechanism distinct from simple sequestration.

Comprehensive research report: Bacillus subtilis MinC (minC; UniProt Q01463)

1) Key concepts and definitions
- Identity and family: MinC is the septum site-determining protein of Bacillus subtilis (strain 168), part of the MinC family. It is a dimeric inhibitor of FtsZ assembly and functions within the MinCDJ/DivIVA system that spatially regulates division-site selection in rod-shaped Gram-positive bacteria (B. subtilis lacks MinE). Domain architecture is bipartite, with an N-terminal inhibitory domain (MinCN) and a conserved C-terminal domain (MinCC) that mediates dimerization and binding to MinD (ATPase) and, in some contexts, the FtsZ C-terminal tail (CTP) (park2018mincandftsz pages 2-3, blasios2013geneticandbiochemical pages 1-2, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27).
- Core pathway context: B. subtilis employs both the Min system and nucleoid occlusion (Noc) to ensure medial Z-ring formation and to prevent polar septation. DivIVA senses negative membrane curvature and recruits MinJ, which bridges to MinCD. This organizes a stationary bipolar gradient that relocalizes to nascent septa, contrasting with the MinE-driven oscillatory system of E. coli (feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31, eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1).

2) Molecular function and mechanism
- Direct FtsZ inhibitor: MinC binds FtsZ and suppresses assembly. In B. subtilis, MinC interaction sites on FtsZ map to helices H9/H10 in the C-terminal domain and to the extreme C-terminal peptide; mutations in these regions confer Min resistance while maintaining normal FtsZ polymerization, indicating a specific inhibitory interface (blasios2013geneticandbiochemical pages 1-2).
- Domain-resolved activities: Multiple modes of inhibition are supported. MinCN can cap/sequester FtsZ subunits, whereas MinCC reduces filament bundling and mediates MinD-dependent targeting. When complexed with MinD on membranes, MinC/MinD promotes fragmentation of FtsZ filaments and increases subunit detachment rates, distinguishing MinC-alone inhibition (sequestration/capping) from MinC/MinD-mediated filament severing (park2018mincandftsz pages 2-3).

3) Interactions and spatial regulation
- Interactors: MinC forms a functional complex with MinD (a Walker-type ATPase). MinJ (membrane protein) bridges DivIVA to MinCD, enabling DivIVA-mediated curvature sensing to position the Min complex. This architecture both concentrates Min activity at cell poles and, during septation, at double rings flanking the septum (eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31).
- Localization dynamics: Super-resolution imaging shows DivIVA and MinJ assemble as double rings that flank the nascent septum, recruiting MinCD to positions that are spatially separated from the midcell FtsZ ring, thereby allowing cytokinesis to proceed while still suppressing ectopic polar Z-rings. After division, DivIVA structures collapse into polar patches; the Min gradient reforms in daughter cells (eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27).

4) Biological process and localization
- Process: MinC acts in cell division site selection and divisome remodeling. It prevents Z-ring assembly at the poles and contributes to disassembly of polar Z-rings after cytokinesis, ensuring one division event per cycle and proper cell sizing (feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31, eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1).
- Localization: MinC is targeted to the membrane via MinD and, in B. subtilis, is enriched at cell poles under DivIVA‚ÄìMinJ control. During active division, Min components accumulate at septal sites as flanking rings/patches; MinC itself is positioned so as not to directly overlap the FtsZ ring at midcell, consistent with regulated inhibition (eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27).

5) Mutant phenotypes and pathway cross-talk
- Phenotypes: Loss-of-function mutations in minC/minD/minJ produce characteristic minicells (DNA-less polar buds), elongated rods, persistent polar Z-rings, and evidence of impaired divisome disassembly. The Min system also helps recycle FtsZ from polar rings; failure to recycle increases cell length while division time may be maintained by accumulating sufficient FtsZ at midcell (feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27).
- Divisome disassembly: Genetic studies indicate MinCDJ prevents minicell formation by promoting disassembly of completed cytokinetic rings, acting not only at the level of FtsZ polymerization but also on membrane-integral divisome components downstream (summarized in later work) (feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31).
- Nucleoid occlusion: The Min system complements Noc. Synthetic defects arise when both pathways are compromised, underscoring parallel negative regulation of division over chromosomal DNA and near poles (feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27).

6) Quantitative and structural data
- Binding site mapping: A screen identified 13 Min-resistant FtsZ mutants clustering in H9/H10 and in the CTP, showing that these discrete surfaces mediate MinC binding in B. subtilis. Mutants retained normal polymerization but had reduced MinC interaction, supporting specificity (blasios2013geneticandbiochemical pages 1-2).
- Kinetics on membranes: In vitro supported bilayer assays show that MinC decreases FtsZ attachment rates and increases detachment rates; in the presence of MinD, fragmentation of filaments is favored, consistent with a severing mechanism rather than simple sequestration (park2018mincandftsz pages 2-3).
- Spatial ultrastructure: DivIVA/MinJ double-ring architecture flanking the septum, stable at division sites and collapsing into polar patches after cytokinesis, provides quantitative nanoscale organization that rationalizes how MinC activity is permitted at the right time and place (eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1).

7) Recent developments and latest research
- Dynamic Min system in B. subtilis: Contrary to an entirely static view, live-cell imaging and modeling demonstrate dynamic localization of Min proteins to active division sites, often forming clusters, with a dynamic equilibrium driven by ongoing septation. This aligns some mechanistic parallels with E. coli while preserving B. subtilis-specific DivIVA/MinJ guidance (mBio 2021) (feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27).
- Emerging mechanistic models: Work integrating domain functions and MinC/MinD activities supports a model of MinC/MinD-induced filament fragmentation at membranes and spatial separation from the Z-ring via DivIVA/MinJ ring architecture, refining earlier ‚Äúgradient only‚Äù models (park2018mincandftsz pages 2-3, eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31).

8) Current applications and real-world implementations
- Tool for dissecting spatial regulation: The B. subtilis MinCDJ/DivIVA system is widely used as a model for understanding spatial cues and reaction‚Äìdiffusion or curvature-sensing mechanisms in bacterial cytokinesis, informing synthetic biology designs for spatial patterning and division control (feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27, eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1).
- Targeting division: While FtsZ has been extensively pursued as an antibacterial target, the B. subtilis studies here primarily support mechanistic understanding of how MinC/MinD modulate FtsZ rather than directly demonstrating clinical applications; nonetheless, Min-mediated fragmentation and disassembly pathways provide conceptual avenues for interfering with Z-ring robustness (park2018mincandftsz pages 2-3, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31).

9) Expert opinions and authoritative syntheses
- DivIVA-mediated spatial regulation is proposed as the primary means to hold Min activity away from the midcell FtsZ during cytokinesis, allowing division to proceed‚Äîan interpretation supported by super-resolution imaging (mBio 2011). This emphasizes cellular architecture as a regulatory layer for Min activity (eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1).
- The 2021 mBio analysis argues that Min in B. subtilis exhibits dynamic enrichment at septa and acts downstream to control active division sites and Z-ring disassembly, bridging the conceptual gap between Gram-negative oscillatory and Gram-positive curvature-guided systems (feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27).

Conclusion
MinC (Q01463) in B. subtilis strain 168 is a Min family FtsZ inhibitor that acts within the DivIVA‚ÄìMinJ‚ÄìMinCD axis to prevent polar division and to promote timely disassembly of spent Z-rings. Its bipartite domain architecture underlies multiple inhibitory modes, including MinD-dependent filament fragmentation at membranes. Spatial organization by DivIVA/MinJ double rings reconciles strong local Min activity with uninterrupted midcell cytokinesis. Phenotypic signatures (minicells, elongated cells, persistent polar FtsZ rings) validate its central role. While 2021 mBio data provide the most recent comprehensive, peer-reviewed dynamic view, additional 2022+ models and nanoscopy advances are consistent with these mechanistic foundations and merit integration as they become available (feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 24-27, park2018mincandftsz pages 2-3, blasios2013geneticandbiochemical pages 1-2, feddersen2021dynamicsofthe pages 27-31, eswaramoorthy2011cellulararchitecturemediates pages 1-1).
