Key concepts and definitions with current understanding
- Molecular identity and domain architecture: B. subtilis FliY is a multidomain protein with an N-terminal CheY-binding region homologous to the FliM N-terminus, a middle CheC-like phosphatase domain with conserved Glu/Asn catalytic residues, and a C-terminal FliN-like domain that places FliY within the FliN/MopA/SpaO family associated with the flagellar C-ring (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 47-51, gonzalezbonet2010structuralbiochemicaland pages 94-99, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 57-61, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 65-70, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 70-77). Functionally, FliY in Gram-positive bacteria replaces or supplements FliN as a switch component in the C-ring (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 128-132).
- Molecular function and substrate: FliY is a CheY-P phosphatase that catalytically dephosphorylates the response regulator CheY when phosphorylated (CheY-P), contributing to signal termination near the motor. FliY contains two CheC-like active centers; mutagenesis indicates one dominant catalytic site, and activity is catalytic rather than simple sequestration (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 57-61, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 61-65, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 65-70, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 70-77).
- Localization and interactions: FliY localizes at or near the flagellar rotor (C-ring), with its C-terminus homologous to FliN/FliMc. B. subtilis FliY can functionally complement a Salmonella fliN mutant, reinforcing its C-ring role; biochemical reconstitution indicates interactions with switch proteins in some systems (gonzalezbonet2010structuralbiochemicaland pages 94-99, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 61-65, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 128-132).
- Pathway placement: B. subtilis employs a “three-phosphatase” system for CheY-P: CheC, FliY, and CheX, in contrast to E. coli’s CheZ. FliY is motor-associated, likely providing local dephosphorylation at the site of CheY-P action to fine-tune rotational switching (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 70-77, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 128-132).

Current applications and real-world implementations
- Synthetic biology/engineering of motility: Knowledge that B. subtilis utilizes a motor-associated CheY-P phosphatase (FliY) rather than CheZ informs rational design of chemotaxis-modulated strains for rhizosphere colonization or bioprocessing; experimental evolution work shows motility/chemotaxis (including fliY) adapt under plant-associated selection, relevant for agricultural biostimulants (hu2023parallelgeneticadaptation pages 8-11, mahmoud2024comparativegenomicanalysis pages 16-18).
- Antimicrobial strategies: Reviews propose targeting chemotaxis/motility pathways; although not FliY-specific, understanding motor-localized phosphatase control (FliY) shapes hypotheses for disrupting chemotaxis-driven colonization in pathogens that deploy analogous systems (fu2024decipheringbacterialchemorepulsion pages 7-9).

Expert opinions and analysis from authoritative sources
- Structural/biochemical analyses highlight FliY as a hybrid switch protein with CheC-like catalytic centers positioned at the C-ring, likely optimizing local CheY-P turnover to set motor bias; functional overlap with FliN is supported by heterologous complementation, but FliY has unique catalytic roles absent from canonical FliN (gonzalezbonet2010structuralbiochemicaland pages 94-99, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 61-65, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 128-132).
- System-level interpretation: The three-phosphatase architecture in B. subtilis provides robustness and tunability; CheX often exhibits higher in vitro activity, while FliY’s localization may compensate via proximity effects, ensuring rapid termination of CheY-P near its target (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 128-132, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 70-77).

Relevant statistics, data, and specific findings
- Dual active sites and catalytic dominance: In Thermotoga maritima FliY, both active sites bind CheY-P but one site (Glu132/Asn135) dominates catalysis; mutation of both sites abolishes activity. Example assay conditions show catalytic turnover even when CheY is present at hundreds of μM, indicating enzymatic dephosphorylation rather than stoichiometric binding (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 57-61).
- Complementation and localization evidence: B. subtilis FliY complements Salmonella fliN amber mutants, restoring motility, consistent with a C-ring structural role overlapping FliN; sequence motifs (GGXG) suggest conserved switch interactions (gonzalezbonet2010structuralbiochemicaland pages 94-99, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 61-65, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 70-77).
- Recent evolution data: fliY was among motility/flagellar genes mutated during root-associated evolution experiments (2023), alongside fliG, fliM, flhB, hag, indicating its participation in adaptive tuning of motility/chemotaxis during host interaction (hu2023parallelgeneticadaptation pages 8-11).

Primary function, pathway role, and localization (concise synthesis)
- Primary function: Motor-associated CheY-P phosphatase that catalyzes dephosphorylation of CheY-P; contains CheC-like catalytic centers and an N-terminal CheY-binding element (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 57-61, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 65-70).
- Substrate specificity: CheY-P (response regulator of chemotaxis) (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 57-61, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 70-77).
- Localization: C-ring of the flagellar motor; FliY serves in many Gram-positive bacteria as FliN-like switch component with added phosphatase activity (gonzalezbonet2010structuralbiochemicaland pages 94-99, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 128-132).
- Pathway: Part of B. subtilis chemotaxis network; contributes to termination and shaping of CheY-P signals together with CheC and CheX; compensates for absence of CheZ in B. subtilis (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 70-77, fu2024decipheringbacterialchemorepulsion pages 7-9).

Outstanding questions and limitations
- Precise in vivo kinetics and stoichiometry for B. subtilis FliY at the motor remain incompletely defined; much quantitative kinetic/structural detail derives from T. maritima FliY or in vitro constructs. High-resolution placement within the C-ring and dynamic interactions with FliM/FliG in B. subtilis would benefit from new cryo-EM or in situ biophysical studies (sircar2013architectureofthe pages 61-65, sircar2013architectureofthe pages 128-132).
