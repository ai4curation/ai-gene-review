Comprehensive research report: amyE (UniProt P00691), Bacillus subtilis 168

1) Identity verification and key definitions
- Gene/protein identity: amyE encodes the secreted alpha-amylase of Bacillus subtilis (strain 168), a glycoside hydrolase family 13 (GH13) enzyme that hydrolyzes internal α-1,4-glycosidic linkages in starch and related α-glucans (retaining mechanism). This aligns with UniProt P00691 and the target organism. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 5-6, jaiswal2024thermostableαamylasesand pages 4-5)
- Family/domains: GH13 alpha-amylases possess a catalytic (β/α)8 barrel (domain A) with an inserted domain B and a C-terminal β-sandwich (Amy_C). Many Bacillus enzymes also carry carbohydrate-binding modules (CBMs); homology-based analyses of B. subtilis α-amylases closely related to AmyE indicate a C-terminal CBM26 module. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 3-5, paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 5-6)

2) Primary biochemical function and catalytic mechanism
- Reaction: Endo-acting hydrolysis of (1→4)-α-D-glucan linkages in starch and maltooligosaccharides, generating mainly maltose and glucose under many assay conditions; cyclodextrins and branched substrates are also hydrolyzed at lower rates in representative B. subtilis enzymes. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 5-6, paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 6-8)
- Mechanism: GH13 enzymes use a double-displacement α-retaining mechanism with a catalytic nucleophile (Asp), a general acid/base (Glu), and a transition-state stabilizing Asp; all embedded in conserved GH13 sequence motifs within the (β/α)8 scaffold. Expert reviews and recent syntheses reaffirm these features. (jaiswal2024thermostableαamylasesand pages 4-5)

3) Substrate specificity and product profile
- Substrate spectrum: Representative B. subtilis α-amylase highly similar to AmyE (97.4% identity to P00691) shows activity on starches with preference potato > wheat > corn > rice; it hydrolyzes amylose/amylopectin more weakly than soluble starch, and acts on γ- and β-cyclodextrins at lower rates. Products from common starches are predominantly maltose and glucose. These data are informative for AmyE due to near-identity and shared GH13 architecture. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 5-6, paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 6-8)

4) Domain architecture and sequence features
- Signal peptide: A canonical N-terminal signal peptide directs Sec-dependent secretion; in a closely related B. subtilis α-amylase, cleavage is between residues 31–32. During extracellular maturation, the C-terminus can be processed, consistent with the modular C-terminal domain and/or CBM removal in secreted forms. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 3-5, paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 6-8)
- Catalytic core: The A/B domains constitute the GH13 catalytic machinery; the C-terminal Amy_C domain and optional CBM26 contribute to stability and raw-starch binding. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 3-5, paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 5-6)

5) Cellular localization and secretion pathway
- Secretion route: AmyE is exported via the Sec pathway. Strach et al. (2023) directly visualized AmyE-mCherry and observed discrete, persistent “secretion zones” within the Gram-positive cell wall; these zones appear primarily at the transition to stationary phase and correspond to surface-detectable amylase activity. SecA/SecDF dynamics at the membrane support Sec-mediated translocation followed by slow diffusion through the peptidoglycan mesh. (strach2023proteinsecretionzones pages 1-2)
- Quantitative secretion dynamics: In B. subtilis, cells with visible AmyE secretion puncta increased from ~4–5% in mid-exponential phase to ~23% at transition and ~19–34% in stationary phase (strain- and condition-dependent). These data substantiate extracellular localization and phase-dependent secretion heterogeneity. (strach2023proteinsecretionzones pages 2-3)

6) Regulation of expression (promoters; growth phase)
- Native/engineering promoters: Put et al. (2024) catalog promoter resources in Bacillus, including Pamy (amyE promoter, described as intermediate strength) and inducible promoters (PxylA, Pgrac, Pspank/Pspank-hy, Pglv, PsacB). Signal peptides including SPamyE are used as secretion tags in expression cassettes. These parts underpin controlled expression of endogenous and heterologous proteins. (put2024bacillussubtilisas pages 20-21, put2024bacillussubtilisas pages 21-22)
- Growth phase: Secretion zones and extracellular activity of AmyE become prominent at the transition to stationary phase in laboratory overexpression, suggesting physiological coupling between secretion capacity and growth phase. (strach2023proteinsecretionzones pages 1-2)

7) Biochemical properties and quantitative data (representative for AmyE homologs)
- Optima: A B. subtilis α-amylase 97% identical to AmyE shows temperature optimum ~65 °C and pH optimum ~6.0; retains substantial activity across 55–65 °C and tolerates mildly acidic pH in the wild-type secreted form. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 5-6, paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 6-8)
- Specific activity: Recombinant enzyme specific activity ~1,280 U/mg under standard assay (65 °C, pH 6.0; DNS assay). (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 5-6)
- Metal/detergent effects: Activity is EDTA-tolerant in that study; strong inhibition by Cu2+ (>90%) and partial inhibition by Fe2+/Zn2+; SDS (1%) ~80% inhibition, whereas nonionic detergents had minor effects. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 8-10)
- Processing: In the secreted native form, apparent molecular weight can be lower than the full precursor, consistent with signal peptide removal and possible C-terminal trimming. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 6-8)

8) Current applications and real-world implementations
- Industrial enzyme: Bacillus α-amylases are industrial workhorses in starch processing (baking, brewing, syrups); recent studies of B. subtilis enzymes highlight raw-starch activity and process-relevant stability profiles. (paloyan2024molecularcloningand pages 8-10)
- Secretion host and parts: Bacillus subtilis is widely used as a secretion host; engineered strains and toolkits leverage SPamyE for secretion and a palette of promoters (Pamy, Pveg, P43, PxylA, Pgrac, etc.) for tunable expression. (put2024bacillussubtilisas pages 20-21, put2024bacillussubtilisas pages 21-22)
- Neutral chromosomal integration site: The amyE locus is a standard “neutral” integration site in B. subtilis 168. For example, Pérez‑Lorente et al. (2023) integrated constructs with pDR111 at amyE (selected by spectinomycin), demonstrating contemporary use in 2023 work. (perezlorente2023sporulationactivatedvia pages 12-13)
- Secretion optimization: Reviews emphasize extracellular protease-deficient strains and genome reductions to stabilize secreted products; engineered backgrounds include sequential knockouts (e.g., nprE, aprE, amyE) to reduce proteolysis and modulate background enzymes for improved heterologous protein recovery. (liu2025engineeringbacillussubtilis pages 2-4)

9) Expert opinions and analysis (authoritative sources, 2023–2024)
- GH13 mechanism and engineering: Contemporary reviews affirm the canonical GH13 retaining mechanism and highlight protein engineering to tune thermostability, Ca2+ dependence, and catalytic traits—principles applicable to AmyE. (jaiswal2024thermostableαamylasesand pages 4-5)
- Secretion biology in Gram-positives: Strach et al. (2023) provide an updated, single-molecule perspective on the spatial organization of secretion through the thick cell wall, relevant for AmyE localization and for optimizing secretion of heterologous proteins fused to SPamyE. (strach2023proteinsecretionzones pages 1-2)
- Bacillus as expression chassis: Put et al. (2024) consolidate modern promoter/toolbox options, secretion capacity considerations, and strategies for heterologous pathway expression—situating amyE (as a locus and signal peptide source) within current best practices. (put2024bacillussubtilisas pages 20-21, put2024bacillussubtilisas pages 21-22)
