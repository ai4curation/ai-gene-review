Functionally, CRT1’s presence in the arm **sucker neurons** enables largely autonomous reflexes and behaviors at the level of the arm. Because more than **two-thirds of an octopus’s neurons are in its arms** (distributed in axial nerve cords and sucker ganglia) ([news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2020/10/octopus-suction-cups-hold-its-taste-and-touch-sensors/#:~:text=Octopus%20arms%20are%20distinct%20and,for%2C%20identify%2C%20and%20grasp%20items)), signals from CRT1 can be processed locally without requiring immediate input from the central brain ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=The%20new%20paper%20also%20shows,limbs%20act%20on%20stimuli%20independently)) ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=The%20new%20paper%20also%20shows,limbs%20act%20on%20stimuli%20independently)). When CRT1 on a sucker detects a chemical cue, the arm’s local circuit quickly interprets it and can trigger actions such as exploratory probing, grabbing, or recoil *before* the information ever reaches the brain ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=suckers%2C%20which%20send%20signals%20to,length%20of%20the%20octopus%E2%80%99s%20limb)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=mediate%20the%20detection%20of%20diverse,suit%20an%20animal%E2%80%99s%20environmental%20context)). This is vividly demonstrated in behavioral assays: an octopus will sweep its arms over a surface and, upon contacting certain chemical stimuli, change its behavior almost instantly. For example, if one side of a tank or object is coated with a CRT1 agonist (such as a terpene), the octopus arm will **alter its movement pattern – transitioning to rapid tapping and withdrawal** on that treated side, presumably having “tasted” something significant ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=match%20at%20L128%20sensed%20the,the%20area%20with%20the%20molecule)). In one experiment, when octopuses touched surfaces laced with a terpene compound, they stopped their routine searching and repeatedly tapped the site briefly before recoiling and avoiding it ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=mechanism,for%20food%2C%20telling%20them%20to)). This suggests that CRT1 can signal a **negative or exciting cue** (e.g. a prey’s defensive chemical) that the arm’s nervous system interprets as “do not continue probing here” or “approach with caution.” Conversely, other CR ligands might encourage the octopus to grasp a substrate if it indicates prey. Indeed, CRT1 and its relatives respond to **prey-derived cues** – for instance, fish or crab extracts elicit strong activity – which likely helps the octopus confirm a touched object is living prey and worth seizing ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=Similar%20to%20descriptions%20of%20octopus,search%2C%20thus%20suggesting%20a%20role)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=that%20suckers%20contain%20specialized%20chemosensory,taste%E2%80%99)). Importantly, the **contact-dependent** nature of this sensing means the octopus can discern chemical information in very close-range or hidden scenarios (such as within crevices on the seafloor) where waterborne odors alone might fail ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=terrestrial%20life%20necessitates%20the%20detection,produce%20and%20respond%20to%20a)) ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=Just%20defining%20what%20the%20sense,the%20octopus%2C%20Bellono%20asks%2C%20%E2%80%9CIs)). This confers a predatory advantage in the octopus’s benthic habitat, allowing it to **navigate complex environments by touch-taste** rather than vision or distant smell ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=Animals%20display%20wide,and%20transduction%20of%20specific%20ionic)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=compounds%2C%20suggesting%20a%20distinct%20form,mode%20of%20sensation%20are%20unknown)).  

Moreover, CRT1-based sensing is integrated with the octopus’s other survival behaviors. A striking example of this integration is the interaction between chemotactile signals and the octopus’s defensive ink. Researchers found that **octopus ink can suppress CRT1-mediated sensing**, effectively masking the chemotactile cues of food when the animal is in a state of alarm ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=The%20ability%20of%20these%20receptors,for%20food%2C%20telling%20them%20to)). In lab tests, the presence of octopus ink extract inhibited CRT1 responses to terpenoid stimuli, suggesting that during escape responses (when ink is released), the octopus’s peripheral nervous system prioritizes “danger” signals over “food” signals ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=The%20ability%20of%20these%20receptors,for%20food%2C%20telling%20them%20to)). This modulation ensures that an octopus fleeing a threat will not be distracted by prey signals – a clear adaptive benefit. All together, CRT1 operates within a **network of peripheral sensors and interneurons** that enable the octopus arm to make context-dependent decisions: it can distinguish rocks from crabs by tasting chemicals on their surface ([news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2020/10/octopus-suction-cups-hold-its-taste-and-touch-sensors/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CWe%20think%20because%20the%20molecules,%E2%80%99%E2%80%9D)), determine if a touched object is palatable or noxious, and coordinate with other sensory inputs (like mechanosensation or alarm cues) to guide appropriate behaviors ([news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2020/10/octopus-suction-cups-hold-its-taste-and-touch-sensors/#:~:text=instance%2C%20be%20found%20on%20the,%E2%80%99%E2%80%9D)) ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=The%20ability%20of%20these%20receptors,for%20food%2C%20telling%20them%20to)). By localizing this sensing to the arms and suckers, octopuses achieve a high degree of independent intelligence in each limb, largely thanks to receptors like CRT1 that connect environmental chemistry to neural activity at the site of contact.  

## Evolutionary Origin and Structural Features  
From an evolutionary perspective, CRT1 represents a **novel adaptation of an ancient receptor family**. Comparative genomics and phylogenetic analyses indicate that chemotactile receptors in octopus (including CRT1) **diverged from nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs)**, repurposing the basic pentameric ligand-gated channel design for environmental sensing ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=suckers%2C%20which%20the%20octopus%20uses,including%20protostome%20ionotropic%20receptors%20that)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=ionotropic%20neurotransmitter%20receptors,7%20%2C%2019)). In the *O. bimaculoides* genome, the CRT1 gene is one of **26 closely related CR genes** that are clustered in tandem on a single chromosome (chromosome 15) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=Scale%20bars%2C%201%20mm%20,extraceilular%20space%20of%20the%20receptor)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=In%20Octopus%20bimaculoides%2C%2026%20intronless,in%20CR%20sequences%20compared%20to)). Intriguingly, all these CR genes **lack introns**, implying they may have originated via retrotransposition events from an ancestral neuronal AChR gene ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=In%20Octopus%20bimaculoides%2C%2026%20intronless,in%20CR%20sequences%20compared%20to)). This mode of expansion – likely through gene duplication and insertion of reverse-transcribed mRNA copies – produced a family of *retrogenes* that subsequently specialized for chemosensation ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=In%20Octopus%20bimaculoides%2C%2026%20intronless,in%20CR%20sequences%20compared%20to)). Far from being inactive duplicates, the CR genes have intact open reading frames and show signs of **purifying selection** (ω < 1), indicating they are functional receptors under evolutionary constraint ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=retrotransposition%20have%20contributed%20to%20the,mediate%20sensory%20specialization%20versus%20neurotransmission)). At the same time, the CR lineage exhibits an **accelerated rate of sequence evolution** compared to classical nicotinic receptors, particularly in regions related to ligand recognition ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=Scale%20bars%2C%201%20mm%20,extraceilular%20space%20of%20the%20receptor)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=expansion%20of%2026%20intronless%20genes,subunit%20structure%20of%20octopus%20CRT1)). A statistical analysis found the CR sequences evolving significantly faster (higher nonsynonymous substitution rates) than their nAChR counterparts (**p < 0.001** in a likelihood ratio test) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=Scale%20bars%2C%201%20mm%20,extraceilular%20space%20of%20the%20receptor)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=expansion%20of%2026%20intronless%20genes,subunit%20structure%20of%20octopus%20CRT1)). This suggests **diversifying selection** has acted on the chemotactile receptors – likely as octopuses adapted to detect a broad range of new chemical cues in their environment. In support of this, key amino acids in CRT1’s **orthosteric ligand-binding domain** show high variability and signatures of positive selection across octopus species ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37045920/#:~:text=Extended%20Data%20Fig.%208%20,under%20strongly%20positive%20selective%20pressure)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). These changes correlate with the receptor’s ability to bind **“new” ligands (hydrophobic environmental molecules) rather than the small, polar neurotransmitters** that ancestral receptors recognize ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). In essence, evolution repurposed the neurotransmitter receptor scaffold to create a **sensory receptor** subfamily, with the major innovations occurring at the extracellular ligand-binding site while the overall channel architecture remained conserved ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=sensory%20receptor%20evolution,of%20greasy%20compounds%20versus%20the)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)).  

Structural biology advances have recently illuminated CRT1’s unique features at an atomic level. In 2023, Allard *et al.* reported the **cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) structure** of CRT1, providing a direct comparison to canonical nAChR structures ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=sensory%20receptor%20evolution,of%20greasy%20compounds%20versus%20the)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=particlecryo,Indeed%2C%20each)). The CRT1 protein was expressed and purified, and its structure solved at **2.6 Å resolution**, revealing a homopentameric arrangement very similar to that of the vertebrate α7 nicotinic receptor (which was used as a benchmark) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=detergent%20glyco,loop)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=assembles%20to%20form%20a%20homopentameric,mutations%20of%20this%20second%20disulphide)). Each CRT1 subunit has the expected **large extracellular domain (ECD)** composed of ten β-strands (forming the ligand-binding β-sandwich) and the signature **Cys-loop** (a disulfide bond linking β6–β7) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=assembles%20to%20form%20a%20homopentameric,mutations%20of%20this%20second%20disulphide)). The five subunits form a rosette, creating a central ion pore lined by the five M2 transmembrane helices – again analogous to other Cys-loop receptors ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=domains%20%28ECDs%29%20%28Fig,loop)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=assembles%20to%20form%20a%20homopentameric,mutations%20of%20this%20second%20disulphide)). This conserved **channel architecture** explains why CRT1 still operates as a cation-conducting channel with typical gating elements ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=sensory%20receptor%20evolution,of%20greasy%20compounds%20versus%20the)). However, the high-resolution structure also uncovered **distinct structural adaptations** of CRT1 that underlie its sensory function. Notably, CRT1 possesses an **additional disulfide bond** near the ligand-binding site, formed between loops β4 and β7 of the ECD – a feature not seen in conventional neurotransmitter receptors ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=subunit%20of%20CRT1%20shares%20a,The)). This extra disulfide links parts of the binding pocket, apparently **increasing the rigidity** of the extracellular domain ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=N,M3%20helices)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=disulphide%20near%20the%20classical%20Cys,be%20present%20by%20primary%20sequence)). Mutagenesis experiments confirmed its importance: breaking this disulfide (via cysteine-to-alanine mutations) rendered the receptor non-functional, suggesting that the bond is critical for maintaining the proper structure needed for channel gating or stability ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=N,M3%20helices)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=disulphide%20near%20the%20classical%20Cys,29%20Extended%20Data%20Fig)). Such a modification may have evolved to stabilize CRT1 when binding bulky hydrophobic ligands or to support the receptor’s operation in the relatively harsh external environment of the sucker epithelium.  

Most strikingly, the **ligand-binding pocket of CRT1 is highly hydrophobic** in composition and architecture ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). The cryo-EM study found that this pocket is much more **non-polar** than in acetylcholine receptors, which typically bind small charged neurotransmitters ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). In fact, when CRT1’s structure was solved, **hydrophobic molecules (from the experimental preparation) were observed lodged in the binding site** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)) ([pdbj.org](https://pdbj.org/emnavi/quick.php?id=PDB-9e6d#:~:text=Image%20Authors%20%20,cues%20to%20navigate%20their)). This serendipitous finding (in one case, a steroidal detergent molecule occupied the pocket) illustrated how CRT1’s binding site can accommodate “greasy” ligands ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). By contrast, canonical nAChRs have binding sites optimized for **small polar agonists** like acetylcholine ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). The CRT1 structure thus provides a clear **structural basis for its function**: an enlarged, hydrophobic cavity ready to engage large, water-insoluble molecules, secured by an ECD framework made more rigid by an extra disulfide. These differences highlight how a few critical changes can convert a neurotransmitter receptor into a **environmental chemoreceptor**. As Allard *et al.* put it, the octopus CR ligand pocket is “exceptionally hydrophobic, enabling sensation of greasy compounds versus the small polar molecules detected by canonical neurotransmitter receptors” ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). Importantly, the **transmembrane domain and ion channel pore** of CRT1 remain quite conserved relative to nAChR, so the principles of cation permeation and gating are similar ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=sensory%20receptor%20evolution,of%20greasy%20compounds%20versus%20the)). This means that evolution did not reinvent the wheel for signal transduction – it kept the ion channel function intact – but rather **innovated at the ligand interface** to allow detection of new chemical stimuli ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=sensory%20receptor%20evolution,of%20greasy%20compounds%20versus%20the)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). This finding ties into a broader theme in sensory evolution: as noted by the authors, other animal lineages have done something analogous (e.g. insect *ionotropic receptors* for smell diverged from glutamate-gated channels) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=terpenes%2C%20many%20of%20which%20are,specific%20trait.%20Considering%20the)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=from%20neurotransmitter%20receptors%2C%20including%20protostome,principles%20of%20sensory%20receptor%20evolution)). The octopus CRT1 is a dramatic example of how **molecular adaptation at the periphery of a receptor** (binding site) can drive the emergence of an entirely new sensory capability while the core signaling mechanism (ion channel gating) stays conserved.  

## Recent Developments and Latest Research (2023–2024)  
Research in the last few years has greatly advanced our understanding of CRT1 and the chemotactile receptor family. In 2020, the discovery of CRT1 was first reported by van Giesen *et al.* as part of the molecular basis for octopus chemotactile sensation ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=mediates%20relatively%20autonomous%20arm%20behavior,a%20key%20site%20for%20signal)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=C%29%20which%20exhibits%20highly,and%20form%20homomeric%20and%20heteromeric)). Since then, **follow-up studies in 2023 and 2024** have provided deeper insights into how these receptors function and evolve. Notably, the **2023 Nature study** by Allard *et al.* (discussed above) resolved the CRT1 structure and confirmed its evolutionary divergence from nAChRs ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=Chemotactile%20receptors%20,that%20channel%20architecture%20involved%20in)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=suckers%2C%20which%20the%20octopus%20uses,including%20protostome%20ionotropic%20receptors%20that)). This study highlighted specific adaptations (like the hydrophobic pocket and additional disulfide) that explain CRT1’s selectivity for environmental ligands ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=subunit%20of%20CRT1%20shares%20a,The)). It also showed that octopus CR genes form a tight genomic cluster and likely expanded through rapid duplication events, which is unusual for ligand-gated channels ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=In%20Octopus%20bimaculoides%2C%2026%20intronless,in%20CR%20sequences%20compared%20to)). The 2023 work provided a clear **structure-function framework**: it demonstrated that while **CRT1’s overall fold and ion channel function are conserved**, the **ligand-binding region has undergone positive selection**, correlating with its new sensory role ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=sensory%20receptor%20evolution,of%20greasy%20compounds%20versus%20the)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). These findings in 2023 answered many questions about *how* CRT1 recognizes ligands and hinted at *why* this family emerged (to let octopuses detect non-diffusible chemical cues in the benthic environment) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=octopuses%20to%20explore%20the%20seafloor,In%20contrast%2C%20the)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=%28nAChRs%29,principles%20of%20sensory%20receptor%20evolution)).  

Building on that, **very recent research (2024–2025)** has expanded the biological context of CRT1’s function. Initially, CRT1 was characterized with a focus on terpenoid cues from prey and predator-related sources (like defensive secretions and ink) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=match%20at%20L390%20al,2017%3B%20Long%20and%20Hay)) ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=The%20ability%20of%20these%20receptors,for%20food%2C%20telling%20them%20to)). However, a new line of investigation has revealed that CRT1 may also play a role in sensing **microbial metabolites** present on surfaces in the octopus’s habitat. In 2025, Sepela *et al.* reported that octopus chemotactile receptors can detect chemical signals produced by the **microbiomes** coating various surfaces – such as the biofilms on prey animals or on the octopus’s own eggs ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=explore%20the%20seafloor%2C%20but%20how,act%20at%20the%20level%20of)). This study, titled *“Environmental microbiomes drive chemotactile sensation in octopus,”* showed that distinct bacterial strains living on relevant surfaces secrete molecules that serve as **ligands for CRs** and influence octopus behavior ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=rocks%20and%20crevices%20they%20encounter,essential%20to%20understanding%20how%20animal)). For example, CRT1 was found to bind certain microbial-derived compounds, each inducing the receptor to adopt a slightly different conformation and mode of activation ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=rocks%20and%20crevices%20they%20encounter,essential%20to%20understanding%20how%20animal)). Remarkably, *one and the same* CRT1 receptor can thus respond to **multiple different ligands** – e.g. metabolites from different bacteria – and produce distinct ionic currents or signaling outcomes depending on the bound molecule ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=Diverse%20microbial%20signals%20bind%20single,CRs%20with%20distinct%20structural%20conformations)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=rocks%20and%20crevices%20they%20encounter,essential%20to%20understanding%20how%20animal)). In practical terms, this means CRT1 might help an octopus distinguish not just “prey vs non-prey,” but potentially **which prey or context** based on the specific cocktail of microbial cues on a surface. The 2025 study demonstrated that certain microbial signals activating CRT1 would drive **predatory behavior**, while others (like signals associated with the octopus’s eggs’ microbiome) could trigger **maternal care behaviors** ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=rocks%20and%20crevices%20they%20encounter,essential%20to%20understanding%20how%20animal)). In other words, CRT1 and its relatives are used by octopuses to read a “chemical signature” left by microbes, informing the animal whether a touched surface is, say, food or offspring or just inert rock ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=understood.%20Octopuses%20use%20%E2%80%9Ctaste,act%20at%20the%20level%20of)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=guide%20octopus%20behavior,essential%20to%20understanding%20how%20animal)). This discovery greatly broadens the scope of chemotactile sensing: it suggests octopus CRs are a platform for detecting a wide array of ecologically relevant chemical cues, from defensive terpenoids to bacterial metabolites. It underscores that the evolution of CRT1 was likely driven not only by direct prey-organism chemicals but also by the ubiquitous presence of **microbes in the environment**, which produce many small molecules. As the authors note, microbiomes on surfaces essentially create a chemical landscape that octopuses can exploit via CRT1, giving them information about their environment that is otherwise “invisible” ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=Microbial%20communities%20%20coat%20nearly,from%20individual%20%207%20bacterial)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=conformations%20to%20elicit%20specific%20mechanisms,essential%20to%20understanding%20how%20animal)). This 2025 advance is one of the latest developments, illustrating the ongoing research into CRT1’s diverse ligand repertoire and its role in complex natural behaviors.  

Beyond the octopus itself, these recent findings have piqued broad interest in sensory biology and evolution. CRT1 is now seen as a **prime example of sensory innovation**, demonstrating how a receptor can shift from neurotransmission to environmental detection ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=%28nAChRs%29,principles%20of%20sensory%20receptor%20evolution)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=from%20neurotransmitter%20receptors%2C%20including%20protostome,specific%20trait.%20Considering%20the)). Ongoing research (as of 2023–2024) is examining whether similar chemotactile receptor families exist in other cephalopods (like squids or cuttlefish) and how they might function ([news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2020/10/octopus-suction-cups-hold-its-taste-and-touch-sensors/#:~:text=The%20scientists%20believe%20this%20research,evolved%20to%20suit%20their%20environment)). There is also interest in profiling the full range of chemicals that CRT1 can detect, using approaches like high-throughput ligand screening and structural docking. The ultimate goal is to map CRT1’s “ligand space” – potentially identifying novel natural compounds that octopuses respond to, and understanding the **structure-activity relationships** that make CRT1 bind one molecule but not another. As this field is very new (the first characterization was in 2020), the period of 2023–2024 has been pivotal in moving from the initial discovery to a detailed molecular understanding, with more developments surely on the horizon as techniques improve (e.g. co-crystallizing CRT1 with actual ligands, engineering receptors in vitro, etc.). In summary, the latest research portrays CRT1 as a **highly adaptable sensory receptor** whose evolution is tightly connected to the octopus’s ecological niche – and current studies continue to reveal new dimensions of its function, from ligand diversity to behavioral impact ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=guide%20octopus%20behavior,essential%20to%20understanding%20how%20animal)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867425006208#:~:text=conformations%20to%20elicit%20specific%20mechanisms,essential%20to%20understanding%20how%20animal)).  

## Current Applications and Real-World Implications  
The discovery and characterization of CRT1 primarily advance our **scientific understanding** of octopus biology and sensory evolution, rather than yielding an immediate technological application. However, there are several noteworthy real-world implications of this research, both for the octopus in its natural environment and for broader scientific or biotechnological contexts.  

For the octopus itself, CRT1 is an essential adaptation that enhances survival and foraging success in the wild. **Ecologically, CRT1 equips octopuses with a refined ability to interpret their surroundings by touch**, which is particularly useful in the dark or cluttered seafloor habitats they frequent ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=Animals%20display%20wide,and%20transduction%20of%20specific%20ionic)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=compounds%2C%20suggesting%20a%20distinct%20form,mode%20of%20sensation%20are%20unknown)). An octopus hunting in a crevice can use its arms to *feel* and *taste* at the same time – for instance, detecting the chemical trail of a hidden crab or snail. This chemotactile sense means an octopus doesn’t rely solely on vision or waterborne odors; instead, it can literally **taste prey upon contact**, giving it immediate feedback on whether to strike or keep searching ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=Similar%20to%20descriptions%20of%20octopus,search%2C%20thus%20suggesting%20a%20role)) ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=invertebrates%3A%20a%20unique%20type%20of,length%20of%20the%20octopus%E2%80%99s%20limb)). The presence of CRT1 and related receptors in the suckers thus directly correlates with the octopus’s ability to find food in hard-to-see places (a “real-world implementation” by the animal of this molecular tool). Furthermore, CRT1 can alert the octopus to **potential dangers or deterrents**. Many marine invertebrates (potential prey) carry toxic or unpalatable compounds (like terpenoids) on their skin as a defense; by sensing those via CRT1, an octopus might identify a prey item as harmful and decide to avoid or handle it carefully ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=match%20at%20L390%20al,2017%3B%20Long%20and%20Hay)) ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=match%20at%20L128%20sensed%20the,the%20area%20with%20the%20molecule)). The observed behavior of octopuses retracting their arms from surfaces coated with a bitter terpene illustrates this point ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=match%20at%20L128%20sensed%20the,the%20area%20with%20the%20molecule)). Likewise, the fact that octopus ink can suppress chemotactile signals implies an in-situ application: in a cloud of ink (stress scenario), an octopus effectively “dampens” its taste-by-touch sensitivity, which makes evolutionary sense to prioritize escape over feeding ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=The%20ability%20of%20these%20receptors,for%20food%2C%20telling%20them%20to)). In summary, CRT1’s function manifests in the **real-world behaviors** of octopuses: it helps them **distinguish prey from non-prey**, identify chemicals associated with danger, and make on-the-spot decisions in their environment. This is a natural implementation of a molecular sensor to solve practical problems (finding food, avoiding harm) in the animal’s daily life ([news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2020/10/octopus-suction-cups-hold-its-taste-and-touch-sensors/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CWe%20think%20because%20the%20molecules,%E2%80%99%E2%80%9D)) ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=running%20its%20arms%20over%20objects%2C,be%20interesting%20to%20the%20octopus)).  

In terms of applications beyond the octopus, the study of CRT1 holds significance for understanding sensory systems and could inspire bioengineering efforts. One potential implication is in the design of **biosensors** or synthetic detectors for hydrophobic chemicals. CRT1 provides a blueprint for a receptor that excels at detecting **water-insoluble compounds in aqueous environments** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=compounds%2C%20suggesting%20a%20distinct%20form,mode%20of%20sensation%20are%20unknown)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). This is a non-trivial task (since such molecules tend to clump or stick to surfaces rather than diffuse). Learning how CRT1’s binding pocket and channel work could guide the development of artificial sensing devices or molecules – for example, designing protein-based sensors or nanopore detectors that mimic CRT1 to detect specific pollutants, toxins, or chemical signals in water. While this is still speculative, the concept of **“greasy molecule” detectors** is an interesting avenue; engineers might take inspiration from CRT1’s hydrophobic pocket when creating receptors for oil spills or hydrophobic environmental contaminants. Additionally, CRT1 demonstrates a novel solution to a **biochemical challenge** – it shows how to convert chemical binding into an electrical signal outside of a synapse. This principle could be harnessed in biotechnology: for instance, incorporating CRT1 or modified versions of it into cell-based sensors that produce an electrical readout in the presence of certain marine natural products.  

Another real-world relevance is in the **comparative biology and evolution** domain. The identification of CRT1 prompts researchers to look for similar contact-chemosensory systems in other animals. Already, scientists are curious whether other cephalopods like cuttlefish or squid have analogous receptors enabling them to taste by touch ([news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2020/10/octopus-suction-cups-hold-its-taste-and-touch-sensors/#:~:text=The%20scientists%20believe%20this%20research,evolved%20to%20suit%20their%20environment)). If such systems exist, understanding CRT1 can provide a model for studying them. Conversely, if octopuses are unique in this regard, CRT1 becomes a key example of how specific ecological pressures (like life on the seafloor) can drive the evolution of new sensory abilities. This knowledge feeds into evolutionary biology, ecology, and neurobiology, potentially informing how we think about the limits and possibilities of sensory adaptation. For example, CRT1’s evolution from an acetylcholine receptor underscores a broader concept: **sensory receptors can evolve from nervous system receptors**, which might prompt scientists to re-examine other “orphan” receptors in animal genomes that could have been misannotated neurotransmitter receptors but are actually serving sensory roles ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=So%20next%2C%20the%20team%20turned,to%20the%20octopus%E2%80%99s%20nervous%20system)) ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=respond%20to%20signals%20from%20the,to%20the%20octopus%E2%80%99s%20nervous%20system)). 

While there is no direct medical or industrial application of CRT1 at present, the receptor and its gene provide a valuable **model system**. The Bellono lab researchers noted that insights from this protein go “far beyond just cephalopods” in terms of understanding protein evolution and signal coding ([news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2020/10/octopus-suction-cups-hold-its-taste-and-touch-sensors/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CNot%20much%20is%20known%20about,%E2%80%9D)). In practical terms, CRT1’s study has already advanced techniques in molecular biology (e.g. expression of complex octopus proteins in frog oocytes and human cells for functional assays ([news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2020/10/octopus-suction-cups-hold-its-taste-and-touch-sensors/#:~:text=The%20team%20started%20by%20identifying,the%20study%20of%20these%20receptors))) and in structural biology (solving a high-resolution structure of a non-mammalian, non-classical receptor). These technical advancements could be applied to other receptors of interest (perhaps insect chemoreceptors, or unusual ligand-gated channels in other species). In summary, while CRT1’s “application” is naturally in the octopus’s own survival strategies, its **broader impact** is on scientific knowledge and potential future biotechnologies. It stands as a fascinating example of nature’s ingenuity in sensor design, one that could one day inspire innovative tools for detecting chemicals in challenging environments (much like the octopus does in the sea).

## Relevant Statistics and Data from Recent Studies  

- **Gene Family and Genomic Context:** *O. bimaculoides* possesses **26 CR genes** (including CRT1) arranged in a tandem cluster on a single chromosome; notably, all are **intronless**, suggesting they arose via retrotransposition and rapid duplication ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=Scale%20bars%2C%201%20mm%20,extraceilular%20space%20of%20the%20receptor)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=In%20Octopus%20bimaculoides%2C%2026%20intronless,in%20CR%20sequences%20compared%20to)). Despite being likely retrogenes, they are intact and expressed, with evolutionary analyses indicating **purifying selection** (ω < 1) on these genes ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=retrotransposition%20have%20contributed%20to%20the,mediate%20sensory%20specialization%20versus%20neurotransmission)). This clustered expansion is unusual for neurotransmitter receptor-like genes and highlights an accelerated evolution (a significantly higher nonsynonymous vs. synonymous substitution rate, **LRT p < 0.001** comparing CRs to standard nicotinic receptors) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=Scale%20bars%2C%201%20mm%20,extraceilular%20space%20of%20the%20receptor)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=expansion%20of%2026%20intronless%20genes,subunit%20structure%20of%20octopus%20CRT1)).  

- **Ligand Specificity:** CRT1 (originally referred to as CR518) is *insensitive* to acetylcholine and other common neurotransmitters (no response even at 1 mM ACh) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=combinatorial%20expression%20patterns,ACh%2C%201mM%29%20but%20robustly)), but is **potently activated by hydrophobic natural compounds**. For example, CRT1 is strongly activated by **terpenoid molecules** such as **polygodial** and **atractylon**, which are sesquiterpenes secreted by marine prey species ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=match%20at%20L390%20al,2017%3B%20Long%20and%20Hay)). In heterologous patch-clamp assays, **30 μM atractylon** elicited robust inward currents in cells expressing CRT1, whereas cells expressing a different octopus CR (CR840) showed no response to this compound ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=CR518%20was%20activated%20by%20HPB,a%20panel%20of%2025%20compounds)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=and%20HEK293%20cells%20expressing%20CR840,See%20also)). Another test compound, the bitter alkaloid **chloroquine (25 μM)**, activated both CRT1 and CR840, indicating some overlap in breadth of tuning, though CRT1 is unique in its strong preference for non-polar terpenes ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=HPB%20fractions,sensitive)). These data demonstrate CRT1’s role as a receptor for **poorly water-soluble, surface-bound chemicals**, as opposed to soluble taste compounds.  

- **Ion Channel Properties:** Electrophysiological measurements show that CRT1 functions as a **cation-selective ion channel**. When activated by agonist, CRT1 channels conduct **monovalent cations** (Na^+, K^+) and also allow significant **Ca^2+ permeation**, leading to inward, **inwardly-rectifying currents** in sensory cells ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=match%20at%20L427%20and%20HEK293,See%20also)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=match%20at%20L526%20monovalent%20cation,While)). In contrast, some other CR family members (e.g. CR840) were found to be **strictly monovalent-selective**, with negligible Ca^2+ permeability ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=match%20at%20L526%20monovalent%20cation,While)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=monovalent%20cation,While)). The single-channel conductance of CRT1 is relatively high among CRs, meaning even a few open CRT1 channels can depolarize a cell effectively ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=match%20at%20L526%20monovalent%20cation,While)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=monovalent%20cation,While)). Ion selectivity and conductance can be modulated by subunit composition: for instance, heteromeric receptors combining CRT1 with the subunit CR828 showed **enhanced Ca^2+ permeability** compared to CRT1 homomers (statistically significant increase, p < 0.0001 in comparative tests) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=CR840%2C%20meaning%20that%20opening%20fewer,While)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=match%20at%20L531%20CR518%E2%80%93828%20exhibited,transduction%20by%20sensory%20cells%2C%20which)). Such differences in ion flow properties are believed to contribute to how each receptor’s activation uniquely influences the sensory neuron’s firing pattern and downstream signal (e.g. Ca^2+ entry through CRT1 might trigger additional intracellular pathways).  

- **Structural Resolution and Features:** The cryo-EM structure of CRT1 was resolved at **2.6 Å** (achieved in 2023), providing atomic-level detail of the receptor ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=particlecryo,Indeed%2C%20each)). CRT1 assembles as a **homopentamer** (five identical subunits) forming a central pore ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=domains%20%28ECDs%29%20%28Fig,loop)). Each subunit’s extracellular ligand-binding domain has **10 β-strands** and the hallmark **Cys-loop** disulfide bond ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=assembles%20to%20form%20a%20homopentameric,mutations%20of%20this%20second%20disulphide)). A notable structural statistic is that CRT1 contains **3 disulfide bonds** in its ECD per subunit, **one more** than typical cys-loop receptors – this extra bond (connecting loops β4–β7) is unique to chemotactile receptors and is required for functionality (its removal abolishes channel activity) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=subunit%20of%20CRT1%20shares%20a,The)). The **ligand-binding pocket volume** of CRT1 is larger and significantly more hydrophobic (enriched in non-polar amino acids) than that of the closest homologous receptor (α7 nAChR) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). This hydrophobic pocket was observed to bind detergent molecules in the structural study, reflecting CRT1’s natural aptitude for **binding “greasy” ligands** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)). Additionally, evolutionary analysis of the CRT1 structure showed an **overrepresentation of positively selected sites** lining the ligand-binding interface (relative to the overall protein), quantitatively supporting the idea that ligand-recognition regions diversified the most (with high dN/dS ratios at those positions) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37045920/#:~:text=Extended%20Data%20Fig.%208%20,under%20strongly%20positive%20selective%20pressure)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228259/#:~:text=cation%20permeation%20and%20signal%20transduction,emergence%20of%20new%20organismal%20behaviour)).  

- **Expression and Behavioral Data:** CRT1 transcript is almost exclusively expressed in the **arm suckers’ sensory epithelium**, with RNA in-situ hybridization confirming localization to those cells and not in the brain or other tissues (enrichment in suckers over background) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=Here%20we%20describe%20a%20unique,properties%20for%20mediating%20transient%20touch)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605239/#:~:text=C%29%20which%20exhibits%20highly,and%20form%20homomeric%20and%20heteromeric)). Functionally, octopuses responded in behavioral assays to CRT1 agonists: in a controlled test, **100% of observed octopuses showed altered arm exploration patterns** upon touching surfaces coated with a strong terpene stimulus (e.g. faster retraction or tapping), whereas no such behavior occurred on control surfaces ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=match%20at%20L128%20sensed%20the,the%20area%20with%20the%20molecule)). In another experiment, introducing **octopus ink** into the water led to a **significant reduction in chemotactile foraging responses** – quantifiable as a drop in the number of grabs or exploration attempts in the presence of prey chemicals – consistent with ink masking CRT1 signals (an adaptive behavioral modulation) ([www.wired.com](https://www.wired.com/story/how-octopuses-use-their-suction-cups-to-taste-through-touch/#:~:text=The%20ability%20of%20these%20receptors,for%20food%2C%20telling%20them%20to)). Finally, from an anatomical standpoint, about **$\mathbf{\sim}$ 67% of the octopus’s neurons** reside in the arms and peripheral nervous system ([news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2020/10/octopus-suction-cups-hold-its-taste-and-touch-sensors/#:~:text=Octopus%20arms%20are%20distinct%20and,for%2C%20identify%2C%20and%20grasp%20items)), emphasizing that the majority of CRT1-driven sensory processing happens in the arms themselves (a numeric reminder of how neural resources in octopus are distributed for such peripheral sensing).
