Gene Description

ROT1 encodes an essential endoplasmic-reticulum membrane protein that acts as an ER protein-folding chaperone or chaperone-associated assembly factor. Rot1 faces the ER lumen, genetically and physically cooperates with ER folding and glycosylation factors such as Kar2/BiP and Ost3, prevents aggregation in vitro, and supports maturation of secretory and membrane proteins. Its defects cause impaired ER protein folding, reduced N- and O-glycosylation of glycoproteins, cell wall defects, altered actin polarity, and bud-growth phenotypes; these downstream phenotypes are best interpreted as consequences of the core ER proteostasis/glycoprotein maturation role.

Core Functions

Rot1 is an essential ER membrane chaperone or chaperone-associated assembly factor that supports folding and maturation of newly synthesized secretory and membrane proteins. It cooperates with Kar2/BiP and with the Ost3-containing glycosylation machinery, preventing degradation or underglycosylation of a subset of ER clients. N-glycosylation is therefore a direct core process coupled to Rot1-dependent ER proteostasis, while cell-wall, actin-polarity, and bud-growth phenotypes are downstream readouts.

Research Report: Saccharomyces cerevisiae ROT1 (YMR200W; UniProt Q03691)

Executive summary

ROT1 encodes an essential, endoplasmic reticulum (ER)–localized, multi-pass membrane chaperone (Rot1) whose best-supported primary role is to promote efficient protein glycosylation—especially N-glycosylation via functional coupling to the Ost3-containing oligosaccharyltransferase (OST) complex—and to support maturation/stability of subsets of glycoproteins (including cell-wall–related proteins), with downstream impacts on cell wall integrity and ER proteostasis. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-1, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3)

A key mechanistic anchor is that Rot1 physically associates with Ost3 and that genetic perturbation (rot1-1) combined with loss of OST3 (but not OST6) causes pronounced growth defects, cell-wall drug sensitivities, underglycosylation of multiple N-glycoproteins, and strongly reduced OST activity. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic media 724ed7b6)

Recent work (2024 preprint) repositions Rot1 as a practical experimental lever: because Rot1 is described as a UPR-independent ER chaperone, inducible Rot1 expression can compensate for loss of canonical UPR signaling (HAC1 deletion) in the context of maintaining euploidy, highlighting Rot1 as a tool to modulate ER folding capacity independent of the UPR transcriptional program. (bartolutti2024uprdeficiencyin pages 5-7)

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

Rot1 as an ER chaperone/assembly factor (definition)

In this context, an ER chaperone is a factor that promotes protein folding, prevents aggregation, and/or helps assemble or position protein-processing machinery at the ER translocon. Rot1 is described as an essential ER-localized protein with chaperone-like/anti-aggregation activity and genetic interaction with the major ER Hsp70 chaperone Kar2/BiP, placing Rot1 in ER folding quality control. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 6-6)

N-glycosylation via OST and the Ost3/Ost6 isoforms (definition)

N-glycosylation in yeast occurs when the OST complex transfers a preassembled oligosaccharide from a dolichol-linked donor to Asn residues on nascent proteins in the ER. Yeast has OST complexes with alternative subunits Ost3 or Ost6, which can differ in substrate preferences/efficiency. The strongest Rot1-linked phenotypes are specific to the Ost3-containing context (synthetic defects with ost3Δ but not ost6Δ), implicating Rot1 particularly in the Ost3-dependent branch of N-glycosylation. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3)

O-mannosylation (definition)

Protein O-mannosylation is initiated in the ER by PMT O-mannosyltransferases and often increases on misfolded proteins. Rot1 perturbation affects O-mannosylation for specific substrates and reduces in vitro PMT activity in a sensitized genetic background, linking Rot1 to ER quality control that couples folding state to O-glycan initiation. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 3-4)

2) Functional annotation: molecular function, processes, and pathways

2.1 Subcellular localization and topology

Rot1 is described as an ER-localized essential membrane protein/chaperone. Its functional effects map to ER luminal processes (OST/PMT activities, folding/quality control), consistent with an ER membrane-embedded protein acting on luminal substrates or luminal domains of glycosylation machinery. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 8-9, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 6-6)

2.2 Primary molecular function (best-supported)

Rot1’s primary function is best described as an ER chaperone/assembly factor required for efficient glycosylation rather than a catalytic enzyme with a discrete small-molecule substrate. Evidence indicates Rot1 is required for:
- Efficient N-glycosylation (underglycosylation of multiple N-glycoproteins in sensitized mutants; reduced OST activity). (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 3-4)
- O-mannosylation of selected proteins (e.g., Cts1) and reduced in vitro PMT activity under sensitized conditions. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 3-4, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 6-7)

Mechanistically, Rot1 physically associates with the OST subunit Ost3, supporting a model in which Rot1 helps stabilize/assemble/position the Ost3-containing OST complex at the translocation site for efficient co-translational N-glycosylation. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 4-5, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 6-6)

2.3 Pathway context

Rot1 sits at the intersection of:
- ER protein folding/proteostasis and UPR-linked stress: rot1 alleles can activate the UPR, consistent with ER folding stress, and Rot1 is genetically connected to Kar2/BiP. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 6-6)
- N-glycosylation (OST function) and dolichol-linked donor supply: Rot1 perturbation combined with ost3Δ reduces OST activity and is associated with reduced cis-prenyltransferase activity (Rer2-pathway), linking to dolichol precursor synthesis. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 6-6, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 3-4)
- Cell wall integrity: glycosylation defects of cell-wall proteins and drug sensitivities (e.g., Calcofluor White, Congo Red, caffeine) connect Rot1-dependent glycoprotein maturation to cell wall maintenance. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-1, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3)

3) Key experimental evidence (high-specificity) supporting function

3.1 Genetic interaction specificity with OST3 (not OST6)

In growth/drug assays, rot1-1 alone is reported as only mildly affected under tested conditions, but rot1-1 ost3Δ exhibits abolished growth/temperature sensitivity and enhanced sensitivity to cell wall–perturbing compounds, while rot1-1 ost6Δ does not show the same synthetic interaction—supporting an Ost3-specific functional linkage. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3)

3.2 In vivo glycosylation readouts (substrate-level)

In the rot1-1 ost3Δ background, multiple model glycoproteins show underglycosylation by immunoblot mobility shifts, including CPY (with forms lacking 1–3 N-glycan chains), DPAPB, Mid2, and Gas1. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3)

3.3 Enzymatic activity assays: OST and PMT

Biochemical assays on microsomes show:
- Strongly reduced OST activity in rot1-1 ost3Δ relative to wild-type and single mutants. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3)
- PMT (O-mannosyltransferase) activity reduced to ~45% of wild type in rot1-1 ost3Δ microsomes (quantitative statistic), indicating a measurable O-glycosylation defect under sensitized conditions. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 3-4)

3.4 Physical interaction between Rot1 and Ost3

Affinity/co-immunoprecipitation experiments show Rot1 and Ost3 co-fractionation consistent with a physical association, supporting a direct mechanistic link between Rot1 and the OST complex. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 4-5, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic media 724ed7b6)

Figure-based evidence: Cropped figures from the 2012 study document both decreased OST activity in rot1-1 ost3Δ and Rot1–Ost3 co-immunoprecipitation, providing visual support for these key claims. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic media 724ed7b6, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic media e1a607cd)

4) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

4.1 Rot1 as a UPR-independent ER chaperone tool (2024)

A 2024 bioRxiv preprint describes Rot1 explicitly as an ER chaperone not induced by the UPR and uses an aTC-inducible ROT1 allele as a tool to increase ER folding capacity independently of the canonical UPR transcription factor HAC1. Inducing Rot1 expression enabled construction/maintenance of hac1Δ strains (haploid and diploid) while retaining euploidy, and euploidy retention was validated by whole-genome sequencing of 2 independent UPR-deficient populations under induced Rot1. (bartolutti2024uprdeficiencyin pages 5-7)

4.2 Expert framing/interpretation in 2024 work

The 2024 work interprets Rot1 overexpression as evidence that baseline ER folding capacity is a key determinant of whether UPR loss drives adaptive aneuploidy; i.e., increasing chaperone capacity can remove selection for chromosome copy-number changes. This expands Rot1’s role from a glycosylation-linked ER factor to a generalizable experimental handle on ER folding stress outcomes. (bartolutti2024uprdeficiencyin pages 7-9, bartolutti2024uprdeficiencyin pages 5-7)

Note on 2023–2024 peer-reviewed ROT1-focused literature: In the retrieved corpus, direct 2023–2024 peer-reviewed studies specifically centered on S. cerevisiae Rot1 were limited; the principal 2024-specific evidence available here is the above preprint. (bartolutti2024uprdeficiencyin pages 5-7)

5) Current applications and real-world implementations

5.1 Functional genomics / proteostasis engineering

Rot1 is used experimentally in two complementary ways:
1) Mechanistic glycosylation biology: as a sensitizing factor to probe OST isoform function (Ost3 vs Ost6), substrate underglycosylation, and coupling between folding and glycan transfer. (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 1-3, pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 3-4)
2) Proteostasis/UPR systems biology: as an inducible, UPR-independent ER chaperone to manipulate ER folding capacity and test causal links between ER stress buffering and genome stability (aneuploidy/euploidy). (bartolutti2024uprdeficiencyin pages 5-7)

5.2 Antifungal-relevant biology (contextual)

Because Rot1 homologs are reported as fungal-restricted in the cited yeast glycosylation work, Rot1-family proteins are of interest as fungal-specific ER proteostasis/glycosylation factors, conceptually aligning with antifungal strategies that target cell wall/glycosylation pathways (though the evidence here is functional/biological rather than a direct drug-development program in S. cerevisiae). (pasikowska2012theessentialendoplasmic pages 7-8, janik2026theessentialrot1 pages 1-2)

6) Relevant statistics and quantitative data (from retrieved studies)
