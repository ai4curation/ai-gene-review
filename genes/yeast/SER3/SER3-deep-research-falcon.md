## Research report: **SER3 (YER081W; UniProt P40054) in *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* S288c**

### 0) Gene/protein identity verification (mandatory)
The UniProt accession **P40054** corresponds to the *S. cerevisiae* S288c protein **Ser3**, encoded by **SER3 (YER081W)**, described as a 3-phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase–family enzyme involved in de novo serine biosynthesis and studied alongside its paralog **SER33**. Primary biochemical literature explicitly describes SER3/SER33 as the yeast PHGDH homologs catalyzing the first committed step of the phosphorylated serine synthesis pathway (SSP) (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 1-2, becker‐kettern2018nad(p)hxrepairdeficiency pages 32-38).

### 1) Key concepts and current functional understanding

#### 1.1 The phosphorylated serine synthesis pathway (SSP) in yeast
In budding yeast, de novo L-serine synthesis from the glycolytic intermediate **3-phosphoglycerate (3PGA)** proceeds through the SSP, which includes the first step catalyzed by **Ser3/Ser33**, followed by Ser1 (phosphoserine aminotransferase) and Ser2 (phosphoserine phosphatase). Genetic evidence indicates **SER3 and SER33 are functionally redundant**: single deletions (**ser3Δ** or **ser33Δ**) can grow without serine supplementation, while disrupting downstream steps (SER1 or SER2) causes strong serine-related growth defects and sphingolipid vulnerability (esch2020uptakeofexogenous pages 2-3).

#### 1.2 Enzymatic reaction: transhydrogenase vs dehydrogenase
A central modern update is that yeast Ser3/Ser33—though historically annotated as NAD+-dependent “3-phosphoglycerate dehydrogenases”—**operate primarily as 3PGA–α-ketoglutarate (αKG) transhydrogenases**.

**Mechanism (two half-reactions, with enzyme-bound NAD cycling internally):**
- **Oxidation:** 3PGA → 3-phosphohydroxypyruvate (PHP/3PHP)
- **Coupled reduction:** αKG → **D-2-hydroxyglutarate (D-2HG)**

Thus, the physiologically relevant stoichiometry links serine biosynthesis to **D-2HG formation** and αKG reduction, rather than producing freely diffusible NADH as the key output (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 2-4, becker‐kettern2018nad(p)hxrepairdeficiency pages 32-38, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 1-2). This contrasts with human PHGDH, which is primarily an NAD+-dependent dehydrogenase (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 2-4, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 1-2).

*Visual evidence:* Paczia et al. provide a pathway/mechanism schematic contrasting yeast transhydrogenation with the classical dehydrogenase reaction (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead media 59304816, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead media d6cbc613, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead media 9f5426bd).

#### 1.3 Substrate specificity and side-activity annotation
Because the Ser3/Ser33 mechanism couples 3PGA oxidation to αKG reduction, these enzymes also appear in the literature as **αKG reductase / 2-oxoglutarate reductase** activities in appropriate assay contexts, consistent with UniProt’s alternative annotation (EC 1.1.1.399) and the observed D-2HG product formation (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 2-4, becker‐kettern2018nad(p)hxrepairdeficiency pages 32-38).

### 2) Regulation, pathway integration, and cellular context

#### 2.1 Product feedback inhibition by serine
Ser3/Ser33 are subject to **allosteric feedback inhibition by L-serine**, a classic control feature for committed steps in amino-acid biosynthesis.

Quantitatively, reported inhibitory concentrations (IC50) for the transhydrogenase activity are:
- **Ser3:** **590 ± 54 µM**
- **Ser33:** **61 ± 5 µM**
indicating Ser33 is much more serine-sensitive (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 10-11).

Corresponding inhibition curves are shown in Paczia et al. figures (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead media 59304816, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead media d6cbc613, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead media 9f5426bd).

In vivo, adding serine to cultures strongly suppresses the transhydrogenase-linked product **2HG** in SER3/SER33 rescue strains (**−85%** and **−92%**, respectively), consistent with feedback inhibition operating in cells (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 12-14).

#### 2.2 Coupling to redox state and respiration (transhydrogenase pathway)
The transhydrogenase mechanism provides a conceptual advantage under conditions where the cellular **NAD+:NADH ratio is low**, because the yeast enzymes do not rely on soluble NAD+ as the terminal electron acceptor in the same way as classical dehydrogenases (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 1-2, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 11-12).

Paczia et al. further propose a broader “transhydrogenase pathway” in which cytosolic Ser3/Ser33 activity is connected to mitochondrial metabolism via downstream oxidation steps; this is diagrammed in their in vivo pathway schematic (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 11-12, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead media 59304816).

#### 2.3 Inhibition by damaged NAD(P)H (NADHX) and metabolite repair
A distinct regulatory/physiology layer comes from **NAD(P)HX metabolite repair**. In yeast deficient for NAD(P)HX dehydratase (YKL151C), damaged NADH derivatives (NADHX) accumulate and are linked to depletion of serine pools; mechanistically, NADHX is reported to inhibit the **first serine synthesis step** carried out by Ser3/Ser33 (becker‐kettern2018nad(p)hxrepairdeficiency pages 32-38). This connects SER3 function to cellular “metabolite damage/repair” networks.

#### 2.4 Domain architecture and oligomerization
A 2023 mechanistic analysis of TORC1-related signaling and the serine synthesis pathway summarizes Ser3/Ser33 as multi-domain enzymes including an **ACT domain** implicated in oligomerization (tetrameric assembly) and allosteric control (cecil2023molecularlogicofa pages 21-25, cecil2023molecularlogicof pages 21-25). (Note: this 2023 source is captured as “unknown journal” in the retrieval metadata; conclusions about domain roles should therefore be interpreted as a synthesis that is consistent with earlier enzymology but may not be peer-reviewed in the same way.)

### 3) Phenotypes and quantitative experimental observations

Key experimentally supported phenotypes and quantitative results are summarized below and in the table artifact.

- **Redundancy in serine prototrophy:** ser3Δ or ser33Δ single mutants can grow without serine, implying redundancy; by contrast, disruptions in SER1/SER2 are much more severe (esch2020uptakeofexogenous pages 2-3).
- **2-hydroxyglutarate production in vivo:** In a ser3Δser33Δ background complemented with plasmids, **SER3** or **SER33** rescue strains show strong intracellular 2HG accumulation compared with rescue by **human PHGDH** (17-fold and 12-fold higher, respectively) (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 11-12). This is consistent with the yeast transhydrogenase mechanism producing D-2HG.
- **Serine suppresses 2HG formation:** Adding serine decreases intracellular 2HG by **85% (SER3 rescue)** and **92% (SER33 rescue)** (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 12-14), supporting feedback inhibition in vivo.
- **Redox ratio shift with fermentation:** In chemostats, the NAD+:NADH ratio decreases from roughly **~200 to <35** as dilution rate increases and metabolism shifts toward fermentation (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 12-14). This provides physiological context for why a transhydrogenase mechanism might be beneficial.

### 4) Localization: what can be concluded from the retrieved evidence
Within the retrieved full-text evidence, SER3/Ser3 is consistently discussed as catalyzing the **cytosolic** SSP step (“first step of the cytosolic yeast de novo serine synthesis pathway”) and as part of a pathway that functionally links cytosolic serine synthesis to mitochondrial respiration (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 11-12, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 10-11). However, the retrieved excerpts did **not** provide a direct experimental localization assay (e.g., microscopy, fractionation) for Ser3 itself; therefore, localization is best stated as *cytosolic pathway participation* rather than definitive compartment assignment (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 11-12, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 10-11).

### 5) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

Direct SER3 enzymology breakthroughs in the retrieved corpus are primarily 2018–2020 (with deep mechanistic biochemistry in 2019). However, 2023 work provides an important *regulatory integration* perspective:

- **TORC1-nutrient signaling linkage (2023):** A 2023 mechanistic analysis reports that TORC1 activity affects serine/glycine pools (rapamycin lowers them) and describes **TORC1/Pib2-dependent phosphorylation of Ser33** (paralog of Ser3), suggesting signaling control over serine synthesis capacity via post-translational modification (cecil2023molecularlogicof pages 21-25, cecil2023molecularlogicofa pages 21-25). This should be interpreted as the most “recent” SER3-pathway-relevant development available in the retrieved evidence, though it is primarily Ser33-focused.

- **Industrial/bioprocess context (2023):** A 2023 cultivation study using wheat bran as nutrient resource reports changes in yeast growth and transcriptional responses; although SER3 is not singled out in the retrieved excerpt, such media-driven transcriptional shifts provide applied context for metabolism reprogramming that can impinge on central carbon intermediates feeding the SSP (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 10-11).

### 6) Applications and real-world implementations

#### 6.1 Metabolic engineering: coupling growth and product formation
SER3/SER33 are not only annotated metabolic genes; they have been used as **engineering handles** to re-route carbon and reducing power.

- **Dicarboxylic acids / succinate chassis design (2021):** In model-guided yeast chassis development, SER3 and SER33 are described as the 3-phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase isozymes catalyzing the first serine-pathway step, and deletion of **SER3/SER33 (Δser3,33)** is used to force alternate serine supply routes and couple flux in ways useful for production phenotypes (pereira2021model‐guideddevelopmentof pages 1-2).

- **Succinic acid engineering strategy (reviewed 2017):** A metabolic engineering overview describes strategies where SER3/SER33 deletions (with other targets) contribute to coupling succinate production to growth; yields and adaptation strategies (evolution to restore growth without glycine supplementation; yield reported as 0.07 mol/mol glucose under aerobic conditions in one engineered/evolved context) are discussed (turanlıyıldız2017advancesinmetabolic pages 3-5). While older, this provides a concrete real-world example of SER3/SER33 manipulations used in industrial biotechnology design.

#### 6.2 Serine availability and sphingolipid homeostasis
Serine is a substrate for sphingolipid biosynthesis; a 2020 study shows that **exogenous serine uptake** is important to maintain sphingolipid homeostasis and places SER3/SER33 within the broader cellular serine supply network (esch2020uptakeofexogenous pages 2-3). This helps interpret SER3’s physiological importance not only for amino-acid pools but also for lipid metabolism inputs.

### 7) Expert analysis and interpretation (evidence-constrained)

1. **Mechanistic reinterpretation matters for annotation:** The best-supported update is that SER3 should be functionally described not merely as “3-phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase” but as a **3PGA–αKG transhydrogenase** that produces **D-2HG** as a coupled product (paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 2-4, paczia20193phosphoglyceratetranshydrogenationinstead pages 1-2). This directly affects how one interprets its role in redox balance and metabolite byproducts.

2. **SER3 sits at a metabolic–signaling interface:** While SER3 itself is not the phosphorylation target emphasized in 2023 evidence, the pathway it initiates is presented as responsive to TORC1 signaling through regulation of the paralog Ser33, consistent with nutrient control over biosynthetic capacity (cecil2023molecularlogicof pages 21-25).

3. **Metabolite damage can gate serine synthesis:** NAD(P)HX accumulation links “metabolite repair” to serine biosynthesis control via inhibition of the Ser3/Ser33 step (becker‐kettern2018nad(p)hxrepairdeficiency pages 32-38). This expands SER3’s relevance beyond canonical amino-acid biosynthesis into stress/quality-control metabolism.
