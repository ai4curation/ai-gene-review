| Gene identity / synonyms | **POP2** is the *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* gene encoding **Pop2p**, also called **Caf1** (CCR4-associated factor 1); literature explicitly treats Pop2/Caf1 as the yeast orthologous CAF1-family deadenylase subunit and distinguishes it from unrelated genes in other organisms. | Primary papers describe the protein as “Pop2, also known as Caf1” and as a component of the yeast Ccr4–Not deadenylase complex. | (pqac-00000004, pqac-00000005, pqac-00000028) |
| Organism | Target verified as **Saccharomyces cerevisiae** (baker’s yeast; the cited primary literature is explicitly in budding yeast). | Tucker et al. and Daugeron et al. both study the major cytoplasmic mRNA deadenylase in *S. cerevisiae*; Thore et al. solve the structure of the **yeast** Pop2 protein. | (pqac-00000007, pqac-00000027, pqac-00000028) |
| Protein family / domains | Pop2/Caf1 belongs to the **Pop2/Caf1 (CAF1) family** and contains an **RNase D / DEDD superfamily nuclease domain** with an RNase H-like fold; in yeast, the active site is somewhat **non-canonical/divergent** relative to other CAF1 homologs. | Thore et al. report the crystal structure of the Pop2 nuclease domain at **2.3 Å** and classify it in the DEDD/RNase D family; reviews and comparative studies note the yeast active site is divergent relative to canonical CAF1 homologs. | (pqac-00000001, pqac-00000003, pqac-00000014, pqac-00000030) |
| Complex membership | Pop2/Caf1 is a catalytic-module component of the **CCR4–NOT** deadenylase complex, interacting with **Ccr4** and the **Not** proteins; Caf1 also bridges the deadenylase module to the scaffold. | The yeast Ccr4–Not complex is described as containing two nucleases, **Ccr4 and Pop2/Caf1**; biochemical and genetic work places Pop2/Caf1 in the major cytoplasmic deadenylase. | (pqac-00000007, pqac-00000028, pqac-00000014) |
| Primary biochemical function | Pop2/Caf1 functions in **mRNA deadenylation**, i.e., **3′ poly(A) tail shortening** that initiates major mRNA decay pathways and can also affect translation. | Tucker et al. show CCR4 and CAF1 are required for normal in vivo deadenylation; Daugeron et al. identify POP2 as a nuclease involved in mRNA deadenylation; modern reviews place CCR4–NOT deadenylation upstream of decapping and Xrn1-mediated decay. | (pqac-00000007, pqac-00000002, pqac-00000009, pqac-00000013) |
| Catalytic activity / substrate specificity | Recombinant yeast Pop2 nuclease domain has **intrinsic RNase activity** and degrades **poly(A)** in vitro; it can also act on **poly(C)** and **poly(U)**, indicating **broad RNA substrate specificity** rather than strict poly(A)-only activity. However, in vivo in budding yeast, **CCR4 is generally considered the dominant catalytic deadenylase**, with Pop2/Caf1 contributing important but partly distinct or regulatory functions. | Thore et al. show in vitro activity toward **poly(A), poly(C), and poly(U)** and loss of activity after active-site mutagenesis; Daugeron et al. show **poly(A)** is a better competitor than poly(G) or poly(C); multiple reviews note that in *S. cerevisiae* most deadenylation activity may be carried by Ccr4 despite Pop2’s intrinsic RNase activity. | (pqac-00000001, pqac-00000003, pqac-00000004, pqac-00000006, pqac-00000030) |
| Functional division with Ccr4 | Current model: **Ccr4** is the principal deadenylase on many yeast mRNAs, while **Pop2/Caf1** has important roles that can be partly separate from direct catalysis, including coupling deadenylation to translation state and/or aiding recruitment or module organization. | Ohn et al. conclude Caf1 has functions in deadenylation **separate from its contact to Ccr4**; Webster et al. show **Ccr4** can remove Pab1-protected proximal tails, while **Caf1** preferentially acts where Pab1 protection is reduced. | (pqac-00000010, pqac-00000012, pqac-00000032) |
| Cellular localization | Pop2/Caf1 and Ccr4 localize predominantly to the **cytoplasm**, matching a role in cytoplasmic mRNA deadenylation/decay. Under some conditions, CCR4–NOT is linked to **P-bodies** and to **ribosome-associated** regulation, although direct P-body localization of Pop2 itself is less specifically documented than general complex behavior. | Tucker et al. report by indirect immunofluorescence that the majority of **Ccr4p-myc and Caf1p-myc are present in the cytoplasm**; later reviews place CCR4–NOT in cytoplasmic decay pathways, stress-dependent P-bodies, and ribosome-associated regulation. | (pqac-00000033, pqac-00000035, pqac-00000036, pqac-00000032, pqac-00000037) |
| Pathway context | Pop2/Caf1 acts in the canonical **deadenylation-dependent mRNA decay pathway**: poly(A) shortening by CCR4–NOT, then **decapping**, then **5′→3′ degradation by Xrn1**; the complex is also linked to translational repression and codon-optimality sensing. | Reviews from 2023–2024 summarize that recruitment of CCR4–NOT is the key initiating event; after deadenylation, decapping and Xrn1 follow; ribosome/codon-optimality coupling helps determine substrate fate. | (pqac-00000009, pqac-00000013, pqac-00000016, pqac-00000018, pqac-00000023) |
| Reporter-mRNA decay phenotype | **POP2 deletion/disruption stabilizes mRNA** and impairs deadenylation. | In Daugeron et al., the **MFA2 mRNA half-life approximately doubled** in the **pop2** disruption mutant relative to wild type, with accumulation of longer poly(A)-containing species. | (pqac-00000025) |
| Poly(A)-tail length / deadenylation endpoint phenotypes | Caf1/Pop2 defects cause **longer residual poly(A) tails** and slower deadenylation. | Ohn et al.: wild-type **GAL1-L** tails shortened to **8–12 nt within 9 min**, whereas defective **caf1** alleles remained at about **30–35 A’s** over the same period; a reported **caf1Δ** showed median endpoints of **~16–20 A’s** versus **8–12 A’s** in wild type. Tucker et al. further report wild-type shortest tails near **10–12 A**, versus **20–26 A** in **ccr4Δ**, with **PGK1pG** deadenylation slowing from **~2–3 nt/min** to **~1 nt/min** in **ccr4Δ**. | (pqac-00000024, pqac-00000036) |
| In vitro / structural assays | Key assays include recombinant nuclease assays, active-site mutagenesis, poly(A)-competition assays, and X-ray crystallography of the nuclease domain. | Thore et al. solved the Pop2 nuclease-domain structure at **2.3 Å** and showed active-site mutations abolished RNase activity; Daugeron et al. used recombinant Pop2 fragments and competition assays showing **poly(A)** outcompetes poly(G)/poly(C). | (pqac-00000028, pqac-00000029, pqac-00000004) |
