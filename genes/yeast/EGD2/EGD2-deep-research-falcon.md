# Research Report: Functional Annotation of **EGD2 / YHR193C** (UniProt **P38879**) in *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* (S288c)

## Executive summary
**EGD2 (YHR193C; UniProt P38879)** encodes the sole *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* **nascent polypeptide‑associated complex (NAC) α subunit** (“NAC‑alpha”, **Egd2**). NAC is a **ribosome‑associated cotranslational chaperone/adaptor** that binds near the **ribosomal exit tunnel** and engages nascent chains very early during translation, influencing nascent-chain folding, aggregation avoidance, and targeting decisions, particularly **interplay with SRP‑dependent ER targeting** and auxiliary roles in **mitochondrial protein biogenesis**. (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 1-2, pech2010dualbindingmode pages 1-2)

Recent yeast studies (2024) strengthen mechanistic understanding that NAC (including Egd2) can **co-occupy the ribosomal tunnel exit** together with the Zuo1/Hsp70 system in vivo (rather than being strictly mutually exclusive), and that **EGD2 loss has only partial effects** in some mitochondrial quality-control contexts (mitophagy), consistent with EGD2 acting as a supportive/auxiliary component within a broader cotranslational proteostasis network. (tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2, ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 pages 1-2, ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 media efe47529)

## 1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

### 1.1. What is NAC?
The **nascent chain‑associated complex (NAC)** is an abundant, conserved cotranslational factor that binds **ribosome‑nascent chain complexes** close to the exit tunnel and can interact with **very short nascent chains**, consistent with an early role in nascent chain handling. (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 1-2, pech2010dualbindingmode pages 1-2)

In budding yeast, NAC is generally an **α/β heterodimer**, though evidence supports that certain NAC subunits can also form homodimeric species under some conditions. (nyathi2015analysisofthe pages 7-9, alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 1-2)

### 1.2. Yeast NAC subunit composition and nomenclature
*Saccharomyces cerevisiae* encodes:
- **One α subunit: Egd2 (EGD2 / YHR193C)**
- **Two β paralogs: Egd1 (β1) and Btt1 (β′/β3)**
forming two main heterodimers **Egd2/Egd1 (αβ1)** and **Egd2/Btt1 (αβ3)**. (ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2, alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 1-2, tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2)

The β paralog **Btt1** is much less abundant than Egd1 (reported as ~**100‑fold** lower in some studies; also described as **~20–100‑fold** lower), making **Egd2/Egd1** the predominant heterodimer under standard conditions. (ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2, schilke2024functionalsimilaritiesand pages 1-3)

### 1.3. Where does Egd2 act?
Egd2 functions primarily as a **cytosolic**, **ribosome‑associated** factor at the **60S tunnel exit region**, contacting ribosomal proteins in the vicinity (e.g., contacts to Rpl17/Rpl35 are discussed across mechanistic studies). (nyathi2015analysisofthe pages 7-9, pech2010dualbindingmode pages 1-2)

The ribosome tunnel exit is a “hub” for multiple biogenesis factors, including NAC and the Zuo1/Hsp70 (RAC–Ssb) system; recent evidence indicates co-occupancy in vivo (see section 2.2). (ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 pages 1-2)

### 1.4. What is the “function” of Egd2 (not an enzyme reaction)
EGD2 does **not** encode an enzyme or transporter with substrate specificity. Instead, Egd2 is a **protein biogenesis factor** whose “primary function” is best described as:
- a component of a **general cotranslational chaperone/adaptor complex** that helps manage nascent chain fate (folding/aggregation avoidance and targeting factor access) at the ribosome exit. (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 1-2, ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2)

## 2) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

### 2.1. 2024: NAC and Zuo1/Hsp70 can coexist at the ribosome tunnel exit in vivo
A major 2024 advance in yeast is in vivo cross-linking evidence showing that **NAC and Zuotin/Hsp70 systems can coexist** and physically cross-link at the ribosome tunnel exit, including when translation initiation is inhibited. This supports a model where NAC’s globular domain is **modestly shifted** relative to mutually exclusive configurations inferred from some cryo-EM models, while still allowing productive Hsp70 positioning for nascent chain interaction. (ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 pages 1-2)

Quantitative context from this work: NAC associates with ribosomes at approximately **~1:1**, while RAC association is lower at **~0.3–0.5:1**. (ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 pages 1-2)

Visual evidence: the study provides schematic/model figures depicting **co-occupancy** of NAC with Zuo1 and Ssb at the tunnel exit. (ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 media efe47529, ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 media 7869bf08)

### 2.2. 2024: NAC subunits and mitochondrial quality control (mitophagy)
A 2024 *Scientific Reports* study focusing on **Egd1 (β subunit)** in mitophagy reports that:
- Mitophagy is **strongly reduced** in **egd1Δ** cells.
- By contrast, loss of the sole α subunit **Egd2** causes only a **partial reduction** in mitophagy, consistent with Egd2 being supportive but not the primary limiting subunit for this phenotype.
- Mechanistically, **Atg32 phosphorylation** is decreased in egd1Δ, and forced Atg32 hyperphosphorylation can almost completely restore mitophagy in egd1Δ. (tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2)

Importantly for Egd2’s functional annotation, this paper also notes:
- NAC is a peripheral component of cytoplasmic ribosomes interacting with nascent chains.
- **Loss of Egd2 causes partial defects in mitochondrial protein import.**
- Cells lacking both Egd1 and Egd2 produce polypeptides normally but have **fewer ribosomes associated with the mitochondrial surface**, linking NAC subunits (including Egd2) to ribosome positioning relevant for mitochondrial protein biogenesis. (tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2)

### 2.3. 2024: Proteotoxic aggregation models (polyQ/prions) implicate NAC as an aggregation organizer
A 2024 *PLOS ONE* yeast study reports that **disruption of NAC** reduces polyglutamine (polyQ) aggregation/toxicity: NAC disruption improved viability in cells expressing expanded polyQ, **delayed and reduced aggregation**, and altered aggregate morphology; it also changed de novo prion induction. (dublinryan2024disruptionofthe pages 1-2)

While this study is not uniquely about Egd2, it directly includes yeast NAC composition (Egd2 as α; Egd1/Btt1 as β) and reinforces that NAC is a key determinant of aggregate organization in vivo. (dublinryan2024disruptionofthe pages 1-2)

### 2.4. 2024: Comparative subunit-function analysis underscores abundance and paralog-specific roles
A 2024 *Cell Stress and Chaperones* paper emphasizes that NAC is extremely abundant (near ribosome stoichiometry), with Nacβ2 (Btt1) much less abundant than Nacβ1 (Egd1), and describes subunit-specific functional specialization (including links to CCR4–Not/Caf130 effects on Rpl4 mRNA in other contexts). It also notes “recent reports” linking β subunits to selective mitochondrial degradation and protein import (consistent with the mitophagy work above). (schilke2024functionalsimilaritiesand pages 1-3, schilke2024functionalsimilaritiesand pages 9-10)

## 3) Experimentally supported functions, pathways, and localization of Egd2

## 3.1. Ribosome binding and physical positioning at the exit tunnel
Mechanistic mapping of NAC in yeast indicates it is **quantitatively associated with ribosomes** and contacts ribosomal proteins at the tunnel exit; Egd2 (αNAC) is reported to contact **Rpl17** near **Rpl31**, supporting the proposal of an adapter surface at **Rpl31/Rpl17** for ribosome-associated factors. (pech2010dualbindingmode pages 1-2)

Further, the first ~**23 amino acids** of βNAC are sufficient to target an otherwise non-associated protein to ribosomes, consistent with a major ribosome-anchoring role for the β subunit, while α (Egd2) contributes additional tunnel-exit contacts and functional capacity. (pech2010dualbindingmode pages 1-2, ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2)

## 3.2. Chaperone function and interaction with other cotranslational proteostasis systems (RAC–Ssb)
A functional dissection of yeast NAC shows that while deletion of NAC alone can be mild, combined perturbation with the Hsp70 system reveals essential overlap:
- **nacΔssbΔ** cells show aggravated defects including impaired growth on **L‑canavanine** or **hygromycin B**, increased aggregation of newly synthesized proteins, and reduced translational activity due to ribosome biogenesis defects.
- The abundant **αβ-NAC (Egd2/Egd1)** heterodimer (but not αβ′/Egd2/Btt1) can suppress these defects, and this rescue requires ribosome association and high expression.
- Deletion of the α‑NAC **UBA domain** enhances aggregation-preventing activity, suggesting a negative regulatory role for this domain in vivo. (ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2, ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 2-4)

These observations support annotation of Egd2 as part of a ribosome-bound chaperone system acting **in parallel/overlap** with RAC–Ssb to preserve cotranslational proteostasis. (ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2)

## 3.3. Modulating SRP-dependent ER targeting and preventing aggregation of secretory nascent chains
A key mechanistic role for NAC is to regulate access of biogenesis factors at the exit site, including SRP pathway components. Transcript-level RNC association analysis indicates overlap between NAC and SRP substrates and suggests NAC **modulates SRP specificity and fidelity in vivo**. (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 1-2)

Direct functional evidence in yeast indicates Egd2 can protect SRP-dependent nascent chains from aggregation:
- In an SRP mutant background (**sec65‑1**), deleting **EGD2** increases detergent-insoluble aggregation after shift to **37°C**, and pulse-labeling shows increased aggregation of newly synthesized proteins.
- Conversely, overexpression of Egd2 (or NAC) reduces aggregation of newly synthesized proteins and reduces ubiquitinated species in aggregates.
- Egd2 retains residual ribosome binding even without Egd1, and cross-linking data supports an Egd2 homodimeric species (a **52‑kDa** cross-linked adduct). (nyathi2015analysisofthe pages 7-9)

Together these findings support that Egd2-containing NAC acts as an anti-aggregation factor particularly when SRP-dependent cotranslational targeting is compromised. (nyathi2015analysisofthe pages 7-9)

## 3.4. Mitochondrial protein biogenesis and mitophagy (auxiliary roles)
Evidence supports NAC involvement in mitochondrial protein targeting/import and associated quality control:
- RNC association analyses found mRNAs encoding mitochondrial proteins enriched with association to Btt1 and Egd2, and a synthetic growth defect between **egd2Δ** and mitochondrial targeting factor **mft1Δ** was reported, consistent with an auxiliary mitochondrial targeting role. (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 17-18)
- In 2024 mitophagy work, **loss of Egd2** caused **partial defects in mitochondrial protein import** and **partial reduction in mitophagy**, while combined loss of Egd1 and Egd2 reduced ribosome association with the mitochondrial surface. (tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2)

Thus, Egd2’s best-supported mitochondrial annotation is as a **supportive NAC subunit** contributing to efficient mitochondrial protein import and ribosome positioning, rather than a dedicated mitophagy factor. (tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2)

## 4) Current applications and real-world implementations

### 4.1. Yeast as a discovery platform for cotranslational proteostasis and targeting
Because yeast NAC is genetically tractable and its loss can be buffered unless combined with other perturbations, **EGD2 and NAC mutants** are used as sensitized backgrounds to reveal:
- mechanisms of cotranslational folding and nascent-chain aggregation,
- interplay between NAC, SRP, and ribosome-associated Hsp70 systems,
- determinants of organelle targeting and ribosome localization (ER/mitochondria). (nyathi2015analysisofthe pages 7-9, ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2, tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2)

### 4.2. Proteotoxicity/aggregation model systems
The 2024 polyQ study suggests that modulating NAC can change aggregation kinetics/morphology and toxicity in yeast models of human misfolding proteins. Although this is not a clinical implementation, it is a real-world research application: NAC perturbation serves as a tool to explore mechanisms of aggregation and prion biology and to identify potentially “protective” cellular remodeling states. (dublinryan2024disruptionofthe pages 1-2)

## 5) Expert opinions and analysis (authoritative sources)

### 5.1. NAC as a general cotranslational chaperone and regulator of targeting-factor access
The combination of systematic RNC association and mechanistic ribosome-contact studies supports a consensus view that NAC is a **general cotranslational chaperone** and that it helps regulate access of other biogenesis factors at the exit tunnel, including SRP, rather than being solely an antagonist of SRP. (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 1-2, pech2010dualbindingmode pages 1-2)

### 5.2. Functional modularity: EGD2 is important but often buffered
Multiple yeast studies highlight that **loss of NAC alone is often mild**, but strong phenotypes emerge when combined with defects in other cotranslational networks (e.g., Ssb/RAC or SRP pathway mutants). This is consistent with an expert interpretation of Egd2 as a **robustness factor** that provides redundancy and buffering capacity in proteostasis and targeting pathways. (ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2, nyathi2015analysisofthe pages 7-9)

### 5.3. Updated view (2024): simultaneous tunnel-exit occupancy is plausible in vivo
In vivo cross-linking evidence from 2024 supports a revised, more flexible model of the tunnel exit where NAC can coexist with Zuo1/Hsp70 systems, implying that factor competition and coordination is dynamic and context-dependent. (ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 pages 1-2)

## 6) Statistics and quantitative data (recent studies and key mechanistic measurements)

- **Relative abundance of NAC β paralogs:** Btt1/Nacβ2 is reported as ~**100×** less concentrated than Egd1/Nacβ1 in yeast (also reported as **~20–100×** lower), explaining why Egd2/Egd1 is predominant. (ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2, schilke2024functionalsimilaritiesand pages 1-3)
- **Ribosome stoichiometry:** NAC associates with ribosomes at approximately **~1:1**, while RAC associates at **~0.3–0.5:1** (yeast in vivo cross-linking study, 2024). (ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 pages 1-2)
- **Cross-link readouts:** an Egd2 homodimer-like DSS cross-linked species at **~52 kDa** was observed and increased with Egd2 overexpression. (nyathi2015analysisofthe pages 7-9)
- **Gene category responses upon NAC perturbation (systems-level):** induction of categories including **protein folding genes (6%)** and **ribosome biogenesis genes (37%)** was reported in a dataset analyzing responses to NAC perturbation. (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 17-18)
- **Mitophagy phenotype stratification (2024):** Atg32-dependent mitophagy is **strongly reduced** in egd1Δ, while egd2Δ shows **partial/slight** reduction; Atg32 phosphorylation decreases in egd1Δ and forced hyperphosphorylation rescues mitophagy. (tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2)

Egd2 is less critical than Egd1 for mitophagy, but Egd2 loss still causes **partial defects** in mitochondrial protein import and a **partial reduction in mitophagy**, indicating supportive function of α-NAC-containing complexes in mitochondria-directed protein biogenesis (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 17-18, tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2) | A **synthetic growth defect** was reported between **egd2Δ** and mitochondrial targeting factor **mft1Δ**; in mitophagy assays, **egd1Δ** caused strong reduction whereas **egd2Δ** caused only **partial/slight reduction** (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 17-18, tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2) |
| Coexistence with RAC/Zuo1/Hsp70 | Recent in vivo cross-linking indicates NAC does **not have to exclude** the Zuo1/Hsp70 machinery from the tunnel exit. Instead, Egd2/Egd1-containing NAC can coexist with **Zuotin (Zuo1) and Ssb**, with a modest positional shift relative to mutually exclusive cryo-EM models (ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 pages 1-2, ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 media efe47529) | Cross-linking-supported occupancy model: **NAC ~1:1 ribosome**, **RAC ~0.3–0.5:1 ribosome**; coexistence persisted even when **translation initiation was inhibited** (ziegelhoffer2024nacandzuotinhsp70 pages 1-2) |
| Genetic interactions / phenotypes | **EGD2 deletion alone** is often mild or phenotypically silent, but stress-sensitive phenotypes emerge in sensitized backgrounds. **nacΔssbΔ** cells show impaired growth, protein aggregation, and reduced translation/ribosome biogenesis; **αβ-NAC** rescues these defects in a ribosome-binding-dependent manner. In **sec65-1**, EGD2 loss worsens aggregation; in proteotoxicity models, broader NAC disruption can unexpectedly reduce **polyQ aggregation/toxicity** and alter prion induction (ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2, ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 2-4, dublinryan2024disruptionofthe pages 1-2, nyathi2015analysisofthe pages 7-9) | Reported stressors/conditions include **L-canavanine**, **hygromycin B**, and **37°C** temperature shift; NAC disruption in 2024 work improved viability in expanded **polyQ**-expressing cells and changed aggregate morphology/prion induction (ott2015functionaldissectionof pages 1-2, dublinryan2024disruptionofthe pages 1-2, nyathi2015analysisofthe pages 7-9) |
| Systems-level response to NAC loss | Loss of NAC triggers a compensatory proteostasis/ribosome-biogenesis response, consistent with a broad role in managing nascent-chain maturation and translational homeostasis (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 17-18) | In one dataset, induced categories after NAC perturbation included **protein folding genes (6%)** and **ribosome biogenesis genes (37%)** among responsive gene sets (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 17-18) |

## Notes on scope and evidence limitations
- The available evidence strongly supports Egd2’s **ribosome/tunnel-exit association**, roles in **cotranslational chaperoning**, and **SRP/mitochondrial pathway interplay**, but does not provide a single, universally agreed “substrate list” for Egd2 alone because NAC interacts broadly with nascent chains and different subunits show distinct preferences in RNC association assays. (alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 17-18, alamo2011definingthespecificity pages 1-2)
- Several quantitative outcomes in newer yeast studies are described as “partial/slight” rather than giving fold-changes in the accessible excerpts (e.g., Egd2’s magnitude of mitophagy reduction), and deeper numeric extraction would require targeted reading of full result tables/figures beyond the snippets. (tian2024thenascentpolypeptideassociated pages 1-2)
