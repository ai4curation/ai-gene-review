Gene Description

NIPI-3 is a conserved Tribbles family pseudokinase that serves as an adapter protein regulating innate immune signaling and larval development in C. elegans. Despite containing a protein kinase domain, NIPI-3 lacks key catalytic residues and is predicted to be catalytically inactive. It functions primarily in epidermal immunity against fungal pathogens (D. coniospora) by acting upstream of the PMK-1/p38 MAPK cascade to induce antimicrobial peptide genes (nlp-29, nlp-31). In the intestine, NIPI-3 mediates defense against Gram-negative (P. aeruginosa) and Gram-positive bacteria by negatively regulating the C/EBP transcription factor CEBP-1, which in turn controls VHP-1 phosphatase levels and PMK-1 phosphorylation status. NIPI-3 directly interacts with CEBP-1 and represses its transcription. Loss of nipi-3 causes larval arrest at L2-L3, demonstrating its essential role in development beyond immunity. The protein localizes to nuclei in epidermis, intestine, and neurons.

Core Functions

NIPI-3 functions as an adapter pseudokinase that binds to and negatively regulates the CEBP-1 transcription factor. Through this interaction, NIPI-3 coordinates innate immune responses in epidermis (antifungal) and intestine (antibacterial) and modulates MAPK signaling and transcription factor activity.

Conclusions
- NIPI-3 (G5EED4) is a C. elegans Tribbles-family pseudokinase that integrates developmental and innate immune signaling. In epidermal defense, it acts upstream of the canonical p38 MAPK pathway to drive AMP gene induction. In development, it restrains PMK-1 activity by repressing CEBP-1 and its target sek-1, preventing lethal hyperactivation of the p38 module. Tissue-restricted, largely nuclear expression and strong genetic interactions with CEBP-1 and p38 pathway genes position NIPI-3 as a master regulator of stress‚Äìdevelopment tradeoffs. 2022 suppressor and reporter studies expanded this framework, revealing feedback and chromatin/N-terminal acetylation modulators downstream of NIPI-3. Open biochemical questions remain regarding direct molecular mechanisms of CEBP-1 repression and any catalytic functions of NIPI-3. Collectively, these data establish NIPI-3 as a central adaptor orchestrating context-dependent control of p38 MAPK signaling in vivo (Pujol et al., 2008; McEwan et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2016; Malinow et al., 2022).
