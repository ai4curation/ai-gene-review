Gene Description

MKSR-2 (MKS1-related protein 2) is a B9 domain-containing protein that functions as a component of the MKS (Meckel syndrome) module at the ciliary transition zone. It is the C. elegans ortholog of mammalian B9D2. MKSR-2 forms a complex with MKS-1 and MKSR-1 and localizes specifically to the transition zone/basal body of sensory cilia. The protein functions redundantly with nephrocystins (NPHP-1, NPHP-4) to regulate cilia formation and maintenance in amphid and phasmid ciliated sensory neurons. The MKS module, together with the NPHP module, establishes basal body/ transition zone membrane attachments and a ciliary gate that restricts entry of non-ciliary components. Loss of mksr-2 in combination with other MKS/NPHP pathway mutations leads to ciliogenesis defects and altered lifespan via abnormal insulin-IGF-I signaling.

In C. elegans, MKS-5/RPGRIP1L and CEP-290 act as hierarchical assembly factors for MKS-module components; disruption causes mislocalization of membrane proteins (e.g., ARL-13) and altered phosphoinositide composition (Li et al., 2016; PLoS Biol, Mar 2016; https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1002416; Jensen et al., 2015; EMBO J, Oct 2015; https://doi.org/10.15252/embj.201488044) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2, lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 17-20).

Primary function and molecular role of MKSR-2
‚Ä¢ Structural/organizational role at the TZ: MKSR-2 is a core MKS-module protein that is necessary for recruitment/stability of other MKS-module components at the TZ, notably MKS-2/TMEM216 and peripheral proteins such as JBTS-14/TMEM237 and MKS-6/CC2D2A. Conversely, MKSR-2 is not required for TZ localization of the master scaffold MKS-5 (RPGRIP1L), CEP-290, or NPHP-module proteins, placing MKSR-2 within the B9/MKS core downstream of assembly factors (Lange et al., 2021; Dis Model Mech, May 2021; https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.05.22.110668) (lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 17-20, lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 14-17).
‚Ä¢ Gate function: Loss or pathogenic missense mutation of mksr-2 disrupts TZ gate properties, enabling inappropriate entry of periciliary/cell membrane proteins into cilia (e.g., RPI-2/RP2 reporter), indicating a compromised diffusion barrier when MKSR-2 function is strongly perturbed (Lange et al., 2021; Dis Model Mech, May 2021; https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.05.22.110668) (lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 14-17). More generally, TZ assembly factors (MKS-5, CEP-290) establish a ciliary zone of exclusion where B9 components (including MKSR-2) reside (Jensen et al., 2015; EMBO J, Oct 2015; https://doi.org/10.15252/embj.201488044; Li et al., 2016; PLoS Biol, Mar 2016; https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1002416) (lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 17-20).

Localization and complex membership
‚Ä¢ Localization: Endogenous and transgenic reporters localize MKSR-2 to the TZ/basal body of ciliated sensory neurons. Mammalian B9D2 localizes to basal bodies/centrosomes in IMCD3 cells. Localization of B9 proteins is interdependent across the B9 complex (MKS-1, MKSR-1/B9D1, MKSR-2) (Bialas et al., 2009; J Cell Sci; https://doi.org/10.1242/jcs.028621) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 5-6, bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2).
‚Ä¢ Complex membership and hierarchy: MKSR-2 is a core MKS-module component within the B9 subcomplex; TZ localization of many MKS proteins depends on MKS-5 and CEP-290. MKSR-2 is required for proper TZ levels/organization of MKS‚Äë2/TMEM216 and select peripheral MKS proteins but not for CEP‚Äë290/MKS‚Äë5 placement, supporting a hierarchical model (Lange et al., 2021; Dis Model Mech; https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.05.22.110668; Li et al., 2016; PLoS Biol; https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1002416) (lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 17-20, lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 14-17).

Interaction partners and genetic interactions
‚Ä¢ Interactions within MKS module: Genetic and localization dependencies demonstrate functional linkage between MKSR-2 and MKS-1, MKSR-1/B9D1, MKS-2/TMEM216, MKS-6/CC2D2A, and TMEM proteins (e.g., TMEM237/JBTS-14) at the TZ (Bialas et al., 2009; J Cell Sci; https://doi.org/10.1242/jcs.028621; Huang et al., 2011; AJHG, Dec 2011; https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajhg.2011.11.005; Lange et al., 2021; DMM; https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.05.22.110668) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 5-6, lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 14-17).
‚Ä¢ Assembly factors: MKS-5/RPGRIP1L and CEP-290 are master assembly factors required for correct TZ architecture and for placing multiple MKS-module components; MKSR-2 dependence is downstream of these factors for its role in stabilizing/permitting other MKS components at the TZ (Li et al., 2016; PLoS Biol; https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1002416) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2).
‚Ä¢ MKS‚ÄìNPHP cooperation: Genetic analyses show the MKS and NPHP modules act in parallel. Combined loss of an MKS component with NPHP genes (nphp‚Äë1 or nphp‚Äë4) causes strong ciliogenesis and structural defects not evident in single mutants, placing MKSR-2 in a network that cooperates with NPHP factors (Yee et al., 2015; PLoS Genet; https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1005627; Bialas et al., 2009; J Cell Sci; https://doi.org/10.1242/jcs.028621) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2, bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 9-10).

Phenotypes upon mutation (C. elegans)
‚Ä¢ Single mutants: Early studies reported that single mksr-2 mutants show subtle or no gross ciliogenesis defects in standard behavioral assays, suggesting roles in ciliary signaling/composition rather than axoneme assembly; however, they can modify lifespan and insulin‚ÄìIGF signaling readouts (DAF-16 nuclear localization) in combination genotypes (Bialas et al., 2009; J Cell Sci; https://doi.org/10.1242/jcs.028621) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 9-10).
‚Ä¢ Combined MKS‚ÄìNPHP disruption: Synergistic TZ ultrastructure defects (e.g., loss of Y-links, mispositioned/docked TZs, truncated cilia) are observed when MKSR‚Äë2 patient alleles are combined with nphp‚Äë4 null, paralleling synthetic defects seen broadly for MKS and NPHP module combinations (Lange et al., 2021; DMM; https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.05.22.110668; Yee et al., 2015; PLoS Genet; https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1005627) (lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 14-17, bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2).
‚Ä¢ TZ gate malfunction: In mksr‚Äë2 nulls and in the strong JBTS missense allele G155S, a periciliary membrane reporter (RPI‚Äë2/RP2) aberrantly enters cilia, indicating compromised TZ diffusion barrier; P74S shows milder effects (Lange et al., 2021; DMM; https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.05.22.110668) (lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 14-17).
‚Ä¢ Sensory/behavioral consequences: Patient-mimicking alleles cause defects in foraging/roaming and osmotic avoidance, especially in an nphp‚Äë4(‚àí) background, consistent with altered ciliary signaling (Lange et al., 2021; DMM; https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.05.22.110668) (lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 14-17).
‚Ä¢ Ciliogenesis versus adhesion: TZ integrity contributes to neuron‚Äìmatrix adhesion and cilium positioning; triple perturbations of TZ components, including mksr‚Äë2, highlight roles in cell adhesion even when many cilia remain, indicating that aspects of axoneme assembly can persist without intact TZ ultrastructure (Schouteden et al., 2015; J Cell Biol, Jul 2015; https://doi.org/10.1083/jcb.201501013) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2).

‚Ä¢ Disease variant interpretation: C. elegans mksr‚Äë2 CRISPR knock-in alleles mimicking human JBTS variants provide an in vivo platform to assess pathogenicity, quantify TZ composition changes, and measure gate function, supporting functional interpretation of patient variants (Lange et al., 2021; DMM; https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.05.22.110668) (lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 14-17).
‚Ä¢ Modeling ciliopathy mechanisms: Genetic interaction paradigms (MKS √ó NPHP) in C. elegans and mouse define modifier effects and parallel pathway cooperation that likely contribute to human phenotypic variability, informing multi-gene risk assessment (Yee et al., 2015; PLoS Genet; https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1005627) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2).
‚Ä¢ Cross-species conservation: Localization and functional conservation of B9D2/MKSR-2 at centrosomes/basal bodies in mammalian cells enable comparative cell biological studies and targeted assays for TZ assembly and ciliogenesis (Bialas et al., 2009; J Cell Sci; https://doi.org/10.1242/jcs.028621) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 5-6).

Expert opinions and authoritative analyses
‚Ä¢ Foundational and integrative studies from leading groups (Blacque, Leroux, Reiter, Barr) define the TZ as a modular gate with MKS and NPHP modules, where B9 proteins (including MKSR‚Äë2) form a core unit that stabilizes other MKS components and supports barrier function. The cooperative, parallel action of these modules is supported by conserved genetic interactions and localization hierarchies (Bialas et al., 2009; Jensen et al., 2015; Li et al., 2016; Yee et al., 2015; URLs above) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 5-6, lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 17-20, bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2).

Human disease links (Joubert syndrome and related ciliopathies)
‚Ä¢ Genetic association: Human B9D2/MKSR2 is implicated in JBTS. ‚Ä¢ Broader MKS-module context: Multiple TMEM proteins at the TZ (including TMEM237/JBTS‚Äë14) are linked to JBTS and require B9/MKS factors for proper function; B9D1/B9D2 requirements are documented in C. elegans models and human genetics (Huang et al., 2011; AJHG; https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajhg.2011.11.005) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2).

‚Ä¢ Biochemical mechanisms by which the B9 domain of MKSR-2 contributes to membrane association or scaffolding at the TZ remain incompletely understood. Conclusion
Collectively, C. elegans mksr‚Äë2 encodes a TZ-localized B9D2 protein that operates as a core organizer within the MKS module to establish and maintain the ciliary diffusion barrier and to scaffold recruitment of other MKS/TMEM factors. Its function is downstream of master TZ scaffolds (MKS‚Äë5/RPGRIP1L, CEP‚Äë290) and cooperates in parallel with NPHP-module proteins to support ciliogenesis and ciliary signaling. Patient-mimicking missense alleles in worms corroborate B9D2‚Äôs role in JBTS pathogenesis by quantifiably altering TZ composition and gate function (Bialas 2009; Jensen 2015; Li 2016; Yee 2015; Lange 2021; URLs and dates above) (bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 5-6, lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 17-20, bialas2009functionalinteractionsbetween pages 1-2, lange2021interpretingthepathogenicity pages 14-17).
