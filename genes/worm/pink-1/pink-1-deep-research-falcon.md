Comprehensive research report: C. elegans pink-1 (UniProt Q09298)
1) Key concepts and definitions with current understanding
- Identity and domains: C. elegans pink-1 encodes a serine/threonine kinase homologous to human PINK1. It contains an N-terminal mitochondrial targeting sequence (MTS) and a transmembrane segment that anchor PINK-1 to the outer mitochondrial membrane (OMM), with a cytosolic catalytic kinase domain featuring canonical N- and C-lobes and PINK1-specific insert regions; a conserved C-terminal region supports activation and substrate recognition (JBC 2009; EMBO J 2021) (samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 2-3, onishi2021molecularmechanismsand pages 7-9).
- Localization and processing: Under normal mitochondrial membrane potential, PINK1 is imported, cleaved by PARL in the inner membrane, retrotranslocated to the cytosol, and degraded via the N-end rule pathway. Upon mitochondrial depolarization/damage, full-length PINK1 is stabilized on the OMM, accumulates, dimerizes, and autophosphorylates to become active (Biophysical Reviews 2021; EMBO J 2021) (markaki2021mitophagymechanismsin pages 1-2, onishi2021molecularmechanismsand pages 7-9).
- Biochemical function: Activated PINK1 phosphorylates ubiquitin at Ser65 and also phosphorylates Parkin (PRKN) on Ser65 in its Ubl domain, promoting Parkin activation. Phospho-ubiquitin amplifies Parkin recruitment in a feed-forward loop that labels damaged mitochondria with ubiquitin chains, recruiting autophagy receptors and driving mitophagy. Deubiquitinases counterbalance this cascade (Biophysical Reviews 2021; EMBO J 2021; Autophagy 2022) (https://doi.org/10.1007/s12551-021-00894-7; https://doi.org/10.15252/embj.2020104705; https://doi.org/10.1080/15548627.2021.1975914) (markaki2021mitophagymechanismsin pages 1-2, onishi2021molecularmechanismsand pages 7-9, zhang2022themultifacetedregulation pages 17-18).
- Pathway role in C. elegans: The PINK-1/PDR-1 (Parkin homolog) pathway mediates mitophagy and mitochondrial quality control in worms, with additional cooperation from the mitophagy receptor DCT-1/BNIP3 depending on context (Antioxidants 2024) (https://doi.org/10.3390/antiox13111343) (ganguly2024mitochondrialqualitycontrol pages 14-15).

2) Recent developments and latest research (prioritize 2023–2024)
- Chemical activation of PINK1-dependent mitophagy in vivo: Spautin-1, despite being a macroautophagy inhibitor, was shown in 2024 to promote PINK1–PRKN-dependent mitophagy by binding TOMM20/TOMM70, preventing PINK1 import/cleavage and stabilizing full-length PINK1 on the OMM. In C. elegans, spautin-1 induced neuronal mitophagy and improved associative learning in an Alzheimer’s-like model; these benefits were abolished by neuronal pink-1/pdr-1 knockdown, demonstrating pink-1 dependence in vivo (Autophagy, published August 2024, https://doi.org/10.1080/15548627.2024.2383145) (yi2024spautin1promotespink1prkndependent pages 6-9, yi2024spautin1promotespink1prkndependent pages 1-2, yi2024spautin1promotespink1prkndependent pages 11-13).
- Neurodegeneration models and mitophagy: A 2024 review synthesizing C. elegans data reported that stimulating mitophagy reverses cognitive deficits in Aβ- and tau-driven models, with rescue requiring functional PINK-1 and PDR-1 and, in some contexts, DCT-1; pink-1 and pdr-1 mutants show depolarized mitochondria, reduced ATP, elevated ROS and Ca2+ (Antioxidants, published November 2024, https://doi.org/10.3390/antiox13111343) (ganguly2024mitochondrialqualitycontrol pages 14-15).

3) Current applications and real-world implementations
- In vivo mitophagy assays: Worm neuronal mitophagy reporters (e.g., mt-Rosella ratiometric biosensor) quantify mitophagic flux; spautin-1 at 1 μM enhances neuronal mitophagy in N2 and rescues mitophagy/learning deficits in AD-like strains in a pink-1/pdr-1–dependent manner, providing a tractable pharmacological tool and readout pipeline in C. elegans (Autophagy 2024) (yi2024spautin1promotespink1prkndependent pages 6-9, yi2024spautin1promotespink1prkndependent pages 11-13).
- Disease-modifying interventions: Pharmacological stimulation of mitophagy in worm AD models improves memory/behavioral phenotypes, and these effects require PINK-1/PDR-1. This positions pink-1 as an essential node for validating mitophagy-enhancing therapeutics in vivo (Antioxidants 2024) (ganguly2024mitochondrialqualitycontrol pages 14-15).
- Genetic interaction mapping: Antagonism between lrk-1 (LRRK2 homolog) and pink-1 informs epistasis tests in stress-response and neuronal morphology assays. Loss of lrk-1 suppresses pink-1 loss-of-function phenotypes (and vice versa for certain ER stress phenotypes), enabling pathway dissection in genetic screens (JBC 2009) (samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 2-3, samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 1-1).

4) Expert opinions and analysis from authoritative sources
- Mechanistic consensus: High-impact reviews concur that PINK1 is a mitochondrial stress sensor whose OMM stabilization upon depolarization is the trigger for a biochemical cascade: autophosphorylated PINK1 phosphorylates Ub and Parkin Ser65, amplifying ubiquitylation of OMM substrates and recruiting autophagy machinery for mitophagy (EMBO J 2021; Biophysical Reviews 2021) (onishi2021molecularmechanismsand pages 7-9, markaki2021mitophagymechanismsin pages 1-2).
- Integration with mitochondrial dynamics: PINK1–Parkin impacts fission/fusion components and mitochondrial motility; in worms, pink-1 loss alters cristae and stress responses, consistent with conserved roles in mitochondrial quality control (Cells 2018; JBC 2009). While Cells 2018 is mammalian-focused, its mechanistic framework aligns with worm observations (https://doi.org/10.3390/cells7100154) (salazar2018theinterplayamong pages 3-5, salazar2018theinterplayamong pages 19-21, samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 2-3).
- Translational relevance in neurodegeneration: Reviews emphasize that mitophagy deficits and oxidative stress are central in PD and AD models, and that interventions restoring PINK1–Parkin signaling show functional rescue in C. elegans, underscoring target validity for therapeutic strategies (Antioxidants 2024; Biophysical Reviews 2021) (ganguly2024mitochondrialqualitycontrol pages 14-15, markaki2021mitophagymechanismsin pages 1-2).

5) Relevant statistics and data from recent and classical studies
- Localization and expression: A rescuing Ppink-1::pink-1::gfp transgene shows broad expression in neurons and other tissues with partial mitochondrial co-localization, validating identity and subcellular targeting in vivo (JBC 2009) (samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 2-3, samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 1-2).
- Ultrastructure and stress phenotypes: pink-1 loss reduces mitochondrial cristae length (approximately 12% reduction in body-wall muscle; >30% in neurons), increases paraquat (oxidative stress) sensitivity, and reduces brood size at 20 °C; these phenotypes are suppressed by lrk-1 loss, demonstrating antagonistic interaction (JBC 2009) (samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 2-3, samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 1-1).
- Biochemistry: PINK1 phosphorylates ubiquitin at Ser65 and Parkin Ser65; phospho-ubiquitin promotes Parkin activation and E3 ligase activity in vitro and in cells, forming a feed-forward mitophagy initiation loop (EMBO J 2021; Biophysical Reviews 2021) (onishi2021molecularmechanismsand pages 7-9, markaki2021mitophagymechanismsin pages 1-2).
- 2024 pharmacology in worms: Spautin-1 induces neuronal mitophagy and improves associative learning in C. elegans AD-like models at low micromolar doses; pink-1/pdr-1 knockdown abolishes the effect, demonstrating pathway dependence. Higher doses inhibit development/growth, consistent with macroautophagy inhibition, underscoring the importance of dosing windows (Autophagy 2024) (yi2024spautin1promotespink1prkndependent pages 6-9, yi2024spautin1promotespink1prkndependent pages 11-13).

Function and pathways in C. elegans pink-1
- Primary function: PINK-1 is a mitochondrial stress-activated serine/threonine kinase whose substrates include ubiquitin (Ser65) and Parkin/PRKN (Ser65). By phosphorylating Ub and Parkin on damaged mitochondria, PINK-1 recruits and activates the E3 ligase PDR-1 (Parkin), leading to ubiquitin-chain deposition on OMM proteins and recruitment of autophagy receptors to drive mitophagy (Biophysical Reviews 2021; EMBO J 2021) (markaki2021mitophagymechanismsin pages 1-2, onishi2021molecularmechanismsand pages 7-9).
- Substrate specificity: The best-established substrates are ubiquitin (Ser65) and Parkin’s Ubl domain (Ser65), with additional context-dependent targets in other species; the Ub/PRKN phosphorylation axis is conserved and central to mitophagy in worms (Biophysical Reviews 2021; Autophagy 2022) (markaki2021mitophagymechanismsin pages 1-2, zhang2022themultifacetedregulation pages 17-18).
- Localization/compartment: PINK-1 acts at the OMM under depolarization; in healthy mitochondria it is imported/cleaved and degraded. Worm transgenes and co-localization confirm mitochondrial targeting, with a cytosolic pool also observed (JBC 2009; Biophysical Reviews 2021) (samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 2-3, markaki2021mitophagymechanismsin pages 1-2).
- Pathway integration: In worms, PINK-1 cooperates with PDR-1/Parkin to mediate mitophagy; DCT-1/BNIP3 can function with or upstream of PINK-1/PDR-1 depending on stimuli (Antioxidants 2024) (ganguly2024mitochondrialqualitycontrol pages 14-15).
- Genetic interactions: lrk-1 (LRRK2 homolog) and pink-1 act antagonistically; lrk-1 loss suppresses pink-1 phenotypes related to oxidative stress sensitivity, mitochondrial ultrastructure, and neurite outgrowth, highlighting interconnected stress-response networks (JBC 2009) (samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 2-3, samann2009caenorhabdititseleganslrk1 pages 1-1).

Conclusions and outlook
C. elegans pink-1 encodes a conserved mitochondrial stress sensor kinase that triggers PDR-1/Parkin-mediated mitophagy by phosphorylating ubiquitin and Parkin at Ser65. Its mitochondrial targeting, import/cleavage/degradation under basal conditions, OMM stabilization upon depolarization, and feed-forward activation of mitophagy are well supported by structural and mechanistic work and validated in vivo in worms. Recent 2024 studies demonstrate that pharmacologic stabilization/activation of PINK1 (e.g., spautin-1) can restore neuronal mitophagy and learning in C. elegans neurodegeneration models in a pink-1/pdr-1–dependent manner, highlighting actionable therapeutic avenues and underscoring the value of worm models for pathway-dissection and compound triage (Autophagy 2024; Antioxidants 2024) (yi2024spautin1promotespink1prkndependent pages 6-9, ganguly2024mitochondrialqualitycontrol pages 14-15).
