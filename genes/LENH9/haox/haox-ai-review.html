It catalyzes the oxidation of L-(S)-lactate to pyruvate, with O‚ÇÇ serving as the electron acceptor and producing H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ[1].   • Products: Pyruvate (used for downstream metabolism) and hydrogen peroxide (H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ). The H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ may act as a byproduct that must be detoxified by peroxidases or similar.

Enzymatic activity and mechanism

  • Enzyme class: LOX is an FMN-dependent oxidoreductase (EC 1.1.3.2) acting on the CH‚ÄìOH group of (S)-lactate with oxygen as acceptor. It belongs to the family of Œ±-hydroxyacid oxidases/dehydrogenases.

  • Mechanism: Studies (mainly on the Aerococcus viridans enzyme) show LOX operates via a flavin-mediated Œ±-hydroxyacid oxidation mechanism[3]. In this model, the enzyme abstracts the Œ±-hydrogen from L-lactate to form a carbanion (often via an active-site base, e.g. a conserved His)[3]. Electrons from the substrate are transferred to the FMN cofactor. The reduced FMN then reacts rapidly with molecular oxygen, yielding H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ and regenerating the oxidized flavin[4][3]. In summary, the substrate‚Äôs Œ±-hydrogen is removed (carbanion or hydride transfer to FMN) and the resulting reduced flavin transfers electrons to O‚ÇÇ to form H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ[4][3].

  • Kinetics: The enzyme shows strict specificity for (S)-lactate (L-lactate)[1]. Kinetic studies indicate large kinetic isotope effects for Œ±-H abstraction, consistent with direct C‚ÄìH bond cleavage in the rate-limiting step[5]. (In LOX the oxidized and reduced forms of FMN are stabilized by conserved residues, similar to other Œ±-hydroxyacid oxidases[3].)

Metabolic pathway involvement

  • Lactate oxidation pathway: LOX provides a route to feed lactate into central metabolism. Under aerobic conditions, LOX converts L-lactate to pyruvate[1]. The pyruvate can then enter the pyruvate metabolism network (KEGG map 00620), for example being converted by pyruvate oxidase (POX) or the pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH) complex into acetyl-CoA and ultimately acetate (with ATP generation)[2]. Indeed, in L. buchneri (a close relative) a pathway has been proposed where LOX (or LDH) supplies pyruvate which is then converted via POX to acetate under oxygen, yielding extra ATP[2].

  • Energy metabolism: This LOX-driven pathway effectively allows the cells to derive extra energy from lactate when oxygen is present. In heterofermentative lactobacilli, such as L. hilgardii/buchneri, the conversion of lactate to acetate (instead of ethanol) yields additional ATP and raises pH, stabilizing aerobic growth[2].

The concomitant H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ production may also inhibit competing microbes.
