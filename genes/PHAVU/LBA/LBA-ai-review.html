Gene Description

LBA (UniProt P02234; LGBA_PHAVU, "PvLba") encodes leghemoglobin alpha / leghemoglobin component A of Phaseolus vulgaris (common/kidney bean), a member of the plant globin family (InterPro IPR000971 Globin, IPR001032 Leghaemoglobin-like, IPR019824 Leghaemoglobin_Fe_BS). It is a small (~16 kDa, 146 aa) monomeric heme protein expressed at very high abundance specifically in the cytosol of infected cells of functional legume root nodules. Its core molecular function is reversible, high-affinity binding of dioxygen at a pentacoordinated ferrous (Fe2+) heme iron. Physiologically, leghemoglobin resolves the nodule "oxygen paradox": it acts as an oxygen carrier/buffer that delivers O2 toward the symbiosomes to sustain the high respiratory flux of nitrogen-fixing bacteroids while keeping free O2 extremely low (sub-micromolar, reported <50 nM in infected cells) so that the oxygen-labile bacterial nitrogenase is not inactivated. This O2-homeostasis role is essential for symbiotic nitrogen fixation. Beyond O2 transport, PvLba also participates in nodule nitrogen/oxidative chemistry: it is nitrated in vivo on distal-pocket tyrosines (mainly Tyr-31, with Tyr-26 and Tyr-134) by a nitrite/peroxide-dependent (ferryl-Lb) mechanism, and the broader nodule hemoglobin network contributes to NO/ROS homeostasis. UniProt also notes a phosphoserine (Ser-46) that modulates the heme pocket. The protein functions in the infected-cell cytosol (a nucleus localization is asserted only by similarity/ISS and is not the site of its O2-carrier function). The gene symbol "LBA" here refers unambiguously to the bean nodule leghemoglobin and is not to be confused with unrelated "LBA" symbols (e.g. mammalian albumin) in other organisms.

Comprehensive research report: Phaseolus vulgaris LBA (UniProt P02234) — leghemoglobin alpha / Leghemoglobin A (PvLba)

0) Executive summary

Leghemoglobin alpha (PvLba; gene symbol LBA) from Phaseolus vulgaris (common/kidney bean) is a nodule-localized plant globin (heme protein) whose primary biological role is to buffer and transport oxygen (O2) in infected nodule cells, enabling high respiratory flux to symbiotic bacteroids while keeping free O2 extremely low to protect oxygen-labile nitrogenase (“oxygen paradox”). Beyond O2 transport, leghemoglobins participate in nitrogen/oxidative chemistry in nodules; in bean nodules PvLba is nitrated in vivo at a key distal-pocket tyrosine (Tyr30), indicating intimate coupling between leghemoglobin redox chemistry and NO/nitrite-derived pathways. Recent (2024) work in model legumes emphasizes gene-specific regulation of nodule hemoglobin families by nitrate and dark stress, and demonstrates that leghemoglobin deficiency triggers nitro-oxidative stress (ROS/NO accumulation). In parallel, 2023–2024 literature documents major real-world deployment of recombinant leghemoglobin (principally soy LegH) in plant-based meat analogues, with detailed regulatory safety evaluations by EFSA.

2) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

2.1 Leghemoglobin vs. other plant hemoglobins

Legume nodules express multiple hemoglobins. “Leghemoglobins” (Lbs) are distinguished from non-symbiotic “phytoglobins” (Glbs) by their very high abundance in nodules, their central role in symbiotic O2 homeostasis, and their specialization for infected nodule cells, whereas Glbs are generally much lower abundance and distributed across multiple tissues with broader roles including NO homeostasis/signaling. (minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 1-2)

2.2 The “oxygen paradox” in symbiotic nitrogen fixation (SNF)

Nitrogenase is highly oxygen-sensitive, but bacteroids require O2 for respiration to generate ATP/reductant for N2 fixation. Leghemoglobin resolves this by maintaining a near-anaerobic free-O2 environment while supporting O2 flux to respiration. In nodules, Lbs can occur at ~1–5 mM in infected host-cell cytosol and deliver O2 toward symbiosomes while buffering free O2. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3, lamoureux2024theeffectof pages 26-29)

2.3 Molecular function of PvLba (primary functional annotation)

PvLba is a heme-binding globin whose primary biochemical function is reversible O2 binding and facilitated diffusion/transport; only the ferrous (Fe2+) heme binds O2 efficiently, consistent with canonical globin chemistry. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3)

3) Molecular and cellular function of PvLba in Phaseolus vulgaris

3.1 Cellular/tissue localization

Bean leghemoglobin A (PvLba) is a hallmark protein of functional legume root nodules, localized in host cells associated with the symbiosis; experimentally, nitrated PvLba and other nitrated proteins are detected in intact bean nodules, indicating in situ localization and modification during nodule development and senescence. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3, sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 3-4)

3.2 Role in O2 delivery and buffering for nitrogen fixation

Leghemoglobins are described as abundant hemeproteins in nodules that function as O2 transporters delivering O2 to symbiosomes. Their high O2 affinity and abundance allow O2 delivery for bacteroid respiration while maintaining very low free O2 compatible with nitrogenase. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3, lamoureux2024theeffectof pages 26-29)

Although the strongest gene-disruption evidence in the retrieved corpus is from Lotus japonicus (RNAi/CRISPR leghemoglobin perturbations), it supports the conserved conclusion that loss of nodule Lb function elevates free O2 and impairs nitrogenase activity/SNF—consistent with the central function expected for PvLba in bean nodules. (lamoureux2024theeffectof pages 26-29, minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 1-2)

4) Nitrogen/oxidative chemistry, post-translational modification (PTM), and mechanistic insights (bean-specific evidence)

4.1 In vivo nitration of PvLba in bean nodules

A key bean nodule study demonstrated that leghemoglobin is nitrated in functional nodules, with Tyr30 (in the distal heme pocket) as the major nitration site and additional nitration observed at Tyr25 and Tyr133. This was supported by immunoblot detection of nitrotyrosine and site-resolved LC–MS/MS identification of nitrated peptides from PvLba. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3, sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 18-25)

Figure-based evidence from the same study includes immunoblot panels for nitrated PvLba, MS/MS spectra supporting nitrated Tyr residues, quantification of Tyr30 nitration across nodule stages/treatments, and a structural model mapping Tyr residues relative to the heme pocket. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated media 8d9f7f38, sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated media 59b8f9fe, sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated media 085516d0, sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated media 7e48c43c)

4.2 Proposed chemical mechanism: nitrite/peroxide-dependent nitration (vs. peroxynitrite)

In vitro assays with purified Lbs supported a mechanism in which nitration requires nitrite + H2O2, and proposed that nitration proceeds via oxoferryl (ferryl) Lb generating reactive nitrogen species (e.g., •NO2) and tyrosyl radicals, while peroxynitrite was not an efficient nitrating agent in this system. This mechanistically links PvLba redox cycling and nodule peroxide/nitrite chemistry to a specific PTM within the heme cavity. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3)

4.3 Integration with ROS/NO homeostasis

Recent synthesis in the nodule hemoglobin field emphasizes that hemoglobins contribute to NO and ROS homeostasis beyond O2 buffering. In Lotus, nodules lacking Lbs accumulate ROS and NO (nitro-oxidative stress), reinforcing that Lb activity (and/or hemoglobin network balance) influences redox and NO-related pathways in nodules. (minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 1-2)

5) Regulation and pathway context: developmental and environmental control (2024 priority)

Direct 2024 regulation data in the retrieved corpus come from Lotus japonicus but are highly informative for leghemoglobin functional annotation because the regulatory logic (developmental induction, nitrate repression, stress responses) is broadly conserved in legume SNF programs.

A 2024 Journal of Experimental Botany study quantified regulation of all nodule hemoglobins in Lotus across development and environmental perturbations (nitrate, dark stress) and used genetic backgrounds affecting nitrate sensitivity and senescence. Key findings include:

  • Nitrate repression of leghemoglobins: Lb1 and Lb2 transcripts decreased by ~58% at 0.5 mM nitrate. (minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 11-13)
  • Gene-specific regulation: Lbs and class-2 Glbs were suppressed by nitrate, while class-1 and class-3 Glbs tended to increase with nitrate, highlighting distinct functional classes (O2 vs NO roles). (minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 1-2)
  • Promoter architecture: nitrate-responsive elements (NREs/dNREs) were found in promoters of several hemoglobin genes, connecting nitrate signaling to hemoglobin regulation. (minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 11-13)
  • Functional consequence of Lb loss: Lb-deficient mutant nodules accumulate ROS and NO and show altered antioxidant/senescence markers, connecting Lb status to nodular redox homeostasis. (minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 1-2)

These 2024 findings support a current understanding in which leghemoglobins operate within a regulated hemoglobin network that integrates O2 delivery, NO metabolism, and stress responses in nodules, and is responsive to external nitrogen availability and carbon status (dark stress). (minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 1-2)

6) Current applications and real-world implementations (with 2023–2024 emphasis)

6.1 Food/industrial deployment of recombinant leghemoglobin (mostly soy LegH)

While this report targets bean PvLba, leghemoglobins as a protein class have a major real-world application in plant-based meat analogues. In 2024, EFSA evaluated soy leghemoglobin produced in genetically modified Komagataella phaffii as a food additive/color for meat analogue products, reflecting mature industrial-scale implementation and regulatory scrutiny. (younes2024safetyofsoy pages 1-2, casacuberta2024assessmentofsoy pages 1-2)

EFSA describes the standardized preparation (LegH Prep) and its constraints (e.g., no viable production-strain cells permitted), composition, and batch-to-batch measurements. (younes2024safetyofsoy pages 8-9)

6.2 Production metrics and functional use in meat analogues (reviewed data)

A 2023 review summarizes that recombinant leghemoglobin production in yeast (e.g., Pichia/Komagataella) has been optimized substantially; cited work reports >83-fold increases in secretion with titers up to 3.5 g/L and a reported heme binding ratio of 93%, consistent with scaling requirements for commercial use as a “heme flavor catalyst” and colorant. (swiatek2023thepotentialfor pages 7-8)

6.3 Agronomic relevance: SNF optimization and leghemoglobin as a target

An expert perspective in 2024 argues that leghemoglobin is central to O2 homeostasis for maximal SNF, and highlights an open applied question: whether leghemoglobin levels are already optimal across legume genotypes. The authors propose assessing genotype variation in leghemoglobin abundance and correlating it with SNF performance; if non-optimal, they point to breeding (exploiting natural variation) and engineering approaches (adding/upregulating Lb genes) as potential strategies. (udvardi2024geneticsandgenomics pages 5-7)

7) Relevant statistics and quantitative data (recent studies prioritized)

Key quantitative values useful for functional annotation and real-world context include:

  • Leghemoglobin abundance in nodules: Lbs occur at ~1–5 mM in infected host-cell cytosol (high-abundance oxygen buffer/transport system). (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3)
  • Low free O2 in infected cells: free O2 is maintained at <50 nM in infected cells (reported in a 2024 mechanistic synthesis), consistent with nitrogenase protection. (lamoureux2024theeffectof pages 26-29)
  • Nitrate repression (2024): Lb1/Lb2 transcripts decreased ~58% at 0.5 mM nitrate in Lotus nodules (gene-specific regulation, relevant to SNF management). (minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 11-13)

Food/regulatory statistics (2024 EFSA opinions):

  • Maximum proposed use level in meat analogue products: 0.8% soy leghemoglobin protein (used for EFSA exposure/iron calculations). (casacuberta2024assessmentofsoy pages 12-12)
  • Estimated dietary exposure (DietEx model, maximum use level): highest mean exposure 7.3 mg LegH/kg bw/day (toddlers/children) and highest 95th percentile 32.9 mg/kg bw/day (toddlers). (younes2024safetyofsoy pages 12-13)
  • Corresponding iron exposure estimates: ~0.03–0.06 mg/kg bw/day (mean) and 0.13–0.27 mg/kg bw/day (95th percentile) based on measured Fe per kg LegH. (younes2024safetyofsoy pages 15-16)
  • In vivo toxicology (rat): 90-day dietary study found no adverse effects up to 2352 mg/kg bw/day (males) and 2894 mg/kg bw/day (females) for LegH Prep from strain MXY0541. (casacuberta2024assessmentofsoy pages 10-11)

8) Expert opinions and analysis (authoritative sources)

  • Leghemoglobin as a potential SNF optimization lever (2024): A 2024 expert review posits that because leghemoglobin is so central to O2 regulation in nodules, it is worth testing whether leghemoglobin quantity/isoform composition varies among genotypes and whether it correlates with SNF performance; if so, it could be improved via breeding or engineering. (udvardi2024geneticsandgenomics pages 5-7)

  • Redox vulnerability and open questions (2024): A 2024 mechanistic synthesis emphasizes Lb susceptibility to oxidative stress (oxidation to ferric state inactivating O2 transport) and notes that proposed ROS-buffering roles (ferryl Lb/globin radicals) remain incompletely examined, highlighting a research gap relevant to stress resilience. (lamoureux2024theeffectof pages 26-29)

9) Practical functional annotation for PHAVU LBA (UniProt P02234)

Primary molecular function: oxygen-binding heme globin; facilitates O2 diffusion/transport and buffers O2 in nodules. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3, lamoureux2024theeffectof pages 26-29)

Biological process: symbiotic nitrogen fixation support via nodule oxygen homeostasis (“oxygen paradox” resolution). (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3, udvardi2024geneticsandgenomics pages 5-7)

Cellular component (best-supported): infected host-cell cytosol of root nodules / in proximity to symbiosomes; leghemoglobin acts to deliver O2 toward symbiosomal membranes. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3, lamoureux2024theeffectof pages 26-29)

Pathway connections: integrates with nodule redox pathways (peroxide handling, ferric/ferrous cycling, antioxidant systems) and NO/nitrite-associated chemistry (PvLba tyrosine nitration; hemoglobin network roles in NO/ROS homeostasis). (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3, minguillon2024dynamicsofhemoglobins pages 1-2)

PTM evidence (bean-specific): in vivo nitration at Tyr30 (distal heme pocket) with additional Tyr25/Tyr133 nitration; nitrite/peroxide-dependent mechanism supported. (sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 1-3, sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated pages 18-25, sainz2015leghemoglobinisnitrated media 8d9f7f38)

description: >
  LBA (UniProt P02234; LGBA_PHAVU, "PvLba") encodes leghemoglobin alpha / leghemoglobin
  component A of Phaseolus vulgaris (common/kidney bean), a member of the plant globin family
  (InterPro IPR000971 Globin, IPR001032 Leghaemoglobin-like, IPR019824 Leghaemoglobin_Fe_BS).
  It is a small (~16 kDa, 146 aa) monomeric heme protein expressed at very high abundance
  specifically in the cytosol of infected cells of functional legume root nodules. Its core
  molecular function is reversible, high-affinity binding of dioxygen at a pentacoordinated
  ferrous (Fe2+) heme iron. Physiologically, leghemoglobin resolves the nodule "oxygen
  paradox": it acts as an oxygen carrier/buffer that delivers O2 toward the symbiosomes to
  sustain the high respiratory flux of nitrogen-fixing bacteroids while keeping free O2
  extremely low (sub-micromolar, reported <50 nM in infected cells) so that the
  oxygen-labile bacterial nitrogenase is not inactivated. This O2-homeostasis role is
  essential for symbiotic nitrogen fixation. Beyond O2 transport, PvLba also participates
  in nodule nitrogen/oxidative chemistry: it is nitrated in vivo on distal-pocket tyrosines
  (mainly Tyr-31, with Tyr-26 and Tyr-134) by a nitrite/peroxide-dependent (ferryl-Lb)
  mechanism, and the broader nodule hemoglobin network contributes to NO/ROS homeostasis.
  UniProt also notes a phosphoserine (Ser-46) that modulates the heme pocket. The protein
  functions in the infected-cell cytosol (a nucleus localization is asserted only by
  similarity/ISS and is not the site of its O2-carrier function). The gene symbol "LBA"
  here refers unambiguously to the bean nodule leghemoglobin and is not to be confused with
  unrelated "LBA" symbols (e.g. mammalian albumin) in other organisms.
