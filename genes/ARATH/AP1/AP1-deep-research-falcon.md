## Research report: Arabidopsis thaliana **APETALA1 (AP1/AGL7)** functional annotation (UniProt **P35631**)

### 0) Target verification (mandatory disambiguation)
The target specified—UniProt accession **P35631**—corresponds to **Arabidopsis thaliana** floral homeotic protein **APETALA1 (AP1)**, also called **AGAMOUS-like MADS-box protein AGL7**, encoded by **At1g69120**. Evidence used here consistently refers to the Arabidopsis floral meristem/organ identity regulator AP1, and its **MIKC-type MADS-box transcription factor** domain architecture (MADS/I/K/C) matches the UniProt-described MADS-box/MEF2_TF and K-box domains. (xu2025ap1isa pages 1-2)

### 1) Key concepts, definitions, and current understanding

#### 1.1 Floral meristem identity (FMI) and “A-class” function
**Floral meristem identity (FMI)** genes drive the transition of a shoot/inflorescence meristem toward a determinate floral program. In classic Arabidopsis genetics, **AP1** is a central FMI regulator and also contributes to **outer-whorl (sepal/petal) organ identity** (“A-function” in the historical ABC framework), although later analyses emphasize that AP1’s meristem-fate role and organ-identity role are tightly intertwined rather than cleanly separable. (okamuro1993regulationofarabidopsis pages 4-6, litt2007anevaluationof pages 14-15)

#### 1.2 What AP1 “does” at the molecular level (definition of function)
AP1 is a **DNA-binding transcription factor** of the plant **MIKC-type MADS-box family**. Its modular architecture includes an N-terminal **MADS (DNA-binding) domain**, an adjacent **Intervening (I) domain**, a **Keratin-like (K) domain** that mediates protein–protein interactions and higher-order oligomerization, and a C-terminal region often involved in regulatory specificity. (xu2025ap1isa pages 1-2, xu2025ap1isa media bf7c6421)

MADS TFs recognize **CArG-box** motifs (e.g., **CC[A/T]6GG** or consensus **CC[W]5–7GG**) and can function as dimers; in floral development they often act in higher-order complexes (“quartets”) that can bind pairs of CArG boxes and loop DNA, modulating transcriptional output. (wu2025researchprogresson pages 5-7, xu2025ap1isa pages 1-2)

#### 1.3 AP1 as a pioneer transcription factor (emerging concept)
Recent mechanistic work supports that AP1 can function as a **pioneer transcription factor**, i.e., it can bind relatively **inaccessible/closed chromatin** and promote **chromatin opening** during fate transitions. This is described as a tetramerization-dependent capability in inducible AP1 systems. (xu2025ap1isa pages 6-8)

### 2) Molecular function, pathways, and regulatory logic

#### 2.1 Upstream regulation: LEAFY (LFY) induces AP1
A high-confidence, classic result is that **LFY transcriptionally induces early AP1 expression** at the developmental stage when floral fate is assumed. Using a steroid-inducible LFY-GR system, AP1 induction was shown to occur rapidly and to be **independent of de novo protein synthesis**, supporting a direct transcriptional relationship (or very immediate downstream regulation). (Wagner et al., 1999; URL: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.285.5427.582; published Jul 1999) (wagner1999transcriptionalactivationof pages 1-2)

Genetically, AP1 and LFY act **synergistically**: lfy ap1 double mutants strongly disrupt floral fate and strongly suppress B-class gene expression (AP3/PI), converting meristems toward shoot-like identity. (Okamuro et al., 1993; URL: https://doi.org/10.1105/tpc.5.10.1183; published Oct 1993) (okamuro1993regulationofarabidopsis pages 4-6)

#### 2.2 AP1 interactions in FMI: CAL and FUL redundancy and meristem fate
AP1 is partially redundant with closely related MADS factors, especially **CAULIFLOWER (CAL)** and **FRUITFULL (FUL)**, in specifying and stabilizing floral meristem identity. Review synthesis highlights that **ap1 cal** double mutants show stronger meristem/flowering defects than single mutants, and that adding ful further enhances failure to form flowers and increases branching. (litt2007anevaluationof pages 8-10)

#### 2.3 Downstream regulation: repressing inflorescence identity programs (AGL24)
A key regulatory logic is that AP1 helps **shut down inflorescence-like identity** in emerging floral organs. Review synthesis of genetic evidence reports that **AP1 together with LFY represses AGL24**; in ap1 mutants AGL24 expression persists and outer organs can develop with inflorescence characteristics. Genetic suppression in **ap1 agl24** supports that repression of AGL24 is a mechanistic component of AP1-mediated floral determination. (litt2007anevaluationof pages 7-8)

#### 2.4 MADS “quartet” complexes and key partners (SEP3, AP3/PI)
AP1 participates in multi-protein MADS complexes that implement organ identity. A representative model is that SEP (E-class) proteins act as cofactors enabling higher-order complexes; AP1 is included in proposed tetramers specifying sepals and petals (e.g., AP1–SEP–AP1–SEP; and petal complexes involving AP3/PI/SEP/AP1). (wu2025researchprogresson pages 5-7)

Mechanistic evidence for AP1 higher-order assembly includes assays showing that mutations disrupting the AP1 K-domain impair **AP1–SEP3 heterotetramer formation** in vitro (EMSA) and compromise AP1’s functional behavior in vivo in inducible systems. (xu2025ap1isa pages 1-2, xu2025ap1isa media bf7c6421)

### 3) Biological roles and phenotypes (functional readouts)

#### 3.1 Loss-of-function phenotypes (ap1)
Primary literature describes ap1 mutants as having mixed floral/inflorescence features: partial conversion of the floral meristem toward an inflorescence meristem with formation of **secondary flowers** in axils of first-whorl organs, **sepals transformed toward bract-like leaves**, and **loss of petals** while inner reproductive organs can remain. (Okamuro et al., 1993; URL: https://doi.org/10.1105/tpc.5.10.1183; published Oct 1993) (okamuro1993regulationofarabidopsis pages 4-6)

#### 3.2 Ectopic expression and fate conversion
Synthesis across sources supports that AP1 expression can be sufficient to convert an inflorescence toward floral fate (a classic FMI hallmark). (wu2025researchprogresson pages 5-7)

In a modern inducible ectopic-expression system, prolonged AP1 induction caused leaf morphological changes (reduced leaf size, curling) and partial conversion of leaf epidermal identity toward sepal-like traits (including abaxial trichomes), consistent with AP1 being able to reprogramme developmental fate. (xu2025ap1isa pages 6-8)

### 5) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

#### 5.1 2024 expert synthesis: ABC model perspective
A 2024 authoritative review in *The Plant Cell* reflects on the ABC model and emphasizes that AP1 was historically challenging to incorporate cleanly because it has a broader role in **floral meristem identity**, not only organ identity, underscoring that AP1 should be interpreted as a meristem-fate regulator embedded in a broader regulatory network rather than a single-whorl selector. (Bowman & Moyroud, 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.1093/plcell/koae044; published Feb 2024) (bowman2024reflectionsonthe pages 2-3)

#### 5.2 2024 chromatin accessibility mechanism upstream of AP1 binding (preprint)
A 2024 bioRxiv preprint reports that a SWI/SNF-type chromatin remodeling complex (SAS-type) establishes inflorescence/floral chromatin accessibility at many flower-development loci and that **SAS-dependent accessibility is indispensable for AP1 genomic binding at those regions**, indicating that AP1 binding is constrained by chromatin state and enabled by specific remodelers during flower development. (Guo et al., 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.1101/2024.02.20.581124; posted May 2024) (guo2024thesaschromatinremodeling pages 1-4)

#### 5.3 Quantitative mechanistic update (closest available dataset-level statistics)
Although outside the requested 2023–2024 window, a high-resolution 2025 study provides quantitative, directly AP1-specific chromatin and transcription statistics that contextualize the 2024 chromatin theme:
- Among **2,194** closed AP1-bound regions, **1,110** increased accessibility (FC>1.2; p<0.1) between uninduced and 48 h after induction. (xu2025ap1isa pages 6-8)
- Differential expression counts after AP1 induction were **214** (2 h), **184** (8 h), and **423** (24 h) using abs(log2FC)>1 and FDR<0.05; a large fraction of DE genes (≈**58%**) had nearby AP1 binding (within −3 kb/+1 kb). (xu2025ap1isa pages 6-8)
- In another AP1 induction context, **250/1,366 (18%)** AP1-bound genes were differentially expressed within 12 h; only **38** genes overlapped between seedling and floral meristem target sets, highlighting context specificity. (xu2025ap1isa pages 8-9)
These provide concrete statistical anchors for the emerging view of AP1 as a chromatin-engaging reprogramming factor. (xu2025ap1isa pages 6-8, xu2025ap1isa pages 8-9)

### 6) Hormone and signaling connections (evidence-based)
AP1’s developmental effects connect to hormone pathways through direct target regulation. In the inducible AP1 system, AP1 directly **represses TEMPRANILLO2 (TEM2)** and **upregulates GA2OX2** (a gibberellin-catabolism gene), proposed to contribute to AP1-dependent abaxial trichome formation; overlap of AP1 targets across contexts included **RGL2** (a DELLA-related gene), consistent with GA/DELLA pathway intersections. (xu2025ap1isa pages 8-9)

### 7) Current applications and real-world implementations

#### 7.1 Genome editing and flowering-time engineering (2023 review)
A 2023 review of CRISPR-based flowering-time engineering compiles AP1/AP1-homolog interventions, including:
- **Arabidopsis**: AP1 included among knockout targets (with TFL1, SVP in a cited study), with outcome described as **abnormal flower development** (qualitative in the table excerpt). (Hodaei & Werbrouck, 2023; URL: https://doi.org/10.3390/plants12234020; published Nov 2023) (hodaei2023unlockingnature’sclock pages 8-9)
- **Soybean**: CRISPR knockout of **GmAP1** (AP1 homolog) associated with **late flowering** (qualitative outcome). (hodaei2023unlockingnature’sclock pages 9-12)
These demonstrate that AP1-like genes are already within the practical “target set” for flowering phenology manipulation, but the excerpted evidence does not provide effect sizes (e.g., days to flowering). (hodaei2023unlockingnature’sclock pages 9-12, hodaei2023unlockingnature’sclock pages 8-9)

#### 7.2 Transgenic/misexpression approaches across species
A multi-species gene regulation review reports that misexpression/manipulation of AP1/AP1-like genes can induce **earlier flowering** and alter meristem fate in diverse species (e.g., rose RcAP1 promoting the inflorescence-to-floral meristem transition). While these are real implementations at the experimental/translational level, quantitative agronomic field metrics are not provided in the excerpted text. (zhou2025researchprogresson pages 2-4, zhou2025researchprogresson pages 13-15)

### 8) Expert opinion / analysis (authoritative perspective)
Across authoritative sources, AP1 is best viewed as a **network hub** in floral fate control: it integrates upstream induction (LFY), enforces floral fate by repressing competing inflorescence programs (e.g., AGL24), and executes organ identity via MADS complex formation with SEP and B-class factors. The 2024 retrospective emphasizes that historical simplifications (pure “A-function”) underrepresent AP1’s broader meristem-fate roles, which is consistent with modern chromatin-centric mechanistic models. (bowman2024reflectionsonthe pages 2-3, litt2007anevaluationof pages 7-8)
