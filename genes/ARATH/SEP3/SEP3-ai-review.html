Gene Description

SEPALLATA3 (SEP3/AGL9; At1g24260) encodes a nuclear MIKC-type MADS-box transcription factor that acts as an E-class floral homeotic regulator in Arabidopsis thaliana. SEP3 binds CArG-box cis-regulatory DNA through its MADS domain and uses its K-box/coiled-coil region to form homo- and heteromeric MADS transcription-factor complexes. In floral tissues, these complexes cooperate with AP1, AP3/PI, AG, SHP/STK/TT16-class MADS proteins and transcription/chromatin coregulators to regulate RNA-polymerase-II target genes controlling floral organ identity, floral organ morphogenesis, ovule development and floral meristem determinacy. The strongest core evidence supports SEP3 as a chromatin-associated transcription factor and interaction hub for floral quartet-like complexes; seed coat and mucilage phenotypes are reported but require full-text review before being treated as curated SEP3 functions.

Research report: Arabidopsis SEPALLATA3 (SEP3/AGL9) — functional annotation

0) Target identity verification (mandatory)

The target protein is Arabidopsis thaliana SEPALLATA3 (SEP3), also called AGAMOUS-like MADS-box protein AGL9, encoded by At1g24260 (UniProt O22456). The literature retrieved consistently uses SEP3/AGL9 for this Arabidopsis floral MADS-box transcription factor and describes MADS and K-box mediated multimerization consistent with the UniProt domain context (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 2-3, jack2001relearningourabcs pages 3-4).

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 SEP3 is an E-class MIKC-type MADS-box transcription factor

SEP3 belongs to the plant MIKC-type MADS-box transcription factors that bind DNA as obligate dimers and assemble into higher-order complexes (“floral quartets”) through additional interaction domains, notably the K domain (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 2-3, hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4).

1.2 The ABCDE / floral quartet model and SEP3’s E-class role

In the extended ABCDE model (often expressed mechanistically as the floral quartet model), organ identities arise from combinatorial transcription factor complexes. Reviews emphasize that E-class SEP proteins are shared subunits across quartets: sepal identity requires A+E, petals A+B+E, stamens B+C+E, and carpels C+E (jack2001relearningourabcs pages 2-3, ruelens2017theoriginof pages 1-2). SEP3 (AGL9) is one of three closely related SEP genes originally highlighted in Arabidopsis (SEP1/SEP2/SEP3) and is expressed mainly in inner whorls (2–4), consistent with participation in petal/stamen/carpel specification (jack2001relearningourabcs pages 3-4).

1.3 Redundancy and specialization among SEP paralogs

SEP gene function is strongly redundant, such that single mutants are weak while higher-order mutant combinations produce strong homeotic phenotypes. The sep1 sep2 sep3 triple mutant produces indeterminate flowers composed largely of sepal-like organs (loss of petals/stamens/carpels), and the sep1 sep2 sep3 sep4 quadruple mutant converts all floral organs to leaf-like organs (ditta2004thesep4gene pages 1-2, malcomber2005sepallatagenediversification pages 2-3).

2) Molecular function and mechanism

2.1 DNA binding and recognition

SEP3 binds DNA through its MADS domain and recognizes CArG-box motifs; it can bind as a dimer and can bind cooperatively as a higher-order complex when two nearby CArG sites are present (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4).

2.2 Protein–protein interaction architecture: the K domain enables tetramerization

Plant MADS proteins gained interaction capacity via helical regions in the K domain. A mechanistic dissection showed that deletion of K-domain residues 161–174 in SEP3 (the SEP3Δtet variant) disrupts cooperative higher-order binding/tetramer formation but retains dimeric DNA binding (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4). Mapping work in yeast interaction assays also indicates that specific K-domain subregions are required for robust PI–SEP interactions (yang2004definingsubdomainsof pages 1-3).

2.3 SEP3 as an interaction hub

SEP3 is repeatedly described as a central hub that can interact with multiple floral MADS-box proteins, enabling formation of floral quartets with A-, B-, and C-class factors (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 2-3, robles2005flowerandfruit pages 4-5). Primary interaction evidence includes:
- AP1–SEP3 interaction detected by yeast two-hybrid screens; SEP3’s K-box was sufficient for much of this interaction (Pelaz et al., 2001; https://doi.org/10.1046/j.1365-313X.2001.2641042.x) (pelaz2001apetala1andsepallata3 pages 2-5, pelaz2001apetala1andsepallata3 pages 1-2).
- Mechanistic and functional emphasis on SEP3’s cooperation with AGAMOUS (AG) (C-class) and with AP3/PI (B-class) in higher-order complexes (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4, robles2005flowerandfruit pages 4-5).

3) Biological processes and pathways

3.1 Floral organ identity specification

Genetic and review literature converge that SEP3 contributes (redundantly with other SEPs) to petal, stamen, and carpel identity (ditta2004thesep4gene pages 1-2, robles2005flowerandfruit pages 4-5). SEP3 loss-of-function alleles show a mild petal-to-sepal conversion phenotype, consistent with partial loss of E-class activity in the petal program (Pelaz et al., 2001; https://doi.org/10.1046/j.1365-313X.2001.2641042.x) (pelaz2001apetala1andsepallata3 pages 2-5, pelaz2001apetala1andsepallata3 pages 5-6).

3.2 Floral meristem determinacy (termination)

A key mechanistic finding is that SEP3–AG tetramerization is required for proper floral meristem determinacy (termination). In sep1/2/3 backgrounds, expression of a tetramerization-defective SEP3Δtet variant can largely restore organ identity but fails to restore determinacy, producing indeterminate “flower-within-a-flower” phenotypes (Hugouvieux et al., 2018; https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gky205) (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 2-3, hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4).

4) Subcellular localization and site of action

SEP3 functions as a sequence-specific transcription factor acting at target promoters and chromatin. In the retrieved Arabidopsis corpus, explicit microscopy-based statements (e.g., SEP3-GFP nuclear localization) were not present; however, multiple lines of evidence demonstrate in vivo chromatin association, including use of SEP3 ChIP-seq datasets and promoter binding/EMSA assays, supporting that SEP3 acts in the nucleus on chromatin as part of transcriptional regulatory complexes (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4, robles2005flowerandfruit pages 4-5).

5) Direct targets and regulatory outputs (with quantitative/statistical evidence)

5.1 Direct targets: KNUCKLES (KNU) and CRABSCLAW (CRC)

Hugouvieux et al. provide strong evidence that a SEP3–AG heterotetramer is required to activate KNU and CRC, both required for meristem determinacy. Their analyses integrate binding evidence (ChIP-seq occupancy at promoters), chromatin context (including MNase-informed considerations), and expression outcomes (qRT-PCR), concluding tetramerization is especially important for activating a subset of targets (including KNU/CRC) (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 9-11, hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4).

Quantitative examples reported include:
- In complementation assays, 15/18 T1 sep1/2/3 plants expressing wild-type SEP3 were phenotypically complemented, whereas 9/11 T1 plants expressing SEP3Δtet showed incomplete flower development (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 4-6).
- Floral organ quantitation included reduced stamen number in complemented lines relative to WT (example values: 5.8 ± 0.4 WT vs 4.5 ± 0.2 and 4.5 ± 0.4 for lines) (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4).
- Computational motif scanning used FIMO with a threshold P < 10e−4 in their binding-site analyses (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4).

6.2 2023 experimental translational work on SEP3 orthologs (relevance to applications)

A 2023 experimental study in Cymbidium sinense cloned SEPALLATA-like genes including CsSEP3 (grouping in the AGL9/SEP3 clade) and showed that ectopic expression of CsSEPs in Arabidopsis induced early flowering, and that CsSEP3 overexpression caused abnormal floral organ phenotypes; protein–protein interaction assays indicated complex formation with a B-class AP3-like factor and SOC1 (Lin et al., 2023; https://doi.org/10.3389/fpls.2023.1209834) (lin2023functionalconservationand pages 1-2). Although not Arabidopsis SEP3 itself, this provides contemporary evidence for SEP3-clade proteins as manipulable regulators for flowering and floral architecture.

7) Current applications and real-world implementations

  1. Engineering flowering time and floral architecture: SEP3 and its SEP3-clade orthologs are frequently used in gain-of-function contexts to shift flowering time and alter flower determinacy. In Arabidopsis, constitutive SEP3 expression promotes early flowering and synergizes with AP1 overexpression (Pelaz et al., 2001; https://doi.org/10.1046/j.1365-313X.2001.2641042.x) (pelaz2001apetala1andsepallata3 pages 2-5). In orchids, SEP3-clade genes similarly induce early flowering and organ changes in transgenic Arabidopsis, suggesting cross-species portability (lin2023functionalconservationand pages 1-2).
  2. Breeding-relevant conceptual framework: Comparative/evolutionary analyses emphasize that SEP3-clade genes are specialized for inner-whorl identity and that SEP-like genes can influence reproductive traits and may be leveraged to modify inflorescence/flower traits in crops (Dreni & Ferrándiz, 2022; https://doi.org/10.3390/plants11212934) (dreni2022tracingtheevolution pages 1-2).

8) Expert opinions and analysis from authoritative sources

Authoritative reviews synthesize SEP3’s role as follows:
- SEP genes function as core cofactors enabling A/B/C MADS proteins to specify organ identity (jack2001relearningourabcs pages 2-3, robles2005flowerandfruit pages 4-5).
- The evolutionary framing of the floral quartet model supports that E-class proteins (SEP/SEP-like) are required in all quartets, rationalizing SEP3’s hub-like position in protein interaction networks (ruelens2017theoriginof pages 1-2).

11) Key takeaways for functional annotation

  • Primary molecular function: sequence-specific DNA-binding transcription factor that operates as a dimer and tetramer in combinatorial MADS complexes (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4).
  • Primary biological function: E-class cofactor required (redundantly) for petal/stamen/carpel identity and, via SEP3–AG tetramers, floral meristem determinacy (ditta2004thesep4gene pages 1-2, hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4).
  • Key partners: AP1/CAL, AG, AP3/PI; SEP paralogs (SEP1/2/4) in redundant genetic networks (pelaz2001apetala1andsepallata3 pages 2-5, ditta2004thesep4gene pages 1-2, robles2005flowerandfruit pages 4-5).

  • Cellular site of action: nucleus/chromatin-associated by function (ChIP-seq/promoter binding), but explicit imaging-based localization was not found in the retrieved Arabidopsis sources and is therefore not asserted beyond functional inference (hugouvieux2018tetramerizationofmads pages 3-4, robles2005flowerandfruit pages 4-5).
