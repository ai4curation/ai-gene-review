Gene Description

Arabidopsis thaliana TFL1 (AT5G03840) encodes a PEBP/CETS-family protein that antagonizes FT/TSF floral activation to maintain indeterminate inflorescence meristem identity and restrain premature floral meristem conversion. TFL1 is a non-DNA-binding transcriptional regulatory mediator: genetic, fusion-protein, and BiFC evidence support FD-dependent transcriptional repression in the nucleus, while cytoplasmic localization and intercellular mobility support movement through meristem tissues. A separate PSV/endomembrane trafficking study supports vacuolar trafficking and membrane/vesicle localization, but this is best treated as non-core relative to the conserved flowering-time and inflorescence-architecture role.

Research report: Arabidopsis thaliana TERMINAL FLOWER 1 (TFL1) (UniProt P93003; gene TFL1; locus AT5G03840)

1) Key concepts, definitions, and current understanding

Identity verification (critical): The target is Arabidopsis thaliana TERMINAL FLOWER 1 (TFL1) encoded by AT5G03840 and annotated in UniProt as P93003. This exact locus (AT5G03840) is explicitly used and experimentally characterized in Arabidopsis in a high-citation Plant Cell study, confirming we are not conflating unrelated “TFL1” symbols from other organisms. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2)

Core definition and biological role: Arabidopsis TFL1 is a member of the phosphatidylethanolamine-binding protein (PEBP) family and functions as a key negative regulator of flowering and a regulator of inflorescence meristem (IM) identity/indeterminacy. In the canonical flowering network, FT (FLOWERING LOCUS T) promotes floral transition whereas TFL1 represses it, despite strong sequence similarity, making FT/TFL1 a paradigmatic example of antagonistic developmental regulators. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2)

Non-enzymatic regulatory protein: TFL1 is not described as an enzyme or transporter in the cited sources; instead, it operates as a non–DNA-binding regulatory protein that exerts effects through protein–protein interactions in transcriptional regulatory complexes (especially via the bZIP transcription factor FD). (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2, colleoni2024floweringtimegenes pages 1-2)

2) Molecular function and mechanism (functional annotation)

2.1 Primary molecular function: FD-dependent transcriptional repression

A central experimentally supported model is that TFL1 negatively modulates FD-dependent transcription to repress floral induction/meristem identity programs. In Arabidopsis, TFL1 physically interacts with FD in the nucleus (bimolecular fluorescence complementation evidence) and genetically requires FD for key phenotypes (fd suppresses both tfl1 and dominant-negative TFL1 fusion phenotypes), supporting a mechanism in which TFL1 functions within FD-containing transcriptional complexes. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2)

Consistent with this, engineered fusion proteins demonstrate functional polarity:
- TFL1-SRDX (repressor domain fusion) behaves like TFL1 and delays flowering.
- TFL1-VP16 (activator domain fusion) converts output toward early flowering/terminal flower phenotypes and behaves dominantly. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2, hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 3-4)

These findings support the annotation that TFL1’s primary functional activity is to promote a repressive transcriptional state at flowering/meristem identity targets, despite lacking a DNA-binding domain itself. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2, colleoni2024floweringtimegenes pages 1-2)

2.2 Antagonism with FT and the FAC/FRC framework

Recent review synthesis (2024) places TFL1 within the flowering “integrator” module centered on FT transport and action at the shoot apex. FT is expressed in leaf companion cells, transported through sieve elements to the shoot apical meristem (SAM), and forms a floral activation complex (FAC) with FD/FD-like bZIPs and 14-3-3 proteins to induce flowering. In this updated mechanistic framing, TFL1 antagonizes FT by competing with FT for chromatin-bound FD at shared targets, shifting target loci away from activation and toward repression. (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 2-4)

Cross-species experimental work in rice provides direct mechanistic precedent for how TFL1-like proteins can oppose florigen action by competing for complex formation: rice TFL1-like RCN proteins bind 14-3-3 proteins and compete with FT-like Hd3a for formation of the Hd3a/14-3-3/FD activation complex, thereby antagonizing flowering and affecting inflorescence development. This supports a broadly conserved balance between activation (FAC) and repression complexes (often termed FRC) as a molecular basis for architecture control across angiosperms. (kanekosuzuki2018tfl1likeproteinsin pages 1-5)

A recent FD-family review further articulates a mechanistic “switch” logic: an inactive FD/14-3-3/TFL1 ternary complex in vegetative contexts versus an active FT/14-3-3/FD complex when FD becomes competent for activation, providing an expert synthesis consistent with the experimental Arabidopsis FD-dependence and the rice competition model. (yang2025multifacetedrolesof pages 9-10)

2.3 Structure–function determinants

TFL1 and FT have highly conserved sequences but opposite functions. A striking mechanistic insight is that single amino-acid changes can swap FT/TFL1-like activity, implying that subtle surface features (rather than gross structural differences) tune partner interactions and transcriptional outcomes. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2)

3) Pathways and biological processes

3.1 Placement in the flowering-time regulatory network

Modern network understanding emphasizes that multiple environmental and endogenous pathways converge on a common set of floral pathway integrators (notably FT), which then activate floral meristem identity genes. TFL1 functions as a counterbalancing integrator-like repressor at the shoot apex/inflorescence meristem, helping maintain indeterminacy and preventing premature termination into a terminal flower. (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 1-2, hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2)

3.2 Long-distance signaling context (FT transport vs local meristem repression)

While TFL1 itself acts locally in meristems, its function is best understood in the context of FT mobility and its delivery to the SAM. Authoritative 2024 synthesis highlights multiple components mediating FT movement from companion cells into and through the phloem and toward the apex, including FTIP1, a QKY/SYP121 trafficking module, and NaKR1 for long-distance phloem trafficking. These components define the upstream delivery of FT that TFL1 must antagonize at the chromatin/FD level in the meristem. (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 2-4)

A 2024 review further notes that FT transport is influenced by membrane interactions and that low temperature can sequester FT at membranes via interaction with phosphatidylglycerol, reducing mobility; this positions TFL1-mediated repression within an environmentally responsive systemic signaling architecture. (colleoni2024floweringtimegenes pages 2-4)

4) Expression patterns, subcellular localization, and mobility

Expression domain: Arabidopsis TFL1 expression is associated with the shoot apical meristem and inflorescence meristem; transcription has been reported in SAM cell layer 3, consistent with a role in maintaining IM fate. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 2-3)

Subcellular localization relevant to function: TFL1–FD interaction is detected in the nucleus, consistent with a transcriptional regulatory mechanism. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2)

Protein mobility within meristems: Beyond transcriptional regulation, TFL1 has been discussed as a mobile signal within meristems (intercellular shuttling in the inflorescence meristem context), aligning with its role in coordinating meristem identity over short spatial scales. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 2-3)

5) Experimental phenotypes, statistics, and data points

Quantitative phenotypic evidence from Arabidopsis provides strong support for functional annotation:

  • tfl1 loss-of-function: early flowering and dramatic reduction in lateral flower bud production—reported as wild type >20 lateral flower buds versus tfl1 ≤5. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 2-3)
  • TFL1-SRDX (repressor fusion): late flowering was observed in 17/20 35S:TFL1-SRDX/Col lines and 10/14 35S:TFL1-SRDX/tfl1-17 lines (T1), supporting that repressive transcriptional activity is central to TFL1 function. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 3-4)
  • TFL1-VP16 (activator fusion): dominant-negative/early-flowering-like phenotypes; 13/14 independent 35S:TFL1-VP16/Col T1 lines had <10 flower buds (versus >20 in controls), consistent with the conclusion that TFL1 normally enforces repression to maintain indeterminacy. (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 3-4)

6) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

6.1 Updated mechanistic models and “expert synthesis” (2024)

Two 2024 reviews provide high-level expert consensus with mechanistic detail:

  • Maple et al., Plant Physiology (Feb 2024) emphasize mechanistic dissection of flowering integrators and provide an updated trafficking-centric description of FT movement and action, describing FT formation of a FAC with FD and 14-3-3 proteins and explicitly noting TFL1 competition with FT for chromatin-bound FD at shared targets. URL: https://doi.org/10.1093/plphys/kiae109 (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 2-4)

  • Colleoni et al., Journal of Experimental Botany (Mar 2024) frame FT/TFL1 as PEBP proteins that are non–DNA-binding and operate through transcriptional partners such as FD, and they broaden the “FT/TFL1 paradigm” to plant architecture through interactions with TCP regulators (e.g., TB1), connecting flowering regulation to domestication and yield-related traits across crops. URL: https://doi.org/10.1093/jxb/erae112 (colleoni2024floweringtimegenes pages 1-2)

6.2 Real-world implementations: genome editing and breeding applications (2024)

Although not performed in Arabidopsis itself, 2024 genome-editing work illustrates direct applications of manipulating TFL1-like genes (leveraging the conserved FT/TFL1 module first established in Arabidopsis):

  • Abdulla et al., Plant Molecular Biology (Jun 2024) applied multiplex CRISPR/Cas9 to edit FT and TFL1 paralogs in petunia, producing conspicuously earlier flowering and (for TFL1-edited lines) shorter internodes and more compact architecture, proposed to be valuable for ornamental pot plant breeding. URL: https://doi.org/10.1007/s11103-024-01454-9 (abdulla2024crisprcas9mediatedmutagenesisof pages 1-2, abdulla2024crisprcas9mediatedmutagenesisof pages 11-12)

This provides a concrete, modern implementation pathway: TFL1-family genes can be engineered to tune determinacy/architecture and flowering phenology for horticultural and potentially agronomic benefit. (abdulla2024crisprcas9mediatedmutagenesisof pages 1-2)

7) Applications and translational relevance

Crop architecture and determinacy: The FT/TFL1 module is widely used as a conceptual and practical handle to modify determinate vs indeterminate growth habits and branching/architecture traits. Recent reviews explicitly highlight PEBP involvement in architecture and interactions with TCP regulators such as TB1, which are domestication-associated in many crops. (colleoni2024floweringtimegenes pages 1-2)

Breeding and biotechnology: 2024 synthesis emphasizes using mechanistic insight into flowering integrators as nodes for adaptation/domestication and breeding (“climate-proofing” crops). TFL1-like manipulation is a common axis because it changes the balance between vegetative growth (indeterminacy/branching) and reproductive commitment. (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 1-2, colleoni2024floweringtimegenes pages 1-2)

8) Limitations and evidence gaps (important for functional annotation)

  • The evidence assembled here strongly supports TFL1 as a PEBP-family transcriptional regulator operating via FD-dependent repression and antagonism with FT (hanano2011arabidopsisterminalflower1is pages 1-2, maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 2-4).
  • However, within the retrieved full-text evidence set, direct Arabidopsis biochemical quantification (e.g., binding affinities to FD/14-3-3, stoichiometry) and 2023–2024 Arabidopsis-specific primary mechanistic updates were not captured; instead, the most detailed Arabidopsis primary mechanistic evidence in this run comes from the foundational Plant Cell 2011 study, complemented by 2024 authoritative reviews and cross-species experimental precedent.
