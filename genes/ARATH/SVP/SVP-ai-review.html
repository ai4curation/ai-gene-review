Gene Description

SHORT VEGETATIVE PHASE (SVP/AGL22; At2g22540) encodes a nuclear MIKC-type MADS-box transcription factor in Arabidopsis thaliana. The protein has a conserved N-terminal MADS DNA-binding domain and a K-box domain typical of plant MADS transcription factors that form homo- and heteromeric regulatory complexes. The best-supported role of SVP is repression of the floral transition and early floral developmental programs through RNA polymerase II-dependent transcriptional regulation. SVP binds CArG-containing cis-regulatory regions at floral regulator genes such as FT and TFS1 and acts with partner MADS proteins, including FLC, FLM, and AP1, and with SEU/LUG corepressor-associated complexes in context-dependent transcriptional repression. Genetic and expression evidence place SVP in ambient-temperature, circadian/light, and vernalization-linked flowering-time networks, but these developmental and environmental phenotypes are downstream consequences of its core nuclear transcription factor activity rather than separate biochemical activities. No curated evidence in the local sources supports the seeded translation-repressor annotations.

Core Functions

SVP is a nuclear RNA polymerase II-specific MADS-box DNA-binding transcription factor. It binds CArG-containing cis-regulatory regions and regulates transcription of flowering-time and floral developmental genes, with the strongest evidence for repression of targets such as FT and TFS1. This transcriptional repression explains SVP's role as a negative regulator of floral transition and as a component of ambient-temperature and vernalization-linked flowering control.

Research report: Arabidopsis thaliana SVP (SHORT VEGETATIVE PHASE) — functional annotation

0) Target verification (critical identity check)

The symbol SVP in this report refers specifically to Arabidopsis thaliana SHORT VEGETATIVE PHASE, locus AT2G22540 (also referenced as AGL22) and the UniProt entry Q9FVC1 (as provided). This identity match is explicitly stated in recent literature that clones/uses SVP (AT2G22540) as a MADS-box transcription factor (kitazawa2023randommutagenesisbasedscreening pages 2-3, virag2025promotermotifprofiling pages 2-3). SVP belongs to the type II/MIKC-type MADS-box transcription factors, with the canonical MADS (M) DNA-binding domain, I domain, K (keratin-like) domain for protein–protein interactions, and C-terminal domain; the K domain supports dimer/tetramer assembly typical of plant MADS transcription factors (kitazawa2023randommutagenesisbasedscreening pages 2-3, zhu2025elucidatethefunction pages 1-5).

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 What SVP is

SVP encodes a MIKC-type MADS-box transcription factor. MIKC-type MADS proteins bind DNA at CArG-box motifs and regulate transcription largely through combinatorial complex formation (dimers/tetramers) mediated in part by the K domain (kitazawa2023randommutagenesisbasedscreening pages 2-3, zhu2025elucidatethefunction pages 1-5).

1.2 What SVP does (primary function)

Across authoritative flowering-time network syntheses, SVP is best described as a transcriptional repressor of the floral transition that restrains activation of floral integrator genes, most prominently FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT) and SUPPRESSOR OF OVEREXPRESSION OF CONSTANS 1 (SOC1) (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6, zhu2025elucidatethefunction pages 1-5). SVP typically functions as part of MADS-box repressor complexes whose composition changes with environmental cues such as temperature and prolonged cold (vernalization) (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 4-5, maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6).

1.3 Key nodes and pathways relevant to SVP

A high-level model of Arabidopsis flowering pathways places SVP (i) in the ambient temperature/thermosensory module via its partnership with FLOWERING LOCUS M (FLM) and (ii) in vernalization-dependent repression via its partnership with FLOWERING LOCUS C (FLC), converging on repression of FT and SOC1 (maple2024floweringtimefrom media a146a697, maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 4-5, maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6).

2) Molecular mechanisms (domains, binding, complexes, targets)

2.1 Domain architecture and complex assembly

MIKC-type MADS-box transcription factors have a modular architecture in which the MADS domain binds DNA, and the K domain is central to higher-order complex assembly (dimerization/tetramerization). Structural/functional work on MADS proteins supports distinct K-domain regions for multimerization, and SVP is part of this same MIKC functional class (kitazawa2023randommutagenesisbasedscreening pages 2-3).

2.2 Core repressor complexes

SVP–FLC complex (vernalization-linked repression)

A 2024 authoritative review in Plant Physiology states that SVP forms a heterodimer with FLC and that this complex negatively regulates FT and SOC1, thereby preventing flowering; vernalization relieves repression mainly by epigenetically silencing FLC, which in turn reduces the FLC–SVP repression of floral integrators (publication date: Feb 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.1093/plphys/kiae109) (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 4-5).

SVP–FLM complex (ambient temperature/thermosensory flowering)

SVP also functions with FLM in a temperature-dependent manner. The low-temperature splice form FLM-β enables formation of repressive complexes that include SVP (and can also involve FLC) to repress floral integrators such as FT and SOC1 (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6). At elevated ambient temperature, SVP repression is weakened because SVP is reduced through lower transcription and increased proteasomal degradation, allowing accelerated flowering (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6).

2.3 Direct targets and binding-site evidence

SVP-containing complexes repress key floral integrators FT and SOC1 (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6, zhu2025elucidatethefunction pages 1-5). Direct promoter-region evidence is also reported: in a 2024 study in International Journal of Molecular Sciences (Dec 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms252312932), SVP (with FLC) binds the CArG region of the AtSOC1 promoter to inhibit SOC1 transcription (dong2024geneandits pages 10-11).

2.4 Genome-wide scope

Beyond the canonical integrators (FT/SOC1), a 2023 study summarizing SVP biology reports that genome-wide binding/occupancy experiments identify approximately ~3,000 SVP-associated targets, consistent with broad regulatory roles beyond flowering time alone (Aug 2023; URL: https://doi.org/10.3390/f14091692) (wang2023transcriptomeanalysisof pages 1-2).

3) Biological processes, expression patterns, and localization

3.1 Subcellular localization

SVP is described as nuclear in flowering network resources that tabulate Arabidopsis flowering genes and their properties (virag2025promotermotifprofiling pages 2-3). This is consistent with its role as a DNA-binding transcription factor.

3.2 Tissue/developmental expression

SVP is reported to be expressed in young leaves and in the apical meristem during the vegetative stage through early bolting, with transcripts declining before later floral organ developmental stages, consistent with SVP acting early to prevent premature flowering (zhu2025elucidatethefunction pages 1-5). Reporter-based evidence also indicates spatially restricted SVP expression in reproductive meristem/flower primordium regions, consistent with roles in maintaining meristematic programs and preventing premature differentiation (beretta2023roleofthe pages 70-74).

4) Phenotypes and functional genetics (with statistics where available)

4.1 SVP loss-of-function (Arabidopsis)

Phenotypic summaries indicate that SVP mutants show altered hormone-related physiology: SVP loss can induce GA20ox2 and increase gibberellin levels, linking SVP repression to GA homeostasis in the control of floral transition (wang2023transcriptomeanalysisof pages 1-2). Additionally, network syntheses note that flm and svp single mutants may have relatively small flowering-time effects in some conditions, suggesting partial redundancy with other repressors or pathway compensation (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6).

4.2 Quantitative promoter-binding-site evidence (SOC1)

While numeric flowering-time values for svp mutants themselves were not present in the retrieved excerpts, quantitative data illustrate functional importance of SVP binding sites in the SOC1 promoter: the 2024 IJMS study reports leaf-number-to-flowering values for soc1 and complementation constructs, including sensitivity to CArG motif mutation in SOC1 promoter context (e.g., soc1 mutant ~28 leaves vs WT ~13.2 leaves, and different complementation outcomes), supporting the biological relevance of the SVP/FLC CArG-region mechanism even though it is not a direct svp mutant measurement (dong2024geneandits pages 10-11).

5) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

5.1 2024 synthesis of mechanism across pathways

The 2024 Plant Physiology review provides a mechanistic integration of flowering-time physiology/genetics that explicitly places SVP in temperature and vernalization pathways, emphasizing SVP–FLM-β repression at low temperature and SVP–FLC repression relieved by vernalization-induced FLC silencing (Feb 2024; https://doi.org/10.1093/plphys/kiae109) (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 4-5, maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6, maple2024floweringtimefrom media a146a697).

5.2 Temperature-dependent control includes proteostasis

A key aspect of “current understanding” emphasized in 2024 synthesis is that SVP repression is not only transcriptionally regulated but also protein-level regulated: elevated temperature promotes conditions in which SVP abundance/activity is reduced, including proteasomal degradation, enabling accelerated flowering (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6).

5.3 SVP as a conserved node motivating translational research

Recent 2023–2024 work in woody perennials reinforces that SVP-like/DAM clades represent conserved regulatory logic linking seasonal cues to flowering/dormancy, motivating breeding and biotechnology targeting of these genes (ding2024molecularadvancesin pages 5-6, chu2024molecularmechanismsof pages 4-6).

6) Current applications and real-world implementations (SVP-like translational biology)

Direct manipulation of Arabidopsis SVP itself is primarily a research tool; however, SVP-like/DAM genes are being manipulated in crop and tree systems to alter flowering/dormancy phenology.

6.1 Populus SVP-like overexpression (transgenic implementation; 2023)

A 2023 study in Forests reports stable Populus transgenic lines overexpressing an SVP-like (SVL) gene and leaf RNA-seq profiling showing 477 differentially expressed genes (342 upregulated, 135 downregulated) (Aug 2023; https://doi.org/10.3390/f14091692) (wang2023transcriptomeanalysisof pages 1-2). The same work cites field observations that SVL overexpression in poplars delays flowering onset and reduces flowering abundance across genotypes, illustrating a real-world phenotype relevant to forestry/biomass systems (wang2023transcriptomeanalysisof pages 1-2).

6.2 Apple MdDAM4 transgenics (dormancy timing control; 2024)

A 2024 Frontiers in Plant Science study generated transgenic apple lines using 35S::MdDAM4 and reports that MdDAM4 overexpression leads to earlier dormancy induction and later dormancy release, whereas silencing/reduced expression yields the opposite effect (extended vegetative growth; failure or delayed entry into dormancy), thereby changing the length of the vegetation period (Jul 2024; https://doi.org/10.3389/fpls.2024.1433865) (lempe2024functionalevidenceon pages 1-2, lempe2024functionalevidenceon pages 2-3). This is a concrete 2024 implementation of SVP-like gene engineering to control seasonal phenology in a woody crop.

6.3 Breeding targets for changing chill requirements (2024 expert synthesis)

A 2024 review in Journal of Experimental Botany highlights GWAS and functional evidence that DAM/SVP loci (e.g., PpDAM6 in peach) can underlie variation in chilling requirement and dormancy release, making these loci plausible targets for breeding/biotech strategies aimed at climate adaptation (Jun 2024; https://doi.org/10.1093/jxb/erae183) (ding2024molecularadvancesin pages 5-6).

7) Expert opinions and analysis (authoritative sources)

A key expert consensus emerging from synthesis work is that SVP should be understood as a context-dependent, complex-forming repressor: its functional output depends on partner availability (FLC vs FLM isoforms), chromatin state (vernalization-driven FLC silencing), and ambient temperature effects on transcription and proteostasis. This is explicit in the 2024 Plant Physiology network review and its pathway diagram placing SVP-containing complexes as regulatory “brakes” upstream of FT/SOC1 (maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 4-5, maple2024floweringtimefrom pages 5-6, maple2024floweringtimefrom media a146a697). The translational literature in woody perennials further supports the view that SVP-like/DAM modules are conserved “seasonal regulators” that can be leveraged to alter phenology (ding2024molecularadvancesin pages 5-6, chu2024molecularmechanismsof pages 4-6).

8) Summary of evidence-backed functional annotation

SVP (Q9FVC1/AT2G22540) is best annotated as a nuclear MIKC-type MADS-box transcription factor whose primary biological role is to repress flowering by forming temperature- and vernalization-responsive MADS repressor complexes (notably SVP–FLM-β and SVP–FLC) that repress FT and SOC1 through CArG-box–mediated transcriptional regulation. SVP-like orthologs (DAM/SVL) are actively used in 2023–2024 translational studies to control flowering abundance and bud dormancy timing in trees.
