Gene Description

JAZ1, also known as TIFY10A, is a nuclear JASMONATE ZIM-domain repressor in Arabidopsis jasmonate signaling. In low-jasmonate states, JAZ proteins repress MYC transcription factors and recruit co-repressor machinery through the TIFY/ZIM domain. JA-Ile promotes COI1-JAZ1 interaction and JAZ1 degradation, releasing jasmonate-responsive transcription. Defense, flower, pollen, wounding, and alkaline-stress annotations are downstream or specialized outputs of this core jasmonate-repressor module.

Research report: Functional annotation of Arabidopsis TIFY10A/JAZ1 (UniProt Q9LMA8; locus At1g19180)

0) Target identity verification (mandatory)

The protein described in the prompt—Protein TIFY 10A / Jasmonate ZIM domain-containing protein 1 (JAZ1)—matches Arabidopsis thaliana gene JAZ1/TIFY10A and is experimentally used/annotated as At1g19180 (Arabidopsis Genome Initiative locus). (grunewald2009expressionofthe pages 2-3, perez2014thenonjaztify pages 10-11)

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Jasmonate signaling core module (COI1–JAZ–MYC)

Jasmonates are lipid-derived plant signaling molecules. The canonical transcriptional output of jasmonate signaling is often described as the JA-Ile–COI1–JAZ–MYC module, where JAZ proteins function as transcriptional repressors in the absence of hormone and are degraded in response to hormone perception. In the prevailing model, the bioactive hormone conjugate (+)-7-iso-jasmonoyl-L-isoleucine (JA-Ile) promotes interaction between JAZ repressors and the F-box protein COI1 (part of an SCF ubiquitin E3 ligase), triggering polyubiquitination and 26S proteasome–dependent degradation of JAZ proteins. This relieves repression on downstream transcription factors (notably MYC bHLH transcription factors), enabling activation of JA-responsive genes. (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 1-2, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 2-3)

1.2 What JAZ1/TIFY10A is (molecular function)

JAZ1/TIFY10A is a plant-specific transcriptional repressor/adaptor protein (not an enzyme) that constrains jasmonate-responsive transcription by binding transcription factors and recruiting co-repressor machinery. JAZ proteins are “crucial interfaces” in the signaling cascade because they couple hormone perception (via COI1) to transcription factor repression/activation cycles. (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 1-2, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 3-5)

1.3 Domain definitions for JAZ1

JAZ proteins are defined by two conserved regions:
- TIFY (ZIM) domain (N-terminal/central): a protein–protein interaction module; the conserved “TIFY” motif (TIFF/YXG) can mediate JAZ–JAZ homo/heteromerization and supports assembly of repression complexes (e.g., via adaptor proteins such as NINJA). (chung2009acriticalrole pages 1-2, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 7-8)
- Jas motif / Jas domain (C-terminal): contains the hormone-responsive degron that mediates JA-Ile–dependent interaction with COI1 and also mediates binding to MYC transcription factors; it can also influence nuclear localization/retention. (grunewald2009expressionofthe pages 2-3, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 2-3, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 3-5)

2) Molecular function, mechanism, and pathways for JAZ1/TIFY10A

2.1 Primary function: repression of jasmonate-regulated transcription

In resting conditions (low JA-Ile), JAZ proteins block MYC transcription factor activity and keep JA-responsive genes repressed. A key repression mechanism is recruitment of co-repressors such as TOPLESS (TPL) (and TPL-related proteins) via the adaptor NINJA, forming a repressive complex associated with MYC-bound promoters. (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 1-2, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 7-8)

2.2 Signal-triggered switching via COI1-mediated degradation

Upon JA-Ile accumulation, JAZ proteins act as components of a hormone-dependent COI1–JAZ co-receptor, enabling COI1 recognition and ubiquitination of JAZs, followed by proteasome degradation. This converts the system from “repressed” to “active” transcription by removing JAZ restraints from MYC factors. (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 1-2, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 2-3)

Mechanistic detail on the Jas degron from authoritative synthesis includes:
- The Jas motif includes a minimal “degron” sequence and a bipartite structural element (loop + α-helix) important for COI1 binding near the JA-Ile pocket, with a JAZ1 loop sequence described as ELPIARR. (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 2-3)
- Basic residues in JAZ1 (notably R205/R206) are required for COI1 binding in the JA-Ile-dependent recognition mechanism. (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 1-2)

2.3 Interacting partners (validated and functionally relevant)

Authoritative review evidence supports that JAZ proteins (including JAZ1/TIFY10A) directly interface with:
- COI1 (hormone-dependent co-receptor interaction) (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 1-2, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 2-3)
- MYC family transcription factors (MYC2 first described; also MYC3/MYC4 interact with JAZs) (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 3-5, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 1-2)
- NINJA adaptor and TOPLESS (TPL) co-repressor complex for transcriptional repression (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 1-2, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 7-8)

A mechanistic extension emphasized in more recent Arabidopsis work is that JAZ removal allows MYC transcription factors to engage coactivators such as Mediator subunit MED25, consistent with a repressor→activator transition. (kaur2024arabidopsistranscriptomicsreveals pages 1-2)

2.4 Crosstalk: auxin regulation of JAZ1 expression

A specific feature of JAZ1/TIFY10A is that its expression can be stimulated by auxin, indicating hormone crosstalk at the transcriptional level rather than purely through JA-induced degradation. (grunewald2009expressionofthe pages 1-2)

3) Subcellular localization: where JAZ1 acts

JAZ1/TIFY10A is a nuclear protein and can accumulate in nuclear bodies/speckles when expressed as fluorescent fusions, consistent with a role in nuclear transcriptional regulatory complexes. (grunewald2009expressionofthe pages 2-3, grunewald2009expressionofthe media 3d6330ee)

Domain contributions to localization include:
- The Jas motif contributes to nuclear retention/sorting; deletion of the Jas domain can disrupt nuclear localization and increase cytoplasmic accumulation in the broader JAZ literature synthesized by an authoritative review. (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 3-5)

4) Recent developments and latest research (prioritized 2023–2024)

The most directly relevant 2023–2024 developments in the provided evidence concern tool development and pathway engineering around the JAZ–MYC interface, rather than JAZ1-specific discovery.

4.1 2024 chemical-biology implementation: MYC-selective inhibition using a JAZ-derived peptide

Takaoka et al. (2024, PNAS Nexus; https://doi.org/10.1093/pnasnexus/pgae312; Jul 2024) developed a structure-redesigned (double-stapled) peptide derived from a JAZ interaction region (JAZ10 CMID) to selectively inhibit MYC-family transcription factors, thereby uncoupling JA–ethylene crosstalk in Arabidopsis. This provides a concrete method to perturb jasmonate transcriptional outputs without genome editing and is proposed as a broadly applicable chemical tool, including for non-model plants. (takaoka2024astructureredesignedintrinsically pages 1-2)

Quantitative interaction and functional readouts (selected):
- In vitro, the peptide inhibited MED25–MYC3 interaction with Ki = 71 nM, while inhibiting EIN3 much more weakly (Ki = 1.08 µM), indicating selectivity. (takaoka2024astructureredesignedintrinsically pages 5-7)
- Stapling dramatically increased affinity for MpMYC-Y (Kd 830 nM → 18 nM), supporting cross-species functionality. (takaoka2024astructureredesignedintrinsically pages 5-7)

4.2 2024 Arabidopsis wound-response context: reinforcement of the repression architecture

A 2024 Arabidopsis wound transcriptomics study reiterates the mechanistic architecture whereby JA-Ile promotes JAZ degradation, releasing MYC transcription factors from the JAZ–NINJA–TOPLESS repressor complex and permitting MED25 coactivation of JA-responsive transcription. (Kaur et al., 2024, IJMS; https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25115898; May 2024) (kaur2024arabidopsistranscriptomicsreveals pages 1-2)

6) Current applications and real-world implementations

6.1 Chemical tools for pathway dissection and potential crop translation

The 2024 MYC-selective stapled peptide provides a non-transgenic intervention that can modulate JA outputs and hormone crosstalk, a direction relevant to controlled activation/suppression of defense programs (e.g., to reduce growth penalties) and to functional analysis in non-model plants without genetics. (takaoka2024astructureredesignedintrinsically pages 1-2, takaoka2024astructureredesignedintrinsically pages 5-7)

6.2 Molecular breeding/genome editing strategies centered on JAZ nodes

A 2024 translational review focused on “translating functional genomics to molecular plant breeding” argues that JAZ proteins are tractable targets for crop improvement but that gene family redundancy often requires multiplex editing and careful selection of conserved clades. It recommends prioritizing JAZ1/2 ortholog clades and JAZ3/4/9 clades due to structural conservation and predictable effects, and suggests editing interaction residues rather than deleting genes to tune traits while avoiding global JA disruption. (macconnell2024therhythmsofa pages 34-38, macconnell2024therhythmsofa pages 11-17)

7) Expert analysis and synthesis

Across the authoritative mechanistic review literature and recent applied perspectives, the consensus interpretation is that JAZ repressors (including JAZ1/TIFY10A) act as a molecular switch and scaffold that integrates: (i) ligand-controlled degradation (COI1/Jas degron), (ii) transcription factor binding (MYC), and (iii) co-repressor recruitment (NINJA/TPL). This architecture makes them attractive engineering targets, but their redundancy and interaction network complexity motivate strategies emphasizing (a) tissue/context-specific manipulation, (b) interaction-residue tuning, and (c) higher-order mutant/edited combinations. (pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 1-2, pauwels2011thejazproteins pages 7-8, macconnell2024therhythmsofa pages 34-38)
