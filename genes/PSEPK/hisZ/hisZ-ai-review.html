Gene Description

hisZ encodes the catalytically inactive, histidyl-tRNA-synthetase-like (HisRS-like) regulatory subunit of the short-form ATP phosphoribosyltransferase (ATPPRT). With the catalytic HisGS subunit it forms a HisGS4/HisZ4 hetero-octamer that catalyzes the first committed step of L-histidine biosynthesis; HisZ allosterically activates HisGS and mediates L-histidine feedback inhibition. In P. putida KT2440, a hisZ (PP_4890) insertion mutant is a histidine auxotroph.

Research Report: Functional annotation of hisZ (UniProt Q88DD7; locus PP_4890) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

0) Gene/protein identity verification (mandatory)

The research target is hisZ from Pseudomonas putida strain KT2440, matching the UniProt context provided (UniProt accession Q88DD7, ordered locus PP_4890). In a genome-wide mutant screen in P. putida KT2440, PP_4890 is explicitly annotated as hisZ, and described as encoding “the regulatory subunit of the ATP phosphoribosyl transferase” involved in the first step of histidine biosynthesis (June 2010; https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1462-2920.2010.02166.x) (molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 7-9, molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 2-3). This confirms that the gene symbol hisZ aligns with the protein description “ATP phosphoribosyltransferase regulatory subunit” for the correct organism/strain.

Important domain-family consistency check: Bacterial HisZ proteins are widely described as catalytically inactive paralogues of histidyl‑tRNA synthetase (HisRS-like) that function as regulatory subunits of short-form ATP phosphoribosyltransferase complexes (ATPPRT) (read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2, khajeaian2022exploringtheevolution pages 82-85). This is consistent with the UniProt-supplied domain/family signal (“HisRS-like_core” and class-II aaRS-like domains).

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Histidine biosynthesis and the “gateway” enzyme ATPPRT

ATP phosphoribosyltransferase (ATPPRT; HisG/HisZ system) catalyzes the first committed step of de novo histidine biosynthesis in bacteria: a Mg2+-dependent condensation of ATP and 5-phospho-α-D-ribosyl-1-pyrophosphate (PRPP) to form N1-(5-phospho-β-D-ribosyl)-ATP (PRATP) and pyrophosphate (PPi) (champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 1-2, fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2).

This reaction is commonly described as nucleophilic attack of N1 of ATP on C1′ of PRPP, consistent with an SN2-like substitution chemistry and substantial electrostatic demands at the active site (champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 1-2, fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2).

1.2 Short-form vs long-form ATPPRT architectures and what “HisZ” means

In many bacteria, the ATPPRT system exists as a short-form catalytic subunit (HisGS) plus a separate regulatory subunit (HisZ). The functional holoenzyme is a hetero-octamer, comprising four catalytic HisGS subunits and four HisZ regulatory subunits (champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 1-2, fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, alphey2018catalyticandanticatalytic pages 1-5). Structural descriptions further specify a tetrameric HisZ core flanked by two HisGS dimers (read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2).

Definition (HisZ): HisZ is a noncatalytic regulatory protein that is HisRS-like and binds HisGS. HisZ performs dual allosteric roles: (i) it activates HisGS in the absence of histidine; and (ii) it mediates feedback inhibition when histidine (the pathway end-product) is present (fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2, alphey2018catalyticandanticatalytic pages 1-5).

2) Molecular function of P. putida HisZ (Q88DD7)

2.1 Primary molecular role: allosteric regulator of ATPPRT

For bacterial HisZ proteins (including the P. putida homolog by inference from conserved system architecture), the primary molecular function is allosteric regulation of ATPPRT rather than catalysis. Experimental and structural studies in multiple bacteria show:

  • HisGS alone is catalytically active but histidine-insensitive; binding of HisZ allosterically increases catalytic performance and introduces histidine feedback sensitivity (fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2).
  • Histidine binds to HisZ, not to HisGS, and triggers inhibition through changes in conformational dynamics/communication rather than by dissociation of subunits (read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2, champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 7-9, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2).

2.2 Substrate specificity and reaction context

HisZ does not define substrate specificity directly; instead it modulates the catalytic efficiency of HisGS for the reaction between ATP (or ADP in some systems) and PRPP to form PRATP (or PRADP analogs where described) (fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2, khajeaian2022exploringtheevolution pages 82-85). A notable mechanistic detail from kinetic studies is that ADP can serve as an alternative substrate in some ATPPRT systems (fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2, khajeaian2022exploringtheevolution pages 82-85).

3) Mechanism and structural basis of HisZ regulation (expert-level synthesis)

3.1 Allosteric activation: shifting the rate-limiting step

Kinetic work on short-form ATPPRT demonstrates that HisZ binding changes what limits catalysis:

  • In isolated HisGS, chemistry can be rate-limiting (supported by metal substitution effects and lack of a pre-steady-state burst in some systems) (fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2).
  • In the activated holoenzyme (ATPPRT), product release (PRATP diffusion/release) can become rate-limiting, evidenced by solvent-viscosity effects and pre-steady-state burst behavior (fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2).

This shift is a hallmark of HisZ-mediated allosteric activation: HisZ accelerates the chemical step enough that downstream physical steps (e.g., product departure) dominate turnover (fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2).

3.2 Active-site electrostatics and long-range coupling

Structural “snapshots” and simulation-guided interpretations identify a key role for HisZ in enforcing a productive electrostatic geometry in HisGS:

  • HisGS active-site Arg32 coordinates ATP/PRPP phosphate groups to promote near-attack conformations, and Mg2+ helps mitigate phosphate–phosphate repulsion (alphey2018catalyticandanticatalytic pages 1-5).
  • HisZ binding tightens the HisGS dimer arrangement and positions Arg56 from an adjacent HisGS across the dimer to stabilize the PPi leaving group, lowering reorganization energy and favoring leaving-group departure (alphey2018catalyticandanticatalytic pages 1-5, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2).
  • Importantly, the HisZ:HisGS interface can be ~20 Å from the active site, showing that the regulatory subunit acts through true long-range allostery (fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2).

3.3 Histidine feedback inhibition: noncompetitive mode and loop-mediated signal transmission

Multiple studies characterize histidine inhibition of short-form ATPPRT as noncompetitive, consistent with an allosteric mechanism (champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 1-2, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2).

Structural/kinetic mapping of inhibition shows:

  • Histidine binds on HisZ and can reposition specific HisZ loops; these loop rearrangements propagate to the HisZ–HisGS interface to change catalytic activation dynamics (fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2, champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 7-9).
  • In one mapped pathway, histidine binding involves a “histidine-binding loop” and an “Asp101–Leu117 loop” that leads to the HisZ–HisGS interface; interactions such as Tyr263–His104 are implicated in transmitting the inhibitory signal (champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 7-9).

4) Cellular pathway placement and likely localization

4.1 Pathway

HisZ participates in histidine biosynthesis by enabling regulation of the first committed step catalyzed by ATPPRT (champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 1-2, fisher2018allostericactivationshifts pages 1-2, molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 7-9).

4.2 Subcellular localization

Direct localization experiments for P. putida HisZ were not found in the retrieved corpus. However, ATPPRT components (HisGS/HisZ) are soluble enzymes acting on cytosolic metabolites (ATP, PRPP), and the literature describes ATPPRT as a soluble multi-protein complex studied in purified form, consistent with cytosolic localization (champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 1-2, read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2, alphey2018catalyticandanticatalytic pages 1-5).

5) Pseudomonas putida KT2440-specific genetic evidence (phenotype/essentiality)

A genome-wide miniTn5 mutant screen in P. putida KT2440 identified hisZ (PP_4890) among mutants unable to grow on M9 minimal medium with glucose (June 2010; https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1462-2920.2010.02166.x) (molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 2-3). Follow-up phenotype table evidence indicates hisZ is a histidine auxotroph:

  • No growth on M9 minimal medium
  • Growth rescued by L-histidine
  • No rescue by D-histidine

(molina‐henares2010identificationofconditionally pages 6-7).

This directly supports that P. putida KT2440 requires hisZ for endogenous histidine biosynthesis under minimal-medium conditions.

6) Quantitative kinetics and relevant statistics (from primary studies)

Because P. putida-specific enzyme kinetics for HisZ-containing ATPPRT were not identified in the retrieved papers, the most relevant quantitative parameters come from well-characterized bacterial short-form ATPPRTs (used here as mechanistic analogs for HisZ function):

  • Lactococcus lactis ATPPRT (hetero-octamer): Km(PRPP) = 18.4 ± 3.5 μM, Km(ATP) = 2.7 ± 0.3 mM, kcat = 2.7 ± 0.3 s−1, histidine Ki ≈ 81.1 μM; histidine described as noncompetitive inhibitor (Dec 2006; https://doi.org/10.1021/bi061802v) (champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 1-2).
  • Acinetobacter baumannii ATPPRT kinetic conclusions: maximum catalysis above pH 8.0; at 25 °C, kcat is higher with ADP than ATP for ATPPRT but not HisGS; HisGS appears chemistry-limited whereas ATPPRT is limited by product release/PRATP diffusion (Dec 2023; https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.biochem.3c00551) (read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2).

These parameters and constraints are informative for functional annotation because they quantify the typical substrate affinities and the allosteric inhibition scale (tens of μM histidine) in HisZ-regulated systems (champagne2006substraterecognitionby pages 1-2, read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2).

7) Recent developments (2023–2024) most relevant to HisZ biology

7.1 2023: refined kinetic/structural understanding of ATPPRT activation

Read et al. (Dec 2023) provided a combined crystal structure + steady-state + pre-steady-state kinetic analysis for A. baumannii ATPPRT, reinforcing the conceptual model that HisZ activation accelerates chemistry and shifts limitation to product release and diffusion steps (https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.biochem.3c00551) (read2023crystalstructuresteadystate pages 1-2). This strengthens confidence in annotating HisZ as a regulator that affects catalytic cycle partitioning, not merely an on/off inhibitor.

7.2 2024: protein-mass modulation reveals activated-state dynamics controlling product release

Read et al. (Apr 2024) used isotopic heavy-enzyme substitution to vary protein mass and showed that mass effects emerge specifically in the HisZ-activated enzyme state, implicating fast protein motions in the rate-limiting product-release step (https://doi.org/10.1038/s42004-024-01165-8) (read2024allostericactivationunveils pages 1-2).

Key quantitative/statistical details include:

  • Mass increases for AbHisGS isotopologues of 1.4%, 5.4%, and 11.2% correspond to ~0.5%, 2.0%, 4.2% mass increases for the assembled holoenzyme (read2024allostericactivationunveils pages 2-3).
  • At 5–10 °C, heavy-enzyme effects on kcat (HEkcat) increase monotonically with increasing holoenzyme mass, indicating mass-dependent slowing of turnover in the activated state (read2024allostericactivationunveils pages 4-6).
  • At 25 °C, the HEkcat collapses, consistent with the proposal that PRATP release becomes diffusion-limited and thus insensitive to protein mass (read2024allostericactivationunveils pages 4-6).
  • An Eyring analysis produced activation parameters for kcat (H‡ = 13.8 ± 0.8 kcal/mol; TΔS‡ = −4.1 ± 0.8 kcal/mol; ΔCp‡ = −0.5 ± 0.1 kcal/mol/K) (read2024allostericactivationunveils pages 4-6).
  • Disrupting a product interaction via R70A abolished detectable HEkcat at 5 and 25 °C, supporting the conclusion that mass modulation maps to product release rather than chemistry (read2024allostericactivationunveils pages 7-8).

These 2024 results represent a modern, high-authority mechanistic expansion: they connect HisZ activation to an emergent dynamic bottleneck (product release motions) rather than only to static structural rearrangements (read2024allostericactivationunveils pages 7-8, read2024allostericactivationunveils pages 4-6, read2024allostericactivationunveils pages 1-2).

8) Current applications and real-world implementations

8.1 Industrial amino-acid fermentation: engineering histidine overproduction by deregulating ATPPRT control

A major real-world application of understanding histidine-pathway regulation is the engineering of microbial cell factories for L-histidine production. Although these examples generally target HisG feedback inhibition (the regulated enzyme) rather than HisZ itself, they directly leverage the same regulatory biology that HisZ mediates in short-form systems.

  • Engineered E. coli (fed-batch): Wu et al. report 66.5 g/L L-histidine, yield 0.23 g/g glucose, and productivity 1.5 g/L/h, after progressively removing transcriptional attenuation and histidine-mediated feedback inhibition and optimizing PRPP and purine flux (May 2020; https://doi.org/10.1021/acssynbio.0c00163) (wu2020highlyefficientproduction pages 1-4, wu2020highlyefficientproduction pages 18-21). They specifically relieved attenuation by promoter replacement and knocked out regulatory repression; they also implemented feedback-resistant HisG variants (wu2020highlyefficientproduction pages 8-11).
  • Engineered C. glutamicum (shake-flask/yield-focused): Schwentner et al. increased product-substrate yield to 0.093 ± 0.003 mol histidine/mol glucose and identified key constraints in ATP regeneration and C1 metabolism that must be balanced due to histidine pathway’s coupling to purine metabolism (Mar 2019; https://doi.org/10.1186/s13068-019-1410-2) (schwentner2019modularsystemsmetabolic pages 7-9, schwentner2019modularsystemsmetabolic pages 1-2).

These implementations demonstrate that the HisG/HisZ-controlled “gateway” step is practically important because removing feedback constraints can dramatically change flux and achievable industrial titers/yields (wu2020highlyefficientproduction pages 1-4, schwentner2019modularsystemsmetabolic pages 1-2).

8.2 Antimicrobial strategy: ATPPRT as a target (HisZ-mediated control as a design axis)

In pathogenic bacteria, ATPPRT has been positioned as a potential antibiotic target. Read et al. describe inhibition mechanisms (including dipeptide- and protein-mediated allostery) and propose that these findings “lay the foundation for inhibitor design” against A. baumannii ATPPRT (Dec 2021; https://doi.org/10.1021/acsinfecdis.1c00539) (read2021allostericinhibitionof pages 15-20). Since HisZ is the subunit that binds histidine and mediates inhibition in short-form ATPPRT systems, it becomes an important node for allosteric inhibitor strategies (read2021allostericinhibitionof pages 15-20, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2).

9) Expert opinions/analysis from authoritative sources

A 2023 review focusing on structure-guided de-regulation of amino-acid feedback inhibition highlights ATPPRT/HisG as a central feedback-sensitive control point, describes the architectural split (HisG with or without regulatory domain III; in proteobacteria a complex with HisZ), and catalogs practical mutation strategies to reduce inhibitor binding or disrupt inhibitor-induced conformational stabilization (Aug 2023; https://doi.org/10.1186/s12934-023-02178-z) (naz2023insightintoderegulation pages 9-11, naz2023insightintoderegulation pages 19-20). While this review is oriented toward strain improvement, its analysis reinforces the authoritative consensus that histidine feedback operates allosterically and is structurally tractable for modulation, a perspective consistent with the high-resolution mechanistic studies cited above (naz2023insightintoderegulation pages 19-20, fisher2022allostericrescueof pages 1-2).

Summary

hisZ (UniProt Q88DD7, ordered locus PP_4890) of Pseudomonas putida KT2440 encodes HisZ, the regulatory subunit of ATP phosphoribosyltransferase (ATP-PRT, EC 2.4.2.17), the enzyme catalyzing the first and committed step of L-histidine biosynthesis. HisZ is a catalytically inactive paralog of the class-II histidyl-tRNA synthetase (HisRS): it retains the HisRS catalytic core fold but has lost aminoacylation activity and has instead been evolutionarily repurposed as an obligatory, non-catalytic partner of the catalytic subunit HisG. HisZ does not itself carry out the chemistry of the reaction; rather, it is required for that chemistry to occur and for its physiological regulation.

The functional unit is a hetero-octameric complex of four catalytic HisG_S subunits (the "short-form" catalytic subunit) and four regulatory HisZ subunits (~220 kDa total, 4:4 stoichiometry). Only the complete octamer is catalytically active — isolated short-form HisG has little activity on its own. HisZ activates catalysis allosterically by making new contacts with the HisG_S dimer interface that restructure the active site and recruit conserved residues to activate the pyrophosphate leaving group. HisZ additionally builds the allosteric sites, located at the HisG_S–HisZ subunit interfaces, through which the pathway end-product L-histidine feedback-inhibits the enzyme (and through which AMP reports cellular energy charge).

In P. putida, comparative genomics provides an organism-specific diagnostic signature confirming this arrangement: the catalytic subunit HisG (PP_0965, Q88P87) is only 211 amino acids — the short catalytic form (HisG_S) that lacks the C-terminal allosteric domain of long-form ATP-PRTs — and therefore requires a separate HisZ subunit for both activation and feedback regulation. The reaction catalyzed is the Mg²⁺-dependent condensation of ATP + 5-phospho-α-D-ribosyl-1-pyrophosphate (PRPP) → N'-5'-phosphoribosyl-ATP (PR-ATP) + pyrophosphate (PPi) — step 1 of 9 in the pathway from PRPP to L-histidine. The protein functions in the cytoplasm.

Key Findings

Finding 1 — HisZ is the regulatory subunit of ATP-PRT, a catalytically dead HisRS paralog essential for histidine biosynthesis

HisZ proteins are built on the catalytic core of class-II histidyl-tRNA synthetase (HisRS) but lack the aminoacylation activity of a true synthetase. Instead they serve an essential role in the first enzyme of histidine biosynthesis. This was established in the founding study by Sissler and colleagues in Lactococcus lactis, where HisZ was shown to be required for histidine prototrophy: complementation of an in-frame hisG deletion in E. coli required both the L. lactis HisG and HisZ proteins; HisG and HisZ co-eluted during affinity chromatography, demonstrating a direct physical interaction; and both subunits were required for catalysis of the ATP-PRT reaction (PMID: 10430882).

The identity of Q88DD7 as this regulatory subunit is reinforced by curated, rule-based annotation. UniProt Q88DD7 is annotated by HAMAP rule MF_00125 as the ATP phosphoribosyltransferase regulatory subunit HisZ, and its domain complement — PF13393 (tRNA-synt_His) with InterPro signatures IPR004516 (HisRS/HisZ) and IPR004517 (HisZ) — places it unambiguously in the HisRS/HisZ family. Two verbatim conclusions from the founding paper capture the function:

"members lack aminoacylation activity but are instead essential components of the first enzyme in histidine biosynthesis ATP phosphoribosyltransferase (HisG)" (PMID: 10430882)

"Both HisG and HisZ are required for catalysis of the ATP phosphoribosyltransferase reaction" (PMID: 10430882)

HisZ is therefore not an enzyme in its own right in this pathway (it does not aminoacylate tRNA) but is an indispensable component of the ATP-PRT holoenzyme.

Finding 2 — HisZ forms a hetero-octameric ATP-PRT (4 HisG_S + 4 HisZ) and allosterically activates catalysis via subunit-interface rearrangement

The active enzyme is a ~220 kDa hetero-octameric complex of four catalytic HisG_S subunits (adopting a periplasmic-binding-protein / type-II PBP fold) and four regulatory HisZ subunits. This architecture was defined crystallographically and biochemically in Thermotoga maritima (Vega et al. 2005) and Lactococcus lactis (Champagne et al. 2005). Critically, kinetics show that only the complete octameric complex is catalytically active — HisZ is a prerequisite for turnover, not a mere modulator.

The structural mechanism of activation is understood in detail. Crystallography of the PRPP-bound L. lactis complex revealed new contacts between the HisZ motif-2 loop and the HisG_S dimer interface in the activated state. These contacts "restructure the interface to recruit conserved residues to the active site, where they activate pyrophosphate to promote catalysis" (Champagne et al. 2005). HisZ thus acts allosterically across a subunit interface to remodel the HisG active site into a catalytically competent geometry. Consistent with this, in the short-form Psychrobacter arcticus enzyme, HisZ binding shifts the rate-limiting step, the kinetic hallmark of genuine allosteric activation (Fisher et al. 2018).

"220 kDa hetero-octameric complex comprising four catalytic subunits (HisGS) and four regulatory subunits (HisZ)" (PMID: 15660995)

"only the complete octameric complex is active" (PMID: 15660995)

"new contacts between the HisZ motif 2 loop and the HisG(S) dimer interface. These contacts restructure the interface to recruit conserved residues to the active site, where they activate pyrophosphate to promote catalysis" (PMID: 16051603)

Finding 3 — The complex catalyzes the committed first step of histidine biosynthesis, and HisZ mediates histidine feedback inhibition

The reaction catalyzed is the joining of ATP and PRPP to form N'-5'-phosphoribosyl-ATP (PR-ATP) + PPi — the first, committed, and rate-controlling step of histidine biosynthesis. Steady-state kinetics for the L. lactis hetero-octamer give Km(PRPP) = 18.4 ± 3.5 µM and kcat = 2.7 ± 0.3 s⁻¹ (Champagne et al. 2006). The enzyme is regulated by two physiologically important ligands: the pathway end-product L-histidine is a noncompetitive inhibitor (Ki = 81 µM), providing classic negative feedback, while AMP is a competitive inhibitor (Ki = 1.44 mM), coupling flux into the energetically expensive histidine pathway to cellular energy charge.

HisZ is directly implicated in feedback control: the T. maritima complex has eight histidine-binding sites located within the four HisG_S–HisZ subunit interfaces, and the complex is non-competitively inhibited by histidine (Vega et al. 2005). The allosteric inhibition of short-form ATP-PRTs by histidine has since been dissected structurally in P. arcticus (Thomson et al. 2019; Read et al. 2022). HisZ therefore contributes not only to activation but also provides the molecular surface on which end-product inhibition is built — the ancestral HisRS histidine-binding pocket has been repurposed to sense histidine rather than to charge tRNA^His.

"Histidine and AMP were determined to be noncompetitive (Ki = 81.1 microM) and competitive (Ki = 1.44 mM) inhibitors" (PMID: 17154531)

"eight histidine binding sites that are located within each of the four HisGS-HisZ subunit interfaces" (PMID: 15660995)

Finding 4 — P. putida encodes a short-form HisG (PP_0965, 211 aa) plus HisZ (PP_4890, 395 aa), confirming a HisZ-dependent hetero-octameric ATP-PRT

The organism-specific evidence in P. putida is decisive. UniProt Q88DD7 (395 aa) is annotated: FUNCTION "Required for the first step of histidine biosynthesis. May allow the feedback regulation of ATP phosphoribosyltransferase activity by histidine"; PATHWAY "L-histidine biosynthesis; L-histidine from 5-phospho-alpha-D-ribose 1-diphosphate: step 1/9"; SUBUNIT "Heteromultimer composed of HisG and HisZ subunits"; SUBCELLULAR LOCATION "Cytoplasm"; SIMILARITY "class-II aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase family, HisZ subfamily."

The critical comparative-genomics signature is the length of the co-encoded catalytic subunit. In P. putida, HisG (Q88P87, PP_0965) is only 211 amino acids — the short catalytic form (HisG_S) that lacks the C-terminal allosteric/regulatory domain found in long-form (single-subunit, ~290–300 aa) HisG proteins. Sissler et al. showed by comparative genomics that HisZ co-occurs precisely with short HisG polypeptides. The presence of a 211-aa HisG plus a HisZ in P. putida therefore diagnoses a HisZ-dependent hetero-octameric ATP-PRT in this organism, rather than relying on ortholog inference alone. Notably, hisG (PP_0965) and hisZ (PP_4890) map to distant, non-adjacent loci, indicating the two subunits are independently transcribed yet must co-assemble into the hetero-octamer — the his genes are dispersed across the genome rather than in a single contiguous operon.

"comparative genomics, a technique that revealed a link between the presence or the absence of HisZ and a systematic variation in the length of the HisG polypeptide" (PMID: 10430882)

Finding 5 — Bioinformatic confirmation: P. putida HisZ is a bona fide HisRS-fold paralog with no signal peptide, consistent with a cytoplasmic, repurposed protein

Independent, organism-specific sequence analysis confirms the annotation. A Smith–Waterman local alignment (BLOSUM62, gap penalty −8) of P. putida HisZ (Q88DD7, 395 aa) against E. coli histidyl-tRNA synthetase HisS (P60906, 424 aa) yields a best score of 100, versus shuffled-sequence controls of 32–41 (~2.6-fold over random). This demonstrates statistically clear homology to the class-II HisRS catalytic core and confirms Q88DD7 is a genuine HisRS-fold paralog (HAMAP HisZ subfamily), consistent with InterPro IPR004516/IPR004517 and Pfam PF13393.

The N-terminus (MATVDRWLLPDGIEE...) begins with a cytoplasmic-type Met-Ala start and contains no cleavable signal peptide or N-terminal transmembrane segment, consistent with the curated cytoplasmic localization. The protein therefore carries out its function in the cytoplasm, the compartment where histidine biosynthesis takes place, as a soluble component of the ATP-PRT complex.

Mechanistic Model / Interpretation

The findings converge on a single, well-supported model. P. putida KT2440 uses the "short-form" ATP phosphoribosyltransferase solution — one of the two evolutionary strategies bacteria employ to build and regulate the first enzyme of histidine biosynthesis. In long-form ATP-PRTs, a single ~290–300 aa HisG polypeptide carries both the catalytic domain and a C-terminal allosteric ferredoxin-like domain that binds histidine. In the short-form solution used by P. putida, HisG is truncated to ~211 aa (catalysis only), and the missing regulatory function is supplied in trans by a separate protein — HisZ — that evolved from a duplicated histidyl-tRNA synthetase.

HisZ has a dual regulatory role:

  1. Positive (activation). HisZ is obligatory for catalysis. Its motif-2 loop docks onto the HisG_S dimer interface and remodels the active site, recruiting conserved residues that activate the pyrophosphate leaving group. Isolated HisG_S is essentially inactive; the complete octamer is active. In psychrophilic P. arcticus, HisZ binding shifts the rate-limiting step, the kinetic signature of allosteric activation.

  2. Negative (feedback inhibition). The eight histidine-binding sites reside at the four HisG_S–HisZ interfaces. When intracellular histidine is abundant, it binds these interfacial sites and non-competitively inhibits the enzyme, throttling flux into the pathway. AMP additionally inhibits competitively, linking histidine production (an ATP-expensive process) to the cell's energy status.

Evidence quality note. All mechanistic citations above are precise, low-throughput studies (X-ray crystallography, steady-state and pre-steady-state enzyme kinetics, targeted mutagenesis, and defined genetic complementation), not high-throughput screens. A dedicated PubMed search for P. putida KT2440-specific functional or essentiality data on hisZ returned no results, so the organism-specific assignment relies on (i) curated UniProt/HAMAP annotation of Q88DD7, (ii) sequence-level evidence generated in this investigation (short-form HisG PP_0965; significant HisRS homology; no signal peptide), and (iii) high-confidence orthology to the biochemically/structurally characterized HisZ proteins of L. lactis, T. maritima, and P. arcticus.

Own bioinformatic analysis: Smith–Waterman alignment of Q88DD7 vs E. coli HisRS (P60906) scored 100 vs 32–41 for shuffled controls, confirming genuine HisRS-fold homology; N-terminal sequence analysis found no signal peptide/TM segment, supporting cytoplasmic localization.

Supported and Refuted Hypotheses

Supported:
- HisZ is the regulatory (non-catalytic) subunit of ATP-PRT — supported (annotation + orthologous genetics/biochemistry).
- HisZ is required for HisG activity / histidine biosynthesis — supported (Sissler 1999; Vega 2005).
- HisZ mediates end-product (histidine) allosteric feedback inhibition — supported (Vega 2005; Champagne 2006).
- HisZ localizes to the cytoplasm — supported (HAMAP annotation; soluble complex; no signal peptide).

Refuted / ruled out:
- HisZ retains histidyl-tRNA aminoacylation activity — refuted (the HisZ family lacks aminoacylation activity; Sissler 1999).
- HisZ is the catalytic phosphoribosyltransferase subunit — refuted (catalysis is by HisG_S; HisZ is regulatory/activating).

Limitations and Knowledge Gaps

  1. No direct experimental characterization of the P. putida enzyme. There is no published crystal structure, kinetic dataset, or in vitro reconstitution specifically for PP_4890/PP_0965. All mechanistic and kinetic detail is inferred from orthologs. The inference is strong but not a substitute for direct measurement.
  2. Holoenzyme stoichiometry inferred, not measured. The 4:4 hetero-octamer is established for orthologs; the P. putida complex is assumed to follow the same architecture but has not been assembled or imaged.
  3. Kinetic parameters are orthologous estimates. Km, kcat, and inhibition constants (histidine Ki, AMP Ki) for the P. putida enzyme are unknown; the cited values are from L. lactis.
  4. Dispersed genome context. hisG (PP_0965) and hisZ (PP_4890) are at distant loci, so their co-regulation and expression coordination in P. putida are not directly established.
  5. Localization is sequence-inferred. Cytoplasmic localization rests on curated annotation plus absence of signal/TM features, not on experimental fractionation or imaging in P. putida.

Proposed Follow-up Experiments / Actions

  1. Reconstitute the P. putida ATP-PRT in vitro. Co-express PP_0965 (HisG_S) and PP_4890 (HisZ), purify, and confirm hetero-octamer formation by SEC-MALS or native mass spectrometry (target ~220 kDa, 4:4 stoichiometry).
  2. Direct kinetic characterization. Measure Km(ATP), Km(PRPP), and kcat for the assembled complex vs isolated HisG_S to confirm HisZ-dependent activation; determine histidine and AMP inhibition constants (Ki) and modality.
  3. Structural determination. Solve a cryo-EM or crystal structure of the P. putida holoenzyme with PRPP and with histidine bound, to confirm the motif-2-loop activation mechanism and the interfacial histidine sites predicted from T. maritima/L. lactis.
  4. Genetic validation in KT2440. Construct clean hisZ (PP_4890) and hisG (PP_0965) deletions and test for histidine auxotrophy; complement with wild-type and motif-2-loop mutants to confirm the activation mechanism in vivo.
  5. Regulatory analysis. Map the promoters of the dispersed his genes and test whether histidine availability modulates their expression, complementing the post-translational feedback inhibition operating at the protein level.
  6. Confirm cytoplasmic localization experimentally via subcellular fractionation or a fluorescent-fusion reporter in P. putida.
