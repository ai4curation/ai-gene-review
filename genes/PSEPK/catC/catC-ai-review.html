Gene Description

catC encodes a muconolactone delta-isomerase family enzyme (EC 5.3.3.4) in the catechol ortho-cleavage branch of the beta-ketoadipate pathway. In Pseudomonas putida KT2440, CatC acts downstream of catechol 1,2-dioxygenase and muconate cycloisomerase to convert muconolactone to beta-ketoadipate enol-lactone, linking catechol degradation to the rest of central aromatic-compound assimilation.

Functional annotation research report: catC (Muconolactone Δ-isomerase; EC 5.3.3.4) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 (UniProt: Q88GK7)

Executive summary

The Pseudomonas putida KT2440 gene catC encodes muconolactone Δ-isomerase (also called muconolactone isomerase), an intracellular enzyme in the catechol (ortho-cleavage) branch of the β-ketoadipate pathway that enables assimilation of diverse aromatic substrates by converting muconolactone to (3-)oxoadipate enol-lactone/ketoadipate enol-lactone, which is subsequently funneled to central carbon metabolism (acetyl‑CoA and succinyl‑CoA). (okamuraabe2016betaketoadipicacidand pages 2-2, ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria media 82e82b1a, lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

A major modern application of this step is metabolic engineering for cis,cis‑muconic acid production, where catB/catC deletions are used to block downstream muconate metabolism, enabling high titers such as 74 g/L muconic acid from catechol at 1.0 mol/mol yield in fed‑batch processes as summarized in recent engineering work. (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

1. Target identity verification (avoiding gene-symbol ambiguity)

Across authoritative pathway depictions and classic genetic/regulatory studies in Pseudomonas, catC is consistently defined as muconolactone isomerase (muconolactone Δ-isomerase), EC 5.3.3.4, acting in the catechol branch downstream of catechol 1,2‑dioxygenase (CatA) and muconate cycloisomerase (CatB). (okamuraabe2016betaketoadipicacidand pages 2-2, rothmel1991functionalanalysisof pages 1-2, kukor1988cloningandexpression pages 1-2)

In P. putida specifically, CatC is discussed as part of the cat regulon that enables growth on aromatics (e.g., benzoate-derived catechol), with CatR controlling expression of catBC and influencing catA. (rothmel1990nucleotidesequencingand pages 1-2)

2. Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

2.1 The β-ketoadipate pathway and “catechol branch”

The β-ketoadipate pathway is a central bacterial route that funnels diverse aromatic compounds into TCA-cycle entry metabolites, typically acetyl‑CoA and succinyl‑CoA, via ring cleavage and subsequent processing of ring-opened intermediates. (ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria pages 8-10, lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

Within this pathway, the catechol branch comprises the enzymatic steps that convert catechol to intermediates including cis,cis‑muconate, then through lactonization/isomerization steps to a common lower pathway intermediate (enol-lactone), ultimately yielding acetyl‑CoA and succinyl‑CoA. (ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria pages 8-10, lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

2.2 What CatC does (enzyme definition)

CatC (muconolactone Δ-isomerase; EC 5.3.3.4) catalyzes an intramolecular isomerization in the catechol branch. In a 2024 pathway schematic, the CatC step is explicitly labeled as conversion of (S)-5-oxo-2,5-dihydrofuran-2-acetate to 3-oxoadipate enol-lactone. (ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria media 82e82b1a)

A KT2440-focused pathway depiction places CatC at the step muconolactone (ML) → ketoadipate enol-lactone (KEL). (okamuraabe2016betaketoadipicacidand pages 2-2)

3. Molecular function: reaction, substrates/products, and specificity

3.1 Reaction assignment (best-supported from available evidence)

The strongest direct evidence in the retrieved corpus for CatC’s chemistry comes from pathway schematics:

  • Muconolactone → ketoadipate enol‑lactone in P. putida KT2440 pathway context. (okamuraabe2016betaketoadipicacidand pages 2-2)
  • (S)-5-oxo-2,5-dihydrofuran-2-acetate → 3-oxoadipate enol-lactone, explicitly annotated as the CatC-catalyzed step. (ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria media 82e82b1a)

Together, these support the standard functional annotation that CatC converts a muconolactone intermediate (generated from cis,cis‑muconate by CatB) into an enol‑lactone that is shared with the protocatechuate branch downstream. (ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria pages 8-10, ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria media 82e82b1a)

3.2 Evidence gaps: kinetics/mechanism for KT2440 CatC

The retrieved sources firmly establish EC number, pathway position, and genetic regulation, but do not provide kinetic constants (Km/kcat), catalytic residues, or substrate-scope measurements for the specific KT2440 protein (UniProt Q88GK7). (rothmel1991functionalanalysisof pages 1-2, ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria media 82e82b1a)

Accordingly, substrate specificity beyond the canonical catechol-branch intermediate(s) should be treated as inferred from pathway context rather than experimentally quantified for Q88GK7 in the evidence set assembled here.

4. Biological role and pathway context in P. putida

4.1 Pathway integration and physiological role

CatC enables assimilation of aromatics that converge on catechol by preventing accumulation of lactone intermediates and enabling conversion to lower-pathway intermediates that ultimately yield succinyl‑CoA and acetyl‑CoA for the TCA cycle. (ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria pages 8-10, okamuraabe2016betaketoadipicacidand pages 2-2)

4.2 Genomic/operon context and regulation (CatR control)

Classic P. putida regulatory work shows:

  • catBC is an operon encoding CatB (EC 5.5.1.1) and CatC (EC 5.3.3.4). (rothmel1991functionalanalysisof pages 1-2)
  • catR is divergently transcribed relative to catBC and the catR and catBC promoters overlap (reported as a 2 bp overlap). (rothmel1991functionalanalysisof pages 1-2)
  • CatR is a LysR-family positive regulator; CatR binds the catR–catBC control region in vitro with or without inducer, but cis,cis‑muconate is required for in vivo activation of the catBC promoter. (rothmel1991functionalanalysisof pages 1-2, rothmel1990nucleotidesequencingand pages 1-1)

Engineering-focused summaries in the P. putida KT2440 biomanufacturing literature also treat cat genes as a functional unit (e.g., catA with catBC and catR in pathway descriptions) in the context of modulating flux to muconate. (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

4.3 Cellular localization

No direct microscopy/fractionation localization result for CatC in KT2440 was retrieved. However, CatC is repeatedly described as a metabolic enzyme acting on soluble intracellular intermediates of aromatic catabolism, with no evidence in the assembled sources for membrane anchoring, periplasmic localization, or secretion. (okamuraabe2016betaketoadipicacidand pages 2-2, lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

Thus, the best evidence-supported annotation is intracellular (cytosolic) localization, with the explicit caveat that this is contextual inference rather than direct localization measurement.

5. Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

5.1 2024 pathway evidence and comparative functional context

A 2024 metagenomic comparative study of wild giant panda gut microbiomes identified the catechol branch β‑ketoadipate genes (catA, catB, catC) as significantly enriched in bamboo-eating pandas’ gut microbiota, and presents a pathway schematic explicitly mapping catC to the muconolactone→enol‑lactone conversion. (Publication date: March 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.1128/spectrum.03819-23) (ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria pages 8-10, ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria media 82e82b1a)

Although not performed in KT2440, this study reflects current systems-level usage of catC as a robust marker of catechol-branch capability in diverse Pseudomonas-associated communities. (ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria pages 8-10)

5.2 2024–current engineering context for aromatic valorization (CatC relevance via ΔcatBC)

Recent metabolic-engineering narratives for P. putida KT2440 emphasize that blocking CatB/CatC is a foundational intervention for accumulating cis,cis‑muconate (muconic acid) as a product, demonstrating continued relevance of catC as a controllable node in lignin/aromatic valorization. (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

6. Current applications and real-world implementations

6.1 Biomanufacturing: muconic acid as a platform chemical (CatC as a knockout target)

In engineered P. putida KT2440, targeted deletion of catB and catC blocks downstream metabolism of muconic acid and enables accumulation; this is described as a prerequisite for muconic acid production strains in recent engineering summaries. (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

Quantitative examples reported in the retrieved engineering evidence include:

  • 74 g/L muconic acid from catechol, yield 1.0 mol/mol in fed‑batch (summarized as Kohlstedt et al., 2022 in the engineering source). (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)
  • A reported prior result that catB/catC deletion enabled 100% yield muconic acid from catechol and benzoic acid (summarized as Kohlstedt et al., 2018). (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

These implementations position CatC not merely as a degradative enzyme but as an industrial control point: removing catC increases product retention (muconate) at the expense of growth/complete mineralization. (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

6.2 Environmental biotechnology: aromatic hydrocarbon biodegradation capacity in Pseudomonas

While not directly assaying catC, a 2024 proteogenomic study of Pseudomonas veronii SM‑20 shows the continued practical significance of aromatic-catabolic capacity in the genus: the strain degraded ~25% of phenanthrene over 30 days starting from 600 µg/mL, with ~2.5‑fold biomass increase (protein content). (Publication date: April 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.3390/microorganisms12040753) (zavalameneses2024proteogenomiccharacterizationof pages 9-11)

This supports the broader applied context in which β‑ketoadipate/catechol-branch modules (including cat genes) are commonly used as part of bacterial aromatic degradation strategies, even when the specific CatC step is not singled out as the limiting factor. (zavalameneses2024proteogenomiccharacterizationof pages 9-11)

7. Expert synthesis and authoritative interpretations

7.1 Regulatory architecture as a canonical LysR-controlled catabolic module

The catR/catBC arrangement in P. putida is a well-characterized example of a LysR-family regulator controlling a catabolic operon, including overlapping divergent promoters and inducer-dependent activation. These classic mechanistic transcription studies remain the most authoritative evidence for regulation of catC expression. (Rothmel et al., 1990–1991; URLs: https://doi.org/10.1128/jb.172.2.922-931.1990 and https://doi.org/10.1128/jb.173.15.4717-4724.1991) (rothmel1990nucleotidesequencingand pages 1-1, rothmel1991functionalanalysisof pages 1-2)

7.2 CatC as an engineering “valve” in aromatic funneling

Modern P. putida metabolic engineering practice treats catB/catC as a downstream “valve” on catechol-derived carbon: deletion prevents conversion beyond muconate, enabling high-titer muconate accumulation but preventing complete channeling into acetyl‑CoA/succinyl‑CoA. (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60)

Rothmel et al. 1990, 1991 provide promoter mapping, DNA binding, and induction data. https://doi.org/10.1128/jb.172.2.922-931.1990; https://doi.org/10.1128/jb.173.15.4717-4724.1991 (rothmel1990nucleotidesequencingand pages 1-1, rothmel1991functionalanalysisof pages 1-2, rothmel1990nucleotidesequencingand pages 1-2)
Cellular localization No direct localization experiment for KT2440 CatC was retrieved in the available evidence. However, CatC acts on soluble aromatic-catabolic intermediates of the β-ketoadipate pathway and is discussed exclusively as part of intracellular metabolism; thus the best-supported annotation is cytosolic / intracellular enzyme, with no evidence for secretion, membrane anchoring, or periplasmic localization. Inference from pathway context in P. putida aromatic catabolism and absence of transport/signal-peptide evidence in the retrieved literature. Okamura-Abe et al. 2016; Lund 2025 pathway summary (okamuraabe2016betaketoadipicacidand pages 2-2, lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60, lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 32-37)
Physiological role CatC enables assimilation of catechol-derived aromatics by funneling ring-cleavage products into the common lower β-ketoadipate route that yields succinyl-CoA and acetyl-CoA for the TCA cycle. Ning et al. 2024 states catechol and protocatechuate branches converge and end in succinyl-CoA and acetyl-CoA; Okamura-Abe et al. 2016 places CatC in this route in KT2440. https://doi.org/10.1128/spectrum.03819-23; https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbiosc.2015.11.007 (ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria pages 8-10, okamuraabe2016betaketoadipicacidand pages 2-2)
Current applications In metabolic engineering, ΔcatB ΔcatC is a standard strategy to block downstream muconate metabolism and accumulate muconic acid (MA) from lignin-derived aromatics. This is central to biorefinery and nylon/PET precursor production schemes using P. putida KT2440. Recent engineering summaries note deletion of catB/catC as prerequisite for MA production strains and as a recurring chassis modification. (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60, politi2026strategiesforrenewable pages 31-33)
Quantitative implementation examples Reported values include 74 g/L muconic acid from catechol, yield 1.0 mol/mol in fed-batch; 100% yield from catechol and benzoic acid after catB/catC deletion in one cited study; in lignin/upstream-engineered strains, up to 30.5 g/L muconate and 100% mol/mol yield from p-coumaric acid were reported; deletion of crc raised muconate yield from p-coumarate by ~70% and from ferulate by >2-fold in engineered derivatives. Lund 2025 thesis excerpt citing Kohlstedt et al. 2018/2022 and others; Politi 2026 thesis excerpt summarizing engineered KT2440 derivatives. (lund2025metabolicengineeringof pages 57-60, politi2026strategiesforrenewable pages 31-33)
Evidence limitations Direct kinetic constants, purified-enzyme biochemistry, and localization experiments for KT2440 Q88GK7 were not available in the retrieved evidence set. Functional annotation is therefore strongest for identity, pathway placement, regulation, and engineering relevance, with substrate/product assignment supported mainly by pathway diagrams and operon literature rather than a recent dedicated biochemical study of KT2440 CatC. Absence of such data in retrieved classic and recent sources despite clear agreement on EC, pathway role, and regulation. (okamuraabe2016betaketoadipicacidand pages 2-2, rothmel1990nucleotidesequencingand pages 1-1, rothmel1991functionalanalysisof pages 1-2, ning2024pseudomonasassociatedbacteria media 82e82b1a)

catC notes

  • catC in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 corresponds to UniProt Q88GK7 / locus PP_3714 and is annotated as muconolactone delta-isomerase, EC 5.3.3.4 [UniProt:Q88GK7 "RecName: Full=Muconolactone Delta-isomerase"; "Reaction=(S)-muconolactone = (4,5-dihydro-5-oxofuran-2-yl)-acetate;"].
  • The Falcon deep research places CatC in the catechol branch of the beta-ketoadipate pathway, downstream of CatA and CatB [file:PSEPK/catC/catC-deep-research-falcon.md "CatC functions in the catechol branch of the β-ketoadipate pathway, downstream of CatA and CatB."].
  • Falcon also supports that catBC is an operon under CatR control rather than an isolated enzyme annotation [file:PSEPK/catC/catC-deep-research-falcon.md "catBC is an operon encoding CatB (EC 5.5.1.1) and CatC (EC 5.3.3.4)."].
  • Review decision: keep GO:0016159 as the core MF term, but narrow GO:0042952 beta-ketoadipate pathway to GO:0019615 catechol catabolic process, ortho-cleavage, because UniProt places CatC at the catechol-derived step 3/3 rather than generically across the whole pathway [UniProt:Q88GK7 "4,5-dihydro-2-furylacetate from catechol: step 3/3."].
