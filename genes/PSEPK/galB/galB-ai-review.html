Gene Description

galB encodes 4-oxalmesaconate hydratase, a zinc-containing hydratase in the gallate degradation cluster. GalB converts oxalomesaconate enol to 4-carboxy-4-hydroxy-2-oxoadipate as the third step of the KT2440 gallate catabolic pathway.

    > In order to detect gene and protein names which are assigned to products of different genes and thus result in erroneous cross-references, dedicated lists are created for each organism separately. Organism-specific lists are necessary, since terms that are ambiguous in one organism might be explicit in another. For example, ADORA2 is an ambiguous gene name in Homo sapiens but not in mouse, and GALT in mouse but not in H.sapiens.
    > In a first step, ambiguous names within the databases were extracted. If a name occurs in at least two entries describing different genes or proteins (splice variants count as one gene/protein), this particular name is marked as ambiguous and is excluded from the mapping process. In a second step, corresponding gene names occurring in the manually annotated sections of RefSeq as well as in UniProt were analyzed. Entries containing the same gene product name and having a one-to-many or many-to-many relation (e.g. one Swiss-Prot entry maps to many RefSeq entries) were scrutinized for misleading annotation. This process is done manually by inspecting additional information like sequence similarity or functional information about the involved entries. In most of the cases, the exclusion of the ambiguous gene names resulted in correct one-to-one relations.
    > As statistical analysis revealed (Supplementary Material S2) that gene names with less than four letters are exceptionally error-prone, only gene names with at least four letters are kept for mapping purposes. However, gene names with less than four letters can be queried, e.g. a search for the tumor suppressor 'p53' reveals the respective entries with the official gene name 'TP53'. 1. Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Gallate (gallic acid) degradation and the “gal” cluster

In bacteria that catabolize lignin-derived aromatics, gallic acid (gallate) can be funneled into central metabolism via an oxidative ring-cleavage pathway that produces 4‑oxalomesaconate (OMA), which exists in equilibrium with its enol tautomer 4‑carboxy‑2‑hydroxymuconate (CHM) at physiological pH. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 16-23)

A key biochemical point for functional annotation is that while databases and pathway maps may name the relevant hydratase step “OMA hydratase”, the enzyme can be selective for the CHM (enol) tautomer, with OMA→CHM conversion accelerated by an OMA tautomerase (GalD). (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150)

1.2 What GalB catalyzes (reaction definition)

Experimental characterization of GalB from P. putida KT2440 supports the reaction:

  • Substrate: 4‑carboxy‑2‑hydroxymuconate (CHM, the enol tautomer of OMA)
  • Product: 4‑carboxy‑4‑hydroxy‑2‑oxoadipate (CHA)

This CHM→CHA step is the hydratase transformation in the gallate pathway. Downstream, CHA is cleaved by an aldolase to yield pyruvate and oxaloacetate, linking aromatic catabolism to central carbon metabolism. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 110-116, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110)

2. Primary function of galB (enzyme activity, substrate specificity, mechanism)

2.1 Substrate specificity: CHM vs OMA

Although OMA and CHM are tautomers, GalB (and related CHM/OMA hydratases) are reported to act on CHM (enol) rather than OMA (keto), and GalD accelerates the tautomerization that supplies the correct substrate isomer for the hydratase step. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150)

This distinction matters for functional annotation: UniProt’s EC assignment (OMA hydratase) is compatible with the mechanistic reality that the enzyme consumes the CHM tautomer that is derived from OMA in vivo. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110, pinto2023firstgenomescalemetabolic pages 10-12)

2.2 Cofactor/metal dependence

GalB is a divalent metal-dependent enzyme. Purified GalB contains bound Zn2+, but activity can be supported by Fe2+ or Co2+, with turnover behavior depending on the metal; one report summarizes relative turnover as Fe2+ > Co2+ >> Zn2+, while still noting Zn2+ as the predominant metal observed in aerobically purified enzyme. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 150-155)

2.3 Structural family, domains, and catalytic residues

A key and somewhat counterintuitive feature of GalB is that it performs hydratase chemistry while adopting a PIG‑L / deacetylase-like metallohydrolase fold rather than the more common amidohydrolase/TIM-barrel folds seen in other aromatic-pathway hydratases. GalB is described as a single α/β Rossmann-like domain with a divalent metal-binding motif His‑X2‑Asp‑Xn‑His, consistent with UniProt’s assignment to the GlcNAc_PI_deacetylase-related / PIG‑L family. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 150-155, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 110-116, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 124-129)

Mechanistic mutagenesis identifies key residues for catalysis and proton transfer; for example:
- E48A causes a >7000‑fold loss in catalytic efficiency and a large reduction in kcat with minimal effect on metal binding, supporting a catalytic (not merely structural) role. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150)
- H164A yields an approximately 56‑fold decrease in kcat, consistent with a catalytic acid/proton-transfer role. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150)
- R216A strongly reduces turnover (reported ~142‑fold decrease in kcat for CHM), supporting a substrate positioning/electrostatic role. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150)

Together, these data support expert interpretation of GalB as a metal-assisted hydration enzyme with defined acid/base contributions from active-site residues within a PIG‑L-like scaffold. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150)

2.4 Kinetics and substrate scope (selected quantitative data)

Reported kinetic/functional statistics relevant to annotation include:
- Metal-dependent GalB turnover numbers ~2–14 s−1. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110)
- For CHM hydration pH profiles, reported pKa pairs around 6.2/8.6 (Zn2+) and 6.4/8.3 (Co2+), consistent with ionizations relevant to catalysis in a metalloenzyme. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150)
- GalB can also catalyze reversible hydration/dehydration involving CHPD ↔ HMG (a CHM/CHA-related analog system), but CHM is the better substrate; for CHPD hydration relative to CHM, KM increases ~10‑fold and kcat decreases ~23‑fold. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 150-155)

3. Pathway context: where GalB sits in aromatic catabolism

3.1 Gallate 4,5-cleavage route

In the gallate (gallic acid) 4,5-cleavage pathway, upstream ring cleavage converts gallate to OMA/CHM, then GalD shifts tautomer distribution toward CHM, and GalB hydrates CHM to CHA, enabling downstream aldol cleavage to central metabolites. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 16-23)

Supporting comparative evidence from another organism (Sphingomonas paucimobilis SYK‑6) shows that a gallate dioxygenase can produce OMA as the ring-cleavage product, and disruption of an OMA hydratase gene (ligJ) affects growth on syringate, reinforcing the biochemical logic that OMA/CHM hydratase is a critical downstream step in syringyl-aromatic catabolism. (kasai2005characterizationofthe pages 1-2, kasai2005characterizationofthe pages 7-8)

3.2 Intersection with protocatechuate 4,5-cleavage and PDC

The protocatechuate (PCA) 4,5-cleavage pathway produces PDC (2‑pyrone‑4,6‑dicarboxylate) as a stable intermediate. PDC is hydrolyzed by a lactonase (LigI) to yield OMA/CHM, which then feeds into the same OMA/CHM→CHA chemistry used by GalB/LigJ-type hydratases. The presence of GalD homologs in 4,5-cleavage clusters is used to argue that LigI’s product is often OMA (keto), which is then enzymatically tautomerized to CHM (enol) for hydration. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 16-23, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150)

Direct subcellular localization assays for GalB were not identified in the retrieved sources. However, GalB is repeatedly handled experimentally as a purified soluble enzyme acting on intracellular aromatic-catabolic intermediates (CHM/CHA and analogs), consistent with a cytosolic localization typical of downstream catabolic enzymes acting on imported/processed small molecules rather than on extracellular polymers. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 124-129, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 1-6, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110)

Although not always naming galB explicitly, 2023 work highlights the practical relevance of the PCA 4,5-cleavage/PDC/OMA-CHM module for lignin valorization and bioproduct synthesis.

6. Current applications and real-world implementations

6.1 Lignin valorization via aromatic funneling

The gallate pathway sits within the broader challenge of converting heterogeneous lignin-derived aromatics into uniform central intermediates. Reviews of lignin valorization describe funneling through aromatic ring-cleavage routes including PCA 4,5-cleavage, where downstream processing yields central metabolites (e.g., pyruvate) that can feed production of bioplastics and other products. (liang2025ligninvalorizationthrough pages 6-7, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110)

6.2 PDC production as a concrete implementation (industrial relevance)

PDC is a proposed polyester monomer and has been produced in engineered microbial systems. The 2023 study provides a quantitative benchmark for implemented production systems, including bioreactor-optimized conditions (pH effects at scale) and high titers/productivities, and also summarizes earlier work where P. putida strains reached up to 22.7 g/L PDC in fed-batch fermentation using lignin-derived aromatics (p‑coumaric and/or ferulic acid). (zhou2023multistepbiosynthesisof pages 5-8, zhou2023multistepbiosynthesisof pages 1-2)

While GalB itself is downstream of PDC in the metabolic map (PDC→OMA/CHM→CHA), it is part of the same enzymatic “funneling-to-central-carbon” module; therefore, GalB’s function contributes to enabling complete assimilation rather than accumulation of ring-cleavage intermediates. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 16-23, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110)

7. Expert analysis / authoritative interpretations

  1. Tautomer-specific catalysis is central to correct annotation. A recurring expert-level theme is that “OMA hydratase” nomenclature can mask the biochemical detail that the hydratase consumes CHM, and that GalD is required to accelerate OMA→CHM conversion under physiological conditions. This implies that genome annotations should often treat galD + galB as a functional pair for robust flux from OMA to CHA. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 105-110, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150)

  2. Convergent evolution complicates purely sequence-based annotation. GalB and LigJ can perform analogous CHM-hydration chemistry with shared stereospecificity despite very low sequence identity and distinct folds; this supports the interpretation that structure-informed annotation is important for correct functional assignment in aromatic catabolic pathways. (mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 1-6, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 146-150, mazurkewich2016characterizationofthe pages 110-116)
