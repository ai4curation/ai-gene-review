Functional Annotation Report: MurB (Q88LM5, PP_1904) — Pseudomonas putida KT2440

1. Summary / Answer

MurB (UniProt Q88LM5; gene murB; ordered locus PP_1904) of Pseudomonas putida KT2440 is UDP-N-acetylenolpyruvoylglucosamine reductase (EC 1.3.1.98; also called UDP-N-acetylmuramate dehydrogenase). It is a cytoplasmic, monomeric FAD-dependent flavoenzyme that catalyzes the second committed step of the cytoplasmic phase of peptidoglycan (cell-wall) biosynthesis: the NADPH-dependent reduction of the enolpyruvyl group of UDP-N-acetylglucosamine-enolpyruvate (the product of MurA) to yield UDP-N-acetylmuramic acid (UDP-MurNAc), the essential precursor that MurC–MurF subsequently convert into UDP-MurNAc-pentapeptide. The enzyme is essential for viability and is a validated antibacterial drug target.

Gene-identity verification

The gene symbol murB matches the UniProt protein description (UDP-N-acetylenolpyruvoylglucosamine reductase, EC 1.3.1.98, MurB family), the organism (Pseudomonas putida KT2440), and the domain architecture (FAD-bd_PCMH cofactor-binding fold; MurB signature IPR003170). The literature retrieved is unambiguously for this enzyme family. No mechanistic study exists on the P. putida KT2440 protein itself; however, the enzyme is one of the most thoroughly characterized in bacterial cell-wall biology, and — importantly — a high-resolution crystal structure exists for the ortholog from the same genus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa MurB (23805286), providing a strong basis for direct inference. Function is therefore assigned by (i) unambiguous family/domain membership and (ii) homology to biochemically and structurally characterized MurB enzymes, most closely the Pseudomonas ortholog.

2. Primary Function: the reaction catalyzed

MurB catalyzes:

UDP-N-acetyl-3-O-(1-carboxyvinyl)-D-glucosamine (enolpyruvyl-UDP-GlcNAc) + NADPH + H⁺ → UDP-N-acetylmuramate (UDP-MurNAc) + NADP⁺

This is the reductive conversion of an enol ether to the D-lactyl ether that defines N-acetylmuramic acid. In the Mur pathway, MurA first transfers phosphoenolpyruvate onto UDP-GlcNAc to make the enolpyruvyl adduct; MurB then reduces that enolpyruvyl group, completing formation of UDP-MurNAc (Benson et al., 8448160; Benson, Walsh & Hogle, 8805513).

Substrate specificity. The physiological substrates are highly specific: the sugar-nucleotide enolpyruvyl-UDP-GlcNAc as the reducible substrate and NADPH (not NADH) as the hydride donor. The enzyme contains one tightly, stoichiometrically bound FAD cofactor that is reduced and re-oxidized during each catalytic cycle (8448160). The E. coli enzyme is a 38-kDa monomer purified to homogeneity with catalytically competent FAD (8448160).

3. Catalytic Mechanism (flavin-mediated, two half-reactions)

MurB is a classic example of a flavoenzyme running a ping-pong (two-half-reaction) mechanism at a single active site:

  1. Reductive half-reaction (NADPH → FAD). A hydride is transferred from NADPH — specifically the 4-pro-S hydrogen — to enzyme-bound FAD, generating FADH₂ (Benson et al., 8448160). In the P. aeruginosa ternary complex, the NADP⁺ nicotinamide ring stacks against the si face of the FAD isoalloxazine, an unusual geometry for hydride transfer (Chen et al., 23805286).

  2. Oxidative half-reaction (FADH₂ → enolpyruvyl substrate). FADH₂ reduces the enolpyruvyl group: a hydride is delivered to the β-methyl of the incipient 3-O-lactyl moiety while a proton from solvent adds to the α-carbon, producing the D-lactyl ether of UDP-MurNAc (8448160). Ser229 is the catalytic proton donor for this step — the S229A mutant suffers an ~10⁷-fold kinetic defect in the second half-reaction (Benson, Walsh & Hogle, 9020778).

Single shared active site. After NADP⁺ departs, the sugar-nucleotide binds the same pocket used by the nicotinamide. In P. aeruginosa MurB, both substrates share a binding site located between two lobes of substrate-binding domain III, with the nicotinamide and enolpyruvyl moieties strikingly superimposable and both poised for hydride exchange with FAD; a potassium ion in the active site aids substrate orientation, and channel flexibility lets the non-reactive parts of the two substrates adopt different conformations (23805286).

Conformational gating. Substrate binding drives a large motion of domain 3 (residues ~219–319) that closes the substrate channel over the ligand; the Tyr190/Tyr254 stacking pair is disrupted, and Tyr190 swings over to hydrogen-bond the α-phosphate of the sugar-nucleotide (Benson, Walsh & Hogle, 8805513). NMR confirmed that NADP⁺ binding induces chemical-shift changes across both the sugar-nucleotide and FAD pockets (Constantine et al., 9150408).

Conserved catalytic residues. Site-directed mutagenesis in S. aureus MurB defined distinct roles: Arg213 maintains the FAD electronic state, Arg176 binds the enolpyruvyl-UDP-GlcNAc substrate, and His259 contacts both the sugar-nucleotide and NADP⁺ (Nishida et al., 16236703). These residues are expected to be conserved in the P. putida ortholog.

4. Subcellular Localization

MurB functions in the cytoplasm. Peptidoglycan biosynthesis is compartmentalized across cytoplasm, inner membrane, and periplasm; the cytoplasmic phase forms the soluble nucleotide precursors through the Mur enzymes, converting UDP-GlcNAc into UDP-MurNAc-pentapeptide (Amera et al., 42128180). MurB is explicitly described as catalyzing "an essential cytoplasmic step in the synthesis of peptidoglycan" (Shapiro et al., 22068704). It is a soluble, monomeric protein with no membrane-spanning segments (consistent with the FAD-bd_PCMH soluble fold), acting on freely diffusible sugar-nucleotide substrates before the pathway moves to the membrane (MraY/MurG produce lipid I/II).

5. Pathway Context and Biological Process

MurB occupies a fixed, non-redundant position in the linear Mur (peptidoglycan precursor) pathway:

UDP-GlcNAc
   │  MurA (enolpyruvyl transfer from PEP)
   ▼
enolpyruvyl-UDP-GlcNAc
   │  MurB  ◄── NADPH + FAD   (THIS ENZYME, EC 1.3.1.98)
   ▼
UDP-MurNAc
   │  MurC (add L-Ala)
   ▼
UDP-MurNAc-L-Ala → (MurD, MurE, MurF) → UDP-MurNAc-pentapeptide
   │  MraY / MurG (membrane) → Lipid I / Lipid II
   ▼
peptidoglycan (periplasm)

MurB's product UDP-MurNAc is the demonstrated substrate for the next enzyme MurC (UDP-MurNAc:L-alanine ligase), as shown by coupled enzymatic synthesis using MurA (MurZ) + MurB to make UDP-MurNAc that then supports the MurC ligase reaction (Raymond et al., 14659546; see also MurC structure/function, Seo et al., 33950018). The broader biological process is biosynthesis of the murein sacculus, required for cell growth, elongation, division, and osmotic protection (Dhar et al., 29185941).

6. Essentiality and Physiological Importance

  • Genetic essentiality. In Bacillus subtilis, murB lies in the dcw ("division and cell wall") cluster immediately upstream of divIB; attempts to inactivate it were unsuccessful, and growth rate, morphology, and antibiotic resistance depend on the MurB expression level (Real & Henriques, 16484183).
  • Conditional-lethal genetics. In Staphylococcus aureus, temperature-sensitive murB point mutants halt peptidoglycan synthesis at the restrictive temperature with reduced reductase activity, confirming murB is essential for growth; five of six causal mutations map to substrate-binding domain 3 (Matsuo et al., 12757953; Nishida et al., 16236703).
  • Drug-target validation. Because interrupting this step is lethal and the enzyme has no human counterpart, MurB is an actively pursued antibacterial target: multiple inhibitor classes (4-thiazolidinones, 3,5-dioxopyrazolidines/pyrazolidine-3,5-diones, tetrazoles) inhibit MurB and reduce peptidoglycan synthesis and bacterial growth (Yang et al., 16436710; Gilbert et al., 17004716; Hrast et al., 29363274), and dedicated high-throughput assays have been developed (Shapiro et al., 22068704).

For P. putida (an environmental, non-pathogenic soil bacterium and biotechnology chassis), MurB performs the same housekeeping role in building and maintaining the Gram-negative cell wall; its essentiality is inferred from the universal conservation and demonstrated essentiality of MurB across bacteria.

9. Limitations and Future Directions

  • No direct study of PP_1904 exists; function is assigned by homology (strongly supported by the P. aeruginosa structure). A recombinant enzyme assay or complementation of an E. coli murB(Ts) strain with PP_1904 would give direct confirmation.
  • Kinetic parameters (kcat, Km for enolpyruvyl-UDP-GlcNAc and NADPH) specific to P. putida are unknown.
  • Whether P. putida MurB participates in any species-specific protein–protein interactions with divisome/elongasome components (as reviewed generally in 27136593) has not been examined.
  • Potential P. putida-specific roles in stress/solvent tolerance (a hallmark of this organism) via cell-wall remodeling remain unexplored.
