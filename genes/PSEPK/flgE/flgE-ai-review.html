Gene Description

FlgE is the structural hook protein of the Pseudomonas putida KT2440 bacterial flagellum. It forms the flexible connector between the basal body motor and the filament, so its core biology is flagellar architecture and flagellum-dependent motility rather than cytosolic function or swarming-specific regulation.

Research Report: Functional annotation of flgE (PP_4388; UniProt Q88ER5) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

1) Target verification (critical identity check)

The UniProt accession Q88ER5 corresponds to FlgE, the flagellar hook protein, encoded by flgE (ordered locus PP_4388) in Pseudomonas putida strain KT2440. The retrieved literature discussing FlgE consistently uses the symbol flgE/FlgE for the flagellar hook structural subunit in Gram-negative bacteria, and no alternate meaning for “flgE” emerged within the KT2440/Pseudomonas motility context. Consistency is also supported by the KT2440 flagellar-cluster study noting a flgF–flgE region within the flagellar locus. (leal‐morales2022transcriptionalorganizationand pages 4-4)

2) Key concepts, definitions, and current understanding of FlgE function

2.1 What FlgE is

FlgE is the polymer-forming subunit of the bacterial flagellar hook, a curved tubular structure that links the basal body (rotary motor) to the filament (propeller). The hook’s mechanical role is as a “universal joint”: it transmits torque from the motor to the filament while permitting bending needed for propulsion and reorientation. This universal-joint function is a central definition in recent authoritative reviews of flagellar architecture and assembly. (minamino2023structureassemblyand pages 1-3, nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 1-3)

2.2 Where FlgE acts (cellular localization)

Flagellar components are assembled by a dedicated flagellar type III secretion system (fT3SS) located at the base of the growing structure. Structural subunits are exported from the cytoplasm, travel through the central channel of the growing flagellum, and assemble at the distal end. In Gram-negative bacteria, FlgE polymerizes to form the hook at the tip of the distal rod, i.e., in the periplasm/extracellular portion of the basal body–hook–filament axis rather than in the cytosol. (minamino2023structureassemblyand pages 1-3, nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 4-6, halte2024flhefunctionsas pages 1-2)

2.3 Role in the assembly pathway (biological process)

The canonical assembly order is basal body → hook → filament. Hook completion is coupled to an export substrate specificity switch (from rod/hook-type substrates to filament-type substrates). A key concept is that the hook is built until a defined length is reached; then a “ruler” system triggers export switching and subsequent filament completion. (minamino2023structureassemblyand pages 1-3)

3) Structural biology and domain architecture (with domain-to-function interpretation)

3.1 FlgE fold and polymerization mechanism

Cryo-EM-derived models support a conserved mechanism in which FlgE monomers have intrinsically disordered N- and C-terminal regions, which become ordered upon assembly into the hook, forming an α-helical coiled-coil inner core (domain D0). This disorder-to-order transition helps prevent premature assembly in the cytosol and contributes to mechanical stability once polymerized. (minamino2023structureassemblyand pages 8-9)

3.2 Hook geometry and protofilaments

The hook is an axial helical polymer built from ~11 protofilaments; FlgE subunits are incorporated sequentially during growth (described in terms of helical “start” geometry in structural models). (minamino2023structureassemblyand pages 8-9)

3.3 Relationship to rod proteins (rod–hook interface)

FlgE shares structural features with the distal rod protein FlgG (e.g., shared domains D0, Dc, D1 and similar coiled-coil architecture), supporting a structural continuity that facilitates assembly of the hook onto the distal rod tip. (nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 4-6)

Visual evidence (figures)

Minamino & Kinoshita (2023) provide (i) a schematic flagellum diagram highlighting the hook as universal joint, and (ii) a figure showing the FlgE atomic model and its domain organization within the hook architecture. (minamino2023structureassemblyand media 18ece2c6, minamino2023structureassemblyand media 37d10345)

4) Quantitative parameters and key data points

4.1 Hook length control

A 2024 mechanistic description of Gram-negative flagellar biogenesis states that once the hook reaches ~55 nm, the molecular ruler FliK catalyzes a substrate-specificity switch in the export gate (terminating hook assembly and enabling filament assembly). This provides a useful quantitative reference for FlgE-dependent structure size control. (halte2024flhefunctionsas pages 1-2)

4.2 Rod/hook structural counts

A 2024 review summarizes that the rod is built as a helical structure with 11 protofilaments and that the distal rod comprises 24 FlgG subunits, with FlgE assembling at the distal rod tip. These values are commonly used to parameterize rod–hook architecture. (nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 4-6)

4.3 Recent structural resolution benchmarks

A 2024 cryo-EM study of Salmonella basal body–hook complexes reports multiple deposited hook-containing maps/models spanning approximately 3.4–7.4 Å resolution (depending on the assembled state and refinement region), enabling near-atomic interpretation of rod/hook-associated assemblies. (tan2024structuralbasisof pages 13-14)

5) Pseudomonas putida KT2440-specific evidence (regulation, expression, and phenotypic context)

Direct single-gene flgE (PP_4388) knockout/phenotype papers for KT2440 were not located in the retrieved full texts. Therefore, organism-specific annotation here relies on: (i) KT2440 flagellar-cluster regulation studies, (ii) KT2440 transcriptomics mentioning FlgE, and (iii) close-strain (P. putida KT2442) genome-engineering phenotypes affecting the entire flagellar cluster that includes flgE.

5.1 Regulatory context: FleQ and the flagellar hierarchy in KT2440

A genome-wide ChIP-seq study of FleQ (the master regulator of motility/biofilm programs in pseudomonads) in P. putida KT2440 reports FleQ binding to promoters in flagellar gene clusters and indicates broad conservation with P. fluorescens F113, where a flgBCDE region is explicitly among FleQ-associated loci. While the KT2440 excerpt does not name PP_4388 directly, it supports the interpretation that FlgE production in KT2440 is embedded in FleQ-controlled flagellar transcriptional programs. (blancoromero2018genomewideanalysisof pages 5-6)

A KT2440-focused flagellar transcription study describes the flagellar cluster as a ~70 kbp region containing 59 genes, and indicates that in P. putida the hook–basal body complex (HBB) structural components are regulated within the Class II tier (FleQ/σ54-dependent) rather than requiring an additional tier for HBB completion as in some other polarly flagellated bacteria. This places the flgE-containing hook module within the core, early-to-mid assembly transcriptional program. This paper also explicitly mentions the flgF–flgE region within the cluster context. (leal‐morales2022transcriptionalorganizationand pages 13-13, leal‐morales2022transcriptionalorganizationand pages 4-4, leal‐morales2022transcriptionalorganizationand pages 11-12)

5.2 Expression evidence: nitrogen limitation transcriptomics

In a KT2440 transcriptome analysis under nitrogen limitation during mcl-PHA-associated conditions, FlgE (flagellar hook protein) is listed among a set of genes that are significantly upregulated across multiple backgrounds (WT and regulatory mutants). The excerpt does not provide the specific fold-change for FlgE, but the directionality suggests that environmental/stress conditions can upregulate hook/chemotaxis-associated functions in KT2440. (dabrowska2020transcriptomechangesin pages 6-8, dabrowska2020transcriptomechangesin pages 8-10)

5.3 Real-world implementation/engineering: deleting the flagellar cluster to redirect resources

A biotechnology-oriented study in P. putida KT2442 (a close derivative used as an industrial chassis) engineered large chromosomal deletions targeting motility structures. Deletion of a large flagellar gene cluster (PP4329–PP4397) produced a strain that lacked flagella and motility and showed major reductions in biofilm formation and intracellular c-di-GMP, while increasing PHA production.

Quantitatively, the flagellar-cluster deletion strain showed biofilm reductions up to 91.3% (crystal violet assay) and increased PHA performance depending on substrate: with sodium hexanoate, biomass +19.1%, PHA yield +73.4%, and PHA content +45.6%; with lauric acid, biomass +11.4%, PHA yield +53.6%, and PHA content +37.9%. Although flgE was not singled out, this work demonstrates real-world exploitation of flagellar-system burden (including the hook module) to improve bioproduction traits in the KT2440 lineage. (wang2018deletionof76 pages 1-2, wang2018deletionof76 pages 11-13, wang2018deletionof76 pages 8-11)

6) Expert opinions and synthesis from authoritative sources

Recent expert reviews emphasize that high-resolution cryo-EM has enabled atomic-level discussion of flagellar parts including the hook, and they treat the hook/FlgE module as a paradigmatic example of how a bacterial supramolecular machine achieves a balance of rigidity (shaft/rod) and flexibility (hook/universal joint) to transmit torque while allowing bending. (minamino2023structureassemblyand pages 1-3, nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 1-3)

A 2024 study framing flagellum assembly mechanistically reaffirms that FlgE polymerization occurs after distal rod formation and that hook completion is checkpointed by a length-control mechanism (FliK), coupling morphogenesis to export switching. (halte2024flhefunctionsas pages 1-2)

7) Summary: functional annotation statement for flgE (PP_4388; Q88ER5) in P. putida KT2440

Primary function: FlgE is a structural flagellar hook protein that polymerizes to form the hook, a curved tubular universal joint connecting the basal body to the filament and transmitting torque during swimming/swarming. (minamino2023structureassemblyand pages 1-3)

Biological process/pathway: FlgE participates in flagellar assembly downstream of rod construction; it is exported by the flagellar type III secretion system and assembles at the distal rod tip. Hook growth is length-controlled and coupled to the export substrate switching that enables filament completion. (minamino2023structureassemblyand pages 1-3, halte2024flhefunctionsas pages 1-2)

Localization: Polymerized FlgE forms the hook located outside the cytoplasm (periplasmic/external axial structure), assembling at the distal end of the growing rod/hook structure. (nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 4-6, halte2024flhefunctionsas pages 1-2)

Structural basis: FlgE contains a coiled-coil core (D0) derived from termini that are disordered in the monomer, and forms an ~11-protofilament helical polymer. (minamino2023structureassemblyand pages 8-9, nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 4-6)

KT2440-specific context: flgE is part of the KT2440 flagellar cluster and is embedded in the FleQ/σ54-controlled hierarchical program for basal body/hook-basal body complex biogenesis. Transcriptomics in KT2440 report flgE upregulation under nitrogen limitation. In the KT2440 lineage, deletion of the flagellar cluster (including the hook module) can abolish motility and substantially alter biofilm/c-di-GMP and improve industrial PHA production metrics. (leal‐morales2022transcriptionalorganizationand pages 11-12, leal‐morales2022transcriptionalorganizationand pages 4-4, leal‐morales2022transcriptionalorganizationand pages 13-13, dabrowska2020transcriptomechangesin pages 6-8, wang2018deletionof76 pages 1-2)

  • Domain architecture is hook-centric despite some family-level overlap with rod/basal-body proteins: UniProt reports Flagellar hook protein FlgE/F/G-like D1, Flagellar hook protein FlgE D2, and a more generic Flagellar basal-body/hook protein C-terminal region, which explains why some automated pipelines may confuse hook proteins with other axial components.   • Falcon deep research supports the same curation direction and adds useful mechanistic context: FlgE is the polymer-forming subunit of the bacterial flagellar hook, it polymerizes to form the hook at the tip of the distal rod, and this occurs rather than in the cytosol. That strengthens removal of the cytosol and basal-body CC calls while supporting hook localization and structural-molecule activity.
