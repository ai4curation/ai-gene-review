bioD (PP_0366, UniProt Q88QW8) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440: ATP-Dependent Dethiobiotin Synthetase

Target: UniProt Q88QW8 · Gene bioD · Ordered locus PP_0366 Organism: Pseudomonas putida (strain ATCC 47054 / DSM 6125 / NCIMB 11950 / KT2440) EC number: 6.3.3.3 · Family: Dethiobiotin synthetase family (Pfam AAA_26 PF13500; InterPro DTB_synth_BioD IPR004472, P-loop_NTPase IPR027417)

Summary

The gene bioD (ordered locus PP_0366, UniProt accession Q88QW8) of Pseudomonas putida KT2440 encodes ATP-dependent dethiobiotin synthetase (DTBS/DTB synthetase, EC 6.3.3.3), a soluble cytoplasmic enzyme that catalyzes the penultimate step of de novo biotin (vitamin B7) biosynthesis. The enzyme converts 7,8-diaminopelargonic acid (DAPA, also called 7,8-diaminononanoic acid) and CO₂/bicarbonate into dethiobiotin (DTB) in an ATP- and Mg²⁺-dependent reaction, forming the ureido (five-membered urea) ring of the biotin bicyclic scaffold and releasing ADP + inorganic phosphate. Biotin synthase (BioB) then performs the final step — insertion of sulfur to close the thiophane ring and complete biotin.

Mechanistically, dethiobiotin synthetase is one of the best-characterized ATP-dependent carboxylases in structural enzymology. High-resolution crystallographic and kinetic-crystallography studies of the Escherichia coli ortholog established that the reaction proceeds via an initial N7-carbamate formed from CO₂, which is then phosphorylated by ATP to a mixed carbamic–phosphoric anhydride intermediate, followed by intramolecular cyclization to form the ureido ring. Two Mg²⁺ ions bridge the phosphate groups and stabilize the reaction intermediate. The enzyme is a homodimeric α/β P-loop NTPase whose composite active site sits at the dimer interface, with DAPA binding one subunit and ATP the other. Nucleotide selectivity is set by a "gate-keeper" asparagine that hydrogen-bonds the adenine base and excludes other NTPs.

Although no biochemical study has been performed on the P. putida protein itself, its identity as a functional dethiobiotin synthetase is secure. The Q88QW8 sequence (226 aa) shares 48% identity with the experimentally characterized E. coli enzyme, retains the DTBS-family deviant Walker-A P-loop and family-diagnostic motifs, and carries no signal peptide or transmembrane segment — consistent with a cytoplasmic location. Independently, genome-wide transposon screening in P. putida KT2440 recovered biotin auxotrophs, confirming the biotin biosynthetic pathway (of which bioD is a required member) is functional and essential for growth on minimal medium. Because the biotin pathway is absent in humans, DTBS/BioD is a validated antibacterial and antitubercular drug target.

Key Findings

Finding 1 — bioD is a dethiobiotin synthetase catalyzing the penultimate step of biotin biosynthesis

UniProt annotation of Q88QW8 (via HAMAP-Rule MF_00336) assigns the protein to the dethiobiotin synthetase family with EC 6.3.3.3, carrying the DTB_synth_BioD domain (IPR004472). Biochemical and structural studies of orthologs firmly establish the reaction catalyzed: dethiobiotin synthetase performs the ATP-dependent formation of dethiobiotin from CO₂ and 7,8-diaminopelargonic acid (DAPA), the penultimate step of biotin biosynthesis, immediately before biotin synthase (BioB) carries out the final sulfur-insertion step.

Crystallographic work on the E. coli enzyme describes "the crystal structures of six complexes of homodimeric Escherichia coli dethiobiotin synthetase with a variety of substrates, substrate analogs, and products" (PMID: 7669756), confirming DTBS/BioD is a homodimeric enzyme acting on DAPA-derived substrates. More recent work explicitly restates that "dethiobiotin synthetase (DTBS) catalyzes the penultimate step of biotin biosynthesis" (PMID: 34597430).

Critically for the P. putida assignment, a genome-wide mutant screen of KT2440 grown on minimal medium recovered biotin auxotrophs — the study reports that "we also found auxotrophs for biotin, nicotinate and vitamin B12 that were not predicted in silico" (PMID: 20158506) — demonstrating that the biotin biosynthetic pathway (which includes bioD/PP_0366) is functional and required for growth of this organism in the absence of exogenous biotin.

Finding 2 — Reaction mechanism: carbamate formation, ATP-dependent phosphoryl transfer, and cyclization

The catalytic mechanism has been dissected in atomic detail by kinetic crystallography of the E. coli enzyme at 1.6–1.9 Å resolution, capturing both the enzyme·MgATP·DAPA complex and a trapped enzyme·MgADP·carbamic-phosphoric-anhydride intermediate. The reaction is initiated when CO₂/bicarbonate reacts with the N7 amino group of DAPA to form a carbamate; ATP then phosphorylates this carbamate to yield a mixed carbamic–phosphoric anhydride; finally, the second amino group attacks intramolecularly to close the ureido ring, releasing phosphate. The authors state that "the reaction is initiated by the formation of a carbamate and proceeds through a phosphorylated intermediate, a mixed carbamic phosphoric anhydride" (PMID: 9576910).

Two magnesium ions play an essential catalytic role at the phosphate-transfer center: "these magnesium ions compensate the negative charges at both phosphate groups after phosphoryl transfer and contribute to the stabilization of the reaction intermediate" (PMID: 9576910). The phosphoryl transfer proceeds with inversion of configuration at phosphorus, consistent with a single in-line attack.

The nucleotide-binding architecture was mapped to a classical mononucleotide fold. The E. coli structure places "the phosphate groups close to the phosphate binding loop Gly8–Thr16 between β-strand β1 and the N-terminus of α-helix α1" (PMID: 7669756) — the Walker-A-like P-loop — with divalent-cation coordination provided by Asp54, Thr16, and Glu115.

Finding 3 — Homodimeric α/β P-loop enzyme with active site at the dimer interface

The 1.80 Å structure of E. coli DTBS revealed the enzyme's quaternary organization: each subunit folds as a single globular α/β domain, and the biological unit is a homodimer whose active site straddles the subunit interface. The structural study "establishes that the enzyme active site is situated at the dimer interface, with the substrate binding to one monomer and ATP to the other" (PMID: 7881906). This composite-active-site architecture explains why the enzyme is obligately dimeric.

The DTBS fold belongs to the diverse P-loop NTPase superfamily. The overall fold "closely resembles that of three other enzymes, adenylosuccinate synthetase (purA), Ha-ras-p21, and nitrogenase iron protein" (PMID: 7881906) — a set of proteins related by fold rather than by detectable sequence similarity (the SIMIBI / adenylosuccinate-synthetase-like clade). DTBS shows no significant sequence similarity to proteins outside its own family. This is fully consistent with the UniProt annotation of Q88QW8, which carries both the P-loop_NTPase (IPR027417) and DTB_synth_BioD (IPR004472) domain signatures.

Finding 4 — Position in the cytoplasmic biotin ring-assembly module; ATP-specificity gate-keeper

Biotin biosynthesis is organized into two conceptual stages: (1) synthesis of the pimelate moiety (via BioC–BioH in E. coli and related γ-proteobacteria) and (2) assembly of the bicyclic ring by the sequential action of BioF (KAPA synthase) → BioA (DAPA aminotransferase) → BioD (DTBS) → BioB (biotin synthase). A review of the pathway describes how "a pimelate moiety is synthesized and used to begin assembly of the biotin bicyclic ring structure" (PMID: 21437340). BioD performs the ring-closure carboxylation that generates the ureido ring — notably a non-biotin-dependent carboxylation, distinguishing it from the biotin-dependent carboxylases downstream in metabolism.

Nucleotide specificity across the family is governed by a small number of C-terminal residues. In the E. coli-type enzyme, "mutational analysis performed alongside in silico experiments revealed a gate-keeper role for Asn175 in Escherichia coli DTBS that excludes binding of other nucleotide triphosphates" (PMID: 25801336), conferring ATP preference. Interestingly, the Mycobacterium tuberculosis enzyme lacks this residue and shows broad NTP promiscuity with highest affinity for CTP — illustrating that nucleotide recognition, not the core chemistry, is the most variable feature among orthologs.

The pathway's absence in humans makes the enzyme a clean drug target: "due to its importance in bacterial pathogenesis, and the absence of a human homologue, DTBS is a promising target for the development of new antibacterials" (PMID: 25801336).

Finding 5 — The P. putida sequence carries the DTBS-family deviant Walker-A P-loop

The Q88QW8 sequence retrieved from UniProt is 226 residues long and begins MSQAFFIAGTDTDVGKTTIAAGLLH…. This N-terminal region contains the dethiobiotin-synthetase deviant Walker-A phosphate-binding loop (G-x-x-x-x-GKTT), with the diagnostic GKTT at approximately residues 15–18. This directly corresponds to the E. coli DTBS Gly8–Thr16 P-loop identified crystallographically (PMID: 7669756). The overall protein length (~226 aa) is typical of monofunctional bacterial dethiobiotin synthetases (the E. coli enzyme is 225 aa). This sequence-level evidence links the P. putida ortholog directly to the experimentally characterized enzyme family, rather than relying solely on automated database annotation transfer.

Finding 6 — 48% sequence identity to experimentally characterized E. coli DTBS

A global pairwise alignment (Needleman–Wunsch) of P. putida BioD (Q88QW8, 226 aa) against E. coli BioD (UniProt P13000, 225 aa) yields 48.2% identity over 222 aligned columns (107/222 identical residues). Both proteins retain the DTBS deviant Walker-A P-loop (P. putida GTDTDVGKTT vs E. coli GTDTEVGKTV) and the family-diagnostic motifs EGAGGW (P. putida IEGAGGWRVP; E. coli VEGAGGWFTP) and the ILVVGV(K/R)LGCI nucleotide/catalytic loop.

This level of identity is far above the ~30% threshold generally taken to indicate conserved three-dimensional structure and function, establishing unambiguous orthology between the P. putida enzyme and the reference enzyme whose structure and carbamate/anhydride mechanism were determined crystallographically (PMID: 7669756, PMID: 9576910). Function, mechanism, and structural fold can therefore be confidently transferred to Q88QW8.

Finding 7 — Sequence-based localization: cytoplasmic, no signal peptide

Kyte–Doolittle hydropathy analysis of Q88QW8 (226 aa) shows the N-terminal 30-residue mean hydropathy is 0.42 — there is no hydrophobic signal-peptide h-region and no Sec/Tat export signal. The overall grand average of hydropathy (GRAVY) is 0.283. Internal hydrophobic windows (the strongest being a 19-residue peak hydropathy of 1.95 near residue 136, within the conserved ILVVGVRLGCISHALL catalytic-core loop) map to buried β-strands of the globular α/β P-loop fold, not to transmembrane helices. This is fully consistent with the experimentally determined soluble, cytosolic, homodimeric structures of DTBS orthologs, in which "the protein forms a homodimer with each subunit folded as a single globular alpha/beta domain" (PMID: 7881906), and with the established fact that de novo biotin ring assembly occurs in the bacterial cytoplasm.

Structural logic

BioD is an obligate homodimer. Its P-loop NTPase fold (shared with adenylosuccinate synthetase, Ras, and nitrogenase Fe-protein) binds and orients MgATP through the Walker-A/deviant P-loop (GKTT), while the second, adjacent subunit contributes residues that bind DAPA — placing the composite catalytic machinery at the dimer interface. This explains three otherwise-independent observations: (i) the requirement for dimerization; (ii) the strict Mg²⁺ dependence for phosphoryl transfer; and (iii) the concentration of species-variable residues in C-terminal nucleotide-recognition loops (the gate-keeper Asn and adjacent motifs), which tune nucleotide specificity without altering the conserved DAPA-carboxylation chemistry.

Limitations and Knowledge Gaps

  1. No direct biochemistry on the P. putida protein. All kinetic and structural data derive from orthologs (E. coli, M. tuberculosis, H. pylori, A. thaliana). The function, mechanism, and localization for Q88QW8 are inferred from strong sequence/domain homology (48% identity to E. coli), not measured directly. No purified-enzyme assay, K_m, k_cat, or crystal structure exists for PP_0366.

  2. Nucleotide specificity not experimentally confirmed for Q88QW8. While the sequence retains E. coli-type C-terminal motifs, the specific gate-keeper residue and any NTP promiscuity have not been tested in the P. putida enzyme.

  3. Localization is bioinformatic. The cytoplasmic assignment rests on the absence of a signal peptide/TM segment and on the soluble nature of orthologous structures — no experimental fractionation or microscopy has localized the P. putida protein.

  4. Gene-level essentiality is indirect. The KT2440 mutant screen recovered biotin auxotrophs but did not, in the material reviewed here, pin the phenotype specifically to PP_0366 versus other biotin genes; essentiality of bioD is inferred from pathway logic.

  5. Pathway upstream context in P. putida not detailed here. The exact route to the pimelate moiety and DAPA (BioC/BioH/BioF/BioA homologs) in P. putida was not fully mapped in this investigation, though the presence of biotin auxotrophy implies a complete pathway.

Conclusion

bioD / PP_0366 / Q88QW8 encodes ATP-dependent dethiobiotin synthetase (DTBS, EC 6.3.3.3), a soluble, cytoplasmic, homodimeric P-loop enzyme that catalyzes the penultimate step of de novo biotin biosynthesis: the Mg²⁺/ATP-dependent carboxylation and ureido-ring closure of 7,8-diaminopelargonic acid (DAPA) with CO₂ to form dethiobiotin, releasing ADP + Pi. It acts immediately downstream of the aminotransferase BioA and upstream of biotin synthase BioB in the cytoplasmic biotin-ring-assembly module. The reaction proceeds via an N7-carbamate and a mixed carbamic–phosphoric anhydride intermediate stabilized by two Mg²⁺ ions, with the composite active site formed at the dimer interface. Direct experimental characterization exists for orthologs (E. coli, M. tuberculosis, H. pylori, Arabidopsis); the P. putida assignment is supported by 48% sequence identity to E. coli DTBS, conserved DTBS domains and the diagnostic Walker-A P-loop in its 226-aa sequence, the absence of any export signal, and the biotin auxotrophy of pathway mutants in KT2440.
