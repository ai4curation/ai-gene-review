## Research report: Functional annotation of **pcaK** (UniProt **Q88N40**, locus **PP_1376**) in *Pseudomonas putida* KT2440

### Executive summary
The protein encoded by **pcaK** in *Pseudomonas putida* KT2440 (UniProt Q88N40; PP_1376) is an **inner-membrane major facilitator superfamily (MFS) aromatic acid/H+ symporter** that mediates high-affinity uptake of **4‑hydroxybenzoate (4‑HBA)** and **protocatechuate (PCA)**, feeding these substrates into the **protocatechuate branch of the β‑ketoadipate pathway** for aromatic catabolism and enabling chemotactic responses to aromatic acids. Its transporter function is supported by radiolabeled uptake assays, membrane fractionation, energy-inhibitor tests consistent with proton-motive-force coupling, genetic knockouts/complementation, and site-directed mutagenesis of conserved charged residues required for transport. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2, wada2021characterizationofaromatic pages 1-2, ditty2002chargedaminoacids pages 1-2)

### 1) Target verification (gene/protein identity)

#### 1.1 Matching symbol, description, and organism
Multiple primary sources explicitly link **pcaK** to a **4‑hydroxybenzoate permease** in *Pseudomonas putida* and classify it as an MFS transporter with 12 predicted transmembrane (TM) segments. (harwood1994identificationofthe pages 1-2, nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2)

A modern KT2440-focused transporter study identifies **pcaK as PP_1376** in *P. putida* KT2440 and places it among aromatic acid/H+ symporters. (wada2021characterizationofaromatic pages 1-2)

#### 1.2 Protein family/domains (current understanding)
Functional and sequence-based classification places PcaK in:
- **Major facilitator superfamily (MFS)** transporters, typical **12-TM** architecture. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2, ditty2002chargedaminoacids pages 1-2)
- The **aromatic acid/H+ symporter (AAHS)** family, described as founded by PcaK. (ditty2002chargedaminoacids pages 1-2)

Together, this matches the UniProt-provided description (“4‑hydroxybenzoate transporter”, MFS family) for Q88N40.

### 2) Key concepts and definitions

#### 2.1 What PcaK does (molecular function)
**PcaK is a secondary active transporter**: it imports aromatic acids across the cytoplasmic membrane by coupling substrate uptake to the **proton motive force (PMF)** (H+ symport). (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2, nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 3-4)

#### 2.2 Substrates and specificity
**Transported substrates (direct experimental evidence)**
- **4-hydroxybenzoate (4‑HBA)** and **protocatechuate (PCA)** are transported by PcaK in heterologous *E. coli* expression systems and in *P. putida* mutant comparisons. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2, nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 3-4)

**Non-substrate inhibitor**
- **Benzoate inhibits 4‑HBA uptake** but is **not itself transported** (i.e., competitive interaction without net uptake through PcaK). (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2, nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 3-4)

**Physiological rationale**
At physiological pH, aromatic carboxylic acids are predominantly ionized and generally do not passively diffuse across membranes efficiently, making dedicated uptake systems (such as PcaK) a critical control point for aromatic catabolism and bioconversion. (wada2021characterizationofaromatic pages 1-2)

### 3) Experimental evidence for function, kinetics, and localization

#### 3.1 Membrane localization and topology
PcaK is experimentally supported to be an **integral membrane protein**:
- After heterologous expression in *E. coli*, PcaK is found in membrane fractions and partitions into the Triton X‑114 detergent phase, consistent with a hydrophobic inner-membrane protein. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 2-3, nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 3-4, nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 2-2)
- Sequence predictions and family assignment support a typical MFS **12-TM** topology. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2, ditty2002chargedaminoacids pages 1-2)

#### 3.2 Kinetic parameters and uptake capacity
A foundational kinetic characterization reports:
- **Km = 6 mM** and **Vmax ≥ 25 nmol·min−1·mg protein−1** for 4‑HBA uptake. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2)
- Uptake can accumulate substrate **~30–60×**, demonstrating concentration against a gradient typical of energized transport. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 4-4)

Additional uptake rates reported under defined assay conditions include **6.1 ± 0.5** and **22.1 ± 2.4 nmol·min−1·mg protein−1**, emphasizing dependence on assay setup and conditions. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 3-4, nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 4-5)

#### 3.3 Energy coupling (PMF dependence)
Multiple inhibitor/energy perturbation experiments support **PMF-driven** transport:
- Uptake is abolished by uncouplers such as **CCCP** and **DNP**, and by respiratory inhibition (**KCN**) under tested conditions. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 4-4, nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 2-3)
- Experiments separating ATP levels from transport activity indicate uptake is **not directly dependent on cellular ATP**, consistent with **H+ symport** typical of MFS transporters. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 4-4)

#### 3.4 Substrate competition profile (binding specificity)
Competition assays show strong inhibition of 4‑HBA uptake by:
- **Benzoate (~84% inhibition)** and **PCA (~79% inhibition)** at 20-fold excess, with weaker/partial effects by some related benzoates and 3‑hydroxybenzoate, while catechol and aromatic amino acids do not significantly inhibit in this setup. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 3-4)

This supports recognition of aromatic carboxylates and an overlap between 4‑HBA and PCA binding.

#### 3.5 Genetic evidence: knockout, complementation, and physiological role
In KT2440, targeted deletion demonstrates that PcaK is a key (and for PCA, essential under tested conditions) uptake route:
- **ΔpcaK loses growth on PCA across 2, 5, and 20 mM**, leading authors to conclude **pcaK appears essential for PCA uptake** in their growth conditions. (wada2021characterizationofaromatic pages 5-7)
- For 4‑HBA, ΔpcaK shows strong growth impairment at **2–5 mM** but this defect is largely masked at **20 mM**, consistent with the possibility of lower-affinity alternative transport at high substrate. (wada2021characterizationofaromatic pages 5-7)
- Plasmid complementation with pcaK restores growth on PCA/4‑HBA, tying phenotype directly to the gene. (wada2021characterizationofaromatic pages 5-7)

#### 3.6 Mutational determinants (mechanistic insight)
Site-directed mutagenesis of conserved charged residues in the AAHS/MFS framework shows these residues are required for transport:
- A conserved **DGXD motif** in TM1 (aspartates), **arginines in TM4 and TM11**, and residues including **Glu144** and **Asp386** are highlighted as conserved and functionally required; mutations impair or abolish 4‑HBA transport. (ditty2002chargedaminoacids pages 1-2)

These results support a conserved mechanistic core for substrate/H+ coupling and/or conformational cycling.

### 4) Biological processes and pathway context

#### 4.1 Role in the β-ketoadipate pathway (protocatechuate branch)
PcaK imports 4‑HBA and PCA, which feed into the **protocatechuate branch** of the **β‑ketoadipate pathway**, a central aromatic ring-cleavage route that funnels diverse aromatics into TCA-cycle intermediates. The pcaRKF gene cluster and subsequent functional studies link transport (pcaK) with chemotaxis and degradation of 4‑HBA in *Pseudomonas*. (harwood1994identificationofthe pages 7-8, nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2)

#### 4.2 Integration with chemotaxis
PcaK has been described as dual-function, linking aromatic-acid uptake with **chemotactic behavior**:
- pcaK mutants show impaired chemotaxis toward aromatic acids, and mutations that eliminate transport also abolish chemotaxis to 4‑HBA, indicating mechanistic coupling between transport and sensory response in this system. (nichols1997pcakahighaffinity pages 1-2, ditty2002chargedaminoacids pages 1-2, harwood1994identificationofthe pages 1-2)
