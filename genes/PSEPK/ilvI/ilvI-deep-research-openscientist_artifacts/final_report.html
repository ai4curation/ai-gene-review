Functional Annotation of ilvI (Q88DY8, PP_4680) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

1. Summary / Answer

Q88DY8 (gene ilvI, locus PP_4680) is the large, catalytic subunit of acetohydroxyacid synthase (AHAS; also called acetolactate synthase, EC 2.2.1.6) — a thiamine-diphosphate (ThDP)-dependent enzyme that catalyzes the first and committed step of branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) biosynthesis (valine, leucine, isoleucine). Acting in the cytoplasm as part of an A₂B₂ holoenzyme with a small regulatory subunit (PP_4679/ilvH), it condenses two molecules of pyruvate into (2S)-2-acetolactate (the Val/Leu precursor), or condenses pyruvate with 2-ketobutyrate into 2-aceto-2-hydroxybutyrate (the Ile precursor), releasing CO₂. The reaction requires ThDP, a divalent metal (Mg²⁺), and FAD.

Gene-identity verification confirms this is the correct protein: UniProt, KEGG, GO, and InterPro all independently annotate Q88DY8/PP_4680 as the AHAS large subunit, and the surrounding literature on the AHAS enzyme family is fully consistent with the provided protein description, domains, and organism.

Note on nomenclature: In enterobacteria (E. coli), there are three AHAS isozymes whose large catalytic subunits are named ilvB (AHAS I), ilvG (AHAS II), and ilvI (AHAS III); each pairs with a small regulatory subunit (ilvN, ilvM, ilvH, respectively) (Vyazmensky et al., 2009, 19653643). The P. putida gene retains the ilvI name for its single AHAS large subunit. The symbol is therefore unambiguous here and the literature on AHAS applies directly. This is not the same as, e.g., unrelated genes that share the "ilvI" text in other contexts.

3. Primary Function: Reaction and Substrate Specificity

AHAS catalyzes a ThDP-dependent carboligation. UniProt records the canonical reaction (RHEA:25249):

2 pyruvate + H⁺ → (2S)-2-acetolactate + CO₂ (EC 2.2.1.6)

The enzyme has two physiological condensations that partition flux between the BCAA branches (Liu, Li & Wang, 2016, 27576495):

  • Pyruvate + pyruvate → (2S)-2-acetolactate → downstream to L-valine and L-leucine.
  • Pyruvate + 2-ketobutyrate → 2-aceto-2-hydroxybutyrate → downstream to L-isoleucine.

Catalytic mechanism. ThDP (activated by the divalent metal ion, Mg²⁺) is deprotonated at C2 to a reactive ylide; it attacks the first pyruvate and, after decarboxylation (loss of CO₂), forms a hydroxyethyl-ThDP (enamine/carbanion) intermediate. This carbanion then attacks the carbonyl of a second 2-oxoacid (pyruvate or 2-ketobutyrate), and product release regenerates the ylide (Gokhale & Tilak, 2015, 25882218; Liu et al., 2016, 27576495).

Cofactors (UniProt COFACTOR; GO): ThDP (1 per subunit), Mg²⁺ (1 per subunit), and FAD. FAD is a non-catalytic, structurally required cofactor — a vestige of AHAS's evolutionary descent from the FAD/redox-dependent pyruvate oxidase family (Gokhale & Tilak, 2015, 25882218; Liu et al., 2016, 27576495).

Substrate specificity (from a close bacterial homolog). Kinetic characterization of the orthologous Ralstonia eutropha AHAS (ilvBH) shows the enzyme has weak affinity for the first substrate pyruvate (Kₘ ≈ 10.5 µM) but strong selectivity toward 2-ketobutyrate as the second substrate (specificity ratio R ≈ 140), explaining efficient isoleucine synthesis when 2-ketobutyrate is available (Lu et al., 2015, 25081555). While measured in R. eutropha, this behavior is characteristic of the conserved bacterial AHAS catalytic subunit to which PP_4680 belongs.

Substrate specificity inferred from conserved active-site residues. A more ortholog-specific inference comes from the active site itself. E. coli AHAS isozyme I (IlvB) is unusual in being non-specific for 2-ketobutyrate versus pyruvate as the acceptor substrate; this is caused by two residues (Gln480 and Met476) that replace a Trp and Leu conserved in the other AHAS isozymes, accelerating the ligation/product-release steps (Belenky et al., 2012, 22443469). Aligning Q88DY8 to these positions shows that P. putida IlvI retains the conserved Trp (Trp486) — exactly as in E. coli AHAS III (Trp484) — rather than the AHAS I Gln. It therefore mirrors the AHAS III active site and is predicted to be specific for 2-ketobutyrate over pyruvate as the second substrate, i.e., an efficient producer of the isoleucine precursor. This structure/evolution-based prediction is more reliable than generic family-level transfer because it is anchored on the specific catalytic residues of the 67%-identical AHAS III ortholog.

4. Quaternary Structure, Regulatory Subunit, and Localization

Holoenzyme architecture. Active AHAS is a holoenzyme of a catalytic (large) subunit + a regulatory (small) subunit. The crystal structure of the AHAS holoenzyme shows a A₂B₂ arrangement (two catalytic + two regulatory subunits), with the regulatory-subunit dimer clamping the catalytic dimer; a conserved salt bridge acts as a trigger that opens the catalytic activation loop, boosting catalytic-subunit activity (Zhang et al., 2020, 32538427). The isolated large subunit has only a small fraction of full activity, and holoenzyme activity is fully restored upon reconstitution with the small subunit (Vyazmensky et al., 2009, 19653643).

The partner small subunit in P. putida. Chromosomal context strongly corroborates this: PP_4680 (ilvI, K01652, large subunit) is immediately adjacent to PP_4679 (K01653, "acetolactate synthase small subunit" = ilvH/regulatory subunit), and directly upstream lies PP_4678 (K00053, ketol-acid reductoisomerase / ilvC), the enzyme catalyzing the next step of the pathway. This ilvC–ilvH–ilvI clustering is typical of co-regulated BCAA-biosynthesis genes and confirms the functional pairing of PP_4680 with PP_4679 (KEGG genome positions: PP_4680 5317649–5319373; PP_4679 5317156–5317647, same strand).

Allosteric feedback inhibition. The regulatory subunit carries paired ACT domains; the end-product amino acid(s) valine (and/or leucine) bind at the interface between two ACT domains, transmitting inhibition to the catalytic subunit (Barak & Chipman, 2012, 22198286). This makes AHAS the rate-controlling gatekeeper of the pathway: BCAA end-products shut down their own synthesis at the first committed step. Homolog kinetics quantify the effect (R. eutropha AHAS: L-valine IC₅₀ ≈ 1.2 mM, L-isoleucine ≈ 2.3 mM, L-leucine ≈ 5.4 mM; Lu et al., 2015, 25081555). Barak & Chipman also note that the regulatory subunits of "typical" single AHAS enzymes in non-enterobacteria resemble E. coli AHAS III (ilvIH) — consistent with the P. putida ilvIH-type system.

Subcellular localization. AHAS is a soluble cytoplasmic enzyme (GO:0005948, "acetolactate synthase complex"; no signal peptide or transmembrane region). Its function — a biosynthetic condensation feeding downstream cytoplasmic enzymes (ilvC, ilvD, and the transaminases/leucine-branch enzymes) — is carried out entirely within the cytoplasm.

PP_4680 is the dedicated biosynthetic AHAS. P. putida KT2440 encodes four TPP-family proteins that KEGG maps to the AHAS-large-subunit ortholog K01652 (PP_1157, PP_1394, PP_3365, PP_4680). However, several lines of evidence single out PP_4680 as the true anabolic BCAA enzyme: (i) the genome contains only one small regulatory-subunit gene, PP_4679 (K01653), and it sits immediately adjacent to PP_4680 in the ilvC–ilvH–ilvI cluster; (ii) PP_4680 is uniquely annotated "acetohydroxybutanoate synthase / acetolactate synthase," i.e., competent for the 2-ketobutyrate→isoleucine branch that defines the biosynthetic enzyme; and (iii) the other three loci are standalone TPP enzymes with no cognate small subunit or ilv-operon context (likely catabolic acetolactate/2-oxoacid-decarboxylase-type activities). At the sequence level, Q88DY8 carries the canonical ThDP-binding fingerprint GDG…N (…YDKGDGNVIKP…, GDG at residue 369), the hallmark motif that coordinates the ThDP–Mg²⁺ cofactor in this enzyme family. Together these confirm PP_4680/ilvI as the correct, dedicated biosynthetic AHAS catalytic subunit.

Orthology confirmed by sequence identity. Global (Needleman–Wunsch) alignment shows Q88DY8 (574 aa) is 67.0% identical to the E. coli AHAS isozyme III large subunit (IlvI, P00893; also 574 aa) — versus only 44.8% to the AHAS I large subunit (IlvB, P08142). This markedly higher identity to E. coli AHAS III, plus the identical chain length, establishes Q88DY8 as a specific ortholog of the AHAS III (ilvIH) lineage rather than a generic AHAS. This is fully consistent with the observation that "typical" single bacterial AHAS enzymes resemble E. coli AHAS III (Barak & Chipman, 2012, 22198286), and it independently validates the ilvI gene name from evolutionary evidence.

5. Pathway Context

AHAS catalyzes step 1 of 4 in two parallel routes (UniProt PATHWAY; KEGG ppu00290 "Valine, leucine and isoleucine biosynthesis"):

  • L-valine from pyruvate: step 1/4 → acetolactate → (ilvC) → (ilvD) → 2-ketoisovalerate → (ilvE/transaminase) → valine. 2-Ketoisovalerate also branches to leucine (leuABCD) and is a precursor for pantothenate/CoA.
  • L-isoleucine from 2-oxobutanoate (2-ketobutyrate): step 1/4 → 2-aceto-2-hydroxybutyrate → (ilvC) → (ilvD) → 2-keto-3-methylvalerate → (ilvE) → isoleucine. (2-Ketobutyrate itself derives from threonine via threonine dehydratase, ilvA.)

Because it initiates all three BCAAs and their derived cofactor precursors, and because the pathway is absent in animals, AHAS is the well-known molecular target of >50 commercial herbicides (sulfonylureas, imidazolinones, triazolopyrimidines) and an antimicrobial/anti-tuberculosis drug target (Liu et al., 2016, 27576495; Gokhale & Tilak, 2015, 25882218; Lonhienne et al., 2018, 29440497).

6. Hypotheses: Supported and Refuted

Supported 1. ilvI/PP_4680 encodes the AHAS large catalytic subunit — supported by UniProt, KEGG K01652, GO, InterPro, and direct concordance with AHAS literature. 2. It catalyzes ThDP/Mg²⁺/FAD-dependent condensation of pyruvate (±2-ketobutyrate) as the committed step of BCAA biosynthesis — supported (UniProt reaction, 27576495 25882218). 3. It functions as a cytoplasmic holoenzyme with the adjacent small subunit PP_4679 (ilvH) and is feedback-inhibited by valine via ACT-domain sites — supported by structural/kinetic literature (32538427 22198286 19653643) and by the P. putida ilvC–ilvH–ilvI gene cluster.

Refuted / ruled out - That the ilvI symbol refers to an unrelated non-AHAS gene — refuted; all databases and family/domain evidence converge on AHAS. - That the large subunit alone constitutes the physiologically regulated enzyme — refuted; full activity and valine sensitivity require the small subunit (19653643 22198286).

7. Evidence Quality and Limitations

  • Enzyme identity, reaction, cofactors, pathway, and localization rest on strong, convergent database annotation (UniProt/KEGG/GO/InterPro) plus authoritative reviews and primary structural studies of the AHAS family — high confidence.
  • Direct biochemical characterization of the P. putida KT2440 enzyme specifically was not located in the searchable literature; the quantitative substrate-specificity and feedback-inhibition parameters cited (Kₘ, IC₅₀, selectivity R) come from a close bacterial homolog (R. eutropha ilvBH, 25081555) and from E. coli isozyme studies. These are transferable by strong homology but are inferences, not measurements on Q88DY8.
  • Future directions: heterologous expression and kinetic assay of PP_4680 + PP_4679 to measure its own Kₘ/kcat and valine IC₅₀; verification that PP_4679 is the cognate regulatory subunit by co-purification; and testing whether P. putida encodes any second AHAS large subunit (an ilvBN-type paralog) elsewhere on the chromosome.
