Functional Annotation Report: acnA-I (PP_2112, UniProt Q88L24)

Aconitate hydratase (Aconitase, EC 4.2.1.3) — Pseudomonas putida KT2440

1. Summary (Answer to the Research Question)

acnA-I (PP_2112, Q88L24) encodes a cytoplasmic, [4Fe-4S]-cluster-dependent aconitase (aconitate hydratase, EC 4.2.1.3) that catalyzes the reversible, stereospecific isomerization of citrate to D-threo-isocitrate via the enzyme-bound intermediate cis-aconitate — the second step of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA/Krebs) cycle. Sequence, structural, and evolutionary evidence identify it specifically as an AcnA-type enzyme (73% identical to the biochemically characterized Escherichia coli AcnA), i.e. the stable, iron/oxidative-stress-inducible "maintenance" aconitase, as opposed to the exponential-phase catabolic AcnB or the propionate/2-methylcitrate-dedicated aconitases encoded elsewhere in the genome. Like other AcnA/IRP-family proteins, it is predicted to be bifunctional: when its Fe-S cluster is lost under iron limitation or oxidative stress, the apo-protein can act as a post-transcriptional mRNA-binding regulator.

Gene identity was verified at every step: the symbol acnA-I, the aconitate hydratase description, the aconitase/IPM-isomerase family, the AcnA_IRP swivel and aconitase domains, and the organism P. putida KT2440 are all mutually consistent with the enzyme characterized below. There was no evidence of a same-symbol collision with an unrelated gene.

2. Molecular Function and Reaction Catalyzed

2.1 The reaction

AcnA-I is an aconitate hydratase (aconitase), EC 4.2.1.3, catalyzing:

citrate ⇌ cis-aconitate + H₂O ⇌ D-threo-isocitrate

The overall reaction is a net isomerization (a dehydration followed by a stereochemically distinct re-hydration) that moves a hydroxyl group from the C3 to the C2 position of the tricarboxylic acid, proceeding through the tightly bound intermediate cis-aconitate (UniProt Q88L24 catalytic activity: "citrate = D-threo-isocitrate"; UniPathway: tricarboxylic acid cycle; isocitrate from oxaloacetate: step 2/2). Aconitases are iron–sulfur proteins that "catalyse the reversible isomerization of citrate and isocitrate via the intermediate cis-aconitate in the Krebs cycle" (Baothman et al., 2013, 23637460).

2.2 Catalytic cofactor and mechanism

Catalysis depends on a [4Fe-4S] cluster (UniProt cofactor annotation; keywords 4Fe-4S, Iron-sulfur). Three of the four irons are ligated by cysteine residues; the fourth iron (Fe4) is coordinatively unsaturated and directly binds the substrate's hydroxyl and a carboxylate, acting as a Lewis acid during the dehydration/rehydration. Crystallographic studies of the homologous mitochondrial aconitase established that "Fe4 of the [4Fe-4S] cluster is five-coordinate," and that the mechanistic model extends "to all three natural substrates of aconitase" — citrate, cis-aconitate, and isocitrate (Lauble et al., 1994, 8151704).

Residue-level evidence for an intact active site in AcnA-I: - The three cluster-ligating cysteines occur in the canonical single-Cys + Cys-X-X-Cys geometry: Cys454, Cys520, Cys523 in PP_2112. - These map one-to-one onto the curated E. coli AcnA ligands Cys435, Cys501, Cys504 (UniProt P25516), whose Fe-S cluster was characterized by EPR and magnetic-CD spectroscopy and shown to "strongly resemble those of the mammalian enzymes" (Jordan et al., 1999, 10585860). - The active-site stretch ITSCTNTSNP (surrounding the first ligand cysteine) is identical in PP_2112 (452–461) and E. coli AcnA (433–442), indicating full conservation of the catalytic pocket.

2.3 Substrate specificity

AcnA-I is expected to act on the canonical TCA tricarboxylates (citrate/cis-aconitate/isocitrate). In some AcnA enzymes the same active site can also hydrate 2-methyl-cis-aconitate to 2-methylisocitrate in the 2-methylcitrate (propionate) cycle (Horswill & Escalante-Semerena, 2001, 11294638). However, in P. putida KT2440 that propionate-catabolic role is delegated to a separate, dedicated gene cluster (see Section 4), so AcnA-I's physiological substrate is the standard TCA-cycle citrate/isocitrate pair.

3. Subcellular Localization

AcnA-I functions in the cytoplasm (cytosol). UniProt Q88L24 annotates only structural domains (aconitase large subunit, residues 76–581; AcnA/IPM swivel, 710–837) and contains no signal peptide and no transmembrane segment, ruling out periplasmic, membrane, or extracellular localization. This is consistent with the aconitase/IRP1 family being soluble cytosolic proteins — mammalian IRP1/cytosolic aconitase is "a well-established cytosolic protein" (Gu et al., 2022, 36142654). The TCA cycle and the enzyme's potential mRNA targets both reside in the same compartment.

acnA-I (PP_2112) sits alone on the chromosome, whereas acnA-II/prpF/acnB form a contiguous cluster associated with 2-methylcitrate / propionate catabolism. This mirrors the observation that acnA-type genes recur "in a number of putative prp operons" (Horswill & Escalante-Semerena, 2001, 11294638) — but importantly, PP_2112 is the copy that is not in such an operon, marking it as the general TCA-cycle housekeeping/maintenance aconitase.

Pathway: The reaction is step 2 of the TCA cycle (citrate → isocitrate), feeding isocitrate to isocitrate dehydrogenase and, at branch points, to the glyoxylate shunt. In P. putida, whose metabolism is organised around the TCA cycle and gluconeogenesis, this step is central to energy generation and to biosynthetic precursor supply (2-oxoglutarate, succinyl-CoA, oxaloacetate).

The ~2-fold identity gap between the AcnA clade (>54%) and the AcnB clade (~35%) unambiguously assigns PP_2112 to the AcnA subfamily. Because E. coli AcnA is biochemically well characterized, this orthology (not merely family membership) licenses transfer of its established properties to PP_2112.

Independent structural support: the AlphaFold model of AcnA-I (AF-Q88L24-F1) is very high confidence (mean pLDDT 92.4; 96.8% of residues >70, 80.4% >90), indicating the four-domain aconitase architecture (three large-subunit domains plus the AcnA/IPM swivel domain closing over the active-site cleft) is modeled reliably — consistent with a canonical aconitase fold competent for [4Fe-4S] binding and catalysis.

Properties of the AcnA archetype (transferable to AcnA-I): - A stable, aerobic stationary-phase enzyme, specifically induced by iron and oxidative stress, with higher affinity for citrate and a broad pH optimum — "consistent with its role as a maintenance or survival enzyme during nutritional or oxidative stress," in contrast to the catabolic, less stable AcnB (Jordan et al., 1999, 10585860). - In Salmonella, acnA expression is controlled by CRP (glucose starvation), FNR (oxygen starvation), Fur (iron starvation), and SoxR (oxidative stress) (Baothman et al., 2013, 23637460).

6. Secondary (Moonlighting) Function: Cluster-Dependent RNA Binding

AcnA-family proteins are bifunctional, resembling the vertebrate iron-regulatory proteins (IRP1) in "having alternative mRNA-binding and catalytic activities" (Tang & Guest, 1999, 10589714). When the labile [4Fe-4S] cluster is disassembled by oxidants or iron starvation, the apo-protein switches to a site-specific RNA-binding mode: - In E. coli, apo-AcnA/AcnB bind the 3′UTRs of acn mRNAs, and the apo-forms have >3-fold higher mRNA affinity than the holo-enzymes; "the apo-proteins function as post-transcriptional regulators by site-specific binding to acn mRNAs," with AcnA stabilizing sodA (superoxide dismutase) mRNA — coupling aconitase status to the oxidative-stress defence (Tang et al., 2002, 11932448). - The switch is cluster-dependent, exactly as for mammalian IRP1, which is "a bifunctional protein with mutually exclusive RNA-binding or enzymatic activities that depend on the presence of a 4Fe-4S cluster" (Gu et al., 2022, 36142654).

PP_2112 carries the UniProt RNA-binding keyword (GO:0003723) and the AcnA_IRP swivel domain (IPR044137), so this regulatory potential is structurally encoded. Direct experimental demonstration of RNA targets specifically for the P. putida protein has not been reported; this role is inferred from strong orthology and conserved domain architecture.

8. Limitations and Future Directions

  • Direct experimental data specific to PP_2112 are sparse. Targeted PubMed searches returned no primary P. putida AcnA-I characterization; conclusions rest on (i) curated database annotation, (ii) very high orthology to the experimentally characterized E. coli/Salmonella AcnA, and (iii) residue-level structural conservation. This is strong but indirect for the moonlighting role.
  • Future work: purify AcnA-I and measure kinetic constants (Km for citrate/isocitrate) and pH profile; test single/double aconitase knockouts (acnA-I vs acnA-II/acnB) for growth-phase, iron-, and oxidative-stress phenotypes; perform EPR/MCD on the cluster; and use RNA-immunoprecipitation/CLIP to identify apo-AcnA-I mRNA targets in P. putida.
