Gene Description

cheA encodes the chemotaxis histidine kinase CheA of Pseudomonas putida KT2440. The protein is a 747 aa phosphorelay kinase with an N-terminal HPt domain, a central histidine kinase ATPase/dimerization region, and a C-terminal CheW-like domain. In KT2440 literature this locus corresponds to PP_4338/cheA2, the CheA paralog assigned to the canonical chemotaxis pathway rather than the Wsp-like c-di-GMP signaling branch. Its core role is to relay chemoreceptor input by autophosphorylating on histidine and transferring phosphate to response regulators such as CheY and CheB, thereby controlling chemotaxis.

Research report: cheA2 (PP_4338; UniProt Q88EW4) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

Executive summary (identity verified)

The UniProt accession Q88EW4 corresponds to Chemotaxis protein CheA (EC 2.7.13.3) encoded by cheA with ordered locus name PP_4338 in Pseudomonas putida KT2440; this locus is explicitly identified as cheA2 in KT2440 genetic work, which also distinguishes cheA1 (pp1492) and cheA3 (pp4988) paralogs. (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 5-6, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 3-5)

In KT2440, CheA2 (PP_4338) functions as the central histidine kinase of the canonical chemotaxis signaling pathway, required for chemotaxis to maize root exudates in quantitative capillary assays. (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 10-11)

1) Key concepts, definitions, and current understanding

1.1 What CheA is (definition)

CheA is the dimeric histidine kinase at the core of bacterial chemotaxis pathways. It is activated/inhibited by chemoreceptor signaling complexes and initiates a phosphorylation cascade by autophosphorylation and phosphotransfer to response regulators (CheY, CheB). (muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures pages 2-4, vass2023analysisofchew‐like pages 1-3)

1.2 Catalytic reaction and substrate specificity (for Q88EW4)

CheA catalyzes an ATP-dependent autophosphorylation on a conserved histidine in the P1 (Hpt/HPT) domain, driven by the P4 (ATP-binding catalytic) domain, and subsequently transfers the phosphoryl group to response regulators such as CheY (and typically CheB) as part of the chemotaxis two-component-like system. (muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures pages 2-4, vass2023analysisofchew‐like pages 1-3)

Direct biochemical evidence in P. putida shows purified CheA has strong [32P]ATPγP-dependent autophosphorylation and that addition of CheY yields CheY labeling with a reduction in CheA label, consistent with phosphate transfer from CheA to CheY. (he2025coordinatedregulationof pages 3-5)

1.3 Domain architecture (and mapping to UniProt domains)

A canonical CheA comprises five domains (P1–P5):
- P1 (Hpt/HPT): phosphoacceptor histidine-bearing domain
- P2: docking/binding of response regulators (CheY/CheB)
- P3: dimerization domain
- P4 (HATPase_c): ATP-binding/catalytic domain that phosphorylates P1
- P5 (CheW-like): coupling to receptors and CheW for array assembly

These functions and the five-domain model are summarized in authoritative recent reviews and are consistent with UniProt’s CheA-like/CheW-like domain annotations provided in the prompt. (muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures pages 2-4, he2025coordinatedregulationof pages 1-2)

1.4 Pathway role and cellular localization (current understanding)

CheA functions within chemosensory arrays (also called receptor arrays) made of chemoreceptors plus coupling proteins (CheW) and CheA. The core signaling unit (CSU) is commonly defined as one CheA dimer + two CheW + two receptor trimers-of-dimers, which then assemble into larger hexagonal lattices. (muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures pages 2-4)

Although the retrieved KT2440-specific texts did not directly image CheA2 localization, the conserved mechanism implies that KT2440 CheA2 operates at these membrane-proximal/polar chemosensory arrays to integrate receptor inputs and control downstream phosphorylation. (muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures pages 2-4)

2) KT2440-specific functional evidence for cheA2 (PP_4338; Q88EW4)

2.1 KT2440 has three cheA paralogs with distinct roles

In P. putida KT2440, three cheA paralogs are present (cheA1, cheA2, cheA3). Functional analyses support division of labor:
- cheA2 (PP_4338) is required for chemotaxis to maize root exudates (MRE)
- cheA1 aligns with a wsp-like pathway that modulates c-di-GMP/biofilm traits
- cheA3 showed no defect in the specific MRE chemotaxis assay and was described as similar to wild type under those conditions, with broader role unresolved
(lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 10-11)

The key identity mapping—PP_4338 = cheA2—is explicitly stated in the KT2440 mutant construction methods and strain descriptions (including pp4338::km). (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 5-6, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 3-5)

2.2 Mutant phenotypes directly implicate CheA2 in chemotaxis

In KT2440, a cheA2 mutant was reported to be entirely deficient in MRE chemotaxis, supporting CheA2 as the primary kinase in the chemotaxis (che) pathway for this cue. (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 10-11)

In the same study, quantitative capillary assays provided CFU counts in the chemotaxis readout (means ± SD; replicated), demonstrating a measurable chemotactic response to MRE, though many effect sizes for genotype comparisons were presented in figures rather than fully tabulated in text. (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 11-12)

2.3 Genomic/operon context in KT2440

Transcriptional mapping in a KT2440 motility/chemotaxis region placed cheA in a cheYZA operon, i.e., transcriptional linkage with cheY and cheZ in at least one chemotaxis-associated operon context. This supports a functional module organization typical of chemotaxis systems. (navarro‐aviles2010physiologicalandtranscriptomic pages 1-2)

3) Regulation and cross-talk: recent mechanistic insights from P. putida

A 2025 study identified multiple CheA-interacting proteins and showed that at least two regulators, CsoR (a copper-binding repressor) and PhaD, can inhibit CheA autophosphorylation in vitro in a dose-dependent manner, without acting as ATPases, indicating direct modulation of CheA catalytic activity rather than ATP depletion. (he2025coordinatedregulationof pages 3-5, he2025coordinatedregulationof pages 5-8)

The study also reports a CheA-dependent chemorepellent response to copper and quantifies this behavior using response indices and tracking-based metrics; for example, perturbation of CsoR expression altered a response index (RI) from 0.373 ± 0.021 (vector control) to 0.428 ± 0.015 (pcsoR) and changed center-of-mass displacement (e.g., 21.77 ± 3.60 μm vs 13.85 ± 1.92 μm), illustrating how CheA activity integrates environmental stress sensing into motility behavior. (he2025coordinatedregulationof pages 11-13)

4) Recent developments (2023–2024) relevant to CheA2 annotation

4.1 Structural biology of arrays and CSUs (2023–2024)

Recent authoritative work emphasizes that mechanistic understanding of CheA regulation requires treating CheA as part of a supramolecular array, not an isolated kinase.
- A 2024 Annual Review synthesizes evidence that the CSU architecture (CheA dimer, CheW, and receptor trimers-of-dimers) is the fundamental signaling unit and highlights alternative/expanded architectures across bacteria. (muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures pages 2-4)
- 2023 studies further advanced CSU structural models of native arrays and interactions among CheA domains within these assemblies, helping explain long-range coupling between ligand binding and CheA kinase activity. (tran2023bacterialchemoreceptorsignaling pages 12-15)

These developments support annotation of KT2440 CheA2 as a multi-domain kinase whose activity is controlled by array assembly and receptor state, and whose localization is expected to be array-associated. (muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures pages 2-4, tran2023bacterialchemoreceptorsignaling pages 12-15)

4.2 Diversity of CheA architectures (2023–2024)

Comparative analyses show that although CheA is often described as five-domain, noncanonical architectures (e.g., loss of P2, acquisition of receiver domains, split proteins) are widespread across bacteria, which is important when interpreting paralogs (like cheA1–3) and predicting functional specialization. (berry2023diversityandevolution pages 34-41)

5) Current applications and real-world implementations

5.1 Plant-root colonization (KT2440)

Chemotaxis toward root exudates is a key determinant of rhizosphere performance. In KT2440, signaling through both a chemotaxis-associated pathway (involving cheA2) and a wsp-like pathway (involving cheA1) was reported as important for efficient maize root colonization, indicating that CheA paralogs contribute to ecologically relevant host-association outcomes. (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 10-11)

5.2 Bioremediation and pollutant degradation (P. putida strain example)

A soil microcosm study on a different P. putida strain (DLL-1) compared mutants and found that a cheA non-chemotaxis mutant (DAK) lost directed chemotaxis and showed reduced in-situ degradation performance for the hydrophobic pesticide methyl parathion (MP) relative to wild type, despite similar degradation in well-mixed liquid. For example, the cheA mutant degraded ~64.3–85.7% of MP in soil microcosms (reported 5-day endpoints), while both wild type and cheA mutant could degrade MP to nondetectable levels in ~18 h in shaken liquid medium, supporting the practical principle that chemotaxis enhances contact with poorly soluble substrates in heterogeneous environments. (guo2009comparisonofinsitu pages 1-2, guo2009comparisonofinsitu pages 4-5)

7) Expert synthesis for functional annotation of Q88EW4

Primary molecular function (recommended GO-style statement)

CheA2 (PP_4338; Q88EW4) is an ATP-dependent protein histidine kinase that autophosphorylates and transfers phosphoryl groups to chemotaxis response regulators (e.g., CheY), thereby controlling chemotactic motility in P. putida KT2440; genetically, CheA2 is essential for chemotaxis toward maize root exudates. (he2025coordinatedregulationof pages 3-5, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2)

Biological process/pathway

Chemotaxis signaling pathway (chemosensory array → CheA2 → CheY/CheB → flagellar motor/adaptation), contributing to environmental navigation and plant-root colonization. (muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures pages 2-4, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2)

Cellular component/localization

CheA2 is expected to localize to chemosensory receptor arrays/core signaling units (often polar, membrane-associated supramolecular assemblies) through interactions mediated by its P5/CheW-like region and CheW; this is strongly supported for bacterial chemotaxis systems generally, but direct imaging for KT2440 CheA2 was not captured in the retrieved KT2440-specific sources. (muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures pages 2-4, muok2024unpackingalternativefeatures media cd438e27)

Notes on paralogy (to avoid annotation confusion)

KT2440 encodes three CheA paralogs. Functional attribution for PP_4338/Q88EW4 should be limited to cheA2 (chemotaxis-associated), not cheA1 (wsp-like, c-di-GMP/biofilm) or cheA3 (unclear/condition-dependent). cheA Notes

  • Q88EW4 is the KT2440 chemotaxis histidine kinase locus PP_4338; the KT2440
    literature calls this paralog cheA2 [UniProt:Q88EW4 "OrderedLocusNames=PP_4338"]
    [DOI:10.3389/fmicb.2019.00078 "PP_4338 (cheA2)"].
  • UniProt gives the canonical CheA mechanism: transmission of receptor input to
    the flagellar pathway through autophosphorylation and phosphotransfer to CheB
    or CheY [UniProt:Q88EW4 "CheA is autophosphorylated; it can transfer its phosphate group to either CheB or CheY."].
  • The conserved phosphohistidine is His49 in the HPt domain, consistent with a
    phosphorelay histidine kinase [UniProt:Q88EW4 "MOD_RES 49 /note=\"Phosphohistidine\""]
    [UniProt:Q88EW4 "DOMAIN 2..106 /note=\"HPt\""].
  • The strongest KT2440 phenotype is in chemotaxis rather than generic signaling:
    Lopez-Farfan et al. report that a cheA2 mutant was entirely deficient in MRE chemotaxis [DOI:10.3389/fmicb.2019.00078 "a cheA2 mutant was entirely deficient in MRE chemotaxis"].
  • The same paper separates paralog function: cheA1 affected c-di-GMP and
    biofilm formation, whereas cheA2 mapped to the classical chemotaxis pathway
    [DOI:10.3389/fmicb.2019.00078 "a mutant in cheA1 showed reduced c-di-GMP levels and impaired biofilm formation"]
    [DOI:10.3389/fmicb.2019.00078 "indicating the existence of homologs of the P. aeruginosa wsp and che (chemotaxis) pathways"].
  • Annotation consequences:
    keep protein histidine kinase activity, phosphorelay signal transduction system, and chemotaxis; demote or remove broader parent terms like
    phosphorelay sensor kinase activity, transferase activity, transferring phosphorus-containing groups, and signal transduction.
