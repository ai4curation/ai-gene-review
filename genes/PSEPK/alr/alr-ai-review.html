Gene Description

alr encodes a broad-specificity periplasmic amino-acid racemase in P. putida KT2440. Although annotated as alanine racemase by family inference, experimental work shows the enzyme has much higher catalytic efficiency for lysine and arginine than alanine and supports a primary role in L-lysine and L-arginine catabolism through conversion to the corresponding D-enantiomers.

    > In order to detect gene and protein names which are assigned to products of different genes and thus result in erroneous cross-references, dedicated lists are created for each organism separately. Organism-specific lists are necessary, since terms that are ambiguous in one organism might be explicit in another. For example, ADORA2 is an ambiguous gene name in Homo sapiens but not in mouse, and GALT in mouse but not in H.sapiens.
    > In a first step, ambiguous names within the databases were extracted. If a name occurs in at least two entries describing different genes or proteins (splice variants count as one gene/protein), this particular name is marked as ambiguous and is excluded from the mapping process. In a second step, corresponding gene names occurring in the manually annotated sections of RefSeq as well as in UniProt were analyzed. Entries containing the same gene product name and having a one-to-many or many-to-many relation (e.g. one Swiss-Prot entry maps to many RefSeq entries) were scrutinized for misleading annotation. In most of the cases, the exclusion of the ambiguous gene names resulted in correct one-to-one relations.
    > As statistical analysis revealed (Supplementary Material S2) that gene names with less than four letters are exceptionally error-prone, only gene names with at least four letters are kept for mapping purposes. Research Report: Functional Annotation of alr (PP_3722; UniProt Q88GJ9) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

1. Target verification (gene/protein identity)

The literature retrieved unambiguously links the locus PP_3722 in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 to alr, encoding a broad-spectrum amino-acid racemase (often termed BSAR) rather than the canonical alanine-specific housekeeping racemase. In the same genome, DadX (PP_5269) is the alanine-specialized racemase, and multiple experiments distinguish these two enzymes by substrate preference and physiological roles. (radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 1-2, radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 4-5, radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 7-8)

2. Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

2.1 Amino-acid racemases and D-amino acids

Amino-acid racemases catalyze reversible L↔D stereoinversion of amino acids. In bacteria, D-amino acids have canonical structural roles (e.g., in peptidoglycan) and noncanonical roles as secreted metabolites impacting physiology and microbial interactions. Reviews note that many bacteria release D-amino acids into their environment at millimolar concentrations, produced by racemases/epimerases/aminotransferases, and that D-amino acids can affect adhesion and biofilm dynamics. (caldwell2023promisingapplicationsof pages 6-7)

2.2 Broad-spectrum racemases (Bsr/BSAR) vs alanine-specific racemases

In P. putida KT2440, Alr/PP_3722 is experimentally supported as a broad-substrate racemase with strongest activity on basic amino acids (notably Lys; also Arg), whereas DadX/PP_5269 is alanine-specific (catabolic alanine racemase). This distinction is essential for functional annotation because the symbol alr is often used for alanine racemase in many organisms but in KT2440 PP_3722 functions primarily as a broader racemase. (radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 1-2, radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 4-5, radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 7-8)

2.3 Mechanistic context (PLP-dependent alanine-racemase family)

Although PP_3722 itself was not structurally solved in the retrieved corpus, its classification within the alanine racemase family implies a PLP-dependent mechanism. Recent high-resolution, room-temperature serial femtosecond crystallography of Bacillus subtilis alanine racemase captured multiple binding/reaction states and supports a mechanistic model in which catalytic bases and substrate positioning enable racemization; the study reports an apo structure at 2.3 Å resolution from 68,582 indexed images, with reaction-state datasets collected using ~200 mM L-Ala after mixing. These results provide updated mechanistic interpretation applicable to alanine-racemase-family enzymes such as PP_3722. (kim2024exploringthereaction pages 2-3, kim2024exploringthereaction pages 4-6, kim2024exploringthereaction pages 10-11)

3. Core function of P. putida KT2440 Alr (PP_3722)

3.1 Enzymatic reaction and substrate specificity (direct biochemical evidence)

Radkov & Moe (2013; Journal of Bacteriology, Nov 2013; https://doi.org/10.1128/JB.00761-13) report that Alr has the broadest substrate specificity among recovered KT2440 racemases, with measurable racemase activity on 9 of 19 chiral amino acids, and that its highest catalytic efficiency is on lysine. (radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 1-2, radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 4-5)

Quantitative kinetics (Table 2; 2013)

Key kinetic parameters (selected substrates) for Alr/PP_3722 include:
- Lysine (D→L): Km 0.36 ± 0.06 mM, kcat 274.5 ± 6.7 s⁻¹, kcat/Km ~7.63 × 10^5 M⁻¹·s⁻¹.
- Lysine (L→D): Km 8.96 ± 1.52 mM, kcat 1681.7 ± 161.7 s⁻¹, kcat/Km ~1.88 × 10^5 M⁻¹·s⁻¹.
- Alanine (D→L): Km 15.71 ± 2.79 mM, kcat 8.83 s⁻¹, kcat/Km ~5.62 × 10^2 M⁻¹·s⁻¹.
- Alanine (L→D): Km 12.62 ± 0.74 mM, kcat 7.33 s⁻¹, kcat/Km ~5.81 × 10^2 M⁻¹·s⁻¹.
Thus, Alr’s catalytic efficiency on lysine is approximately three orders of magnitude higher than on alanine. (radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 4-5)

In contrast, DadX/PP_5269 shows substantially higher catalytic efficiency than Alr on alanine, supporting that DadX is the principal alanine racemase function in KT2440:
- DadX on alanine (D→L): kcat/Km ~4.85 × 10^3 M⁻¹·s⁻¹.
- DadX on alanine (L→D): kcat/Km ~3.74 × 10^3 M⁻¹·s⁻¹.
(radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 4-5, radkov2013aminoacidracemization pages 7-8)

3.2 Subcellular localization (where the enzyme functions)

Radkov & Moe (2018; Frontiers in Microbiology, Jun 2018; https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2018.01343) experimentally localized Alr activity predominantly to the periplasmic fraction, consistent with a predicted targeting sequence. In stationary phase, Alr specific activity was 134.5 ± 29.8 nmol·min⁻¹·mg⁻¹ in the periplasmic fraction versus 209.7 ± 8.2 nmol·min⁻¹·mg⁻¹ in the total fraction (periplasmic ~64% of total). Marker enzymes supported fraction integrity: alkaline phosphatase retained 72–77% of total periplasmic activity, while cytosolic G6PDH contamination in the periplasmic fraction was only 13.3–16.7%. (radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 3-4)

4. Physiological role in P. putida KT2440 (pathways, processes, and evidence)

4.1 Primary biological role: lysine and arginine catabolism

Multiple lines of evidence indicate PP_3722’s primary in vivo role is amino-acid catabolism, especially L-lysine and L-arginine, via racemization to D-forms.

In vivo metabolite evidence (D-Lys accumulation)

In LB medium, wild-type KT2440 accumulated D-lysine in the extracellular milieu: <100 µM by 4 h and approximately ~1 mM by 8 h. A markerless Δalr mutant produced no detectable D-Lys, while showing increased accumulation of extracellular L-Lys (reported as rising from ~1.5 mM to ~2.5 mM by 10 h), supporting that Alr converts L-Lys to D-Lys in vivo and increases overall lysine consumption. (radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 4-6, radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 3-4)

Growth phenotypes (catabolism as sole C/N)

The Δalr strain showed a diminished capacity to grow when L-Lys or L-Arg was provided as the sole carbon and nitrogen source, consistent with Alr being required to efficiently catabolize these L-amino acids (via conversion to their D-enantiomers). (radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 8-9, radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 6-7)

Pathway hypothesis (compartment-aware model)

Radkov & Moe propose that L-Lys is exported/accessible in the periplasm, racemized by periplasmic Alr to D-Lys, and then further catabolized intracellularly via pathways including the L-pipecolate route; they also conclude that Alr supports L-Arg catabolism, although the downstream D-Arg catabolic route in KT2440 remained unclear at the time. (radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 8-9, radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 1-2)

4.2 Transcriptomics: regulation is focused on amino-acid metabolism (not broad signaling)

RNA-seq comparing wild type vs Δalr indicated that differential expression was limited primarily to amino-acid metabolism genes, consistent with a metabolic (catabolic) function rather than broad signaling effects from secreted D-amino acids. Among upregulated genes in wild type under D-Lys-producing conditions were D-Lys catabolism-associated genes (e.g., PP3596, PP3591, PP5257/PP5258) and a periplasmic amino-acid transporter PP3593 reported with fold-change 27; PP_3722 itself was among the highly differentially expressed genes (reported ~100-fold RPKM difference among overexpressed genes). (radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 4-6, radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 2-3)

4.3 Peptidoglycan: no detectable structural incorporation under tested conditions

Despite being a racemase-family enzyme, PP_3722 does not appear to be a major contributor to peptidoglycan remodeling under the tested conditions in KT2440. Stationary-phase peptidoglycan composition was reported as not significantly different between wild type and Δalr (p > 0.05), and authors state Alr products were not incorporated into peptidoglycan under these conditions. (radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 4-6, radkov2018abroadspectrum pages 8-9)

4.4 Fitness/functional genomics evidence (supporting but secondary)

A BarSeq/RB-TnSeq-derived phenotype summary reports a modest fitness defect for PP_3722 of approximately −1.0 in an arginine-related condition, consistent with a conditional contribution to arginine utilization; it also supports that DadX is more central for alanine metabolism than Alr. (schmidt2023exploitingthepotential pages 73-76)
