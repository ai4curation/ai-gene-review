MurF (Q88N80, PP_1333) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440: The D-Alanyl-D-Alanine-Adding Ligase Completing the Cytoplasmic Peptidoglycan Precursor

Gene: murF (OrderedLocusName PP_1333) · UniProt: Q88N80 · Organism: Pseudomonas putida KT2440 (PSEPK) · EC: 6.3.2.10

Summary

murF (UniProt Q88N80; ordered locus name PP_1333) of Pseudomonas putida KT2440 encodes MurF, the ATP-dependent UDP-N-acetylmuramoyl-tripeptide : D-alanyl-D-alanine ligase (EC 6.3.2.10), also called the D-alanyl-D-alanine-adding enzyme. MurF catalyzes the final cytoplasmic step of peptidoglycan (murein) precursor biosynthesis: it condenses the preformed dipeptide D-Ala-D-Ala onto the terminal carboxylate of the UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide (UDP-N-acetylmuramoyl-L-Ala-γ-D-Glu-meso-diaminopimelate) to produce UDP-MurNAc-pentapeptide ("Park's nucleotide"), with concomitant hydrolysis of ATP to ADP and inorganic phosphate. This is the last soluble, cytoplasmic reaction before the nucleotide precursor is handed off to the membrane-associated translocase MraY and the rest of the lipid-linked pathway.

The identity of the target is well supported. The UniProt description, the MurCDEF/MurF-subfamily assignment, and the diagnostic InterPro Mur-ligase domains (Mur_ligase_N IPR000713, Mur_ligase_cen IPR013221, Mur_ligase_C IPR004101) all match a canonical MurF ligase. Although no biochemical study has yet been published on the P. putida KT2440 enzyme itself, its closest characterized ortholog — MurF of Pseudomonas aeruginosa (PaMurF) — has been crystallized and enzymatically characterized, and both organisms are γ-proteobacteria that build meso-diaminopimelate (meso-DAP)-type peptidoglycan. Deep mechanistic and structural knowledge from P. aeruginosa and Escherichia coli orthologs therefore transfers with high confidence to PP_1333, and this report draws on that body of work while flagging where evidence is by homology rather than direct measurement.

The core conclusions are: (1) MurF performs a defined ATP-dependent amide-bond-forming ligation via an acyl-phosphate intermediate and an ordered kinetic mechanism, placing it in the Mur/folyl-poly-γ-glutamate ligase superfamily; (2) it is a soluble cytoplasmic three-domain enzyme that undergoes a large open-to-closed conformational change upon substrate binding; (3) it is essential and bacteria-specific with no human homolog, making it a validated antibacterial drug target; and (4) it is permissive at the incoming dipeptide (accepting D-Ala-D-Lac depsipeptides and fluorinated analogs) while remaining specific for the UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide acceptor. The D-Ala-D-Ala terminus that MurF installs is the eventual substrate for transpeptidase cross-linking and is the molecular target of glycopeptide antibiotics such as vancomycin.

Key Findings

Finding 1 — MurF is the ATP-dependent D-Ala-D-Ala-adding ligase catalyzing the final cytoplasmic step of peptidoglycan precursor synthesis

MurF catalyzes the reaction:

UDP-MurNAc-L-Ala-γ-D-Glu-meso-DAP  +  D-Ala-D-Ala  +  ATP
        │
        ▼   (MurF, EC 6.3.2.10)
UDP-MurNAc-L-Ala-γ-D-Glu-meso-DAP-D-Ala-D-Ala  +  ADP  +  Pi
        (UDP-MurNAc-pentapeptide = "Park's nucleotide")

This is the last cytoplasmic step of the nucleotide-precursor (Mur pathway) branch of peptidoglycan synthesis. The enzyme forms a peptide (amide) bond between the free amino group of the D-Ala-D-Ala dipeptide and the C-terminal carboxyl group of the meso-DAP (third) residue of the UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide, using the energy of ATP hydrolysis to activate the acceptor carboxylate. For the P. aeruginosa ortholog — the closest characterized relative to the P. putida enzyme — the substrate is explicitly UDP-MurNAc-Ala-Glu-meso-A2pm (PMID: 19099588): "MurF catalyzes the formation of a peptide bond between D-Alanyl-D-Alanine (D-Ala-D-Ala) and the cell wall precursor uridine 5'-diphosphoryl N-acetylmuramoyl-L-alanyl-D-glutamyl-meso-diaminopimelic acid ... with the concomitant hydrolysis of ATP to ADP and inorganic phosphate, yielding UDP-N-acetylmuramyl-pentapeptide." The presence of meso-DAP as the third stem residue is characteristic of Pseudomonas and most Gram-negative bacteria, and confirms that the P. putida enzyme operates on the DAP-type precursor.

Quantitative biochemistry of the purified E. coli enzyme establishes the catalytic parameters: MurF is a ~49 kDa monomer with a turnover number of 784 min⁻¹ for ATP-driven amide bond formation (PMID: 2186811): "The enzyme is a monomer of molecular weight 49,000, and it has a turnover number of 784 min-1 for ATP-driven amide bond formation." That MurF represents the last step in the cytoplasmic phase of peptidoglycan biosynthesis is directly stated for the P. aeruginosa ortholog (PMID: 23695563): "The ATP-dependent UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide:D-Ala-D-Ala ligase MurF catalyses the last step in the cytoplasmic phase of peptidoglycan biosynthesis."

Finding 2 — Mechanism: ordered kinetics and an acyl-phosphate intermediate within the Mur/folyl-polyglutamate ligase superfamily

MurF belongs to the MurCDEF amide-ligase family, which shares four conserved sequence motifs with the folyl-poly-γ-glutamate ligases, together forming a defined ligase superfamily. Both families operate by the same chemical logic: ATP first phosphorylates the C-terminal carboxylate of the nucleotide substrate (the meso-DAP carboxylate in the case of MurF), generating a reactive acyl-phosphate intermediate; the amino group of the incoming nucleophile (D-Ala-D-Ala for MurF) then attacks this activated carbonyl, displacing phosphate and forming the new amide bond. The reaction follows an ordered kinetic mechanism. This was established through comparative sequence and mutational analysis (PMID: 9166795): "both enzyme families proceed through an ordered kinetic mechanism and form product via an acyl phosphate intermediate."

The same study validated the catalytic importance of conserved residues by site-directed mutagenesis. Mutation of conserved E. coli MurF residues (E158 and H188) reduced specific activity by 200- to 4500-fold (PMID: 9166795): "All mutations were highly detrimental to activity with enzyme specific activity reductions of 200-4500-fold, validating the critical nature of these residues." Consistent with this, the temperature-sensitive murF2 (A288T) allele was 181-fold less active, linking the biochemical lesion to the physiological cell-shape/lysis phenotype of murF mutants. These conserved residues are expected to be present in the P. putida ortholog given the family-wide conservation.

Finding 3 — Three-domain architecture with a large open-to-closed conformational change

The crystal structure of E. coli MurF (2.3 Å) reveals three consecutive open α/β-sheet domains (PMID: 11090285): "It contains three consecutive open alpha/beta-sheet domains." These comprise (i) a topologically unique N-terminal domain, (ii) a central mononucleotide-binding fold, and (iii) a C-terminal dinucleotide-binding (Rossmann-like) fold — the classic Mur-ligase architecture corresponding to the InterPro domains carried by Q88N80 (Mur_ligase_N IPR000713, Mur_ligase_cen IPR013221, Mur_ligase_C IPR004101).

Critically, the apo enzyme is catalytically incompetent: it adopts an open, crescent-like conformation with a wide substrate-binding cleft in which the substrates cannot be brought into productive alignment (PMID: 11090285): "The apo-enzyme of MurF crystal structure reveals an open conformation with the three domains juxtaposed in a crescent-like arrangement creating a wide-open space where substrates are expected to bind. As such, catalysis is not feasible and significant domain closure is expected upon substrate binding." This induced-fit domain closure is a hallmark of the Mur ligase family and a key consideration for structure-based inhibitor design.

Direct structural evidence in the Pseudomonas lineage comes from PaMurF, which was crystallized in complex with its UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide substrate (1.92 Å, space group P2₁2₁2₁), capturing the substrate-liganded form for the first time (PMID: 23695563): "allowing study of the enzyme in the substrate-liganded form for the first time." Because PaMurF is the nearest characterized ortholog of PP_1333, this substrate-bound structure is the best available structural proxy for the P. putida enzyme.

Finding 4 — Essential, cytoplasmic, bacteria-specific enzyme and validated antibacterial target

MurF is essential for bacterial viability and is confined to eubacteria with no human homolog, making it an attractive target for new antibacterials. The enzyme functions in the cytoplasm (intracellular stage of peptidoglycan synthesis). Its essentiality and drug-target status are stated explicitly for P. aeruginosa (PMID: 19099588): "we targeted the essential MurF enzyme of the antibiotic resistant pathogen Pseudomonas aeruginosa" — and generally for MurF (PMID: 23878932): "MurF is an essential bacterial enzyme that is involved in the last intracellular stage of peptidoglycan biosynthesis, and therefore it has the potential to be exploited as a target for the development of new antibacterials."

The target has been experimentally engaged: a phage-display-derived peptide inhibitor (MurFp1) was raised against P. aeruginosa MurF (IC₅₀ ≈ 250 µM, Ki ≈ 420 µM), acting by interfering with utilization of the D-Ala-D-Ala substrate. Multiple additional inhibitor classes have been developed against the MurC–F ligases, including azastilbenes (PMID: 33744441), flavonoids (quercitrin, myricetin, (-)-epicatechin) (PMID: 37796382), and virtual-screening hits against S. pneumoniae and E. coli MurF (PMID: 23878932).

Genomically, murF sits within the division-and-cell-wall (dcw) gene cluster that co-locates peptidoglycan-synthesis and cell-division genes; in E. coli, murE and murF may be translationally coupled (PMID: 2681153): "the murE and murF genes, encoding the meso-diaminopimelic acid and D-alanyl-D-alanine-adding enzymes, respectively, may be translationally coupled." This organization couples MurF expression to that of the upstream ligases, ensuring stoichiometric precursor flux.

Finding 5 — Relaxed specificity at the dipeptide: incorporation of D-Ala-D-Lac and fluorinated analogs

MurF is permissive at the incoming dipeptide while remaining specific for the UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide acceptor. In Lactobacillus plantarum, the depsipeptide D-alanyl-D-lactate (D-Ala-D-Lac) produced by the Ddl ligase is added to the precursor by MurF, generating a D-Lac-terminated pentapeptide that confers intrinsic vancomycin resistance (vancomycin binds D-Ala-D-Ala but not D-Ala-D-Lac) (PMID: 17400741): "The ligase Ddl of L. plantarum plays a central role in this specificity by synthesizing D-alanyl-D-lactate depsipeptides that are added to the precursor peptide chain by the enzyme MurF." Similarly, E. coli MurF incorporates fluorinated dipeptides (D-trifluoroAla-D-trifluoroAla) synthesized by the upstream Ddl ligase (PMID: 2186811): "which the D-Ala-D-Ala-adding enzyme can then incorporate to form UDP-N-acetylmuramyl-L-Ala-gamma-D-Glu-meso-2,6-diaminopimelyl-D-trifluoroAla-D-trifluoroAla." This tolerance has two implications: mechanistically, it shows the dipeptide-binding subsite accepts ester/depsipeptide and halogenated substitutions; and physiologically, the substrate specificity that determines the D-Ala-D-Ala vs. D-Ala-D-Lac terminus is set largely by the upstream Ddl ligase rather than by MurF itself.

Chemical mechanism (single active site, sequential/ordered):

Step 1 (activation):   R–COO⁻  +  ATP  →  R–CO–OPO₃²⁻ (acyl-phosphate)  +  ADP
                       (R–COO⁻ = meso-DAP terminal carboxylate of UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide)

Step 2 (ligation):     R–CO–OPO₃²⁻  +  H₂N–(D-Ala-D-Ala)  →  R–CO–NH–(D-Ala-D-Ala)  +  Pi
                       (amide-bond formation, releasing inorganic phosphate)

Catalysis requires the enzyme to transition from its open, incompetent apo conformation to a closed conformation that clamps the three domains around the nucleotide substrate, the dipeptide, and Mg·ATP, aligning the acyl-phosphate with the attacking amine. Conserved residues such as E158 and H188 (E. coli numbering) participate in this chemistry; their mutation collapses activity by orders of magnitude.

Two specificity principles emerge. First, MurF is highly specific for the nucleotide-sugar acceptor (the UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide with its meso-DAP third residue) — this is the recognition element that keeps the enzyme committed to peptidoglycan synthesis. Second, MurF is comparatively relaxed toward the dipeptide donor, incorporating D-Ala-D-Lac and fluorinated dipeptides. The physiological consequence is that the choice between a vancomycin-sensitive (D-Ala-D-Ala) and vancomycin-resistant (D-Ala-D-Lac) precursor is dictated upstream by the Ddl ligase and by dipeptidases (e.g., Aad/VanX-like enzymes), not by MurF. In P. putida KT2440, which uses standard D-Ala-D-Ala chemistry, MurF supplies the canonical pentapeptide whose terminal D-Ala-D-Ala is subsequently consumed by penicillin-binding-protein transpeptidases during wall cross-linking.

Localization: MurF is a soluble cytoplasmic protein. It has no signal peptide or transmembrane segment, and its product (Park's nucleotide) is the last water-soluble intermediate before MraY tethers the muramyl-pentapeptide to the membrane carrier undecaprenyl phosphate. Its ~49 kDa monomeric character and three-domain α/β fold are consistent with a freely diffusing cytoplasmic enzyme.

Limitations and Knowledge Gaps

  1. No direct experimental data on PP_1333. Every mechanistic, kinetic, and structural claim is transferred by orthology, chiefly from P. aeruginosa (structural proxy) and E. coli (enzymology). While the conservation of this essential housekeeping enzyme across γ-proteobacteria makes this inference strong, the specific kinetic constants (Km, kcat), optimal Mg²⁺/pH conditions, and exact catalytic residues of the P. putida enzyme have not been measured.
  2. Essentiality inferred, not demonstrated in P. putida. MurF is essential in E. coli, P. aeruginosa, and S. pneumoniae; the same is expected in P. putida KT2440, but no conditional-knockout or transposon-essentiality confirmation specific to PP_1333 is cited here.
  3. Structural model is an ortholog, not the target. The best structure is PaMurF (similar but not identical sequence). A P. putida structure or an AlphaFold model validated against PaMurF would refine active-site details.
  4. Catalytic-residue numbering is from E. coli. The E158/H188 assignments require sequence alignment to map onto Q88N80; the equivalent residues in PP_1333 were not explicitly identified in this investigation.
  5. Regulation and interactions. Whether P. putida MurF is translationally coupled to murE (as in E. coli), and whether it participates in any cytoplasmic precursor-channeling complex with other Mur enzymes, is unverified for this organism.

Align Q88N80 against E. coli MurF and PaMurF to (i) confirm conservation of the catalytic E158/H188-equivalent residues and the four Mur/folyl-polyglutamate ligase motifs, and (ii) generate/validate an AlphaFold model against the PaMurF substrate-bound structure (PMID: 23695563).
  2. Recombinant enzymology. Clone, express, and purify PP_1333; measure Km for ATP, D-Ala-D-Ala, and UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide, plus kcat, using a coupled phosphate-release or radiochemical assay — benchmarking against the E. coli kcat of 784 min⁻¹ (PMID: 2186811).
  3. Test dipeptide permissiveness in P. putida. Assay incorporation of D-Ala-D-Lac and D-Ala-D-Ser analogs to determine whether PP_1333 shares the relaxed donor specificity seen in L. plantarum MurF (PMID: 17400741) — relevant to intrinsic glycopeptide susceptibility.
  4. Genetic essentiality. Construct a conditional (e.g., arabinose- or IPTG-dependent) murF allele in KT2440 to formally demonstrate essentiality and characterize the depletion phenotype (cell shape, lysis).
  5. Structure determination. Crystallize or cryo-EM the P. putida enzyme with substrate/ADP to capture the closed, catalytically competent conformation directly for this organism.
  6. Inhibitor cross-testing. Evaluate reported Mur ligase inhibitors (azastilbenes, flavonoids, MurFp1-type peptides) against purified PP_1333 to assess conservation of druggable pockets across Pseudomonas species (PMID: 19099588, 37796382, 33744441).

Conclusion

murF / PP_1333 / Q88N80 in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 encodes MurF, the essential, cytoplasmic, ATP-dependent UDP-N-acetylmuramoyl-tripeptide : D-alanyl-D-alanine ligase (EC 6.3.2.10). It catalyzes the final soluble step of peptidoglycan-precursor biosynthesis, ligating D-Ala-D-Ala onto UDP-MurNAc-L-Ala-γ-D-Glu-meso-DAP to make UDP-MurNAc-pentapeptide, using an acyl-phosphate intermediate and an ordered kinetic mechanism in a three-domain fold that closes over its substrates. The enzyme is specific for the UDP-MurNAc-tripeptide acceptor but permissive at the dipeptide donor, is bacteria-specific with no human homolog, and is a validated antibacterial drug target. All conclusions rest on strong orthology to the well-characterized P. aeruginosa and E. coli enzymes; direct characterization of the P. putida protein remains the principal outstanding experimental need.
