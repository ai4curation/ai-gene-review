Gene Description

katA encodes the major monofunctional heme catalase of Pseudomonas putida KT2440. It is a soluble catalase-family enzyme that disproportionates hydrogen peroxide to water and oxygen and serves as a principal peroxide detoxification activity in the oxidative-stress network. katA is strongly induced by hydrogen peroxide and is regulated within the OxyR-dependent peroxide response, but its most specific core GO role is hydrogen peroxide catabolism rather than the broader stress-response terms.

Surprisingly, one of the more difficult challenges a biocurator can face is unambiguously linking the gene or genes discussed in a paper to a record in their MOD, particularly when the gene nomenclature is unclear or the research organism is not identified. Inclusion of sequence or database identifiers for each gene in the paper ensures accurate linking between the results presented in that paper and the particular gene or genes described therein, no matter how the name of a gene may change over time. Papers that lack this information may be impossible to curate. Nomenclature standards and collaborative efforts often strive to give orthologous genes the same symbol and name in multiple species. For example, MGI, under the auspices of the International Committee on Standardized Genetic Nomenclature for Mice (Maltais et al. 2002), and the HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee (HGNC, Bruford et al. 2008), works to co-ordinate nomenclature between mouse, human and rat genes. Research Report: katA (PP_0481; UniProt Q88QK9) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

0. Target verification (mandatory)

The target gene/protein is katA in Pseudomonas putida strain KT2440, corresponding to locus PP_0481 and encoding a catalase (EC 1.11.1.6), consistent with UniProt Q88QK9 and catalase-family membership. A KT2440-focused oxidative-stress review explicitly maps katA = PP_0481 and describes KatA as a monofunctional catalase (catalase family) that decomposes hydrogen peroxide. (kim2014oxidativestressresponse pages 2-4, kim2014oxidativestressresponse pages 5-6)

1. Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 What KatA is

KatA is a monofunctional (heme) catalase that detoxifies hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), a major reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated endogenously during aerobic metabolism and exogenously by environmental insults. In P. putida KT2440, katA (PP_0481) is one of four chromosomal catalase genes, and KatA is commonly discussed as one of the major catalase defenses in the KT2440 lineage. (kim2014oxidativestressresponse pages 2-4, kim2014oxidativestressresponse pages 5-6)

1.2 Reaction catalyzed and substrate specificity

Catalases catalyze the disproportionation of hydrogen peroxide:

2 H2O2 → 2 H2O + O2

This reaction is explicitly described in KT2440 oxidative-stress transcriptomics context, where H2O2 is reduced to water and oxygen by catalase activity. (bojanovic2017globaltranscriptionalresponses pages 10-11)

In functional terms, KatA’s substrate specificity is primarily H2O2 (and related peroxides in the broader catalase functional category), contributing to peroxide detoxification under oxidative stress. (kim2014oxidativestressresponse pages 5-6, bojanovic2017globaltranscriptionalresponses pages 10-11)

1.3 Role in oxidative-stress defense networks

In P. putida, peroxide detoxification is organized into a network of enzymes and regulators. A central regulator is OxyR, a peroxide-responsive transcription factor that controls major peroxide defense genes including catalases (KatA/KatB) and the peroxiredoxin system (AhpC). In the KT2440 lineage, catalases are highlighted as particularly important for handling high peroxide loads, because unlike peroxiredoxins (e.g., AhpC), catalases do not require NADH/NADPH-dependent recycling to sustain activity at high flux. (hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 1-2, kim2014oxidativestressresponse pages 5-6)

2. Core functional annotation for katA (PP_0481)

2.1 Molecular/biochemical function (direct experimental evidence)

A foundational biochemical study in the KT2440 lineage (KT2442 derivative) purified and characterized KatA, linking the purified enzyme to the katA open reading frame. KatA properties reported include:
- ~55 kDa subunit size and ~220 kDa native mass, consistent with a tetrameric catalase.
- High specific activity (~1.0 × 10^3 U·mg−1); reported to be ~30-fold higher than KatB in that study.
These data support KatA as a high-capacity peroxide-degrading enzyme. (hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 2-4)

2.2 Regulation: OxyR control of katA

In the same KT2440-lineage study, OxyR directly regulates katA:
- Sequences resembling OxyR binding sites were identified upstream of katA, and purified OxyR binds promoter regions (reduced and oxidized forms bind DNA; oxidized OxyR is required for transcriptional activation through interaction with RNA polymerase). (hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 6-7, hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 1-2)
- An oxyR1 mutant showed increased katA transcription and strong accumulation of catalase activity. (hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 1-2, hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 6-7)

2.3 Relationship to RpoS and growth phase

Within the KT2440 lineage, catalase defenses show growth-phase dependence. KatA is described as present across growth phases (housekeeping/constitutive baseline with inducibility), whereas KatB is more clearly described as RpoS-dependent and stationary-phase linked; in the OxyR-focused study KatA accumulated throughout growth while KatB accumulated in stationary phase and is described as RpoS-dependent in that context. (hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 1-2, hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 2-2, kim2014oxidativestressresponse pages 5-6)

2.4 Subcellular localization

No direct localization experiment for KT2440 PP_0481 KatA (e.g., fractionation/microscopy) was identified in the retrieved KT2440/KT2442-focused evidence. The available sources treat KatA as a canonical intracellular peroxide-defense enzyme but do not provide explicit localization measurements. This is therefore best considered inferred rather than experimentally localized in the current evidence set. (kim2014oxidativestressresponse pages 2-4, hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 2-4)

3. Quantitative evidence and statistics (recent studies prioritized where available)

3.1 KT2440 transcriptomics: katA is among the most strongly induced H2O2-response genes

A KT2440 transcriptome study of oxidative stress reported very strong early induction of katA (PP_0481) after H2O2 exposure:
- ~936.5-fold upregulation at 7 minutes
- ~7.7-fold upregulation at 60 minutes
- Also ~3.4-fold upregulation under osmotic (NaCl) stress at 60 minutes
This supports katA as part of a rapid oxidative-stress transcriptional response program. (bojanovic2017globaltranscriptionalresponses pages 10-11, bojanovic2017globaltranscriptionalresponses media 06c5154d)

3.2 KT2442 enzymology and OxyR mutant phenotypes quantify catalase capacity

In the OxyR regulatory study, catalase activity was quantified:
- Wild type: catalase activity did not exceed ~30 U·mg−1 protein (early stationary) and declined thereafter.
- oxyR1 mutant: catalase-specific activity was ≥150 U·mg−1 in logarithmic growth and rose to >800 U·mg−1 after 1 day, i.e., ≥10-fold higher than wild type.
This quantitatively links OxyR state to peroxide-defense capacity and indicates how regulatory changes can markedly increase catalase buffering. (hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 2-2, hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 1-2)

3.3 Systems biology (2021): oxidative stress triggers NADPH-supplying flux rewiring (KT2440)

A systems-level KT2440 study (13C tracing, metabolomics, flux analysis) showed that under a sub-lethal H2O2 dose, central metabolism is rapidly reconfigured to generate reducing power for redox defense. Notably, NADPH formation exceeded biosynthetic demands by ~50%, producing an NADPH surplus that fuels peroxide reduction via glutathione-dependent systems. While not katA-specific, this provides a mechanistic context for why P. putida can tolerate oxidative stress and supports the broader redox-defense framework in which KatA functions. (i.2021reconfigurationofmetabolic pages 1-2)

4. Pathways and biological processes involving KatA

4.1 Peroxide detoxification pathway module (OxyR regulon)

In the KT2440 lineage, KatA is best understood as a high-capacity component of the OxyR-controlled peroxide defense module that includes:
- KatA/KatB (catalases for H2O2 detoxification)
- AhpC (peroxiredoxin; effective at low peroxide levels but requires reductant recycling)
- Additional thiol-redox systems (thioredoxin/glutaredoxin networks) that support redox homeostasis, though not necessarily controlled in the same way as katA/katB/ahpC in the specific OxyR-mutant context. (hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 1-2, kim2014oxidativestressresponse pages 4-5)

4.2 Environmental stress links

Beyond direct H2O2 challenge, oxidative stress in P. putida can be triggered by environmental conditions. Under matric (water limitation) stress in P. putida mt-2 (a related strain with orthology to KT2440), oxidative stress increased intracellular ROS and induced oxidative-defense genes including katA. The study used defined stress intensity (−1.5 MPa) and monitored ROS at the single-cell level. This supports the concept that KatA participates in oxidative defense in soil-relevant stresses (important for rhizosphere/bioremediation contexts). (svenningsen2015pseudomonasputidamt2 pages 1-2)

6. Current applications and real-world implementations (how KatA knowledge is used)

6.1 Engineering stress-robust P. putida chassis strains

P. putida KT2440 is widely used as a platform for bioremediation and metabolic engineering, and oxidative stress tolerance is a key enabling phenotype. Systems-level data show that KT2440 can reconfigure metabolism under oxidative stress to generate reducing power (NADPH surplus ~50% above biosynthetic demand), supporting sustained operation of ROS detoxification systems. In such chassis contexts, KatA provides a high-capacity, cofactor-independent peroxide sink that complements NADPH-consuming peroxide reduction systems. (i.2021reconfigurationofmetabolic pages 1-2, hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 1-2)

6.2 Environmental biotechnology: pollutant/water-stress contexts

ROS can be produced during pollutant biodegradation and during environmental stresses (e.g., water limitation). Induction of katA under matric stress in a related P. putida strain supports the idea that catalase capacity contributes to performance and stability under soil-relevant constraints, consistent with bioremediation use-cases. (svenningsen2015pseudomonasputidamt2 pages 1-2)

7. Expert synthesis and interpretation (authoritative sources)

7.1 Why KatA matters specifically (capacity and cofactor independence)

A mechanistic distinction emphasized in the primary OxyR study is that peroxiredoxins such as AhpC have low Km and can be primary scavengers at low peroxide levels, but they require reductant recycling (AhpF + NADH). Catalases (including KatA), as heme enzymes, do not require NADH/NADPH and are therefore well-suited for detoxifying large peroxide loads and preventing runaway oxidative damage. This provides a strong rationale for why katA is repeatedly highlighted as a major oxidative-defense gene and a likely engineering target for stress robustness. (hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 1-2)

7.2 Regulation-focused engineering hypothesis

Because katA transcription and catalase activity can shift by ≥10-fold under altered OxyR state (oxyR1), regulatory tuning of the OxyR network (or katA promoter engineering) is a plausible strategy to modulate peroxide tolerance. The KT2440 transcriptomics data showing extremely rapid katA induction (936.5-fold at 7 min) further suggests that katA sits on a fast-response branch of peroxide defense in KT2440. (hishinuma2006oxyrregulatedthe pages 2-2, bojanovic2017globaltranscriptionalresponses media 06c5154d)

  • UniProt Q88QK9 / locus PP_0481 is annotated as a catalase (EC=1.11.1.6) with heme as cofactor and the reaction 2 H2O2 = O2 + 2 H2O, placing the protein squarely in the monofunctional catalase family.   • KT2440 transcriptomics gives strong strain-specific support for peroxide-response context: under H2O2 stress, PP_0481 katA is among the most induced oxidative-stress genes in the table summarizing differential expression. PMID:28130298

  • A P. putida oxidative-stress regulation paper in KT2442 shows that OxyR controls katA expression together with other principal peroxide-defense genes. This is not the exact KT2440 strain, so use it as close-strain support rather than as sole evidence for the core catalytic function. [PMID:17107553 "OxyR regulated the expression of two major catalases, KatA and KatB, along with peroxiredoxin, AhpC in Pseudomonas putida"; "This mutation was shown to cause the accumulation of a catalase (KatA)"; "OxyR controlled expression of all the principal peroxide-degrading enzymes in P. putida"]

  • An older P. putida study functionally links catalase A to peroxide/disinfectant survival: cells lacking catalase A were more sensitive to peracetic acid, and low doses induced catalase A promoter activity. This supports peroxide-detoxification and hydrogen-peroxide-response context, although the paper is not framed around GO annotation. [PMID:11315113 "were more sensitive to killing by peracetic acid when they lacked a major catalase activity, catalase A"; "Low doses of peracetic acid induced promoter activity of the gene encoding catalase A"]

  • The broad term response to oxidative stress is biologically fair but not the most specific core process. The tighter process term is hydrogen peroxide catabolism, with oxidative-stress response better treated as contextual physiology/regulation. [PMID:24957251 "defensive cellular systems involving induction of stress-sensing proteins and detoxification enzymes"; PMID:28130298 "The transcriptional levels of ... genes encoding detoxification enzymes (katA, katB, aphC, trxB, trx-2) ... had the most drastic changes in the presence of hydrogen peroxide at T1"]
