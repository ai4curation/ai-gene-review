1. Summary (Answer to the Research Question)

PP_1907 encodes a small, soluble, cytoplasmic enzyme of the haloacid dehalogenase (HAD) hydrolase superfamily, subfamily IA — a Mg²⁺-dependent phosphomonoesterase (phosphatase). Its aspartate-nucleophile catalytic machinery is fully intact, so it is confidently a functional phosphatase that hydrolyses a phosphomonoester substrate via a phospho-aspartyl enzyme intermediate. Every automated functional-classification resource (KEGG ortholog K01091, PANTHER PTHR43434, UniProt/TreeGrafter) assigns it the specific activity 2-phosphoglycolate phosphatase (Gph; EC 3.1.3.18), catalysing:

2-phosphoglycolate + H₂O → glycolate + inorganic phosphate (Pᵢ)

placing it in glyoxylate and dicarboxylate metabolism (KEGG pathway ppu00630). By analogy to the biochemically characterised Escherichia coli Gph, the physiological role is a cytoplasmic housekeeping "salvage" reaction that disposes of 2-phosphoglycolate — most notably the 2-phosphoglycolate liberated during repair of oxidatively damaged 3′-phosphoglycolate DNA ends — recovering the two-carbon unit as glycolate for central metabolism.

Important caveat: This assignment is inferential (bioinformatic / structural / evolutionary), not experimental. PP_1907 itself has never been biochemically characterised, and it is one of three paralogous HAD-IA phosphatases in P. putida that carry the K01091 annotation. A closer paralog (PP_0416, 43% identical to E. coli Gph) is the more likely principal 2-phosphoglycolate phosphatase, so PP_1907's precise physiological substrate should be regarded as a well-motivated prediction that may include 2-phosphoglycolate and/or related small phosphometabolites, potentially with partial functional redundancy.

3. Molecular Function — What Reaction Is Catalysed, and Substrate Specificity

3.1 Enzyme superfamily and catalytic mechanism

PP_1907 belongs to the HAD superfamily (eggNOG COG0546; Pfam PF13419 HAD_2; SUPFAM SSF56784; NCBIfam TIGR01509/TIGR01549 "HAD-SF-IA"). HAD-superfamily enzymes are overwhelmingly phosphotransferases/phosphohydrolases that use an aspartate nucleophile to form a covalent phospho-aspartyl intermediate, with an essential Mg²⁺ cofactor. All four diagnostic HAD sequence motifs are present in PP_1907:

UniProt lists Mg²⁺ (ChEBI:18420) as the cofactor (ARBA rule), consistent with this mechanism. This constitutes strong structural/bioinformatic evidence that PP_1907 is a bona fide Mg²⁺-dependent phosphatase, independent of any substrate assignment.

Residue-level conservation with a characterized enzyme. A pairwise global alignment of PP_1907 against the biochemically characterised E. coli Gph (P32662) shows that all six catalytic residues align one-to-one to identical residues (Asp11, Asp13, Thr15, Lys113, Asp168, Asp172 — every position invariant), even though overall identity is only ~29%. Because 2-phosphoglycolate phosphatases of diverse origins share highly conserved catalytic features implying a common mechanism (Pellicer et al., 2003, 13129953), this invariance is strong evolutionary evidence that PP_1907 is a catalytically competent phosphatase using the same aspartyl-phosphotransferase mechanism.

3.2 Structural architecture and substrate-size class

The protein has the canonical two-domain HAD-IA fold: a Rossmann-like α/β core (Gene3D 3.40.50.1000) plus a small α-helical "C1 cap" inserted immediately after Motif I (Gene3D 1.10.150.240 "putative phosphatase domain 2"; IPR023198 "phosphoglycolate-phosphatase-like domain 2"; cap region ≈ residues 17–95). In the HAD superfamily the cap domain is the principal determinant of substrate specificity and catalytic efficiency (Daughtry et al., 2013, 23848398; Biswas et al., 2009, 19726684). A small C1 cap of this type is characteristic of subfamily-IA phosphatases that act on small, mono-anionic phosphometabolites — precisely the class that includes 2-phosphoglycolate phosphatase.

3.3 Specific substrate assignment and its confidence

Multiple independent resources converge on 2-phosphoglycolate phosphatase (EC 3.1.3.18): - KEGG: ppu:PP_1907 → K01091 (phosphoglycolate phosphatase), pathway ppu00630 (glyoxylate/dicarboxylate metabolism). - PANTHER: family PTHR43434 = PHOSPHOGLYCOLATE PHOSPHATASE. - UniProt/TreeGrafter GO: molecular function GO:0008967 phosphoglycolate phosphatase activity. - Sequence homology: among characterised HAD-IA enzymes, PP_1907 is closest to the experimentally validated E. coli Gph (2-phosphoglycolate phosphatase, P32662; 29.0% global identity) — above E. coli YniC (23.6%), YidA sugar-phosphatase (20.4%), NagD (17.6%), and human PGP (24.6%). The E. coli Gph enzyme is highly specific for 2-phosphoglycolate with high catalytic efficiency (Pellicer et al., 2003, 13129953).

Tempering evidence (see §5): P. putida KT2440 contains three K01091-annotated paralogs (PP_0094, PP_0416, PP_1907). PP_0416 is 43.1% identical to E. coli Gph and is explicitly named "Phosphoglycolate phosphatase", making it the strongest candidate for the organism's principal 2-phosphoglycolate phosphatase; PP_0094 (25% identity) is annotated "5′-nucleotidase". The three paralogs share only 25–28% pairwise identity (ancient divergence). Because HAD-IA phosphatases frequently display broad, overlapping substrate spectra (Kuznetsova et al., 2006, 16990279), PP_1907 may act on 2-phosphoglycolate and/or additional small phosphorylated metabolites; the single-substrate label is a prediction, not a measurement.

Its function is therefore carried out intracellularly, in the cytosol.

4.2 Pathway context and physiological role

The reaction sits at the entry of glyoxylate/dicarboxylate metabolism. 2-Phosphoglycolate is a two-carbon phospho-metabolite; its dephosphorylation yields glycolate, which P. putida KT2440 can further oxidise and assimilate — the organism encodes a complete downstream route: glycolate oxidase GlcDEF (PP_3744/PP_3746/PP_3747; glycolate → glyoxylate), glyoxylate carboligase Gcl (PP_4297), and malate synthase (PP_0356), feeding carbon into central metabolism.

The biological rationale comes from the characterised E. coli ortholog: Gph is a constitutively expressed housekeeping enzyme that performs the dissimilation/salvage of intracellular 2-phosphoglycolate formed during DNA repair of 3′-phosphoglycolate ends — DNA lesions generated by oxidative and radiomimetic damage (e.g., bleomycin) — converting it to glycolate that re-enters general metabolism (Pellicer et al., 2003, 13129953). This directly explains the GO:0006281 (DNA repair) annotation attached to PP_1907: the enzyme is not a DNA-repair protein itself but processes a by-product of DNA-end repair. (In photosynthetic/autotrophic organisms 2-phosphoglycolate arises mainly from RuBisCO oxygenase activity; P. putida is a heterotroph, so the DNA-repair/oxidative source is the physiologically relevant one.)
