# Functional annotation research report: **algG** (UniProt **Q88NC9**; locus **PP_1283**) in *Pseudomonas putida* KT2440

## Executive summary (verified target identity)
> UniProt Q88NC9 matches **AlgG**, a predicted **mannuronan C5-epimerase** (EC 5.1.3.37) from **Pseudomonas putida KT2440**; KT2440-specific literature also identifies **algG as PP_1283** within the alginate biosynthetic cluster and reports its transcriptional induction under water-limiting conditions. However, no direct biochemical characterization of **Q88NC9/KT2440 AlgG** was found in the retrieved papers. Accordingly, the functional annotation for this protein is best scoped as a **KT2440 locus supported by strain-specific transcriptomic evidence**, with enzyme reaction, periplasmic localization, and pathway role inferred from closely related **Pseudomonas** AlgG ortholog studies rather than from a direct KT2440 enzyme assay (gulez2014colonymorphologyand pages 6-8, nilsson2011influenceofputative pages 1-2, franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 1-2, jain2003thedualroles pages 1-2, lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 1-2).


*Blockquote: This blockquote verifies that Q88NC9 corresponds to P. putida KT2440 AlgG/PP_1283 and clearly distinguishes direct KT2440 evidence from orthology-based functional inference. It is useful for preventing overstatement in the final annotation.*

**Primary functional assignment:** AlgG is a **polymer-level mannuronan C5-epimerase** (EC **5.1.3.37**) that converts **β-D-mannuronate (M)** residues to **α-L-guluronate (G)** residues in the nascent alginate polymer during periplasmic transit, shaping alginate monomer composition and block structure. This activity is central to the **alginate modification/export complex** in pseudomonads and is strongly modulated by **O-acetylation state** of the substrate. (franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 1-2, jain2003thedualroles pages 1-2, lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 1-2)

## 1. Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

### 1.1 Alginate and why epimerization matters
Alginate is a linear, 1→4-linked copolymer of **β-D-mannuronic acid (M)** and its C5 epimer **α-L-guluronic acid (G)**. In pseudomonads, alginate is synthesized initially as a polymannuronate (poly-M) chain and is subsequently modified in the periplasm by epimerization (AlgG) and acetylation (AlgF/AlgI/AlgJ/AlgX), with composition and substitutions affecting polymer properties and interactions (e.g., hydration, viscosity, gel behavior with cations). (ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological pages 1-2, roblesprice2004algxisa pages 2-3, wang2023strategytocombat pages 2-3)

### 1.2 Reaction chemistry catalyzed by AlgG
AlgG catalyzes **C5-epimerization** at the uronic acid C5 stereocenter in polymeric alginate:

**poly(β-D-mannuronate) → poly(β-D-mannuronate/α-L-guluronate)**

Genetic and biochemical evidence from *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* shows **algG mutants produce alginate lacking G residues**, and expression of algG restores G incorporation, establishing AlgG as the enzyme introducing G residues into pseudomonad alginate. (franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 1-2, franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 7-7)

### 1.3 AlgG vs AlgE-type extracellular epimerases (important distinction)
A central definitional distinction is that pseudomonads typically use a **single periplasmic AlgG** that introduces **limited, patterned epimerization** (predominantly M and alternating MG regions), whereas *Azotobacter* species encode multiple **secreted AlgE family** epimerases that can generate **guluronate-rich G-blocks** and are widely exploited for in vitro alginate tailoring. (lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 1-2, lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 10-12, tøndervik2020exploitingmannuronanc5 pages 1-3)

## 2. Gene/protein context in *P. putida* KT2440 (PSEPK)

### 2.1 Operon/genetic context and expression in KT2440
In *P. putida* KT2440, an alginate biosynthesis gene cluster spanning **PP1277–PP1288** has been described, and algG is identified within this locus as **PP_1283**. (nilsson2011influenceofputative pages 1-2, gulez2014colonymorphologyand pages 6-8)

Under water limitation, whole-transcriptome profiling showed induction of multiple alginate genes in KT2440; specifically, **algG (PP_1283)** exhibited a **log2 fold change of ~+0.44** in the wild type under water-limited versus water-replete conditions (reported as “alginate-C5-mannuronan-epimerase AlgG, putative”). (gulez2014colonymorphologyand pages 6-8)

### 2.2 Biological role of alginate in *P. putida* ecology (hydration and stress)
Work in a closely related *P. putida* strain (mt-2) established that alginate is induced by **matric (water-limiting) stress**, where it contributes to a hydrated microenvironment and alters biofilm architecture; glycosyl composition analysis under matric stress detected mannuronic acid in EPS (consistent with alginate production) in wild type but not in an algD mutant. (chang2007alginateproductionby pages 1-2, chang2007alginateproductionby pages 4-5)

## 3. Molecular function: substrate specificity, mechanism, and localization

### 3.1 Substrate specificity and acetylation sensitivity
Biochemical assays show AlgG acts on **polymeric poly-M substrates** (“polymer-level” epimerase), and that **O-acetylation of mannuronate residues strongly inhibits AlgG activity**; chemical deacetylation of poly-M was required to detect strong epimerization, producing a **nearly 10-fold increase** in reactivity with a G-specific lyase readout compared with control reactions. (franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 7-8, franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 7-7)

This interplay is consistent with broader pathway understanding that acetylation during periplasmic transit can protect residues from epimerization and from certain alginate lyases, impacting both polymer composition and susceptibility to enzymatic remodeling. (ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological pages 1-2, ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological pages 6-7)

### 3.2 Subcellular localization (where AlgG acts)
Direct localization evidence in *P. aeruginosa* indicates AlgG is synthesized as a **signal-peptide-containing precursor** (~60 kDa) and processed to a mature form (~55 kDa) that localizes to the **periplasm**. (franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 1-2)

Mechanistic synthesis and pathway models place AlgG as a periplasmic modifier acting on the polymer as it transits from the inner-membrane polymerase toward outer-membrane export. (roblesprice2004algxisa pages 2-3, ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological pages 3-4)

### 3.3 Mode of action and product pattern (block structure)
Structural/mechanistic work on periplasmic AlgG in *Pseudomonas* indicates:
- A single AlgG can epimerize roughly **22–44%** of mannuronate residues in vivo in pseudomonads, producing polymers with **polymannuronate segments and alternating MG sequences**, rather than long G-blocks. (lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 1-2)
- AlgG requires relatively long substrates; a minimum of **~9 residues** has been reported, with weak binding to a nonamer characterized by **Ka ≈ 5.0 × 10^5 M−1**. (lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 12-13)
- A **processive/sliding** mechanism and active-site geometry support the observation that pseudomonad alginate lacks G–G doublets in vivo (favoring alternating patterns). (lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 12-13)

### 3.4 Catalytic mechanism (expert structural inference)
Structural analysis supports a catalytic architecture in which a histidine (e.g., **His319**) is proposed as a catalytic base, **Arg345** contributes to carboxylate neutralization (contrasting with Ca2+-mediated neutralization in many secreted AlgE enzymes), and water/solvent is implicated as the proton donor with possible positioning by **Tyr314**; a conserved **DPHD** motif is noted for AlgG-family periplasmic enzymes. (lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 1-2, lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 10-12, ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological pages 6-7)

## 4. Pathway integration: alginate polymerization, modification, and export

### 4.1 AlgG in the synthase-dependent secretion system
In the pseudomonad model, Alg8 and Alg44 are implicated in polymerization, and the nascent chain is modified and routed through a periplasmic scaffold/complex involving AlgG, AlgX, and AlgK, and exits via the outer-membrane pore AlgE; the periplasmic lyase AlgL is co-expressed and implicated in clearing mislocalized polymer (and can degrade polymer if not properly shielded). (roblesprice2004algxisa pages 2-3, ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological pages 3-4)

A useful schematic model of this multi-protein system is shown in the retrieved figure. (ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological media 3908e58c)

### 4.2 Dual functional roles: epimerization and secretion/protection
Genetic analysis in *P. aeruginosa* supports a **dual role** for AlgG: (i) epimerization (M→G) and (ii) assisting productive secretion by **protecting the polymer from periplasmic AlgL-mediated degradation**, potentially together with AlgK as part of a periplasmic scaffold guiding polymers to AlgE. (jain2003thedualroles pages 1-2)

## 5. Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

### 5.1 2023: AlgG framed as a determinant of alginate functional properties relevant to anti-biofilm strategies
A 2023 review on enzymatic biofilm dispersal highlights AlgG as the enzyme that epimerizes mannuronic to guluronic acid, affecting ManA/GulA composition and functional properties of alginate. It also compiles a wide reported range of alginate **acetylation rates (4%–57%)**, emphasizing acetylation and epimer composition as determinants of cation-mediated gel behavior and material properties—parameters directly relevant to therapeutic biofilm disruption and biomaterials design. (wang2023strategytocombat pages 2-3)

### 5.2 2024: *P. putida* process implementation—reducing biofilm/foam for fermentation
A 2024 fermentation-oriented study engineered *P. putida* by deleting alginate-associated algA (among other genes), showing that targeting alginate production can reduce biofilm formation and improve cultivation characteristics. The engineered strain showed **40% lower biofilm formation after 72 h** and increased cell densities in small bioreactors: **1.39×10^10 vs 6.4×10^9 CFU/mL** (rich medium) and **6.11×10^9 vs 1.36×10^9 CFU/mL** (mineral medium) for mutant versus wild type. While not specific to algG, the work explicitly lists algG among alginate pathway genes and demonstrates real-world process relevance of alginate-pathway engineering. (frolov2024constructionofthe pages 1-2)

### 5.3 2024: expanding the enzyme toolkit—epimerase activity in non-canonical folds (preprint)
A 2024 preprint reports detectable M→G epimerase side-activity in a gut bacterial alginate lyase with an (α/α)7-barrel fold, contrasting with the parallel β-helix folds typical of AlgG/AlgE epimerases. While this does not change AlgG’s annotation, it is relevant to “latest research” in that it suggests epimerization chemistry may be more widespread than previously recognized and may appear in enzymes annotated as lyases, expanding the search space for alginate-modifying enzymes. (tandrup2024theswissarmy pages 20-23)

## 6. Current applications and real-world implementations

### 6.1 Biofilm control and therapeutics
Because alginate contributes to biofilm mechanical properties and antibiotic tolerance in pseudomonads, enzymatic strategies (e.g., exogenous polysaccharide-degrading enzymes) are used to weaken EPS matrices and sensitize bacteria; the 2023 review emphasizes extracellular enzymes as anti-biofilm tools due to accessibility and reduced reliance on cellular uptake. AlgG is indirectly relevant as the enzyme that sets alginate composition and thus influences matrix properties and response to enzymatic dispersal. (wang2023strategytocombat pages 2-3)

### 6.2 Industrial and biomaterials tailoring of alginate composition
A practical approach to controlling alginate material properties is to tune guluronate content and block structure using epimerases. For example, applying *Azotobacter* mannuronan C5-epimerases increased G content from **32% to as high as 81%**, and the G-rich oligomer drug candidate OligoG CF-5/20 is described as **>85% G** with **DPn ~15–17**, linking epimerization control to biomedical functionality (e.g., mucus rheology and biofilm disruption). While AlgG itself is periplasmic and not generally used as an extracellular processing enzyme, these data contextualize why the AlgG-catalyzed reaction is a key lever in alginate functional engineering. (tøndervik2020exploitingmannuronanc5 pages 1-3)

### 6.3 Environmental and soil microbiology implementations (stress resilience)
In *P. putida* (mt-2), alginate production under matric stress is positioned as an adaptive mechanism in water-limited habitats (soil-like conditions), shaping biofilm architecture and hydration microenvironments. This supports interpreting KT2440 alginate/algG induction under water limitation as part of a broader ecological stress-response strategy. (chang2007alginateproductionby pages 1-2, chang2007alginateproductionby pages 4-5, gulez2014colonymorphologyand pages 6-8)

## 7. Expert opinions and authoritative synthesis

An authoritative 2015 review synthesizes the prevailing model that bacterial alginate is synthesized as polymannuronate and then modified by periplasmic enzymes, with **AlgG and AlgX** described as integral components of a **polymerization/export complex**, and acetylation described as a key determinant of viscosity/water binding and as a protective modification against lyases and further epimerization. This view supports annotating AlgG not only as an epimerase but also as a component functionally coupled to secretion and periplasmic quality control. (ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological pages 1-2, ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological pages 3-4)

## 10. Conclusions for annotating *P. putida* KT2440 AlgG (Q88NC9)

1. **Molecular function:** annotate as **mannuronan C5-epimerase (EC 5.1.3.37)** acting on polymeric alginate to generate guluronate residues (M→G), with activity sensitive to O-acetylation state of the substrate. (franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 1-2, franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 7-8)
2. **Cellular compartment:** **periplasmic enzyme**, likely secreted via an N-terminal signal peptide and processed to a mature periplasmic form (direct evidence in *P. aeruginosa* AlgG; inferred for KT2440 ortholog). (franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 1-2)
3. **Biological process:** part of the **alginate biosynthesis/modification/export system**, working alongside AlgX/AlgK and coordinated with polymerization (Alg8/Alg44), acetylation (AlgF/I/J/X), periplasmic lyase quality control (AlgL), and outer-membrane export (AlgE). (roblesprice2004algxisa pages 2-3, ertesva¥g2015alginatemodifyingenzymesbiological pages 3-4)
4. **KT2440-specific support:** algG is present as **PP_1283** in the KT2440 alg locus and is induced (modestly) under water limitation, consistent with alginate’s role in hydration and stress tolerance in *P. putida* lineages. (gulez2014colonymorphologyand pages 6-8, nilsson2011influenceofputative pages 1-2)

## Limitations of the current evidence base
Within the retrieved corpus, there is **no direct biochemical characterization of KT2440 AlgG (Q88NC9)** (e.g., purified enzyme kinetics or structure). Therefore, mechanistic residues, binding constants, and localization are **best treated as orthology-based inference** from closely related *Pseudomonas* AlgG studies, while KT2440-specific claims should be limited to locus context and expression/phenotype studies. (gulez2014colonymorphologyand pages 6-8, franklin1994pseudomonasaeruginosaalgg pages 1-2, lynne2014catalyticmechanismand pages 1-2)
