Gene Description

Ca2+-dependent pyrroloquinoline quinone (PQQ)-dependent alcohol dehydrogenase (EC 1.1.2.8) that catalyzes the periplasmic oxidation of a broad range of primary and secondary alcohols and aldehydes using cytochrome c as electron acceptor. PedE is the calcium-utilizing counterpart of the lanthanide-dependent PedH (PP_2679), and the two enzymes are inversely regulated by the PedS2/PedR2 two-component system in response to lanthanide availability. PedE is essential for efficient growth on volatile organic compounds (ethanol, 1-butanol, 2-phenylethanol) in the absence of lanthanides. The enzyme has an eight-bladed beta-propeller fold, binds PQQ non-covalently, and requires two Ca2+ ions per subunit (one catalytic in the active site, one structural at the N-terminus). Distinguished from PedH by Ser at position 295 (vs Asp) which coordinates Ca2+ rather than lanthanides. Part of the 2-phenylethanol degradation (Ped) pathway and also contributes to an alternative glycerol metabolic route.

Generally, these data become publicly accessible in databases provided by projects focussing on different aspects of biological sequence information [3,4].     > In order to detect gene and protein names which are assigned to products of different genes and thus result in erroneous cross-references, dedicated lists are created for each organism separately. Organism-specific lists are necessary, since terms that are ambiguous in one organism might be explicit in another. For example, ADORA2 is an ambiguous gene name in Homo sapiens but not in mouse, and GALT in mouse but not in H.sapiens.
    > In a first step, ambiguous names within the databases were extracted. If a name occurs in at least two entries describing different genes or proteins (splice variants count as one gene/protein), this particular name is marked as ambiguous and is excluded from the mapping process. In a second step, corresponding gene names occurring in the manually annotated sections of RefSeq as well as in UniProt were analyzed. Entries containing the same gene product name and having a one-to-many or many-to-many relation (e.g. one Swiss-Prot entry maps to many RefSeq entries) were scrutinized for misleading annotation. This process is done manually by inspecting additional information like sequence similarity or functional information about the involved entries. In most of the cases, the exclusion of the ambiguous gene names resulted in correct one-to-one relations.
    > As statistical analysis revealed (Supplementary Material S2) that gene names with less than four letters are exceptionally error-prone, only gene names with at least four letters are kept for mapping purposes. However, gene names with less than four letters can be queried, e.g. a search for the tumor suppressor 'p53' reveals the respective entries with the official gene name 'TP53'. Organism-specific lists of ambiguous gene and protein names are available for download on the CRONOS home page.

Research report: pedE (PP_2674; UniProt Q88JH5) in Pseudomonas putida/Pseudomonas alloputida KT2440

Executive summary

The pedE gene (PP_2674; UniProt Q88JH5) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 encodes PedE, a periplasmic, Ca2+-dependent pyrroloquinoline quinone (PQQ)-dependent alcohol dehydrogenase (PQQ-ADH; EC 1.1.2.8) that oxidizes a broad range of primary/secondary alcohols and some aldehydes, supporting growth and detoxification of volatile alcohol substrates. (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 1-2, wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 2-4)
PedE is functionally redundant with the lanthanide-dependent PedH (PP_2679), and the two enzymes are controlled by a lanthanide/rare earth element (REE) switch mediated by the PedS2/PedR2 two-component system, tuning expression and oxidation capacity according to Ln availability. (wehrmann2018thepeds2pedr2twocomponent pages 1-2, wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 10-14)
Recent 2024 transcriptomics and single-cell elemental analyses show that this REE switch is not a simple on/off toggle, but rather a fine-tuning response that depends on whether lanthanides are utilizable (light Ln such as La, Nd) or non-utilizable (heavy Ln such as Er), with large (up to ~126-fold) shifts in pedE transcript abundance across conditions. (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 9-11)

1. Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Quinoprotein alcohol dehydrogenases (PQQ-ADHs)

PedE belongs to the bacterial PQQ-dependent alcohol dehydrogenase family, a class of quinoproteins that use PQQ as a redox cofactor to oxidize alcohols (and in some cases aldehydes) in the bacterial periplasm. In KT2440, PedE and PedH are described as part of a periplasmic oxidation system enabling rapid oxidation of volatile alcohols/aldehydes. (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 1-2, wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 1-6)

1.2 Metal dependence: Ca2+ enzymes vs lanthanide enzymes

KT2440 encodes two homologous PQQ-ADHs with different active-site metals:
- PedE is Ca2+-dependent (Ca2+ plus PQQ required for activity). (wehrmann2018thepeds2pedr2twocomponent pages 1-2, wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 2-4)
- PedH is lanthanide (Ln3+)-dependent (requires La3+/Ce3+/Pr3+/Nd3+/Sm3+/Gd3+/Tb3+; inactive with several heavy Ln under tested conditions). (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 2-4, wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 6-10)
A key sequence feature distinguishing Ln-dependent PQQ-ADHs is an active-site Asp ligand (PedH) replacing a Ser in Ca-dependent enzymes (PedE). (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 2-4, wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 6-10)

1.3 The lanthanide/REE “switch”

The “REE switch” (or Ln switch) refers to inverse regulation of pedE and pedH depending on Ln availability, coordinating oxidation capacity with metal availability. Mechanistically, it is mediated by the PedS2/PedR2 two-component system and additional regulatory modules (some not fully identified). (wehrmann2018thepeds2pedr2twocomponent pages 1-2, wehrmann2018thepeds2pedr2twocomponent pages 8-9)

2. Functional annotation of PedE: reaction, substrate scope, kinetics, and localization

2.1 Primary molecular function and reaction catalyzed

PedE is experimentally characterized as a PQQ-dependent alcohol dehydrogenase/ethanol dehydrogenase that oxidizes alcohols (and some aldehydes) in the periplasm of P. putida KT2440. (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 2-4, wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 1-2)
Most biochemical characterization has used a dye-linked assay (PMS/DCPIP), consistent with PedE functioning as a dehydrogenase transferring electrons to periplasmic acceptors in vivo. (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 11-12)

2.2 Substrate scope and example activities

Purified PedE shows activity on multiple linear and aromatic alcohols and aldehydes. Examples (specific activities): ethanol 6.7 ± 0.9 U mg−1, 1-butanol 5.8 ± 0.1 U mg−1, 2-phenylethanol 6.7 ± 1.1 U mg−1, while methanol is a poorer substrate (0.61 ± 0.10 U mg−1). (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 2-4)
Across a larger substrate panel, PedE showed substantial activity on many linear primary alcohols/aldehydes (reported range ~1.9 ± 0.2 to 6.7 ± 0.9 U mg−1), while methanol, 2,3-butanediol and ethanolamine were ~10-fold poorer; cinnamyl aldehyde had no detectable activity in one dataset. (wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 6-10, wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 2-4)

2.3 Enzyme kinetics (ethanol)

A direct comparison of PedE and PedH using ethanol indicates:
- PedE: Vmax ~6.1 U mg−1, KM ~85 µM
- PedH: Vmax ~10.6 U mg−1, KM ~177 µM
Thus, PedH has higher Vmax under the tested conditions, while PedE shows lower KM for ethanol. (wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 6-10)

2.4 Cofactors and metal dependence

PedE activity depends on Ca2+ and PQQ; it is active across 10 µM–10 mM CaCl2 with an optimum around 1 mM, and a reported Ca2+ affinity KD ~64 µM. (wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 6-10)
Assay conditions used to quantify PedE activity include 100 mM Tris-HCl pH 8, mediators PMS/DCPIP, and imidazole, with added CaCl2 and PQQ. (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 11-12)

2.5 Cellular localization

PedE and PedH are repeatedly described as part of a periplasmic oxidation system in P. putida KT2440 that enables rapid oxidation/detoxification of volatile alcohols and aldehydes. (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 1-2, wehrmann2018thepeds2pedr2twocomponent pages 1-2)

3. Biological roles and pathways

3.1 Growth on volatile alcohols and functional redundancy with PedH

Genetic evidence indicates that at least one of PedE or PedH must be functional to support efficient growth on alcohol substrates such as 2-phenylethanol. For example, deletion of both (ΔpedEΔpedH) blocks growth on 2-phenylethanol, while single deletions show metal-dependent growth behavior consistent with the REE switch. (wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 10-14, wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 6-10)

3.2 Glycerol: a PedE/PedH-initiated alternative catabolic route

Proteomics and mutant analysis identified a novel glycerol utilization route initiated by PedE/PedH oxidation (glycerol → glyceraldehyde → glycerate → glycerate-2-phosphate via GarK), operating alongside the canonical glpFKRD pathway and affecting growth parameters. (wehrmann2020thecellularresponse pages 1-2)
Quantitatively (batch growth on glycerol), La3+ shortened lag phase (10.2 ± 0.2 h vs 17.3 ± 0.2 h without La3+) but reduced growth rate (µmax 0.201 ± 0.004 h−1 vs 0.341 ± 0.010 h−1) and final OD600 (0.680 ± 0.014 vs 0.884 ± 0.004); the La-dependent phenotype disappeared in ΔpedEΔpedH, supporting PedE/H causality. (wehrmann2019thecellularresponse pages 12-15)

3.3 Regulatory pathway: PedS2/PedR2 and the REE switch

Mechanistic evidence supports the following model:
- In the absence of lanthanum, sensor kinase PedS2 phosphorylates response regulator PedR2, which activates pedE transcription and contributes to pedH repression.
- In the presence of lanthanum, PedS2 kinase activity decreases, lowering PedR2 phosphorylation, decreasing pedE expression and relieving pedH repression (with additional activation of pedH via other module[s]). (wehrmann2018thepeds2pedr2twocomponent pages 1-2)
Evidence also indicates PedS2 is primary but not the only input into PedR2 phosphorylation (additional kinase activity suggested by mutant phenotypes). (wehrmann2018thepeds2pedr2twocomponent pages 8-9)

3.4 Sensitivity and media dependence

Promoter assays and growth experiments demonstrate that transcriptional and physiological responses can occur at very low La3+ concentrations and are medium-dependent. In one dataset (M9), pedE promoter activity decreased between 100 nM and 100 µM La3+ while pedH induction began at ≥100 nM and peaked near 10 µM; in MP medium, effective concentrations were reported as low as 1–10 nM. (wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 10-14)
With 10 nM La3+, one study reported pedE promoter reductions of ~21-fold in WT and pedH induction of ~37-fold in WT. (wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 10-14)

4. Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

4.1 2024: transcriptome-wide evidence that the Ln switch is “fine-tuning”

A 2024 mSphere study of Pseudomonas alloputida KT2440 (a taxonomic re-assignment of KT2440 used in some recent work) quantified Ln-dependent growth, single-cell Ln association, and transcriptome responses during growth on 2-phenylethanol. (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 1-2, gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 9-11)
Key transcriptomic findings include:
- The ped cluster (including pedE, pedH, pedS2/pedR2) was broadly downregulated upon Ln supplementation (except Er), with log2FC values −1.29 to −6.98 for the cluster. (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 9-11)
- pedE expression varied by >2 orders of magnitude across conditions (up to ~126-fold), with RPKM 410.28 ± 56.55 (no Ln), 210.45 ± 27.53 (Er), and very low values with light Ln (e.g., Nd 4.45 ± 0.29, La 3.25 ± 0.57). (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 9-11)
- The pedE:pedH expression ratio shifted from ~6 (no Ln) to ~2 (Er) and toward pedH dominance with La/Nd/mix; RT-qPCR showed pedH was ~2.4-fold higher with La. (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 9-11)
These data support an updated view that Ln sensing in KT2440 adjusts the pool of Ca- and Ln-dependent ADHs in a graded, element-dependent manner rather than a binary switch. (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 11-13)

4.2 2024: quantitative growth effects of light vs heavy lanthanides

In 2024 experiments on 2-phenylethanol, low-nanomolar La (10–50 nM) produced the shortest lag phase and the highest reported growth rate (r = 1.64 ± 0.13 h−1, doubling time ~0.42 ± 0.03 h), compared to no-Ln (r = 0.88 ± 0.06 h−1, td ~0.79 ± 0.05 h). (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 2-5)
Heavy lanthanides (Dy/Ho/Er/Yb) caused fitness costs, interpreted as mismetallation and interference with Ln sensing/signaling. (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 1-2)
Single-cell ICP-MS quantified cell-associated Ln, for example La 0.058 ± 0.055 fg cell−1 vs Er 0.152 ± 0.106 fg cell−1 after exposures, illustrating element-dependent association/binding. (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 2-5)

4.3 2023: systems-level view of C1 detoxification implicating PedEH

A 2023 mSystems study on P. putida one-carbon (C1) responses states that the ped cluster encodes PedE and PedH, broad-substrate PQQ-ADHs implicated in methanol oxidation to formaldehyde and detoxification, and reports that methanol exposure induces a formaldehyde detoxification response including strongly overexpressed aldehyde dehydrogenase aldB-II/pedI (PP_2680) (log2FC 9.6) and other functions, highlighting a physiological context where PedE/H can contribute to methanol/formaldehyde handling. (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15)

5. Current applications and real-world implementations

5.1 Biocatalysis and whole-cell oxidation of alcohols

PedE is of interest as a biocatalyst for oxidation of alcohols (including aromatic alcohols such as 2-phenylethanol), and whole-cell systems expressing PedE have been used for small-scale biotransformations. (wehrmann2018engineeringthermalstability pages 10-10)

5.2 Electrochemical and biosensor applications (direct electron transfer context)

PQQ-dependent dehydrogenases are discussed as attractive for electrochemical systems (e.g., bioanodes and biosensors), leveraging their periplasmic localization and electron-transfer properties; PedE/PedH are explicitly framed as a class of biocatalysts suitable for such applications. (wehrmann2018engineeringthermalstability pages 9-10)

5.3 Protein engineering for process robustness (thermal stability and solvent tolerance)

A 2018 Microbial Biotechnology study developed a heterologous whole-cell screen in E. coli to engineer PedE stability and solvent tolerance, producing the triple mutant R91D/E408P/N410K. Quantitative outcomes:
- In purified-enzyme assays, the triple mutant increased PedE stability by ~9.3-fold relative to WT after heat challenge, and increased T50 (60 min) by ~7°C (58°C → 65°C). (wehrmann2018engineeringthermalstability pages 3-5, wehrmann2018engineeringthermalstability pages 5-7)
- In whole-cell assays, single mutants increased residual activity after 1 h at 45°C (WT ~23% vs E408P ~69%), and combined variants showed additive improvement (triple ~4× in the whole-cell stability metric). (wehrmann2018engineeringthermalstability pages 3-5)
- The triple mutant showed ~2× higher residual activity after 1 h incubation in 20–50% DMSO (though no activity for WT or mutant above 60% DMSO). (wehrmann2018engineeringthermalstability pages 3-5)
- Reconstitution with PQQ/Ca2+ markedly stabilizes the enzyme (reported residual activity at 45°C: 87 ± 6% for reconstituted enzyme vs 23 ± 2% in the whole-cell assay context), emphasizing the importance of cofactor incorporation for application performance. (wehrmann2018engineeringthermalstability pages 5-7)
These results directly support PedE engineering for bioprocess conditions involving elevated temperature and organic solvents. (wehrmann2018engineeringthermalstability pages 5-7)

6. Expert interpretation and synthesis (authoritative analysis grounded in the literature)

1) PedE as a periplasmic “front-end” oxidation enzyme for volatile alcohols. The consistent description of PedE/PedH as periplasmic quinoprotein dehydrogenases, coupled with growth defects in deletion mutants on volatile alcohols, supports a model where PedE provides rapid periplasmic oxidation that aids both detoxification and catabolic entry. (wehrmann2017functionalroleofa pages 1-2, wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 10-14)

2) Ecological and physiological logic of redundancy. Quantitative metal-binding and activity ranges show PedE is tuned for Ca2+-rich settings (micromolar–millimolar Ca2+), while PedH is tuned for Ln at nano- to micromolar concentrations (KD in the tens of nM range), explaining why organisms maintain both in fluctuating metal landscapes. (wehrmann2017functionalroleof pages 6-10)

3) A revised view (2024) of “switching”: graded, element-dependent tuning. Transcriptomics demonstrate that light vs heavy lanthanides produce distinct expression states (including downregulation of both pedE and pedH yet with ratios favoring pedH under utilizable Ln), consistent with the idea that bacteria discriminate among lanthanides and that growth outcomes can reflect mismetallation or sensing interference with heavy Ln. (gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 9-11, gorniak2024changesingrowth pages 11-13)

description: Ca2+-dependent pyrroloquinoline quinone (PQQ)-dependent alcohol dehydrogenase (EC 1.1.2.8) that catalyzes the periplasmic oxidation of a broad range of primary and secondary alcohols and aldehydes using cytochrome c as electron acceptor. PedE is the calcium-utilizing counterpart of the lanthanide-dependent PedH (PP_2679), and the two enzymes are inversely regulated by the PedS2/PedR2 two-component system in response to lanthanide availability. PedE is essential for efficient growth on volatile organic compounds (ethanol, 1-butanol, 2-phenylethanol) in the absence of lanthanides. The enzyme has an eight-bladed beta-propeller fold, binds PQQ non-covalently, and requires two Ca2+ ions per subunit (one catalytic in the active site, one structural at the N-terminus). Distinguished from PedH by Ser at position 295 (vs Asp) which coordinates Ca2+ rather than lanthanides. Part of the 2-phenylethanol degradation (Ped) pathway and also contributes to an alternative glycerol metabolic route.
