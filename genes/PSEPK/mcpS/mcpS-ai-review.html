Gene Description

mcpS encodes a methyl-accepting chemotaxis receptor for several organic acids, especially TCA-cycle intermediates such as malate, succinate, fumarate, and oxaloacetate. As a membrane chemoreceptor, McpS mediates chemotactic signal transduction toward these attractants in P. putida KT2440.

Comprehensive research report: mcpS (UniProt Q88E10; locus PP_4658) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

0) Target verification (mandatory identity check)

The gene symbol mcpS in this report refers specifically to the Pseudomonas putida strain KT2440 methyl-accepting chemotaxis protein McpS, encoded by locus tag PP_4658 (consistent with the user-provided UniProt accession Q88E10). This mapping is explicitly stated in KT2440 chemoreceptor inventories and primary functional studies (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 2-3, parales2013pseudomonasputidaf1 pages 12-15). The protein is consistently described as a methyl-accepting chemotaxis protein (MCP) with a transmembrane architecture and a distinctive HBM (helical bimodular) ligand-binding domain (LBD), aligning with the domain/family description given in the prompt (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3, ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13).

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Chemotaxis and MCPs

Chemotaxis is directed, flagellum-based movement in response to chemical gradients. In canonical bacterial chemotaxis, MCP chemoreceptors detect chemoeffectors and modulate the activity of the histidine kinase CheA (via coupling proteins such as CheW), which then controls the phosphorylation state of downstream regulators (e.g., CheY) that alter swimming behavior (lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2, lugo2016molecularbasicof pages 43-46).

MCPs typically comprise (i) an extracytoplasmic/periplasmic sensory (ligand-binding) region, (ii) transmembrane helices, (iii) an intracellular HAMP signal-conversion module, and (iv) a cytosolic methyl-accepting/signaling region that interfaces with the CheA–CheW array and undergoes adaptation via methylation/demethylation (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3, ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13).

1.2 Direct vs indirect ligand recognition

A current theme in chemotaxis biology is that ligands can be sensed by:
* Direct binding to chemoreceptor LBDs (common for many amino acids, organic acids, and inorganic ions), or
* Indirect mechanisms, where a chemoeffector is bound by a periplasmic solute-binding protein (SBP) that then activates a chemoreceptor (well established in diverse systems) (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 3-4, ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 9-10).

This distinction matters for functional annotation: for McpS, direct binding of organic acids to the isolated LBD was experimentally measured (ITC), supporting a direct-recognition mechanism for at least a subset of its ligands (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3, ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 10-11).

2) Gene product overview: McpS as an organic-acid chemoreceptor

2.1 Primary functional role

McpS is an MCP that mediates chemotaxis toward organic acids, especially tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle intermediates and short-chain carboxylates. Primary experimental work identified McpS as the chemoreceptor for six TCA-cycle intermediates and butyrate, using an mcpS-deficient mutant with loss of taxis that was restored by genetic complementation (lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2, lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3). Subsequent synthesis articles and receptor catalogs consistently list McpS ligands as TCA intermediates, acetate, and butyrate, with direct binding supported by primary experiments (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 10-11, ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13).

Independent KT2440 receptor inventories list McpS (PP_4658) ligands as malate, fumarate, oxaloacetate, succinate, citrate, isocitrate, and butyrate (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 2-3). Parales et al. further describe McpS as the primary receptor for several of these organic acids (succinate, malate, fumarate, citrate), reporting that a KT2440 ΔmcpS strain was unable to respond to those compounds in chemotaxis assays (parales2013pseudomonasputidaf1 pages 12-15).

2.2 Domain architecture and ligand-binding domain (HBM)

A defining feature of McpS is its HBM (helical bimodular) ligand-binding domain. Reviews describe McpS as the first characterized chemoreceptor with this HBM LBD architecture (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13). The HBM LBD is composed of six helices organized into two stacked helical modules, each harboring a ligand-binding site, enabling “bimodular” sensing and integration of signals (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13, lugo2016molecularbasicof pages 43-46).

The original characterization noted that McpS has an unusually large LBD and predicted a 6-helix LBD; SMART/domain analysis predicted a transmembrane region, HAMP linker, and a methyl-accepting (MA) domain in addition to the periplasmic sensory region (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3).

3) Experimental evidence for ligand specificity and chemotaxis phenotypes

3.1 Genetic evidence (mutant and complementation)

Genetic knockout/insertion and complementation experiments are central to McpS functional annotation. In KT2440, an mcpS-deficient mutant was assessed in chemotaxis assays relative to wild type, and plasmid-based complementation (pRK415-mcpS) restored chemotaxis, supporting that McpS is necessary for taxis to its organic-acid ligands (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3). A KT2440 ΔmcpS strain was additionally reported to be unable to respond to key organic acids such as succinate, malate, fumarate, and citrate (parales2013pseudomonasputidaf1 pages 12-15).

3.2 Chemotaxis assay formats used

The McpS phenotype was tested using multiple standard chemotaxis assays:
* Agarose-plug assays (qualitative gradient response) (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3)
* Qualitative and quantitative capillary assays (cell accumulation into capillaries containing chemoeffector) (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3, lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2)
* Swim-plate/soft-agar assays used in comparative receptor studies to visualize migration halos (parales2013pseudomonasputidaf1 pages 12-15)

The availability of convergent results across assay types strengthens confidence that McpS is a bona fide chemotaxis receptor for these organic acids (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3, parales2013pseudomonasputidaf1 pages 12-15).

3.3 Direct ligand binding and biophysical validation

Direct ligand recognition by McpS was tested using the purified recombinant McpS ligand-binding domain (expressed as a His-tagged fragment corresponding to Gly47–Ser283) (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3). Key biophysical evidence includes:

  • Isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC) showed binding of McpS-LBD to TCA intermediates/organic acids and supported a one-site binding model under the experimental conditions described (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3).
  • McpS displayed specificity beyond simple charge complementarity, binding TCA intermediates but not binding close structural analogs such as maleate, aspartate, or tricarballylate (lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2).
  • Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) indicated that ligands that elicit strong chemotaxis (notably malate, succinate, and fumarate) also increase McpS-LBD thermal stability/unfolding enthalpy, consistent with ligand-stabilized conformations that promote signaling (lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2, lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3).
  • Analytical ultracentrifugation/equilibrium sedimentation supported that McpS-LBD exists in a monomer–dimer equilibrium, and that ligand binding (e.g., malate) stabilizes the dimeric state, consistent with the broader principle that ligand-induced dimer stabilization can be part of transmembrane receptor signaling (lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2, lugo2016molecularbasicof pages 43-46).

3.4 Bimodular binding-site specialization (expert synthesis)

Authoritative synthesis describes module-specific ligand recognition in the HBM LBD: malate and succinate bind the membrane-proximal module, whereas acetate binds the membrane-distal module, and signals from the two modules are described as additive (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13, lugo2016molecularbasicof pages 43-46). This “bimodular” design is interpreted as a mechanism to fine-tune chemotaxis across different organic acids (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13).

4) Localization and cellular context

McpS is described as a canonical transmembrane MCP, implying localization in the cytoplasmic membrane with a periplasmic LBD and cytosolic signaling/adaptation regions, consistent with its predicted domain composition (TM + HAMP + MA) and typical MCP topology (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3, ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13). As for signaling context, KT2440 contains a substantial chemotaxis repertoire (e.g., 27 MCPs) and multiple chemosensory proteins/pathways, which provide the signaling “wiring” for McpS output into motility decisions (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 2-3, lugo2016molecularbasicof pages 43-46).

5) Pathways and network context (how McpS fits into KT2440 chemosensing)

5.1 Canonical chemotaxis signaling

In the standard architecture, MCPs (including McpS) regulate CheA kinase activity (typically via CheW), which then controls downstream response regulators to modulate motility (lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2, lugo2016molecularbasicof pages 43-46). While the retrieved texts emphasize that KT2440 contains multiple paralogs of chemotaxis components (e.g., CheA), the primary functional assignment for chemotaxis toward complex root exudates is associated with one chemosensory pathway (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2).

5.2 Chemosensory system multiplicity and ecological adaptation

KT2440 is reported to encode 27 chemoreceptors (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 2-3, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2). A dissertation-style synthesis further reports broader chemosensory inventory numbers in KT2440 such as 46 chemosensory proteins, including 27 MCPs, 6 CheW, 1 CheZ, and three paralogs each of CheA, CheB, and CheR, illustrating a complex multi-pathway signaling architecture beyond the minimal E. coli system (lugo2016molecularbasicof pages 43-46).

6) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

6.1 2024 expert synthesis: ligand-binding principles and ecological roles

Recent review work (2024) emphasizes that ligand recognition by MCPs frequently occurs through direct binding to common LBD architectures (e.g., 4-helix bundle and dCache), but also through SBP-mediated activation routes, highlighting a modern framework where chemotaxis can be integrated with nutrient uptake and metabolism (matilla2024bacterialaminoacid pages 1-2, matilla2024bacterialaminoacid pages 5-7). Although focused on amino acids, these reviews generalize to organic-acid chemotaxis by underscoring how multiple receptors with complementary spectra shape behavior and fitness in complex habitats (matilla2024bacterialaminoacid pages 1-2).

These authoritative sources also highlight ecological functions relevant to Pseudomonas lifestyles, including biofilm formation and root/seed colonization, reinforcing the interpretation that organic-acid sensing via receptors like McpS can improve access to nutrients in the rhizosphere (matilla2024bacterialaminoacid pages 1-2, matilla2024bacterialaminoacid pages 19-20).

6.2 2023–2024 updates specifically on McpS

Within the retrieved corpus, no 2023–2024 primary study was found that newly redefines McpS ligand specificity beyond the earlier biochemical/genetic characterization and later authoritative syntheses. Therefore, the best-supported McpS-specific ligand set remains grounded in the 2010 primary characterization and subsequent curated summaries and inventories (lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 2-3, ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13).

7) Current applications and real-world implementations

7.1 Rhizosphere/root colonization context

Chemotaxis to plant root exudates is described as an essential prerequisite for efficient root colonization, and KT2440 chemoreceptor gene expression is responsive to exudate concentration (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 2-3). In this context, McpS ligands (TCA intermediates/organic acids) are common constituents of root exudates and can function as nutrient cues and attractants, supporting a plausible ecological role for McpS-mediated taxis in rhizosphere navigation (lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 2-3).

7.2 Quantitative implementation-relevant data (root exudate experiments)

In maize root exudate (MRE) experiments with KT2440:
* Root exudate concentrations were operationally defined as 0.1×, 1×, and 10×, corresponding to 0.023, 0.23, and 2.3 g/L (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 3-5).
* Transcript levels were increased for 10 chemoreceptor genes at low MRE, while almost all receptor genes decreased at high MRE (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2).
* In capillary assays reported in the excerpt, example cell counts recovered from capillaries were 1960 ± 339; 2093 ± 191; 2133 ± 280; 1980 ± 254 (means ± SD, as presented) (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 11-12).
* For competitive colonization experiments, initial inocula ratios were approximately 50 ± 3% (means ± SD; six plants; P < 0.01 reported for comparisons) and recovery was performed 6 days after inoculation (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 11-12).

While these data do not isolate McpS alone, they represent real-world context where organic-acid chemotaxis receptors are part of a multi-receptor program for plant association (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 2-3, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2).

7.3 Biosensing and screening approaches using chemoreceptors (methodological applications)

A key implementation direction in the field is using engineered or hybrid receptors to accelerate ligand discovery for chemoreceptors and to create sensitive biosensors. An example approach uses hybrid sensor architectures and a reporter readout to screen ligands and identify new receptor–ligand relationships in soil bacteria, motivated by the difficulty of predicting ligands from sequence alone (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 9-10). Such methods, while not McpS-specific in the retrieved excerpts, represent a practical implementation pathway for expanding functional annotation to other MCPs in KT2440’s large receptor repertoire (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 9-10, lugo2016molecularbasicof pages 43-46).

8) Expert opinions and authoritative analysis

Ortega, Zhulin, and Krell (MMBR, 2017) provide an authoritative synthesis of bacterial chemoreceptor sensory repertoires and highlight McpS (PP_4658) as a key example of a chemoreceptor with an HBM LBD and a ligand spectrum centered on TCA intermediates and short-chain carboxylates (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13, ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 10-11). This framing positions McpS as both a biologically important receptor in P. putida and a structurally informative exemplar for how novel LBD architectures can expand sensory capability (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13).

Matilla & Krell (Journal of Bacteriology, 2024) emphasize modern concepts that chemotaxis often provides access to nutrients and that ligand binding can be direct or SBP-mediated, with multiple receptors combining to produce ecologically adaptive behaviors such as biofilm formation and root colonization (matilla2024bacterialaminoacid pages 1-2, matilla2024bacterialaminoacid pages 5-7). This supports interpreting McpS-driven organic-acid taxis as part of an integrated nutrient-acquisition and habitat-selection strategy in soil/rhizosphere environments (matilla2024bacterialaminoacid pages 1-2, matilla2024bacterialaminoacid pages 19-20).

10) Summary: functional annotation statement (evidence-based)

McpS (PP_4658; UniProt Q88E10) of Pseudomonas putida KT2440 is a transmembrane methyl-accepting chemotaxis protein that detects organic acids—particularly TCA-cycle intermediates and short-chain carboxylates such as butyrate (and summarized also as acetate)—through a distinctive helical bimodular (HBM) periplasmic ligand-binding domain, and couples this detection to chemotaxis signaling to drive directed motility toward these compounds. This function is supported by loss-of-function mutant phenotypes with restoration upon complementation, chemotaxis assays (agarose plug, capillary, swim plate), and direct ligand binding/biophysical stabilization of the isolated LBD measured by ITC and calorimetry/ultracentrifugation (lacal2010identificationofa pages 2-3, parales2013pseudomonasputidaf1 pages 12-15, lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2).

Citrate-binding nuance Review McpS was reported to bind citrate with low affinity, but not citrate-metal ion complexes; this distinguishes McpS from the separate citrate/metal receptor McpQ (ortega2017sensoryrepertoireof pages 11-13) Ortega et al., 2017
Pathway role Review, systems context McpS functions within the canonical chemotaxis cascade in which MCPs signal via CheA/CheW/CheY to alter flagellar behavior; KT2440 carries 27 chemoreceptors and multiple chemosensory pathways (lacal2010identificationofa pages 1-2, lugo2016molecularbasicof pages 43-46) Lacal et al., 2010; Corral-Lugo, 2016
Ecological/functional context Review, transcript/regulation study McpS ligands include abundant root-exudate organic acids; KT2440 chemotaxis receptors, including McpS, are part of a broader rhizosphere-colonization strategy, and KT2440 encodes 27 MCPs whose transcript levels can shift with maize root exudate concentration (lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 2-3, lopezfarfan2019concentrationdependenteffect pages 1-2) López-Farfán et al., 2019; Corral-Lugo, 2016
