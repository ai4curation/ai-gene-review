Gene Description

LapD (PP_0165; UniProt Q88RG5) is an inner-membrane c-di-GMP receptor with a LapD/MoxY periplasmic output domain, HAMP relay, and degenerate GGDEF/EAL signaling module. In Pseudomonas putida KT2440, LapD couples c-di-GMP sensing to sequestration of the periplasmic protease LapG, stabilizing the surface adhesin LapA and promoting surface attachment and biofilm formation. Recent work in KT2440 further shows that the phosphodiesterase DibA interacts with LapD at the membrane and tunes this LapD-centered biofilm control circuit.

Research report: Pseudomonas putida KT2440 lapD (PP_0165; UniProt Q88RG5)

Executive summary

In Pseudomonas putida KT2440, lapD (locus PP_0165, UniProt Q88RG5) encodes an inner-membrane, multi-domain c-di-GMP receptor that performs inside-out signaling to regulate a periplasmic protease (LapG) and thereby control the cell-surface retention versus release of the large adhesin LapA, a key determinant of early/irreversible surface attachment and biofilm formation/dispersion. The KT2440 LapD assignment is supported by explicit homology of PP0165 to characterized LapD proteins and by KT2440 studies of the LapA/LapG biofilm circuit whose post-translational “retention vs cleavage” switch is governed by c-di-GMP (hinsa2006biofilmformationby pages 4-5, gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 3-5, xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2).

0) Mandatory verification: gene/protein identity and symbol ambiguity

Is “lapD” the same as UniProt Q88RG5 in KT2440?

  • Yes, to the level supported by the retrieved primary literature: Hinsa & O’Toole report that P. fluorescens lapD is 71% identical to P. putida KT2440 ORF PP0165, and they predict PP0165 encodes an inner-membrane protein with HAMP, GGDEF, and EAL domains—matching the domain architecture provided for UniProt Q88RG5 (hinsa2006biofilmformationby pages 4-5).
  • Caveat: None of the retrieved KT2440 papers explicitly cites the UniProt accession Q88RG5 in text; linkage is via the KT2440 locus tag PP_0165/PP0165 and the lapD gene symbol (hinsa2006biofilmformationby pages 4-5). Therefore, claims about PP_0165 are made strictly through this documented homology and the consistent Lap-system context in P. putida.

1) Key concepts, definitions, and current understanding

1.1 c-di-GMP signaling and “inside-out” transmembrane control

Cyclic di-GMP (c-di-GMP) is a bacterial second messenger that biases cells toward sessile, biofilm-associated lifestyles. In the Lap system, c-di-GMP is sensed by LapD in the cytoplasm and converted into a periplasmic output—an archetypal inside-out signaling mechanism (chatterjee2013structuralandbiochemical pages 26-32, navarro2011structuralbasisfor pages 15-16).

1.2 What LapD is (conceptual definition)

LapD is best understood as a non-enzymatic c-di-GMP receptor with degenerate GGDEF/EAL modules (i.e., domains related to diguanylate cyclases and phosphodiesterases but lacking key catalytic motifs) that binds c-di-GMP and regulates downstream effectors rather than catalyzing c-di-GMP turnover (newell2009lapdisa pages 2-2).

1.3 LapD-LapG-LapA post-translational switch (core circuit)

The Lap circuit’s core output is the controlled cleavage of the N-terminus of the surface adhesin LapA by the periplasmic protease LapG:
* Low c-di-GMP: LapG is active and cleaves LapA’s N-terminus, releasing LapA from the surface and promoting dispersal (chatterjee2013structuralandbiochemical pages 26-32, xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2).
* High c-di-GMP: c-di-GMP-bound LapD adopts a conformation that sequesters/inhibits LapG in the periplasm, preventing LapA cleavage and maintaining LapA at the cell surface (chatterjee2013structuralandbiochemical pages 26-32, navarro2011structuralbasisfor pages 15-16, xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2).

Mechanistically, structural work defines LapD as autoinhibited in the absence of ligand and activated by c-di-GMP-induced conformational changes transmitted across the inner membrane to the periplasmic output domain (navarro2011structuralbasisfor pages 15-16).

2) Gene/protein features for KT2440 LapD (PP_0165/Q88RG5)

2.1 Domain architecture and predicted enzymatic activity

For KT2440 ORF PP0165 (LapD homolog), the experimentally supported domain model (via strong homology) is:
* Two transmembrane helices anchoring LapD in the inner membrane
* A cytoplasmic HAMP signaling module
* Cytoplasmic GGDEF and EAL-like modules with degenerate motifs (GGEF; and KVL in place of canonical EAL in one homologous context), consistent with signaling rather than catalysis (hinsa2006biofilmformationby pages 4-5).

Independent foundational work on LapD proteins demonstrates that LapD’s GGDEF/EAL modules can be enzymatically inactive while retaining c-di-GMP-binding capability, supporting the “receptor/effector” assignment (newell2009lapdisa pages 2-2).

2.2 Cellular localization

LapD is an inner-membrane protein with a periplasmic output domain that engages LapG; inner-membrane localization is experimentally supported by membrane fractionation of LapD in a closely related Pseudomonas Lap system (hinsa2006biofilmformationby pages 5-6). Structural and mechanistic descriptions place the LapG-binding output module in the periplasm and the c-di-GMP sensing module in the cytoplasm (navarro2011structuralbasisfor pages 15-16).

2.3 Binding partner and molecular output

LapD binds (sequesters) the periplasmic protease LapG in a c-di-GMP-dependent manner (navarro2011structuralbasisfor pages 15-16, collins2020frominputto pages 4-5). LapG in turn cleaves the N-terminus of LapA, controlling whether LapA is retained as a surface-associated adhesin or released (chatterjee2013structuralandbiochemical pages 26-32, collins2020frominputto pages 8-9).

3) Pathways and biological processes in P. putida KT2440

3.1 Biofilm formation and dispersal (LapA and LapG genetics)

In P. putida, LapA is required for robust biofilm formation and irreversible attachment, while LapG promotes dispersal:
* lapA and lapAG mutants form only a monolayer and have ~5-fold reduced irreversible attachment vs wild type (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 3-5).
* A lapG mutant forms more biofilm than wild type and displays altered surface properties; importantly, when intracellular c-di-GMP is lowered by expressing a heterologous phosphodiesterase (yhjH), wild-type biofilms disperse (biomass loss after induction) but lapG biofilms do not disperse, consistent with LapG being the required proteolytic “release” executor downstream of c-di-GMP changes (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 3-5).

These KT2440 results align with the conserved LapD-mediated model in which LapD’s c-di-GMP-dependent control of LapG governs LapA surface retention/release (chatterjee2013structuralandbiochemical pages 26-32, xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2).

3.2 Integration with c-di-GMP metabolism and transcriptional regulation

Beyond post-translational control of LapA localization, KT2440 uses c-di-GMP to regulate lapA transcription, linking signaling state to both LapA production and LapA retention:
* Experimentally increasing c-di-GMP (via diguanylate cyclase WspR) increased lapA promoter activity to ~2× control; decreasing c-di-GMP (via phosphodiesterase BifA) decreased lapA promoter activity to ~1/5 of control (xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2).
* The transcription factor FleQ mediates c-di-GMP-dependent regulation of lapA and associated biofilm phenotypes, integrating LapA expression with c-di-GMP signaling (xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2, xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 4-5).

3.3 Nutrient-stress control of dispersal via PDEs upstream of LapD

Nutrient starvation is a strong dispersal trigger in P. putida biofilms. A key upstream regulator is the c-di-GMP phosphodiesterase BifA, whose loss increases biofilm formation and reduces dispersal:
* In a P. putida ΔbifA mutant, biofilm levels increased ~2-fold and starvation-induced dispersal decreased, consistent with BifA-mediated c-di-GMP hydrolysis being an upstream step required to relieve LapD-mediated inhibition of LapG (jimenez‐fernandez2015thecdigmpphosphodiesterase pages 1-2).

4) Mechanistic and structural insights (authoritative sources)

4.1 Structural mechanism of LapD activation

High-impact structural work (in Pseudomonas fluorescens) elucidates a conserved LapD activation cycle:
* LapD is autoinhibited in the absence of ligand; c-di-GMP binding induces structural rearrangements and dimerization states that switch the periplasmic output domain into a LapG-binding-competent configuration (navarro2011structuralbasisfor pages 15-16).
* Reviews emphasize that a conserved periplasmic residue (e.g., W125) is critical for LapD–LapG interaction (collins2020frominputto pages 4-5).

5) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024 sources)

5.1 2023: Biofilm mechanisms and targetability

A 2023 review synthesizes mechanistic targets for disrupting biofilms (with emphasis on Pseudomonas), reinforcing that adhesion pathways and their regulators (including c-di-GMP signaling) remain high-value intervention points, especially for biofilm-associated persistence and antimicrobial tolerance (lapdUnknownyearroleoflapd pages 2-3).

5.2 2023: Expansion of LapD/LapG logic to other adhesin systems

A 2023 review focused on the P. aeruginosa biofilm matrix adhesin CdrA notes that LapG/LapD-like regulation extends beyond LapA systems, highlighting the broader relevance of periplasmic proteolysis controlled by c-di-GMP receptors as a conserved biofilm-control strategy (lapdUnknownyearroleoflapd pages 2-3).

5.3 2024: Systems-level/phenotypic switching perspectives

2024 thesis-level work referencing KT2440 highlights continued interest in tracking and controlling phenotypic switching associated with biofilm formation, where LapD/LapG-mediated adhesin regulation is used as a representative post-translational biofilm-control module (lapdUnknownyearroleoflapd pages 2-3).

6) Current applications and real-world implementations

6.1 Biofilm control as an engineering and intervention handle

Because LapD controls whether LapA-like adhesins remain surface-tethered (biofilm maintenance) or are proteolytically released (dispersal), LapD/LapG circuits provide a conceptual blueprint for:
* Biofilm dispersal strategies via manipulation of c-di-GMP (e.g., PDE induction) and downstream proteolysis pathways (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 3-5, jimenez‐fernandez2015thecdigmpphosphodiesterase pages 1-2).
* Anti-biofilm target discovery focusing on adhesion and signaling nodes in Pseudomonas biofilms (review synthesis) (lapdUnknownyearroleoflapd pages 2-3).

Limitations of this section: The retrieved sources emphasize mechanistic microbiology and do not provide KT2440-specific deployed industrial case studies explicitly attributing performance changes to LapD. Accordingly, applications are stated at the level of mechanism-enabled strategy, not claimed as established KT2440 industrial deployments.

P. putida dispersal phenotype via LapG/LapD-linked circuit In P. putida, lapA and lapAG mutants were deficient in biofilm formation and showed ~5-fold reduced irreversible attachment, while a lapG mutant formed more biofilm and resisted c-di-GMP-depletion-induced dispersal, consistent with LapD signaling through LapG to govern dispersal (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 3-5). Gjermansen et al. 2010 https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-2958.2009.06793.x
Transcriptional regulation by c-di-GMP/WspR/BifA In KT2440, increasing c-di-GMP by WspR raised lapA promoter activity to about 2× control, whereas lowering c-di-GMP with BifA reduced it to about 1/5 of control, linking LapD-controlled post-translational retention to a broader c-di-GMP transcriptional program (xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2). Xiao et al. 2016 https://doi.org/10.1111/1758-2229.12419
Regulatory connection: FleQ FleQ directly regulates lapA in KT2440 and is required for c-di-GMP-dependent control of lapA expression and the associated biofilm phenotypes, integrating LapD/LapG/LapA output with the global c-di-GMP transcriptional network (xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2, xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 4-5, martinezgilUnknownyearexpressionofthe pages 158-163). Xiao et al. 2016; Martínez-Gil et al. 2014 https://doi.org/10.1111/1758-2229.12419; https://doi.org/10.1128/JB.01287-13
Regulatory connection: BifA phosphodiesterase The phosphodiesterase BifA lowers c-di-GMP, and in P. putida ΔbifA mutants biofilm and pellicle formation increase about 2-fold with reduced starvation-induced dispersal, matching the model in which low c-di-GMP relieves LapD inhibition of LapG (jimenez‐fernandez2015thecdigmpphosphodiesterase pages 1-2). Jiménez-Fernández et al. 2015 https://doi.org/10.1111/1758-2229.12153
Regulatory connection: nutrient stress/stringent response Nutrient starvation promotes c-di-GMP hydrolysis and biofilm dispersal in P. putida by releasing LapG from LapD control, and later work placed this response downstream of the stringent response through positive regulation of bifA and coordinated repression of lapA/lap secretion genes (jimenez‐fernandez2015thecdigmpphosphodiesterase pages 1-2, gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 3-5). Gjermansen et al. 2010; Díaz-Salazar et al. 2017 https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-2958.2009.06793.x; https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-017-18518-0
Structural mechanism/current understanding Structural analyses define LapD as an autoinhibited transmembrane receptor whose c-di-GMP-bound state remodels the periplasmic output domain into a LapG-binding-competent conformation, a canonical “inside-out” signaling mechanism (navarro2011structuralbasisfor pages 15-16, navarro2011structuralbasisfor media 7c6a4e12). Navarro et al. 2011 https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1000588

Conclusions for functional annotation

Primary function: LapD (PP_0165/Q88RG5) is best annotated as an inner-membrane c-di-GMP receptor/transducer that regulates periplasmic proteolysis by LapG, thereby controlling LapA adhesin surface retention vs release, which in turn governs initial/irreversible attachment, biofilm formation, and biofilm dispersal in P. putida (hinsa2006biofilmformationby pages 4-5, gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 3-5, xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2).

Localization: Inner membrane (transmembrane receptor) with a periplasmic LapG-binding output and cytoplasmic c-di-GMP sensing module (hinsa2006biofilmformationby pages 4-5, navarro2011structuralbasisfor pages 15-16).

Pathway context: Integrated into a larger c-di-GMP network in KT2440 that includes PDEs such as BifA and transcriptional control via FleQ, coupling LapA expression and LapA retention to cellular c-di-GMP state and nutrient stress (xiao2016cdigmpregulatesthe pages 1-2, jimenez‐fernandez2015thecdigmpphosphodiesterase pages 1-2).

lapD notes

  • Q88RG5 / PP_0165 is not explicitly named lapD in the current UniProt flatfile, but the domain composition and family calls identify it as a LapD-like receptor: DR Gene3D; 6.20.270.20; LapD/MoxY periplasmic domain; 1. and DR NCBIfam; NF045673; c-di-GMPRcptLapD; 1. [file:PSEPK/lapD/lapD-uniprot.txt]
  • LapD is a membrane c-di-GMP receptor whose cytoplasmic ligand binding is coupled to periplasmic control of LapG. PMID:21304926
  • Activated LapD sequesters the periplasmic protease LapG and prevents cleavage of the surface adhesin LapA. PMID:21304920
  • LapD contains a HAMP domain and degenerate GGDEF/EAL signaling module, so the key conserved activity is c-di-GMP sensing rather than catalytic c-di-GMP turnover. PMID:21304926
  • In P. putida, DibA modulates the cell surface adhesin LapA level in a c-di-GMP receptor LapD-dependent manner and thereby inhibits biofilm formation. PMID:37927230
  • DibA interacts and colocalizes with LapD in the cell membrane, indicating that LapD sits in a broader local c-di-GMP signaling network in KT2440. PMID:37927230
