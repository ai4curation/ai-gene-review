Functional Annotation Report: paaK (PP_3279, UniProt Q88HS4)

Phenylacetate–Coenzyme A Ligase of Pseudomonas putida KT2440

1. Summary (Answer to the Research Question)

paaK (ordered locus PP_3279; UniProt Q88HS4) of Pseudomonas putida KT2440 encodes phenylacetate–coenzyme A ligase (phenylacetyl-CoA ligase, EC 6.2.1.30), an ATP-dependent (AMP-forming) acyl-CoA synthetase of the ANL/adenylate-forming (AMP-dependent synthetase/ligase) superfamily. It catalyzes the first, committed step of the aerobic phenylacetate (PA) catabolic pathway, activating the chemically inert aromatic carboxylic acid phenylacetate to its coenzyme A thioester:

phenylacetate + ATP + CoA → phenylacetyl-CoA (PA-CoA) + AMP + PPi (Mg²⁺-dependent)

The enzyme is a cytoplasmic, soluble ~48-kDa monomer that acts on intracellular phenylacetate. Its product, phenylacetyl-CoA, is the central convergent intermediate (the "phenylacetyl-CoA catabolon") into which many peripheral aromatic pathways (phenylalanine, 2-phenylethylamine, styrene, n-phenylalkanoic acids, environmental aromatics) funnel; PA-CoA is then processed by a distinct ring-epoxidizing/CoA-dependent route to acetyl-CoA and succinyl-CoA for entry into central metabolism. PA-CoA is also the physiological inducer of the pathway.

The gene identity is unambiguous and fully consistent with the UniProt annotation: symbol paaK, EC 6.2.1.30, phenylacetyl-CoA ligase family, AMP-dependent synthetase/ligase domains (IPR000873, IPR028154, IPR045851, IPR042099), in P. putida KT2440.

3. Primary Molecular Function — The Catalyzed Reaction

3.1 Reaction and mechanism

PaaK is an AMP-forming ligase of the ANL (Acyl-CoA synthetase / NRPS adenylation / Luciferase) superfamily. Like all members, it works by a two-step, adenylate-forming mechanism: 1. Adenylation: phenylacetate + ATP → phenylacetyl-adenylate (PA-AMP) + PPi. 2. Thioesterification: PA-AMP + CoA → phenylacetyl-CoA + AMP.

The domain architecture reported for Q88HS4 — a large N-terminal adenylation domain (ANL_N; IPR042099) plus a small mobile C-terminal domain (AMP-binding C; IPR045851/IPR028154) — is the structural hallmark of this superfamily, in which the C-terminal domain rotates (~140°) between an "adenylation" and a "thioester-forming" conformation ("domain alternation") to catalyze the two half-reactions.

3.2 Direct biochemical evidence (purified enzyme)

The enzyme was first purified to homogeneity (513-fold) from Pseudomonas putida and biochemically characterized (Martínez-Blanco et al. 1990, 2324116): - Reaction requirements: ATP, CoA, phenylacetate, and Mg²⁺ (as MgCl₂). - Size: single ~48 ± 1 kDa polypeptide (monomer) by SDS-PAGE. - Optimum: pH 8.2, 30 °C. - Apparent Kₘ: ATP 9.7 mM, CoA 1.0 mM, phenylacetate 16.5 mM. - Inhibition: by Cu²⁺, Zn²⁺, Hg²⁺ and by thiol-reactive reagents (N-ethylmaleimide, DTNB, p-chloromercuribenzoate) — indicating a functionally important cysteine/thiol.

A sensitive coupled fluorometric assay (adenylate kinase / pyruvate kinase / lactate dehydrogenase, monitoring NADH) confirmed the AMP-forming stoichiometry (Rodríguez-Aparicio et al. 1991, 2009287).

The homologous PaaK from Thermus thermophilus provided a clean in-vitro demonstration of the exact reaction and higher substrate affinities: - PA + CoA + MgATP → PA-CoA + AMP + MgPPi, Vmax 24 µmol/min/mg, Kₘ ATP 6 µM, CoA 30 µM, PA 50 µM (Erb et al. 2008, 18414813).

3.3 Gene cloning confirms function

The gene encoding this enzyme (pcl in P. putida U, ortholog of KT2440 paaK) was cloned by Tn5 mutagenesis and shown to encode the 48-kDa PA-CoA ligase; heterologous expression in E. coli conferred PA-CoA ligase activity and the ability to grow on phenylacetate as sole carbon source (Miñambres et al. 1996, 8969218). In E. coli W, paaK was identified within the paaABCDEFGHIJK catabolic operon as the gene "coding for a phenylacetyl-CoA ligase that catalyzes the activation of PA to phenylacetyl-CoA" (Ferrández et al. 1998, 9748275).

4. Substrate Specificity

PaaK is highly specific for unsubstituted phenylacetate: - The purified P. putida enzyme does not use 3-hydroxy-, 4-hydroxy-, or 3,4-dihydroxyphenylacetate as substrates, and shows a specificity distinct from other acyl-CoA-activating enzymes; hydroxy-/methyl-phenylacetyl derivatives and short/long-chain fatty acids (>C5), acetate, butyrate and succinate neither serve as substrates nor induce the enzyme (Martínez-Blanco et al. 1990, 2324116). - T. thermophilus PaaK is highly specific toward phenylacetate, with only low activity on 4-hydroxyphenylacetate (Erb et al. 2008, 18414813).

Comparative structural/kinetic work in Burkholderia cenocepacia (which carries two ligases, PaaK1 and PaaK2) rationalizes this specificity: the specific enzyme has a compact binding pocket, whereas the more promiscuous PaaK1 has a larger binding pocket that can accommodate hydroxylated PA derivatives (3-OHPA, 4-OHPA) (Imolorhe & Cardona 2011, 22919580). P. putida KT2440's PaaK corresponds to the strict phenylacetate-specific type.

Interpretation: Narrow specificity ensures PaaK channels only genuine phenylacetate — the hub metabolite of the catabolon — into the CoA-dependent pathway, avoiding wasteful activation of related aromatics that belong to other routes.

5. Subcellular Localization / Where It Acts

  • PaaK is a soluble, cytoplasmic enzyme. It is a monomeric globular protein with no signal peptide, membrane-spanning region, or secretion signal; the purified protein behaves as a soluble 48-kDa species (2324116).
  • It acts on the intracellular pool of phenylacetate. Phenylacetate first enters the cell via a dedicated, energy-dependent phenylacetic acid transport system (PATS) (active transport, Kₘ ≈ 13 µM, driven by the membrane energization/electron-transport state), after which cytoplasmic PaaK activates it (Schleissner et al. 1994, 8002592).

Thus the enzyme performs its catalytic function in the cytoplasm, at the interface between substrate uptake and downstream CoA-dependent ring processing.

6. Pathway Context and Biological Process

6.1 The phenylacetyl-CoA "catabolon"

PaaK is the committed entry enzyme of the aerobic phenylacetate degradation pathway — a CoA-dependent "hybrid" pathway that is fundamentally different from classical aerobic aromatic catabolism (which uses ring-hydroxylating dioxygenases on free aromatic acids). This pathway operates in ~16% of sequenced bacteria, including E. coli and P. putida (Teufel et al. 2010, 20660314).

In KT2440, genomic analysis identified the phenylacetate (pha/paa) pathway as one of four central aromatic catabolic routes (alongside the protocatechuate and catechol β-ketoadipate branches and the homogentisate pathway) (Jiménez et al. 2002, 12534466). Multiple peripheral (upper) pathways converge on phenylacetyl-CoA, making PaaK the funnel point for: - L-phenylalanine (via phenylpyruvate/phenylacetaldehyde → phenylacetate), - 2-phenylethylamine (via the mao/amine oxidase route), - styrene (via the sty upper pathway, 11959447), - n-phenylalkanoic acids and other environmental aromatics/pollutants.

6.2 Steps downstream of PaaK (fate of the product PA-CoA)

Once PaaK forms phenylacetyl-CoA, the ring is attacked while activated as a thioester (Teufel et al. 2010, 20660314; 2011, 21296885; 2012, 22398448): 1. PaaABC(E) — a multicomponent, di-iron monooxygenase/epoxidase — converts PA-CoA to ring-1,2-epoxyphenylacetyl-CoA (and can deoxygenate it back, an intrinsic detox mechanism). 2. The epoxide isomerizes to a seven-membered O-heterocyclic enol ether, oxepin-CoA. 3. PaaZ (bifunctional (R)-enoyl-CoA hydratase + NADP⁺ aldehyde dehydrogenase) hydrolytically cleaves the ring to 3-oxo-5,6-dehydrosuberyl-CoA. 4. β-oxidation–like steps (PaaFGHIJ homologs) degrade this to acetyl-CoA and succinyl-CoA, which enter the TCA cycle.

6.3 Regulation

  • The product PA-CoA is the true physiological inducer of the pathway: it antagonizes the PaaX transcriptional repressor, de-repressing the catabolic operon (Ferrández et al. 1998, 9748275). This places PaaK's product at the center of pathway induction.
  • In KT2440, phenylacetate metabolism is under carbon catabolite repression (CCR) by glucose/gluconate, acting at the transcriptional level. The intracellular signal is 2-keto-3-deoxy-6-phosphogluconate (KDPG): an edd mutant (no KDPG) shows high phenylacetyl-CoA ligase activity even in glucose+PAA (CCR abolished), whereas wild type is repressed (Kim et al. 2009, 19406896). PA-CoA ligase activity and paaK transcription track pathway induction across conditions (O'Leary et al. 2002, 11959447; Kim et al. 2007, 18156794).

6.4 Biotechnological/physiological relevance

Because PaaK generates phenylacetyl-CoA, expressing the P. putida ligase gene in Penicillium chrysogenum increased the pool of this direct precursor and boosted benzylpenicillin (penicillin G) biosynthesis ~1.8–2.2-fold (Miñambres et al. 1996, 8969218) — direct evidence that the enzyme's product is phenylacetyl-CoA and that PaaK is the rate-relevant activating step.

8. Limitations and Future Directions

  1. Direct structural data for the P. putida KT2440 PaaK (Q88HS4) itself have not been reported; mechanistic/structural inferences draw on homologs (Thermus, Burkholderia, E. coli) and the ANL superfamily. An experimental crystal/cryo-EM structure of the KT2440 enzyme with PA-AMP/CoA would confirm active-site residues and the domain-alternation mechanism.
  2. Most quantitative kinetics derive from P. putida U and thermophilic/other homologs; strain-KT2440-specific kinetic constants and any second paralog should be verified from the KT2440 genome.
  3. In-vivo flux control by PaaK versus the transporter (PATS) and the downstream oxygenase under different carbon regimes merits systems-level study.
