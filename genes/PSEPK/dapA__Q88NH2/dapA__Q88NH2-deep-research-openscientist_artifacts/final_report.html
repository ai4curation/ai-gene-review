1. Summary (answer to the research question)

Q88NH2 is a cytoplasmic biosynthetic aldolase-type lyase that catalyzes the first committed step of the diaminopimelate (DAP) / L‑lysine branch of the aspartate amino-acid pathway. It condenses pyruvate with L‑aspartate‑4‑semialdehyde (ASA) to form (4S)‑4‑hydroxy‑2,3,4,5‑tetrahydro‑(2S)‑dipicolinate (HTPA), the committed precursor of both meso-diaminopimelate (a cross-linking residue of Gram-negative peptidoglycan) and L‑lysine. Catalysis proceeds through a Schiff-base (ping-pong) mechanism using a strictly conserved active-site lysine; the enzyme is a homotetramer of (β/α)₈ TIM-barrel subunits with active sites built across a tight-dimer interface. The gene product is a soluble cytoplasmic enzyme. Its identity as a bona fide DapA-family enzyme is directly supported by the presence of the PROSITE Schiff-base-forming aldolase signature and the catalytic lysine in the Q88NH2 sequence.

The gene symbol is unambiguous and consistent with the UniProt description: "dapA" here denotes the canonical DHDPS/HTPA-synthase, and the domain architecture, family membership, and conserved catalytic motifs all align with the extensive DHDPS literature. Note that most detailed biochemistry comes from orthologs (E. coli, M. tuberculosis, B. anthracis, C. jejuni, plants); direct experimental characterization of the P. putida KT2440 enzyme itself is limited, so organism-specific kinetic/regulatory details below are inferred from family conservation and sequence analysis.

2. Primary function: the reaction catalyzed and substrate specificity

Reaction (EC 4.3.3.7):

pyruvate + L‑aspartate‑4‑semialdehyde → (4S)‑4‑hydroxy‑2,3,4,5‑tetrahydro‑(2S)‑dipicolinate (HTPA) + H₂O

DapA "converts pyruvate and L‑aspartate‑4‑semialdehyde to 4‑hydroxy‑2,3,4,5‑tetrahydrodipicolinic acid" in a multistep reaction (Schmitz et al., 2020, 32356521). Historically the enzyme was named dihydrodipicolinate synthase and assigned EC 4.2.1.52 on the assumption that the product was 2,3‑dihydrodipicolinate; it is now recognized that the direct enzymatic product is the cyclic hydroxy‑tetrahydrodipicolinate (HTPA), and that conversion to dihydrodipicolinate occurs subsequently (spontaneous dehydration and/or the action of the next enzyme DapB). Hence the modern name/EC (4‑hydroxy‑tetrahydrodipicolinate synthase, EC 4.3.3.7). The two substrates are highly specific: the keto-donor is pyruvate (which forms the covalent Schiff-base intermediate) and the aldehyde acceptor is L‑aspartate‑4‑semialdehyde. Reported bacterial kinetics are in the sub-millimolar to low-millimolar K_M range (e.g., M. tuberculosis: K_M^ASA ≈ 0.43 mM, K_M^pyruvate ≈ 0.17 mM; Kefala et al., 2008, 18062777).

Biological role of the product: DHDPS "catalyses the first committed step in the biosynthetic pathway yielding two essential bacterial metabolites, meso-diaminopimelate (DAP) and (S)-lysine" (Domigan et al., 2009, 19595801) — i.e., HTPA is the branch-point precursor that ultimately gives meso-DAP (peptidoglycan cross-link) and L-lysine (protein synthesis).

3. Catalytic mechanism

DHDPS is a class I (Schiff-base) aldolase-type lyase operating by a ping-pong kinetic mechanism (Skovpen & Palmer, 2013, 23902434):

  1. Pyruvate binds first and forms a Schiff base (imine) with the ε-amino group of a strictly conserved active-site lysine. Crystallography of the M. tuberculosis ortholog captured the enzyme "in complex with its substrate, pyruvate, covalently bonded through a Schiff base to the catalytic l-lysine" (Rosa et al., 2025, 41022234).
  2. The resulting enamine performs aldol addition onto the aldehyde carbon of ASA.
  3. Transimination/cyclization and dehydration release the cyclic product HTPA and regenerate the free lysine.

A catalytic proton-relay triad Tyr–Thr–Tyr (e.g., Tyr143–Thr54–Tyr117 in M. tuberculosis; the equivalent Tyr133–Thr44–Tyr107 in E. coli) shuttles protons during catalysis; importantly the second tyrosine is donated across the tight-dimer interface, which is why the oligomeric state is functionally essential (Kefala et al., 2008, 18062777).

Direct sequence evidence in Q88NH2. Analysis of the retrieved 295-aa sequence located the PROSITE Schiff-base-forming aldolase active-site signature (PS00665) within the segment TVPNIIGIKE, placing the catalytic Schiff-base lysine at position ~164 (the P. putida equivalent of E. coli Lys161). A global pairwise alignment of Q88NH2 against the experimentally characterized E. coli DHDPS (UniProt P0A6L2) gave 58.6% identity (170/290 positions) with one-to-one conservation of every catalytic residue:

The E. coli triad was experimentally validated by site-directed mutagenesis — Y133F, T44V and Y107F each "have substantially reduced activity, consistent with the catalytic triad hypothesis" (Dobson, Valegård & Gerrard 2004, 15066435). Their complete conservation in Q88NH2 elevates the annotation from family-level inference to residue-level mechanistic evidence that Q88NH2 is a fully functional DHDPS using the identical mechanism.

Family specificity (not a mis-annotated aldolase). The DapA/NAL superfamily includes several Schiff-base aldolases that share the catalytic lysine but act on different substrates (e.g., N-acetylneuraminate lyase). Q88NH2 is 58.6% identical to E. coli DHDPS but only 29.9% to E. coli N-acetylneuraminate lyase (NanA, P0A6L4) and 31.1% to Thermotoga DHDPS — i.e., it is specifically a DHDPS, not a NAL-type aldolase.

4. Structure, oligomeric state, and subcellular localization

  • Fold: Each subunit comprises an N-terminal (β/α)₈ TIM barrel followed by a C-terminal α-helical domain (Kefala et al., 2008, 18062777), consistent with Q88NH2's InterPro annotations (Aldolase_TIM IPR013785; DapA IPR005263/IPR002220).
  • Active site: Located at the C-terminal face of the barrel and formed by residues from two adjacent subunits across the dimer interface (18062777).
  • Quaternary structure: The functional unit is a homotetramer ("dimer of dimers"), demonstrated to be "tetrameric in solution" by analytical ultracentrifugation for orthologous DHDPS enzymes (18062777). The tight-dimer is the minimal catalytic unit; tetramerization further stabilizes the enzyme.
  • Localization: DHDPS is a soluble cytoplasmic enzyme. Q88NH2 contains no signal peptide or transmembrane segment, consistent with a cytosolic amino-acid biosynthetic enzyme that acts on soluble small-molecule substrates.

5. Pathway context and regulation

Position in metabolism. DapA acts downstream of aspartate kinase and aspartate-semialdehyde dehydrogenase (which produce ASA). By committing ASA to cyclic HTPA, DapA diverts flux into the DAP/lysine branch (as opposed to the homoserine → methionine/threonine branch). Downstream, HTPA is reduced by DapB (4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate reductase) and processed via the DAP sub-pathway to meso-DAP, with DapF/LysA yielding L-lysine. Thus DapA is the gatekeeper of lysine and cell-wall DAP biosynthesis, "essential for bacterial growth and ... considered a target for novel antibiotics" (Skovpen & Palmer, 2013, 23902434).

KEGG pathway placement in P. putida KT2440. KEGG assigns PP_1237/dapA-I to orthology K01714 (EC 4.3.3.7) in Lysine biosynthesis (ppu00300), Biosynthesis of amino acids (ppu01230), and Monobactam biosynthesis (ppu00261). It participates in two modules — M00016 (succinyl-DAP pathway) and M00527 (DAP-aminotransferase pathway) — that both place DapA (K01714) immediately downstream of aspartate-4-semialdehyde dehydrogenase (Asd, K00133) and upstream of DapB (K00215). The routes then diverge and reconverge: - Succinyl-DAP (M00016): DapB → DapD (K00674) → DapC/ArgD (K00821/K14267) → DapE (K01439) → DapF (K01778) → LysA (K01586) - DAP-aminotransferase (M00527): DapB → DapL (K10206) → DapF (K01778) → LysA (K01586)

DapA is the single committed entry step feeding both routes, which converge on DAP epimerase (DapF) and meso-DAP decarboxylase (LysA) to yield L-lysine; the meso-DAP intermediate is drawn off for peptidoglycan cross-linking.

Genomic organization. PP_1237 (nt 1,414,728–1,415,615) is not part of a dap operon: its flanking genes are functionally unrelated (PP_1238 putative lipoprotein, PP_1239 metallo-β-lactamase-family protein, PP_1240 purC). Like many scattered bacterial dap genes, dapA-I appears to be an isolated, monocistronic transcription unit rather than co-transcribed with downstream dap genes — consistent with regulation occurring at the enzyme level (allosteric feedback) rather than through operonic co-expression.

Allosteric regulation. In many organisms the enzyme is feedback-inhibited by L-lysine: "In many organisms, lysine binds allosterically to DapA, causing negative feedback, thus making the enzyme an important regulatory component of the pathway" (Schmitz et al., 2020, 32356521). Lysine binds cooperatively at a cleft on the tight-dimer 2-fold axis (Hill n > 2 in C. jejuni; Atkinson et al., 2013, 23354837 for the structural basis in plants). However, lysine sensitivity is not universal — the M. tuberculosis enzyme "is insensitive to feedback inhibition by (S)-lysine" because the binding site is not conserved (18062777), and the B. anthracis enzyme is likewise not lysine-inhibited (19595801).

Prediction for Q88NH2 (sequence-based). In E. coli DHDPS the L-lysine allosteric pocket at the tight-dimer interface is lined by Tyr106, His56, Glu84, Asn80 and Ala49. My alignment shows all of these are conserved in Q88NH2 (His56→His56, Glu84→Glu87, Tyr106→Tyr109, Asn80→Asn83, Ala49→Ala49). Because it is precisely the loss of these residues that abolishes feedback in M. tuberculosis/B. anthracis, their full conservation predicts that P. putida DapA (Q88NH2) retains E. coli-type L-lysine feedback inhibition. This is a bioinformatic inference; direct kinetic confirmation for the P. putida enzyme has not been reported. Notably, among the three P. putida paralogs (§3b), Q88NH2/dapA-I is the only copy that retains the full lysine-binding pocket, suggesting it carries the feedback-regulated "housekeeping" role while dapA-II may provide unregulated backup activity.

Physiological/biotechnological relevance. Because DAP and lysine are indispensable (cell-wall cross-linking and protein synthesis) and the pathway is absent in mammals, DHDPS is a validated antibacterial and herbicide target (19595801, 23902434), and engineered lysine-feedback-insensitive DHDPS variants are used to boost lysine yields in plants and industrial strains (e.g., 39392917).

7. Limitations and future directions

  • No direct experimental characterization of the P. putida KT2440 enzyme was found in the literature searched; kinetic constants, structure, and regulation are inferred from orthologs and sequence analysis.
  • Paralogy is confirmed (three EC 4.3.3.7 copies: PP_1237/dapA-I, PP_2639/dapA-II, PP_2036). Residue analysis marks Q88NH2 as the canonical DHDPS and dapA-II as a likely lysine-insensitive backup, but the in vivo activity, expression, and genetic essentiality/redundancy of each copy (e.g., via RB-TnSeq fitness data, 38323821) remain to be tested experimentally. Whether dapA-II can rescue a dapA-I knockout is unknown.
  • Lysine feedback sensitivity of Q88NH2 should be tested directly (enzyme kinetics ± L-lysine; identification of the allosteric-site residues by alignment to lysine-sensitive vs. -insensitive orthologs).
  • A structural model (e.g., AlphaFold) could pinpoint the catalytic triad (Tyr/Thr/Tyr) and putative allosteric cleft to firm up the regulatory prediction.
