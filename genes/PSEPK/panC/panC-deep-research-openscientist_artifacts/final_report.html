Functional Annotation of panC (PP_4700, UniProt Q88DW8) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

1. Summary

panC (ordered locus PP_4700; UniProt Q88DW8) of Pseudomonas putida KT2440 encodes pantothenate synthetase (also called pantoate—β-alanine ligase or pantoate-activating enzyme; EC 6.3.2.1; KEGG ortholog K01918). Its primary function is to catalyze the final, committed step of de novo pantothenate (vitamin B5) biosynthesis: the ATP-dependent condensation of (R)-pantoate and β-alanine to form pantothenate, proceeding through a covalent pantoyl-adenylate reaction intermediate and releasing AMP and pyrophosphate. Pantothenate is the obligate precursor of the 4′-phosphopantetheine moiety of coenzyme A (CoA) and acyl carrier protein (ACP), making PanC an essential feeder of central CoA/ACP-dependent metabolism. The enzyme functions as a soluble, cytoplasmic homodimer requiring a divalent metal cofactor (Mg²⁺ or Mn²⁺). The gene is co-organized with panB in an overlapping panB–panC operon, coupling two consecutive pathway steps.

No experimental study has been published on the P. putida KT2440 protein specifically; the annotation is transferred with high confidence from closely related, biochemically and structurally characterized orthologs (E. coli, M. tuberculosis, plants, fungi) supported by ~53–55% sequence identity, an intact catalytic motif, conserved substrate-binding residues, and native operon context.

3. Primary Function: Reaction Catalyzed and Mechanism

3.1 Overall reaction

PanC catalyzes:

(R)-pantoate + β-alanine + ATP → (R)-pantothenate + AMP + pyrophosphate (PPi)

This is the "ATP-dependent condensation of pantoate and beta-alanine to form pantothenate" (Wang & Eisenberg, 2006, 16460002).

3.2 Two-step (ordered) mechanism via a pantoyl-adenylate intermediate

Crystallographic "snapshots" of the M. tuberculosis ortholog captured the enzyme bound to substrates (ATP/AMPCPP, pantoate), the covalent reaction intermediate, and product (AMP), establishing an ordered, two-step mechanism (Wang & Eisenberg, 2006, 16460002):

  1. Adenylation: pantoate attacks the α-phosphate of ATP, forming enzyme-bound pantoyl-adenylate + PPi.
  2. Aminolysis: the amino group of β-alanine attacks the activated pantoyl carbonyl, forming the amide bond of pantothenate and releasing AMP.

A key mechanistic feature is that β-alanine can bind only after the pantoyl-adenylate intermediate has formed — "binding of beta-alanine in the active site cavity can occur only after formation of the pantoyl adenylate intermediate," with "the phosphate group of AMP [serving] as an anchor for the binding of beta-alanine" (Wang & Eisenberg, 2006, 16460002). This adenylate-forming chemistry (activation of a carboxylate as an acyl-adenylate) is characteristic of the pantothenate synthetase family and is echoed by the ATP-sulfurylase-like nucleotide-binding motif noted in the KEGG annotation.

3.3 Conserved catalytic residues

Structural/mutational analysis of the M. tuberculosis enzyme identifies conserved active-site residues His44, His47, Asn69, Gln72, Lys160, Gln164 as important for substrate binding and pantoyl-adenylate formation; alanine substitutions (e.g., H47A, K160A) weaken ATP binding and reduce catalysis (Pandey et al., 2018, 29343701). In P. putida Q88DW8 the corresponding catalytic HxGH motif is intact (His34-Ser35-Gly36-His37), and UniProt annotates His37 as the catalytic proton donor, along with ATP-binding residues (30–37, 149–152, 178, 186–189), (R)-pantoate-binding residues (Gln61, Gln155), and a β-alanine-binding residue (Gln61) — matching the two-substrate/ATP active-site architecture.

4. Substrate Specificity and Cofactor

  • Strict substrate requirement: the purified enzyme "requires d-pantoate, beta-alanine and ATP for activity" (Genschel et al., 1999, 10417331).
  • Stereo/chemo-selectivity: "Neither oxopantoate nor pantoyl-lactone can replace pantoate as substrate," demonstrating tight specificity for reduced, open-chain D-(R)-pantoate (Genschel et al., 1999, 10417331).
  • Relative affinities: the enzyme "has a higher affinity for pantoate (Km 45 µM) than for beta-alanine (Km 990 µM)" (Genschel et al., 1999, 10417331), with kcat ≈ 0.63 s⁻¹ and uncompetitive substrate inhibition at pantoate > ~1 mM.
  • Metal cofactor: the enzyme "is most active in the presence of magnesium or manganese" (37578562), consistent with Mg·ATP being the true substrate for adenylate formation.

These kinetic parameters derive from characterized orthologs; given ~53–55% identity and full active-site conservation, they are expected to hold, at least qualitatively, for P. putida PanC.

5. Biological Process / Pathway Context

5.1 Final step of the four-enzyme pantothenate pathway

Bacterial de novo pantothenate biosynthesis "compris[es] four enzymic reactions catalysed by ketopantoate hydroxymethyltransferase (KPHMT), L-aspartate-alpha-decarboxylase (ADC), pantothenate synthetase (PS) and ketopantoate reductase (KPR) encoded by panB, panD, panC and panE genes" (Chakauya et al., 2008, 18726075). PanC performs the terminal condensation. Its two substrates are supplied by the other pathway branches: - (R)-pantoate branch: α-ketoisovalerate (from branched-chain amino acid metabolism) → ketopantoate (PanB, EC 2.1.2.11) → pantoate (PanE). - β-alanine branch: L-aspartate → β-alanine (PanD, aspartate-1-decarboxylase).

Genetics confirm the gene–function assignment: in E. coli/S. typhimurium, "the panC mutants lack pantothenate synthetase," each showing a single enzyme defect (Cronan et al., 1982, 7037743).

5.2 Downstream role: essential precursor of CoA and ACP

"Pantothenate (vitamin B5) is the precursor of the 4′-phosphopantetheine moiety of coenzyme A and acyl-carrier protein" (Chakauya et al., 2008, 18726075). Pantothenate "is required for the synthesis of coenzyme A (CoA)" (Riske et al., 2023, 37762222). Because CoA and holo-ACP are indispensable for fatty-acid synthesis, the TCA cycle, and myriad acyl-transfer reactions, the pantothenate/CoA pathway is essential and a validated antimicrobial drug target (Wang & Eisenberg, 2006, 16460002; Pepi et al., 2022, 35015509). This is especially relevant for the metabolically versatile, solvent-tolerant lifestyle of P. putida, which relies heavily on CoA/acyl-CoA metabolism.

5.3 Genomic organization — panB–panC operon

In P. putida KT2440, KEGG genome coordinates place panB (PP_4699) at 5,340,894–5,341,694 and panC (PP_4700) at 5,341,691–5,342,554 on the same strand; the two ORFs overlap by 4 bp, the hallmark of translational coupling within a single operon. This mirrors the E. coli cluster where "panB and panC lie adjacent to one another" (Merkel & Nichols, 1996, 8837478). The overlap implies coordinated, stoichiometric expression of two consecutive pathway enzymes.

6. Localization

Pantothenate synthetase is a soluble, cytoplasmic enzyme. Characterized plant orthologs localize to the cytosol with "no N-terminal extension" ("The Lotus and rice PSs are therefore likely to be cytosolic"; Genschel et al., 1999, 10417331); bacterial PanC likewise has no signal peptide or membrane-spanning region (Q88DW8 is a 287-aa soluble protein). The enzyme is active as a homodimer ("Recombinant PS from Lotus … is a dimer," 10417331; the M. tuberculosis enzyme is also dimeric). Thus P. putida PanC carries out its reaction in the cytoplasm, where its substrates (pantoate, β-alanine, Mg·ATP) and downstream CoA-biosynthetic enzymes reside.
