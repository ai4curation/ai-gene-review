Gene Description

PedI is the KT2440 Ped-pathway aldehyde dehydrogenase encoded by PP_2680 (UniProt Q88JG9), currently labeled aldB-II in UniProt. Comparative analysis of the KT2440 alcohol-oxidation region showed that PP_2680 is 97% identical to PedI from Pseudomonas putida U and 88% identical to Pseudomonas aeruginosa ExaC, and the KT2440 authors explicitly adopted Ped nomenclature for the homologous PP_2664-PP_2680 cluster. PedI is therefore best interpreted as the aldehyde dehydrogenase step of the Ped alcohol oxidation system, likely oxidizing phenylacetaldehyde in 2-phenylethanol catabolism and participating more broadly in aldehyde oxidation during alcohol-derived carbon metabolism, including the ethylene glycol response described in KT2440.

Research report: functional annotation of pedI / aldB-II (UniProt Q88JG9) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

0) Identity verification (critical disambiguation)

The symbol pedI is used in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 to denote an aldehyde dehydrogenase encoded by PP_2680, and recent work explicitly equates this gene with aldB-II (“aldB-II (also annotated pedI; locus PP_2680)”). (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 5-6, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15, thompson2020fattyacidand pages 9-12)

Visual evidence from the ethylene glycol metabolism study includes tables and schematics that list PedI = PP_2680 within the ped gene cluster and annotate it as an aldehyde dehydrogenase. (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism media debe4b15, muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism media 95c30952)

Accordingly, this report focuses strictly on UniProt Q88JG9 (AldB-II; PP_2680; pedI) in P. putida KT2440; no conflicting “pedI” identity in this organism is supported by the retrieved evidence. (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15, muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 5-6)

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Aldehyde dehydrogenases (ALDHs)

Aldehyde dehydrogenases catalyze oxidation of aldehydes to the corresponding carboxylic acids (general ALDH function), typically using pyridine nucleotides as redox cofactors. In P. putida KT2440, aldehyde oxidation is biologically important both for catabolism (channeling carbon) and for detoxification (aldehydes such as formaldehyde and glycolaldehyde are reactive/toxic intermediates). (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 7-8, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 17-19)

1.2 The ped (PQQ-dependent ethanol/diol) oxidation system

In P. putida KT2440, the ped gene cluster encodes periplasmic PQQ-dependent alcohol dehydrogenases (notably PedE/PedH) plus an associated aldehyde dehydrogenase (PedI) and regulatory components, supporting oxidation of a broad range of volatile alcohols/diols and downstream aldehyde processing. (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 7-8, li2020unraveling14butanediolmetabolism pages 1-2, muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 5-6)

2) Gene/protein summary for pedI (UniProt Q88JG9)

2.1 Names and identifiers

  • Protein/gene: Aldehyde dehydrogenase AldB-II / PedI
  • Locus tag: PP_2680
  • UniProt: Q88JG9
  • Enzyme class: aldehyde dehydrogenase family; UniProt annotates EC 1.2.1.3 (aldehyde dehydrogenase [NAD+]). (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15, muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 5-6)

2.2 Genomic context and regulation

PedI (PP_2680) is part of the ped region, co-induced and co-regulated with PedE/PedH and the response regulator/sensor components PedR1/PedS1 in ethylene glycol-grown cells, consistent with a coordinated alcohol/aldehyde oxidation module. (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 5-6, muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 4-5)

A schematic of the ped cluster organization (including pedI/PP_2680) is provided in the 2012 ethylene glycol study figures/tables. (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism media 95c30952)

3) Primary biochemical function: reaction and substrate scope

3.1 Best-supported physiological reactions (in vivo)

(A) Ethylene glycol pathway (C2 diol oxidation):
In KT2440, ethylene glycol metabolism is described as initiation by PQQ-dependent ADHs PedE/PedH, followed by oxidation steps involving two ALDHs (PedI and PP_0545), producing glyoxylic acid via intermediates glycolaldehyde and glycolic acid. (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 7-8)

A pathway schematic in that work depicts the flow from ethylene glycol toward glyoxylic acid and highlights roles for PedE/PedH and the ALDH steps, consistent with PedI acting on aldehyde intermediates in this pathway. (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism media 63acbe28)

(B) Methanol/formaldehyde detoxification (C1 stress response):
A systems-level analysis of C1 exposure identified aldB-II (pedI; PP_2680) as highly methanol-inducible and proposed it as part of a thiol-independent formaldehyde oxidation route that oxidizes formaldehyde → formate, contributing to methanol tolerance when aldehyde concentrations are high. (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 17-19)

3.2 Substrate specificity: what is known vs. what remains uncertain

Across multiple studies, ped-cluster dehydrogenases (PedE/PedH/PedI) are described as having broad/relaxed substrate scope, supporting oxidation of several alcohols and aldehyde intermediates (e.g., in 1,4-butanediol metabolism). (li2020unraveling14butanediolmetabolism pages 1-2, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 19-22)

However, within the retrieved evidence, direct purified-enzyme kinetics or a substrate panel specifically for PedI/Q88JG9 are not reported; consequently, detailed substrate ranking (kcat/KM across aldehydes) cannot be stated here without additional primary biochemistry papers. (li2020unraveling14butanediolmetabolism pages 1-2, thompson2020fattyacidand pages 9-12)

4) Cellular localization: where PedI likely functions

The ethylene glycol engineering literature explicitly describes PedI and PP_0545 as cytoplasmic aldehyde dehydrogenases, acting downstream of periplasmic quinoprotein dehydrogenases in the oxidation chain toward glyoxylic acid. (franden2018engineeringpseudomonasputida pages 1-4)

In parallel, RB‑TnSeq interpretation emphasizes a model where PQQ-dependent oxidation occurs in the periplasm, while downstream processing (including aldehyde dehydrogenases) proceeds with transport and intracellular metabolism; PedI is treated as the “genomically proximal aldehyde dehydrogenase” in this context rather than as the periplasmic PQQ-ADH itself. (thompson2020fattyacidand pages 12-14, incha2023excavatingthegenome pages 28-32)

5) Evidence base and quantitative data (statistics from recent studies)

5.1 Transcriptomic induction and physiological data under methanol (2023)

In P. putida KT2440 exposed to 1 M methanol, aldB-II/pedI (PP_2680) was reported as the most highly upregulated gene, with log2 fold-change = 9.6. (Publication: 2023-06; URL https://doi.org/10.1128/msystems.00004-23) (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15)

In the same study, methanol exposure was associated with:
* increased glucose uptake (qG = 6.2 ± 0.1 mmol gCDW−1 h−1) and
* formate secretion (qF = 0.57 ± 0.03 mmol gCDW−1 h−1),
* and an overall growth-rate reduction on methanol stress (~40% decrease). (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15)

5.2 Mutant phenotypes supporting a detoxification role (2023)

Deletion genetics in the 2023 study support functional contribution of AldB-II/PedI to methanol/formaldehyde tolerance: combined loss of aldB-II with other formaldehyde/formate-processing functions (e.g., ∆∆fdhAB in relevant combinations) produced approximately 40% reduction in growth rate (μ) at 250 mM methanol relative to parental strain background, supporting redundant but meaningful detoxification capacity rather than unique essentiality. (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 15-17, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 17-19)

5.3 Fitness (RB‑TnSeq) evidence for redundancy in alcohol metabolism (2020)

Genome-wide RB‑TnSeq profiling shows pedI (PP_2680) disruption yields only minor fitness defects on ethanol and several other alcohols, and no fitness defect on butanol or pentanol, consistent with PedI functioning in a redundant network of aldehyde oxidation rather than being the single indispensable enzyme for short-chain alcohol catabolism. (Publication: 2020-10; URL https://doi.org/10.1128/aem.01665-20) (thompson2020fattyacidand pages 12-14, thompson2020fattyacidand pages 9-12)

6) Pathways and biological processes: integrated view

6.1 Ethylene glycol oxidation to glyoxylate

A widely cited model for KT2440 ethylene glycol processing is: ethylene glycol → glycolaldehyde → glycolate → glyoxylate, initiated by periplasmic PedE/PedH, with subsequent steps involving cytoplasmic ALDHs PedI and PP_0545, producing glyoxylic acid that is then assimilated or further metabolized. (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 7-8, franden2018engineeringpseudomonasputida pages 1-4)

The ethylene glycol pathway schematic figure provides a consolidated depiction of this sequence and the implicated enzymes. (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism media 63acbe28)

6.2 C1 stress handling: methanol and formaldehyde detoxification

Although KT2440 lacks full methylotrophic assimilation, it possesses oxidative “detoxification-like” responses to C1 compounds. Under methanol exposure, PedEH and other broad-substrate dehydrogenases can generate formaldehyde; formaldehyde is then processed by multiple partially redundant routes, including a glutathione-dependent frmAC system and thiol-independent dehydrogenase activities (including AldB‑II/PedI and FdhAB) that contribute under high aldehyde load. (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 15-17, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 17-19)

7) Current applications and real-world implementations

7.1 Plastic-monomer and waste-stream valorization

PedI’s relevance is practical in engineering P. putida for plastic monomer utilization and bio-upcycling, because ethylene glycol and diols generate toxic aldehyde intermediates (e.g., glycolaldehyde) whose detoxification/catabolism can limit growth and productivity. The ethylene glycol engineering literature explicitly includes PedI among cytoplasmic ALDHs implicated in EG conversion toward glyoxylic acid. (franden2018engineeringpseudomonasputida pages 1-4)

7.2 C1 feedstocks (methanol/formate) and tolerance engineering

The 2023 systems-level study frames KT2440 C1 oxidation/detoxification capacity (including AldB-II/PedI) as a foundation for engineering toward synthetic formatotrophy/methylotrophy, where managing formaldehyde toxicity is central. (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 15-17, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15)

8) Expert interpretation and synthesis (authoritative analysis)

  1. PedI/AldB‑II is best viewed as a “high-capacity, stress-responsive aldehyde-oxidation node” rather than a narrowly specialized catabolic enzyme. This interpretation is supported by strong methanol induction (log2FC 9.6) and growth defects only when combined with other formaldehyde-processing deletions, indicating functional redundancy but clear physiological relevance under aldehyde stress. (turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 15-17, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 17-19)

  2. For many alcohol substrates, PQQ-dependent PedE/PedH and multiple ALDHs likely share or compete for aldehyde oxidation, consistent with the weak single-gene fitness phenotype for pedI in pooled mutant assays. This reduces confidence in assigning a single dominant aldehyde substrate under standard conditions without purified-enzyme characterization. (thompson2020fattyacidand pages 12-14, incha2023excavatingthegenome pages 28-32)

  3. Genomic colocation with PedE/PedH and PedR1/PedS1 supports coordinated regulation and ecological function in environments containing volatile alcohols/diols and reactive aldehydes, consistent with observed induction under ethylene glycol and methanol challenges. (muckschel2012ethyleneglycolmetabolism pages 5-6, turlin2023coreandauxiliary pages 12-15)

9) Latest research directions (2024 and beyond): functional annotation at scale

While not pedI-specific, a 2024 mSystems paper demonstrates how independent component analysis (ICA) applied to RB‑TnSeq fitness compendia can accelerate functional annotation in KT2440: the authors analyzed 332 samples / 179 unique conditions, covering 4,732 genes, and derived 84 functional modules explaining ~83% of variance. (Publication: 2024-03; URL https://doi.org/10.1128/msystems.00942-23) (borchert2024machinelearninganalysis pages 2-4)

This direction is relevant because aldehyde dehydrogenases in KT2440 are numerous and functionally redundant; systematic fitness-based module inference can help prioritize which ALDHs are conditionally important (e.g., pedI under specific aldehyde stresses) for targeted biochemical follow-up. (borchert2024machinelearninganalysis pages 2-4, thompson2020fattyacidand pages 12-14)

10) Key limitations and what is still needed

Within the retrieved sources, PedI/Q88JG9 is well supported as an aldehyde dehydrogenase in multiple pathways, but direct purified-enzyme biochemical characterization for PedI itself (substrate panel, kinetics, cofactor specificity NAD+ vs NADP+, oligomeric state) is not present in the excerpts. As a result, substrate specificity can be described only at the pathway/physiology level (formaldehyde/glycolaldehyde-linked roles) rather than as a definitive enzyme kinetic profile. (li2020unraveling14butanediolmetabolism pages 1-2, thompson2020fattyacidand pages 12-14)

Summary

pedI (PP_2680) encodes the aldehyde dehydrogenase AldB‑II (UniProt Q88JG9) in P. putida KT2440. The strongest recent evidence (2023) places AldB‑II/PedI as a highly methanol-induced, thiol-independent formaldehyde-oxidizing activity contributing to tolerance under aldehyde stress, while foundational pathway studies (2012 onward) place it in oxidation chains for ethylene glycol and other diols/alcohols. Across conditions, pedI shows redundancy with other oxidoreductases, consistent with modest single-gene fitness effects but clear induction and combinatorial mutant phenotypes under C1 stress.

pedI notes

  • PP_2680/Q88JG9 is annotated in UniProt as aldB-II, but the KT2440 ethylene glycol study explicitly maps this locus to PedI based on conserved sequence and neighborhood with the Ped cluster PMID:23023748 PMID:23023748.
  • In P. putida U, the Ped pathway requires an aldehyde dehydrogenase step for oxidation of 2-phenylethanol-derived phenylacetaldehyde to phenylacetic acid PMID:18177365.
  • In KT2440, PedI is induced together with PedE/PedH under ethylene glycol conditions, and the pathway model proposes PedI plus PP_0545 oxidize aldehyde intermediates toward glyoxylic acid PMID:23023748 PMID:23023748.
  • Current GOA is minimal and fully electronic: GO:0004029, GO:0004030, and the very broad parent GO:0016491. The literature supports a more specific interpretation than generic aldehyde dehydrogenase.
