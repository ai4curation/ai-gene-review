Functional Annotation Report: scpC / PP_0154 (UniProt Q88RH5)

Pseudomonas putida KT2440 (PSEPK)

1. Summary (Answer to the Research Question)

scpC (PP_0154, Q88RH5) encodes a soluble, cytoplasmic class‑I coenzyme‑A transferase of the acetyl‑CoA hydrolase/transferase family (Pfam PF02550 + PF13336; EC 2.8.3.‑). It catalyzes the reversible transfer of coenzyme A between a short‑chain acyl‑CoA thioester and a free carboxylic acid (a transthioesterification), proceeding through a covalent glutamyl‑CoA / acyl‑glutamyl‑anhydride intermediate on the active‑site glutamate (Glu288). Its physiological substrate pair in P. putida has not been measured directly; the two authoritative computational annotations disagree: the EMBL/UniProt gene name "propionyl‑CoA:succinate CoA transferase" is a homology transfer from E. coli YgfH/ScpC, whereas KEGG assigns it to orthology K18118, succinyl‑CoA:acetate CoA‑transferase (EC 2.8.3.18, AarC‑type) in the TCA cycle. Both describe the same chemistry on cytoplasmic C2–C4 acyl‑CoA/organic‑acid pools feeding central carbon metabolism.

Identity verification (mandatory): The gene symbol scpC is ambiguous. This report concerns the Pseudomonas putida KT2440 CoA‑transferase Q88RH5 (PP_0154). It is not the unrelated streptococcal scpC (a cell‑envelope C5a peptidase/protease). The CoA‑transferase identity is confirmed by the acetyl‑CoA hydrolase/transferase domain architecture, the EC 2.8.3.‑ class, and orthology to E. coli ScpC/YgfH — all consistent with the UniProt record.

3. Primary Function: The Catalyzed Reaction

scpC catalyzes a reversible, ping‑pong CoA transfer:

R1‑CO‑SCoA  +  R2‑COO⁻   ⇌   R1‑COO⁻  +  R2‑CO‑SCoA

Substrate specificity — two candidate assignments (unresolved for the P. putida enzyme):

  1. Propionyl‑CoA:succinate CoA‑transferase (EMBL/UniProt name; EC 2.8.3.‑). Based on the E. coli homolog: "YgfH is propionyl CoA:succinate CoA transferase" (Haller et al., 2000, 10769117). In E. coli this enzyme is the third step of the Sbm cycle converting succinate → propionate.
  2. Succinyl‑CoA:acetate CoA‑transferase (KEGG K18118; EC 2.8.3.18). The best biochemically/structurally characterized member of this orthology group is Acetobacter aceti AarC: "AarC is succinyl‑coenzyme A (CoA):acetate CoA‑transferase, which replaces succinyl‑CoA synthetase in a variant CAC" (Mullins et al., 2008, 18502856).

Both activities are short‑chain (C2–C4) acyl‑CoA:carboxylate CoA‑transfers by the same class‑I mechanism; they differ only in the preferred acyl donor/acceptor pair. No enzyme‑kinetic study of the P. putida protein exists, so its exact preference is inferred, not measured.

4. Pathway Context and Localization

  • Localization: cytoplasmic, soluble. The AarC‑type CoA‑transferase acts on the cytosolic organic‑acid/acyl‑CoA pool — the A. aceti aar genes "support flux through a complete but unorthodox CAC that is needed to lower cytoplasmic acetate levels" (18502856).
  • Pathway (KEGG): PP_0154 is mapped to the citrate cycle (ppu00020) and pyruvate metabolism (ppu00620), consistent with a succinyl‑CoA↔acetate transferase that can bypass succinyl‑CoA synthetase.
  • Genomic context distinguishes it from E. coli: PP_0154 is a standalone gene. Its neighbors (PP_0153 hypothetical; PP_0152 exported protein; PP_0155/PP_0156 pyridine‑nucleotide transhydrogenase) are unrelated to CoA/propionate metabolism. This contrasts with E. coli, where the homolog lies in a four‑gene operon "that encodes homologues of methylmalonyl CoA mutases (Sbm) and acyl CoA transferases (YgfH)" (10769117).
  • Plausible physiological niche (hypothesis). No primary study characterizes PP_0154 in P. putida; its function is annotated by homology only. P. putida KT2440 assimilates acetate via acetyl‑CoA and the glyoxylate shunt, and acetate‑grown cells appear to use "ATP independent acetyl‑CoA recycling as energy saving strategy" (Grether et al., 2026, 42393666). An AarC‑type succinyl‑CoA:acetate CoA‑transferase (the KEGG K18118 activity) would provide exactly this ATP‑independent activation of acetate to acetyl‑CoA, consistent with PP_0154's KEGG mapping to the TCA cycle. This link is a reasonable but untested hypothesis.
  • The Sbm route is absent in P. putida: KEGG shows P. putida KT2440 encodes no methylmalonyl‑CoA mutase (K01847) and no MCM subunit (K11942). Since propionate oxidation in Pseudomonas runs through the 2‑methylcitrate cycle — "the only known and described pathway for propionate oxidation in this organism" (Santos‑Oliveira et al., 2024, 37995793) — scpC cannot be part of an E. coli‑style succinate→propionate cycle here. This argues that the "propionyl‑CoA:succinate" name is a homology over‑transfer, and that the KEGG succinyl‑CoA:acetate CoA‑transferase (TCA‑linked) role is the more parsimonious annotation.
