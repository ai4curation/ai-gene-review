Gene Description

Plasma-membrane major facilitator superfamily nitrate/nitrite porter-family transporter that supports assimilatory nitrate use in Pseudomonas putida KT2440. In this strain, nasA refers to the transporter PP_2092 rather than an assimilatory nitrate reductase catalytic subunit. The protein is a predicted 12-pass membrane protein, is induced under nitrogen limitation in a NasT-associated regulatory context, and is best supported as a nitrate uptake component of the nitrate assimilation system. Nitrite transport remains plausible from family assignment, but the direct physiological evidence in KT2440 is stronger for nitrate than for nitrite as the core substrate.

Research report: functional annotation of nasA (PP_2092; UniProt Q88L43) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

0) Target verification (critical disambiguation)

The gene symbol nasA is used across diverse bacteria for different components of nitrate assimilation systems (e.g., MFS permeases in some organisms; ABC-type transport system components in others). In Pseudomonas putida KT2440, the locus PP_2092 is explicitly identified as a nitrate transporter in an assimilatory nitrate reduction module by genome-context analysis coupled to pooled mutant fitness data (RB-TnSeq/BarSeq). This PP_2092 assignment matches the user-provided UniProt entry Q88L43 description as a NarK/NarU-like MFS nitrate/nitrite transporter. Therefore, the evidence summarized here is restricted to KT2440 PP_2092 and related transporter-family context, and does not generalize from other unrelated “nasA” annotations without caution. (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8, schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin media b1b76177)

1) Key concepts and current understanding

1.1 Assimilatory nitrate reduction vs other nitrate pathways

Assimilatory nitrate reduction is a nutrient acquisition pathway in which extracellular nitrate (NO3−) is imported and reduced to nitrite (NO2−) and then to ammonium for incorporation into biomass. This differs from respiratory nitrate reduction/denitrification, where nitrate functions as a terminal electron acceptor and nitrogen can be lost as gaseous products.

In bacterial nitrate assimilation, a transporter is often required because nitrate and nitrite are charged and do not freely cross membranes. Transport solutions vary by lifestyle: soil-associated bacteria commonly employ MFS-type proton-coupled nitrate uptake (NarK1/NasA-like), whereas aquatic cyanobacteria frequently employ higher-affinity ABC-type nitrate uptake (NrtABCD) with periplasmic/extracellular binding proteins. (jarvis2022proteininteractionnetworks pages 26-32)

1.2 What NasA is (in KT2440)

For P. putida KT2440, NasA = PP_2092 (Q88L43) is best supported as an inner-membrane nitrate uptake transporter that supplies nitrate to the intracellular assimilatory nitrate/nitrite reductases. The UniProt-provided domain context (MFS/NarK-like) is consistent with expert syntheses describing NasA proteins as NarK1-like nitrate/proton symporters (MFS family), rather than ABC transporter subunits. (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8, jarvis2022proteininteractionnetworks pages 26-32)

2) Functional role of nasA (PP_2092/Q88L43) in P. putida KT2440

2.1 Primary function: nitrate transport (substrate specificity)

The strongest KT2440-specific functional evidence is from genome-wide pooled fitness assays across nitrogen sources. In this dataset, PP_2092 is identified as a distinct nitrate transporter and shows a strong fitness defect during growth with nitrate as the sole nitrogen source, while no significant fitness phenotype was detected in the nitrite condition. The authors interpret the lack of a nitrite phenotype as suggesting PP_2092 is more specific for nitrate uptake (or that nitrite uptake can be bypassed under the experimental conditions). (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8)

A key quantitative point is visualized in the paper’s schematic of the assimilatory nitrate reduction module, where PP_2092 is labeled “nitrate transporter” and the nitrate-condition mutant fitness value is shown (fitness ≈ −2.6). (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin media b1b76177)

Interpretation: In RB-TnSeq, a strong negative fitness under nitrate suggests PP_2092 is required for optimal nitrate assimilation. The lack of a nitrite phenotype does not prove that PP_2092 cannot transport nitrite; rather, it suggests nitrate uptake is the limiting step under nitrate-only conditions, while nitrite acquisition may occur via alternative routes (including chemical diffusion of nitrous acid, HNO2, depending on pH). (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8)

2.2 Pathway context: coupling to assimilatory reductases

In the same fitness study, PP_2092 is discussed alongside assimilatory reductases (NarB and NirDB) and cofactor genes required for assimilatory nitrate reduction, supporting its placement in the nitrate assimilation pathway (nitrate uptake → reduction → incorporation). (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8, schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin media b1b76177)

3) Regulation: nitrogen limitation and nitrate-responsive control

3.1 Induction under nitrogen limitation

Transcriptomics during nitrogen-limited cultivation (in the context of mcl-PHA synthesis) reported strong upregulation of nasA (PP_2092) across backgrounds: approximately 14.64-fold (WT), 12.04-fold (relA/spoT mutant), and 12.43-fold (rpoN mutant). These data were generated by RNA-seq (with qRT-PCR used to validate the RNA-seq results overall), supporting that nasA is part of a nitrogen-starvation/limitation responsive program. (dabrowska2020transcriptomechangesin pages 8-10)

3.2 NasS/NasT regulatory linkage

Within the KT2440 nitrate assimilation module, regulation is consistent with a NasS/NasT nitrate/nitrite-responsive system (commonly described as a sensory/regulatory pair controlling expression of nitrate assimilation genes). In the BarSeq study, nasT (PP_2093) and nasS (PP_2094) show notable nitrate-condition fitness effects, consistent with a regulatory role controlling the nitrate assimilation genes that include the transporter PP_2092. Additionally, the transcriptomic study annotates nasA (PP_2092) as putatively regulated by NasT and shows nasT itself is strongly induced (including ~27.66-fold in relA/spoT). (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8, dabrowska2020transcriptomechangesin pages 8-10)

4) Cellular localization and topology

No direct localization experiment (e.g., fractionation, fluorescent protein fusions, proteomics subcellular mapping) for KT2440 PP_2092 was retrieved in the searched corpus. However, multiple lines of inference support that NasA (Q88L43) is an integral inner membrane transporter:

  • Family/domain inference: NasA is described as NarK/NarU-like MFS, which are canonical multi-pass inner-membrane transporters. (jarvis2022proteininteractionnetworks pages 26-32)
  • Expert synthesis: NasA-type proteins are discussed as NarK1-like nitrate/proton symporters (membrane proteins) contrasted with ABC-type uptake systems that include periplasmic/external binding proteins. (jarvis2022proteininteractionnetworks pages 26-32)

5) Recent developments (emphasis on 2023–2024) and how they inform annotation

Direct 2023–2024 primary studies specifically characterizing PP_2092/Q88L43 in P. putida KT2440 were not retrieved. Nevertheless, 2023–2024 research on microbial nitrogen metabolism continues to emphasize transporter-level control and pathway regulation in diverse bacteria, and more recent transporter-focused work (outside KT2440) illustrates that nitrate/nitrite transport can be dynamically regulated and can shape nitrogen-oxide kinetics.

For example, transporter-centric analyses in nitrogen cycling organisms highlight that NarK-like transporters can mediate nitrate/nitrite exchange or proton-coupled fluxes, while alternative nitrite transport channels can contribute higher apparent affinity and change observed kinetics through expression shifts and environmental drivers (nitrite concentration, pH). While this specific kinetic example was retrieved from 2026, it reinforces a general modern view: nitrate/nitrite transporter families are functionally diverse, and physiological context (including pH-dependent passive diffusion of HNO2) can complicate inference from simple growth phenotypes. (lee2026kineticplasticityof pages 9-12, schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8)

Implication for KT2440 NasA annotation: The most defensible KT2440-specific statement remains that PP_2092 is required for growth on nitrate as nitrogen source (fitness defect) and is induced under nitrogen limitation (RNA-seq), while the exact transport mechanism (symport vs antiport) and nitrite permeability remain to be directly established experimentally. (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8, dabrowska2020transcriptomechangesin pages 8-10)

6) Current applications and real-world implementation relevance

6.1 Industrial/bioprocess relevance: nitrogen limitation regimes

P. putida KT2440 is widely used as a biotechnology chassis, and nitrogen limitation is a common cultivation strategy to shift carbon flux (e.g., toward storage polymers such as mcl-PHAs). Transcriptomic profiling under nitrogen limitation during mcl-PHA production shows that nasA (PP_2092) is among the most strongly induced genes annotated as part of nitrogen acquisition/response, suggesting that nitrate uptake capacity can be upregulated during production-relevant nutrient limitation regimes. This provides a mechanistic link between bioprocess nutrient control and activation of nitrate assimilation functions. (dabrowska2020transcriptomechangesin pages 8-10)

6.2 Systems biology application: functional gene discovery for engineering

High-throughput pooled fitness mapping in KT2440 identifies PP_2092 as a functionally important nitrate transporter among hundreds of nitrogen-metabolism genes. This type of dataset is used in practice to prioritize genes for metabolic engineering, to build predictive models of nutrient utilization, and to select genetic parts/regulators (e.g., NasT-linked control) as potential synthetic biology tools. (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8)

7) Quantitative evidence summary (statistics/data)

Key quantitative points directly supporting annotation:

  • Mutant fitness under nitrate: PP_2092 shows a strong nitrate-dependent fitness defect; figure-level value shown ≈ −2.6 in the nitrate condition. (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin media b1b76177)
  • Substrate specificity (genetic evidence): strong phenotype on nitrate but not nitrite, interpreted as suggestive of nitrate specificity (with caveats about nitrite diffusion). (schmidt2022nitrogenmetabolismin pages 6-8)
  • Expression under nitrogen limitation: nasA/PP_2092 upregulated ~12–15-fold across multiple strain backgrounds under N limitation (WT 14.64; relA/spoT 12.04; rpoN 12.43). (dabrowska2020transcriptomechangesin pages 8-10)

9) Gaps and recommended next experiments (to strengthen functional annotation)

Based on the retrieved evidence, the major remaining uncertainties for PP_2092/Q88L43 are transport kinetics and mechanism.

High-priority experiments that would directly resolve open points:
1. Radiotracer uptake or ion-selective assays in WT vs ΔnasA for nitrate and nitrite to quantify Km/Vmax and substrate range.
2. Transport mechanism tests (proton coupling, antiport vs symport), e.g., using pH gradients, membrane potential manipulation, or reconstitution in proteoliposomes.
3. Subcellular localization by fluorescent fusions and membrane fractionation proteomics.
4. Regulatory validation of NasS/NasT control over PP_2092 (promoter/terminator mapping; transcription termination/antitermination assays).

(nasA-notes.md)

nasA notes

  • Identity: nasA in PSEPK is PP_2092 / UniProt Q88L43, annotated as a Nitrate/nitrite transporter rather than a reductase. UniProt also places it in the major facilitator superfamily and specifically the Nitrate/nitrite porter (TC 2.A.1.8) family [file:PSEPK/nasA/nasA-uniprot.txt, "RecName: Full=Nitrate/nitrite transporter"; "Belongs to the major facilitator superfamily. Nitrate/nitrite porter (TC 2.A.1.8) family."].

  • Localization/topology: UniProt calls the protein a Cell membrane Multi-pass membrane protein and predicts 12 transmembrane helices, consistent with an MFS transporter [file:PSEPK/nasA/nasA-uniprot.txt, "SUBCELLULAR LOCATION: Cell membrane"; "Multi-pass membrane protein"; "FT TRANSMEM"].

  • GO/annotation context: UniProt and QuickGO currently associate the protein with GO:0015112 nitrate transmembrane transporter activity, GO:0015113 nitrite transmembrane transporter activity, GO:0042128 nitrate assimilation, and GO:0005886 plasma membrane [file:PSEPK/nasA/nasA-uniprot.txt, "DR GO; GO:0015112; F:nitrate transmembrane transporter activity"; "DR GO; GO:0042128; P:nitrate assimilation"; "DR GO; GO:0005886; C:plasma membrane"].

  • Nitrogen-limitation expression: a KT2440 transcriptome study reported that PP_2092 nasA nitrate transporter was upregulated under nitrogen limitation and assigned to the NasT regulon, with the table entry PP_2092 nasA nitrate transporter 14.64 12.04 12.43 nasT [PMID:33375721 Transcriptome Changes in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 during Medium-Chain-Length Polyhydroxyalkanoate Synthesis Induced by Nitrogen Limitation, "PP_2092 nasA nitrate transporter 14.64 12.04 12.43 nasT"].

  • Regulatory context: the same study concluded that nitrogen limitation caused activation of the nasT regulator, reinforcing that nasA belongs to the nitrate-assimilation response rather than general respiratory nitrate reduction [PMID:33375721 Transcriptome Changes in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 during Medium-Chain-Length Polyhydroxyalkanoate Synthesis Induced by Nitrogen Limitation, "nitrogen limitation caused activation of the nasT regulator"].

  • Nitrate-assimilation pathway context: a 2024 study on NasT in KT2440 assayed cells shifted to M9 medium with 5 mM KNO3 as the sole nitrogen source and showed that cyclic di-GMP inhibits nitrate assimilation by impairing NasT-dependent activation of the assimilatory system [PMID:38000372 Cyclic di-GMP inhibits nitrate assimilation by impairing the antitermination function of NasT in Pseudomonas putida, "M9 medium containing 5 mM KNO3 as the sole nitrogen source"; "Cyclic di-GMP inhibits nitrate assimilation by impairing the antitermination function of NasT in Pseudomonas putida"].

  • Higher-level nitrogen regulation: the P. putida NtrC network study identifies NtrC as the master nitrogen regulator required for expression of genes involved in nitrogen uptake and metabolism in KT2440, providing regulatory context for nitrate-assimilation genes [PMID:19648236 NtrC-Dependent Regulatory Network for Nitrogen Assimilation in Pseudomonas putida, "NtrC is the master nitrogen regulator, which is required to activate the expression of a number of genes involved in nitrogen uptake and metabolism"].

  • Specificity caveat: a recent RWTH Aachen dissertation reported BarSeq support for PP_2092 as the unique nitrate transporter in KT2440, with a nitrate-specific fitness defect and no obvious nitrite phenotype, but this appears to be thesis-level evidence and not yet a primary paper. It is useful as a caution against overcommitting to nitrite transport as a core function [URL:https://publications.rwth-aachen.de/record/975194/files/975194.pdf, "With BarSeq, we were also able to pinpoint the unique nitrate transporter PP_2092 with a fitness value of -2.6. This gene did not exhibit any noticeable phenotype in the presence of nitrite"].
