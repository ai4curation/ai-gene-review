Gene Description

Poly(3-hydroxyalkanoate) polymerase 1 (PhaC1), a Class II PHA synthase that catalyzes polymerization of (R)-3-hydroxyalkanoyl-CoA thioesters into medium-chain-length polyhydroxyalkanoates (mcl-PHAs, C6-C14). PhaC1 is the primary PHA synthase in P. putida KT2440, encoded as the first gene in the phaC1-phaZ-phaC2-phaD operon (PP_5003). PHAs accumulate as intracellular granules serving as carbon and energy storage reserves under nutrient-limiting conditions. The gene is designated phaA in the KT2440 genome annotation but referred to as phaC1 in the broader PHA literature.

Proposed New Ontology Terms

polyhydroxyalkanoate biosynthetic process

Definition: The chemical reactions and pathways resulting in the formation of polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs), polyesters of hydroxyalkanoic acids that serve as carbon and energy storage materials in prokaryotic cells. This includes both short-chain-length PHAs (such as poly-hydroxybutyrate) and medium-chain-length PHAs.

Justification: GO currently has GO:0042619 (poly-hydroxybutyrate biosynthetic process), which is specific to PHB (short-chain, C4). There is no broader term covering the biosynthesis of all polyhydroxyalkanoates, including medium-chain-length PHAs (mcl-PHAs, C6-C14) produced by Class II PHA synthases such as P. putida PhaC1. The cellular component term GO:0070088 (polyhydroxyalkanoate granule) uses the broader polyhydroxyalkanoate terminology, and a corresponding biosynthetic process term is needed. GO:0042619 should be a child of this new term.

Parent term: cellular biosynthetic process

Research Report: Functional Annotation of PhaC1 (UniProt Q88D25, locus PP_5003; UniProt gene name field “phaA”) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

Executive summary

UniProt accession Q88D25 in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 corresponds to PhaC1, a class II polyhydroxyalkanoate (PHA) synthase/polymerase encoded at PP_5003 in the canonical phaC1–phaZ–phaC2 locus, despite the UniProt gene name field listing phaA (a symbol that commonly refers to a different enzyme—β-ketothiolase—in other organisms). Multiple independent sources map PP_5003 ↔ PhaC1 and provide functional evidence that phaC1 is required and sufficient (upon complementation) for PHA accumulation in P. putida backgrounds. (kelly2022proteomicsapproachesfor pages 181-183, cheng2016functionalmetagenomicsusing pages 10-14, kratzl2023cellcellinteractionin pages 34-36)

Functionally, PhaC1 catalyzes the polymerization of (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA (R-HA-CoA) thioesters into medium-chain-length PHA (mcl-PHA), with monomers typically C6–C14 and frequent dominance of C8/C10 units depending on feedstock and pathway routing. Substrates derive primarily from β-oxidation or from de novo fatty-acid synthesis via bridging enzymes (e.g., PhaG and AlkK). PhaC1 operates at/associated with cytoplasmic PHA granules (“carbonosomes”), supported by granule-centric biology and direct fluorescence microscopy positive-control localization of PhaC1 fusion constructs. (mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 16-19, manoli2022syntheticcontrolof pages 1-2, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 7-10, kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 8-10)

Recent (2024) work expanded the granule protein landscape, proposing OprL/Pal as a newly identified phasin and PDH-E1 as a carbonosome-localized candidate GAP, and systematically mapping AP-MS interaction neighborhoods including PhaC1—while cautioning about artefactual association of lipoproteins during granule purification. Separately, 2024 metabolic engineering studies report improved glucose-to-mcl-PHA conversion in engineered P. putida lineages (up to ~59 wt% and 6.8 g/L in optimized flask cultures), and 2024 meta-analysis provides contemporary benchmark statistics for mcl-PHA production and material properties. (kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 7-8, hahn2024currenttrendsin pages 1-3, dong2024modificationofglucose pages 10-11, dong2024modificationofglucose pages 13-14)

1) Critical verification of gene/protein identity (disambiguation of “phaA”)

1.1 Why “phaA” is ambiguous

In much of the PHA literature, phaA often denotes β-ketothiolase (an early enzyme of short-chain-length PHB biosynthesis in many bacteria), whereas PHA synthase/polymerase is typically phaC. For P. putida KT2440, however, locus PP_5003 is repeatedly described as phaC1 / PhaC1, a class II PHA synthase, and UniProt Q88D25 is explicitly labeled as PhaC1 in proteomics studies. (kelly2022proteomicsapproachesfor pages 181-183, kratzl2023cellcellinteractionin pages 34-36)

1.2 Evidence that Q88D25 = PP_5003 = PhaC1

  • Proteomics mapping: Q88D25 is explicitly treated as PhaC1 (“Poly(3-hydroxyalkanoate) polymerase 1”) in a P. putida KT2440 proteomics thesis (including a PhaC1/Q88D25 interactor table). (kelly2022proteomicsapproachesfor pages 181-183)
  • Genomic locus mapping: Multiple sources map PP_5003 to PhaC1, placing it in the first PHA operon with PhaZ (PP_5004) and PhaC2 (PP_5005). (kratzl2023cellcellinteractionin pages 34-36, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 7-10)
  • Genetic/functional evidence: Deletion spanning the phaC1/phaZ/phaC2 region yields a PHA− strain, and in trans expression of phaC1 restores PHA+ phenotype, demonstrating that phaC1 is a functional PHA synthase gene in this context. (cheng2016functionalmetagenomicsusing pages 10-14, cheng2016novelpolyhydroxyalkanoatecopolymers pages 4-5)

Conclusion: For the target organism/strain specified, UniProt Q88D25 (PP_5003) is best annotated as PhaC1 (PHA synthase/polymerase 1; class II). The UniProt gene-name field “phaA” should be treated as a naming inconsistency relative to dominant community usage in PHA pathways, not as indicating β-ketothiolase. (kelly2022proteomicsapproachesfor pages 181-183, kratzl2023cellcellinteractionin pages 34-36, cheng2016functionalmetagenomicsusing pages 10-14)

2) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

2.1 Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs) and mcl-PHAs

PHAs are intracellular polyester storage polymers accumulated under unbalanced growth (typically carbon excess with limitation of N, S, or P), forming insoluble hydrophobic granules (“carbonosomes”). In P. putida KT2440, mcl-PHAs comprise C6–C14 (R)-3-hydroxyacyl monomers. (kratzl2023cellcellinteractionin pages 34-36)

2.2 PHA synthase (PhaC) and class II PHA synthases

The PHA synthase/polymerase PhaC catalyzes polymer formation by using (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA (R-HA-CoA) substrates. In Pseudomonads, class II PhaCs are typically associated with mcl-PHA synthesis. PhaC1 in KT2440 is described as one of two polymerases (PhaC1/PhaC2) in the canonical operon. (manoli2022syntheticcontrolof pages 1-2, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 7-10, cheng2016novelpolyhydroxyalkanoatecopolymers pages 8-9)

2.3 Substrate supply to PhaC1: β-oxidation and de novo fatty-acid synthesis

Two major routes provide (R)-HA-CoA substrates:
* β-oxidation route: carbon flows through fatty-acid degradation; stereospecific enoyl-CoA hydratases (e.g., PhaJ variants) generate (R)-HA-CoA intermediates for PhaC. (mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 13-16)
* De novo fatty-acid synthesis route: (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-ACP intermediates are connected to (R)-HA-CoA via PhaG and AlkK, which are frequently transcriptionally activated under PHA-producing conditions on unrelated substrates. (mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 16-19, salvachua2020metabolicengineeringof pages 1-3)

3) Gene/protein function: biochemical role and substrate specificity

3.1 Primary function (enzyme reaction class)

PhaC1 is a PHA polymerase/synthase that polymerizes (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA monomers into mcl-PHA, functioning as the key chain-elongating enzyme in PHA biosynthesis. (salvachua2020metabolicengineeringof pages 1-3, manoli2022syntheticcontrolof pages 1-2)

3.2 Substrate specificity and monomer profiles

Evidence supports that Pseudomonas class II synthases produce MCL monomers (C6–C10) under tested conditions and broadly C6–C14 in KT2440 depending on substrate and pathway routing. Example data from complementation work show MCL-dominant polymers whose composition changes with carbon source (e.g., octanoate leading to high C8 monomer fraction; gluconic acid yielding C8/C10 mixtures). (kratzl2023cellcellinteractionin pages 34-36, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 7-10, cheng2016novelpolyhydroxyalkanoatecopolymers pages 8-9)

3.3 Genetic evidence (necessity and sufficiency via complementation)

Deletion of a region spanning the 3′ portion of phaC1, phaZ, and 5′ portion of phaC2 yields a PHA− strain; introduction of plasmid Plac::phaC1 restores PHA accumulation (PHA+), establishing phaC1 as a functional PHA synthase gene in P. putida backgrounds. (cheng2016functionalmetagenomicsusing pages 10-14, cheng2016novelpolyhydroxyalkanoatecopolymers pages 4-5)

4) Pathway context and biological processes

4.1 The canonical pha gene cluster in KT2440

In P. putida KT2440, the mcl-PHA machinery is encoded in two adjacent operons, commonly described as:
* phaC1ZC2D operon: PhaC1 (PP_5003), PhaZ (PP_5004), PhaC2 (PP_5005), plus a transcriptional regulator/activator PhaD.
* phaFI operon: phasins PhaF and PhaI, major granule-associated proteins (GAPs) shaping granule structure and participating in regulation. (kratzl2023cellcellinteractionin pages 34-36, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 7-10)

Visual evidence: The gene-cluster organization and biosynthetic routing are schematized in Mezzina et al. (2021) Figures 1–2. (mezzina2021engineeringnativeand media bf25720f, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand media 4199e162)

4.2 The PHA cycle concept in Pseudomonas

Beyond one-way accumulation, Pseudomonas can run a PHA cycle with concurrent synthesis (PhaC1/PhaC2) and degradation (PhaZ). Degradation releases 3-hydroxyalkanoic acids that can be reactivated back to (R)-HA-CoA (ATP-dependent) to re-enter polymerization, linking the cycle to central metabolism robustness. (manoli2022syntheticcontrolof pages 1-2)

5) Cellular localization and macromolecular context

5.1 Where PhaC1 functions in the cell

PHA granules (“carbonosomes”) are intracellular hydrophobic inclusions; proteins involved in polymerization and regulation are associated with granule surfaces. Thus, PhaC1’s function is expected to occur at/associated with these cytoplasmic granules. (kratzl2023cellcellinteractionin pages 34-36, manoli2022syntheticcontrolof pages 1-2)

5.2 Direct evidence for carbonosome association

A 2024 proteomics study explicitly used PhaC1 as a positive control for carbonosome localization in fluorescence microscopy (eYFP fusions), providing direct support for in vivo granule association/localization. (kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 8-10)

Proteomics-based granule purifications can detect PhaC1 on isolated granules but often at low enrichment; authors emphasize contamination/artefactual binding concerns because hydrophobic polymer surfaces can recruit many proteins non-specifically during purification. Therefore, microscopy and genetic/operon context provide stronger localization inference than granule proteome membership alone. (kelly2022proteomicsapproachesfora pages 130-133, kelly2022proteomicsapproachesfor pages 130-133)

6) Regulation and control points impacting PhaC1 function

6.1 Local transcriptional control within the pha locus

PhaD activates transcription of the phaC1/phaC2 operon from promoters including PC1 and PI by binding operator sequences. Phasins (e.g., PhaF) can influence transcript levels and granule-linked regulatory complexes have been proposed (granule–PhaD/PhaF–DNA coupling). (mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 16-19, dong2024modificationofglucose pages 13-14)

6.2 Global regulation integrating carbon state

Global carbon regulation can affect phaC1 translation; for example, Crc/Hfq can inhibit phaC1 translation via a catabolite motif, and GacS/GacA can influence translation, helping couple polymerase expression to nutrient state and carbon flow. (mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 16-19)

7) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

7.1 2024: Systems-level granule biology and protein interaction mapping

Kelly et al. (May 2024, Molecular & Cellular Proteomics) combined whole-proteome quantification, carbonosome proteomes, AP-MS interaction mapping, and microscopy. Key findings relevant to functional annotation of PhaC1 include:
* AP-MS mapping with PhaC1 as a bait: PhaC1 AP-MS returned a limited set of enriched proteins, many annotated as lipoproteins; authors caution these may be artefacts of purification/granule association. (kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 7-8)
* OprL/Pal as a newly identified phasin/GAP: OprL/Pal was highly enriched and supported by carbonosome localization; ΔoprL strains show reduced PHA under nitrogen limitation, indicating functional relevance to PHA biology. (kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 8-10, kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 7-8)
* PDH-E1 carbonosome association: PDH-E1 shows carbonosome-localized fluorescence signal, connecting central carbon metabolism nodes to granule biology. (kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 8-10)

These results represent a shift from solely pathway-gene views to a broader organelle-like understanding of bacterial PHA granules, with an emphasis on discriminating bona fide GAPs from contaminants. (kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 8-10)

7.2 2024: Glucose-to-mcl-PHA engineering in P. putida

Dong et al. (Nov 2024) report multi-pathway modifications to improve glucose utilization toward acetyl-CoA and mcl-PHA. Key quantitative outcomes:
* Engineered strains achieved up to 59.1 wt% mcl-PHA and 6.8 g/L titer under optimized shake-flask feeding. (dong2024modificationofglucose pages 10-11)
* The study contextualizes pha regulation (two-operon organization; PhaD activation at PC1/PI) and finds that phaD overexpression increased growth/glucose use but did not increase PHA, consistent with precursor limitation rather than transcriptional limitation. (dong2024modificationofglucose pages 13-14)

These results support a current consensus in metabolic engineering: increasing polymerase transcription alone is often insufficient, and flux/precursor availability is frequently the limiting factor. (dong2024modificationofglucose pages 13-14)

7.3 2024: Field-wide production and property benchmarks (statistics)

Hahn et al. (Mar 2024) provide a quantitative meta-analysis of mcl-PHA fermentation outcomes and polymer properties:
* Median productivity: fed-batch 0.4 g/L/h vs batch 0.05 g/L/h. (hahn2024currenttrendsin pages 1-3)
* Median material properties: Tg −43°C, Tm 47°C, median MW 104 kDa, median elongation at break 385%, median crystallinity 23%. (hahn2024currenttrendsin pages 1-3)
* KT2440-specific examples include high cell densities and substantial PHA content under co-feeding strategies (e.g., KT2440 reaching 71 g/L with 56% mcl-PHA on glucose + nonanoic acid). (hahn2024currenttrendsin pages 7-9)

8) Current applications and real-world implementations

8.1 Bioplastic production from lignin-derived aromatics

Engineering work in P. putida KT2440 demonstrates production of mcl-PHA from lignin-related aromatics (e.g., p-coumaric acid and lignin streams). A combined engineering strategy (phaZ knockout; β-oxidation deletions; overexpression of phaG/alkK/phaC1/phaC2) produced substantial improvements in titre and yield relative to wild type. (salvachua2020metabolicengineeringof pages 1-3, salvachua2020metabolicengineeringof pages 4-5)

8.2 Feedstock flexibility and co-feeding

Meta-analysis highlights that co-feeding can decouple growth and PHA accumulation (e.g., glycerol for growth plus fatty acids for accumulation), improving PHA content dramatically in P. putida KT2440. (hahn2024currenttrendsin pages 7-9)

8.3 Bioprocess-relevant performance ranges

Hahn et al. summarize that mcl-PHA processes can reach high productivities (up to ~1.93 g/L/h in some pulsed-fed processes) and high biomass; these benchmarks help contextualize what PhaC1-centered engineering must ultimately support at scale (productivity, yield, and polymer properties). (hahn2024currenttrendsin pages 9-10, hahn2024currenttrendsin pages 5-7)

9) Expert analysis and interpretation (authoritative perspectives)

9.1 PhaC1 as the primary functional polymerase in KT2440

The operon architecture (two polymerases) invites questions about redundancy. In proteomics-focused discussions, PhaC1 is often treated as the main active synthase, while simply overexpressing PhaC2 does not necessarily increase mcl-PHA synthesis. This aligns with the broader engineering conclusion that polymerase abundance is only one component of polymer accumulation and that precursor supply and granule biology can dominate outcomes. (kelly2022proteomicsapproachesfor pages 86-91, dong2024modificationofglucose pages 13-14)

9.2 Granule biology is a critical frontier for functional annotation

The 2024 proteomics/AP-MS work emphasizes the difficulty of inferring true granule association from purification-based proteomes and suggests a more nuanced “organelle proteome + interactions + microscopy + genetics” standard for annotation. The identification of OprL/Pal as a candidate phasin illustrates that granule composition may include proteins not previously considered canonical GAPs, affecting how PhaC1’s in vivo activity is scaffolded and regulated. (kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 8-10, kelly2024comprehensiveproteomicsanalysis pages 7-8)

phaA (PP_5003 / phaC1) - P. putida KT2440 Notes

Gene Identity

  • UniProt: Q88D25 (TrEMBL, unreviewed)
  • Locus tag: PP_5003
  • Gene name: phaA (also called phaC1 in literature)
  • Product: Poly(3-hydroxyalkanoate) polymerase 1 (PHA synthase, Class II)
  • EC: 2.3.1.- (acyltransferase)
  • Length: 559 AA
  • PANTHER family: PTHR36837 (PHA/PHB synthase), subfamily SF5 (Poly-3-hydroxybutyrate synthase)

Gene Nomenclature Note

The gene is annotated as "phaA" in the P. putida KT2440 genome (PP_5003), but in the broader PHA literature it is referred to as phaC1. This is the first of two PHA synthases in the pha operon. The "phaA" nomenclature in P. putida should not be confused with phaA in Cupriavidus necator, which encodes beta-ketothiolase (a different enzyme in the PHB pathway).

PHA Operon Organization

The pha locus in P. putida KT2440 contains:
- phaC1 (PP_5003) - PHA synthase 1 (this gene)
- phaZ (PP_5004) - PHA depolymerase
- phaC2 (PP_5005) - PHA synthase 2
- phaD - TetR-like transcriptional regulator

The operon is transcribed as phaC1ZC2D and regulated by PhaD PMID:20406286.

Enzyme Classification

  • Class II PHA synthase: Distinguished from Class I (e.g., C. necator PhaC) by substrate preference for medium-chain-length (mcl, C6-C14) 3-hydroxyalkanoyl-CoA substrates
  • Catalytic mechanism: α/β-hydrolase fold with conserved catalytic triad Cys-His-Asp
  • PhaC box motif: G-G/S-X-C-X-G/A-G (modified lipase box)
  • Domain: PhaC_N domain (Pfam PF07167, residues 75-243)

Function

PhaC1 catalyzes the polymerization of (R)-3-hydroxyalkanoyl-CoA thioesters into polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs) with concomitant release of CoA. The reaction is a transesterification.

Substrate specificity: Class II PHA synthases like PhaC1 from P. putida preferentially polymerize medium-chain-length (mcl) substrates (C6-C14), producing mcl-PHAs. This is distinct from Class I synthases that produce short-chain-length PHB.

Biological role: PHAs accumulate as intracellular granules and serve as carbon and energy storage reserves under nutrient-limiting conditions (especially nitrogen limitation).

Key Literature Findings

  • PhaC1 is the primary PHA synthase in P. putida; overexpression of phaC1 enhanced mcl-PHA biosynthesis up to 2.86-fold, while phaC2 did not activate biosynthesis PMID:17137299
  • Deletion of the pha operon (phaC1-phaZ-phaC2, 3437 bp) creates PHA-deficient strain PpUW2
  • Deletion of PhaC1 causes morphological changes including reduced cell size
  • PHA metabolism involves simultaneous synthesis and degradation (turnover), controlled by coordinated expression of synthases and depolymerase PMID:20406286
  • The phaC1ZC2D transcription unit is regulated by PhaD, which controls its own transcription and that of the phaIF operon
  • P. putida KT2440 genome was sequenced PMID:12534463 and revisited PMID:26913973

Existing GO Annotations (GOA)

Only 2 IEA annotations:
1. GO:0016746 (acyltransferase activity) - IEA via InterPro:IPR011287
2. GO:0042619 (poly-hydroxybutyrate biosynthetic process) - IEA via InterPro:IPR010941, IPR011287

Assessment of Current Annotations

  • GO:0016746 is quite general; more specific acyltransferase terms may be appropriate
  • GO:0042619 refers specifically to poly-hydroxybutyrate (PHB, short-chain), but P. putida PhaC1 produces medium-chain-length PHAs (mcl-PHA), not PHB. This may be an over-specific or incorrect term assignment.

Relevant GO Terms to Consider

  • GO:0016746 (acyltransferase activity) - current, but may need more specific child
  • GO:0042619 (poly-hydroxybutyrate biosynthetic process) - may be incorrect for mcl-PHA producer
  • GO:0016747 (acyltransferase activity, transferring groups other than amino-acyl groups) - more specific MF
  • polyhydroxyalkanoate biosynthetic process - need to check if a broader PHA term exists
  • GO:0005737 (cytoplasm) - PHA granules are cytoplasmic
  • carbon storage/reserve metabolic process terms
