Gene Description

galC encodes 4-carboxy-4-hydroxy-2-oxoadipic acid aldolase, the terminal aldolase in the gallate degradation pathway. GalC cleaves CHA to pyruvate and oxaloacetate, linking gallate-derived carbon to central metabolism.

Falcon (Edison Scientific) deep research report for galC (Pseudomonas putida KT2440)
  • GalC is the CHA (HMG/CHA) aldolase that cleaves CHA to pyruvate and oxaloacetate, the terminal carbon-splitting step of gallate catabolism.
    "GalC is identified as the **HMG/CHA aldolase** responsible for converting CHA into the TCA-cycle metabolites **pyruvate and oxaloacetate**"
  • GalC is the terminal carbon-carbon cleavage step of the gallate pathway, completing conversion of an aromatic compound into central metabolism.
    "GalC is the terminal carbon–carbon cleavage step that converts the ring-cleavage-derived intermediate (CHA) into **pyruvate + oxaloacetate**, thereby completing conversion of an aromatic compound into central metabolism"
  • GalC belongs to the class II, divalent metal-dependent pyruvate aldolases and uses an enolate (not Schiff-base) mechanism.
    "HMG/CHA aldolases as **class II, divalent metal-dependent pyruvate aldolases**, operating via an **enolate** mechanism (not a Schiff-base)"
  • The enzyme is divalent-metal dependent, with Mg2+ featuring in structural discussion and conserved acidic motifs supporting metal binding.
    "The enzyme is described as **divalent-metal dependent**; Mg2+ appears in structural discussion"
  • HMG/CHA aldolases show a secondary oxaloacetate decarboxylase activity, consistent with a shared pyruvate-enolate intermediate.
    "HMG/CHA aldolases are reported to show **secondary oxaloacetate decarboxylase activity**, rationalized by a shared pyruvate-enolate intermediate"
  • GalC adopts an RraA-like fold, consistent with its UniProt LigK/PcmE family and RraA-like domain assignment.
    "A solved structure (discussed for *Pseudomonas putida* F1 homologs) shows an **αββα sandwich fold** that is **structurally similar to RraA family proteins**"
  • GalC is a soluble metabolic enzyme acting intracellularly (likely cytosolic) on cytosolic intermediates.
    "the most defensible annotation from these sources is that GalC functions **intracellularly (likely cytosolic)** as part of central aromatic catabolism"
  • GalC functions within the KT2440 gallate-utilization gene cluster galDCBRPAT.
    "A KT2440 gallate-utilization gene cluster is described as **galDCBRPAT**, encoding enzymatic steps for gallate catabolism plus uptake/regulation"

    > In order to detect gene and protein names which are assigned to products of different genes and thus result in erroneous cross-references, dedicated lists are created for each organism separately. Organism-specific lists are necessary, since terms that are ambiguous in one organism might be explicit in another. For example, ADORA2 is an ambiguous gene name in Homo sapiens but not in mouse, and GALT in mouse but not in H.sapiens.
    > In a first step, ambiguous names within the databases were extracted. If a name occurs in at least two entries describing different genes or proteins (splice variants count as one gene/protein), this particular name is marked as ambiguous and is excluded from the mapping process. In a second step, corresponding gene names occurring in the manually annotated sections of RefSeq as well as in UniProt were analyzed. Entries containing the same gene product name and having a one-to-many or many-to-many relation (e.g. one Swiss-Prot entry maps to many RefSeq entries) were scrutinized for misleading annotation. This process is done manually by inspecting additional information like sequence similarity or functional information about the involved entries. In most of the cases, the exclusion of the ambiguous gene names resulted in correct one-to-one relations.
    > As statistical analysis revealed (Supplementary Material S2) that gene names with less than four letters are exceptionally error-prone, only gene names with at least four letters are kept for mapping purposes. However, gene names with less than four letters can be queried, e.g. a search for the tumor suppressor 'p53' reveals the respective entries with the official gene name 'TP53'. Organism-specific lists of ambiguous gene and protein names are available for download on the CRONOS home page.

1) Key concepts, definitions, and current understanding

1.1 Pathway concept: “bio-funneling” of lignin-derived aromatics

Hardwood (syringyl-rich) lignin depolymerization yields a mixture of aromatic monomers (e.g., syringate/syringaldehyde/vanillate). Many bacteria “funnel” these diverse compounds into a smaller set of intermediates that enter core carbon metabolism. Gallate (gallic acid) and its ring-cleavage intermediates are part of one such funnel. (gu2024bacterialtransformationof pages 21-22, zhang2024tappingintothe pages 10-11)

1.2 The gallate (gallic acid) 4,5-cleavage “Gal” pathway

A KT2440 gallate-utilization gene cluster is described as galDCBRPAT, encoding enzymatic steps for gallate catabolism plus uptake/regulation. In this model, the aromatic ring of gallate is opened by GalA (gallate dioxygenase); the resulting meta-cleavage intermediate is processed through GalD (OMA tautomerase) and GalB (CHM hydratase) to generate CHA, which is then cleaved by GalC (HMG/CHA aldolase) into central metabolites. (mazurkewich2016…the4carboxy2hydroxymuconate pages 23-28)

1.3 Definition of GalC activity (CHA aldolase)

“CHA aldolase” refers to the enzyme catalyzing retro-aldol cleavage of 4-carboxy-4-hydroxy-2-oxoadipate (CHA) to produce pyruvate and oxaloacetate, i.e., direct entry points into central metabolism (TCA/anaplerosis). (mazurkewich2016…the4carboxy2hydroxymuconate pages 110-116, mazurkewich2016…the4carboxy2hydroxymuconate pages 39-44, mazurkewich2016…the4carboxy2hydroxymuconate pages 44-50)

2) Primary function of galC (enzyme reaction, substrates, specificity)

2.1 Catalyzed reaction and products

Across the biochemical/pathway descriptions available in the retrieved texts, GalC is identified as the HMG/CHA aldolase responsible for converting CHA into the TCA-cycle metabolites pyruvate and oxaloacetate. 2.2 Substrate scope and stereochemical preference

The same enzyme family is reported to accept both CHA and HMG (4-hydroxy-4-methyl-2-oxoglutarate) as substrates, motivating the common name “HMG/CHA aldolase.” Prior characterizations summarized in the retrieved evidence report stereochemical preferences for (-)-CHA and (-)-HMG and note that (-)-CHA is considered the physiological enantiomer (though the absolute configuration is described as not established in that summary). (mazurkewich2016…the4carboxy2hydroxymuconate pages 150-155, mazurkewich2016…the4carboxy2hydroxymuconate pages 160-164)

Biochemical/structural synthesis in the retrieved evidence describes HMG/CHA aldolases as class II, divalent metal-dependent pyruvate aldolases, operating via an enolate mechanism (not a Schiff-base). A solved structure (discussed for Pseudomonas putida F1 homologs) shows an αββα sandwich fold that is structurally similar to RraA family proteins, consistent with the family/domain information provided in UniProt for Q88JX9. The enzyme is described as divalent-metal dependent; Mg2+ appears in structural discussion, while assays/reconstitution also used Co2+ and comparisons among homologs indicate variable metal preferences. (mazurkewich2016…the4carboxy2hydroxymuconate pages 34-39, mazurkewich2016…the4carboxy2hydroxymuconate pages 54-59, mazurkewich2016…the4carboxy2hydroxymuconate pages 50-54)
