Gene Description

lapG (PP_0164) encodes the LapG adhesin-processing protease of the LapD/LapG retention circuit in Pseudomonas putida KT2440. The protein is a small exported cysteine peptidase family member whose documented role in KT2440 is to cleave surface-retained LapA after LapD no longer sequesters LapG, thereby reducing adhesiveness and promoting biofilm dispersal.

Research Report: Functional Annotation of lapG (UniProt Q88RG6, locus PP_0164) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

0) Target verification (identity and scope)

The literature explicitly links Pseudomonas putida KT2440 LapG to locus PP_0164, describing it as a transglutaminase-like cysteine proteinase (BTLCP/DUF920-related) acting in the LapD–LapG–LapA adhesin control circuit, consistent with UniProt Q88RG6 domain expectations (papain-like/transglutaminase-like cysteine peptidase superfamily) and the P. putida KT2440 strain context. (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 2-3, rybtke2015thelapgprotein pages 5-6)

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 LapG: a periplasmic, calcium-dependent cysteine protease controlling adhesin display

LapG is best understood as a periplasmic bacterial transglutaminase-like cysteine protease (BTLCP family; DUF920/COG3672) that proteolytically cleaves large cell-surface adhesins to switch cells between stable surface attachment/biofilm and release/dispersal states. (collins2020frominputto pages 8-9, collins2020frominputto pages 9-10)

Mechanistically, LapG family activity depends on (i) an active-site cysteine–histidine–aspartate triad and (ii) a Ca²⁺-binding region; mutation of these features or Ca²⁺ chelation abolishes activity in characterized LapG homolog systems, supporting annotation of LapG as a Ca²⁺-dependent cysteine endopeptidase. (collins2020frominputto pages 9-10, smith2018annterminalretention pages 22-24, rybtke2015thelapgprotein pages 5-6)

1.2 LapA retention/release and two-step secretion (“retention module” model)

The canonical substrate class for LapG-family proteases is the giant RTX adhesin LapA (≈520 kDa in the prototype system), which is secreted via a Lap-associated type I secretion system and retained at the cell surface by an N-terminal retention module that remains in the periplasm/outer-membrane pore context. LapG cleavage removes this N-terminal “plug/retention” segment, releasing LapA from the surface and thereby reducing attachment. (collins2020frominputto pages 4-5, collins2020frominputto pages 8-9)

A widely cited consensus motif for LapG-mediated processing is a dialanine-associated site (double Ala), with the review figure explicitly highlighting a consensus DPxxxLxxTAAG motif and labeling the retention domain as the region removed by LapG proteolysis. (collins2020frominputto pages 4-5, collins2020frominputto media e028d5c2)

1.3 Inside-out c-di-GMP signaling: LapD as the regulator of LapG

LapG does not act constitutively; it is controlled by LapD, an inner membrane c-di-GMP receptor/effector. When intracellular c-di-GMP is high, LapD adopts a conformation that enables it to bind/sequester LapG in the periplasm, limiting LapG access to LapA and thereby maintaining LapA on the cell surface (biofilm/attachment “ON”). When c-di-GMP is low, LapD releases LapG, LapG cleaves LapA, and attachment is reduced (dispersal “ON”). (collins2020frominputto pages 9-10, collins2020frominputto pages 4-5, collins2020frominputto media f163ac6f)

2) Functional annotation of P. putida KT2440 LapG (reaction, substrates, pathway, localization)

2.1 Primary biochemical function (reaction and substrate specificity)

Reaction (functional description): LapG is a periplasmic Ca²⁺-dependent cysteine endopeptidase that cleaves an N-terminal dialanine-associated motif of the adhesin LapA (and, by homology, other LapA-like/RTX adhesins), thereby releasing the adhesin from its surface-retained state. (collins2020frominputto pages 8-9, collins2020frominputto pages 9-10, collins2020frominputto pages 4-5)

Substrate specificity: In the LapA paradigm, LapG recognizes a conserved double-alanine (Ala–Ala) motif and can cleave at multiple AlaAla sites in vivo in the model system; this is consistent with LapG acting as a motif-guided adhesin-processing protease rather than a broad nonspecific protease. (collins2020frominputto pages 8-9, collins2020frominputto pages 9-10)

2.2 Cellular localization and where the reaction occurs

LapG is described as periplasmic, with its substrates’ cleavage region positioned to be periplasm-accessible during surface retention (i.e., when the adhesin is threaded/anchored via the Lap type I secretion pore, making the N-terminus accessible to periplasmic LapG). (smith2017“cellsurfaceassociated pages 1-6, collins2020frominputto pages 4-5)

2.3 Biological role in P. putida KT2440 biofilms (experimental genetics)

P. putida KT2440 LapG (PP_0164) is implicated in starvation/nutrient-limitation-induced dispersal by modifying the cell-surface adhesin LapA, which acts in both surface adhesion and as a biofilm matrix component. (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 2-3)

Genetic evidence supports a LapD→LapG→LapA hierarchy:
- ΔlapG mutants are hyper-biofilm formers and fail to disperse normally, consistent with an inability to release LapA from the surface. (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 2-3, rybtke2015thelapgprotein pages 5-6)
- ΔlapD mutants are biofilm-defective, consistent with loss of LapD-mediated repression of LapG, leading to excessive LapA release. (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 2-3, rybtke2015thelapgprotein pages 5-6)

3) Quantitative data and key statistics (from the cited studies)

3.1 Adhesion/biofilm-associated quantitative phenotypes in P. putida

In P. putida KT2440, ΔlapG mutants show markedly increased cell-surface hydrophobicity: approximately ~40% binding to hexadecane versus almost negligible binding in wild type, and this phenotype is reversed by proteinase K, supporting that an exposed protein (consistent with LapA) drives the increased hydrophobicity/attachment when LapG-mediated release is absent. (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 2-3)

3.2 Quantitative/mechanistic benchmarks from the Lap system literature

  • Atomic force microscopy measurements in the LapA paradigm indicate ~100 nN is required to detach LapA-mediated adhesion from glass, underscoring the mechanical robustness of LapA-dependent surface attachment (used as an expert benchmark for LapA-like adhesion strength). (collins2020frominputto pages 4-5)
  • In the Lap system review, LapA presentation on the surface is proportional to biofilm formation, and intracellular LapA in a lapD mutant is reported as ~half of wild type, consistent with dysregulated processing/localization. (collins2020frominputto pages 8-9)
  • Cross-species processing by LapG orthologs can vary substantially, with reported efficiency differences up to ~375-fold lower for heterologous enzyme–substrate pairs, indicating motif presence alone may be insufficient and that sequence context affects LapG processing. (collins2020frominputto pages 9-10)

4) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

4.1 2024: Direct cleavage-site mapping for an RTX adhesin (expands confidence in motif-based annotation)

A key 2024 advance is experimental identification of a LapG cleavage site within an RTX adhesin family member in Legionella pneumophila: LapG cleaves the RtxA N-terminus between residues 108–109 at a di-alanine-associated region, providing concrete mapping for LapG-family substrate processing and supporting the generality of LapG-mediated adhesin release beyond pseudomonads. (kanaan2024legionellapneumophilacell pages 2-4, kanaan2024legionellapneumophilacell pages 1-2)

4.2 2023: New regulatory layer—post-transcriptional control uncouples adhesin transcripts from surface adhesin abundance

In Pseudomonas fluorescens, a 2023 study showed that deleting fleQ increased lapA/mapA transcripts while markedly reducing detectable LapA/MapA protein; notably, lapG deletion in the ΔfleQ background restores detectable surface-associated adhesins and partially rescues biofilm formation, demonstrating that LapG-mediated release can dominate outcomes when adhesin abundance is limiting and highlighting the importance of post-transcriptional/secretion layers when interpreting LapG phenotypes. (pastora2023theregulatorfleqa pages 2-4)

4.3 2024: Orthologous Lap-like systems integrate with local c-di-GMP networks and other regulators

A 2024 Shewanella study of the Lap-orthologous Bpf system showed that the LapD-like effector (BpfD) binds c-di-GMP and connects to specific diguanylate cyclases and a chemotaxis response regulator, indicating that LapD/LapG-type adhesin control modules can be embedded in broader regulatory networks and can be repurposed to control specific biofilm architectures (e.g., pellicles). (poli2024bpfdisa pages 1-2)

4.4 2023: Expanded substrate scope in pseudomonads (LapG processes non-LapA adhesins)

A 2023 review of the Pseudomonas aeruginosa matrix adhesin CdrA summarizes that LapG can cleave CdrA under low c-di-GMP, leading to both cell-associated and released CdrA pools, reinforcing that LapG-family proteases can act on diverse surface adhesins (not only LapA). (reichhardt2023thepseudomonasaeruginosa pages 1-3)

5) Current applications and real-world implementations (mechanism-driven levers)

5.1 Biofilm control via “adhesin release switch” rather than bactericidal approaches

Because LapG directly controls whether a major adhesin is retained or released, it represents a mechanism-grounded target for biofilm engineering and anti-biofilm strategies that aim to modulate attachment/dispersal rather than kill cells. The Lap system review provides a clear model in which changing c-di-GMP changes LapD sequestration of LapG, enabling reversible switching between surface retention and release. (collins2020frominputto pages 9-10, collins2020frominputto media f163ac6f)

5.2 Environmental/chemical modulation of LapG via Ca²⁺ availability

LapG family activity is Ca²⁺-dependent, and the review notes that weak organic acids such as citrate can chelate Ca²⁺ and modulate LapG activity in vivo (in the prototype system), emphasizing a plausible environmental control axis on adhesin release. (collins2020frominputto pages 9-10)

5.3 Relevance to industrial/environmental P. putida KT2440 use cases

P. putida KT2440 is widely used as a chassis in environmental and industrial microbiology; within that context, LapG can be viewed as a controllable determinant of surface colonization stability (LapA retention) versus detachment/dispersal (LapA release), relevant to biofilm-based bioprocessing and dispersal management under nutrient stress. The core dispersal role for LapG in P. putida under starvation is supported by direct genetic evidence. (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 2-3)

6) Expert synthesis and interpretation (authoritative sources)

Authoritative synthesis from the Annual Review of Microbiology characterizes LapG as the periplasmic executor of an “inside-out” signaling circuit where a cytoplasmic second messenger (c-di-GMP) controls a periplasmic protease via LapD; this constitutes a paradigm for linking intracellular signaling states to extracellular adhesion. (collins2020frominputto pages 9-10, collins2020frominputto pages 4-5)

From a functional-annotation perspective, the most defensible specific description for P. putida LapG (PP_0164; Q88RG6) is:
- Molecular function: Ca²⁺-dependent periplasmic cysteine endopeptidase of the BTLCP family that cleaves N-terminal dialanine-associated motifs on LapA (and likely LapA-like adhesins), releasing adhesin from a surface-retained state. (collins2020frominputto pages 9-10, collins2020frominputto pages 4-5, rybtke2015thelapgprotein pages 5-6)
- Biological process: c-di-GMP/LapD-controlled reversible surface attachment and biofilm dispersal, especially under nutrient starvation. (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 2-3, rybtke2015thelapgprotein pages 5-6)
- Cellular component: periplasm. (smith2017“cellsurfaceassociated pages 1-6, collins2020frominputto pages 4-5)

10) Limitations / open points for P. putida KT2440-specific enzymology

While P. putida LapG (PP_0164) is genetically linked to LapA-dependent dispersal and is predicted/annotated as a transglutaminase-like cysteine protease, most direct biochemical characterization of catalytic residues/cofactors and cleavage motif mapping comes from closely related pseudomonad systems and reviews; P. putida-specific in vitro kinetics and precise LapA cleavage-site mapping were not present in the retrieved KT2440 primary text excerpts. (gjermansen2010characterizationofstarvation‐induced pages 2-3, collins2020frominputto pages 9-10)
