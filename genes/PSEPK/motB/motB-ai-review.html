Gene Description

motB encodes the B subunit of a proton-driven bacterial flagellar stator in Pseudomonas putida KT2440. The protein is a single-pass inner-membrane component with a large periplasmic OmpA-like domain that is expected to bind peptidoglycan and anchor the MotAB stator while ion flow through the stator powers flagellar rotation. In P. putida, this function should be interpreted in the context of a dual-stator motor system rather than as the sole stator in the organism.

The target protein is MotB, annotated as “flagellar motor rotation protein” in Pseudomonas putida KT2440, and corresponds to the chromosomal locus motB (PP4904) with adjacent/partner motA (PP4905) reported for KT2440. This matches the conserved MotB-family flagellar stator B subunit, rather than any unrelated gene that might share a similar symbol in other organisms. 1) Key concepts and current understanding

1.1 What MotB is (definition)

MotB is the B subunit of the MotAB stator complex in bacterial flagellar motors. The stator is a membrane-embedded ion-channel module that converts ion motive force into mechanical torque that rotates the flagellum. MotB functions together with MotA; modern descriptions commonly treat the stator as a MotA_5:MotB_2 assembly. (velezgonzalez2024rotationofthe pages 1-2, nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 8-9)

1.2 Domain architecture (how MotB is built)

Across MotB-family proteins, MotB contains:
- A single transmembrane helix that contributes to the ion pathway; a conserved Asp in this region is required for proton conduction in H+-driven systems. (velezgonzalez2024rotationofthe pages 1-2)
- A periplasmic plug segment adjacent to the TM region that gates the channel (prevents premature ion leak) until the stator is properly engaged with the motor. (velezgonzalez2024rotationofthe pages 1-2)
- A C-terminal OmpA-like peptidoglycan-binding domain that anchors the stator to the cell wall (peptidoglycan). Comparative structural work defines the plug+linker region as lying between the TM catalytic domain and the OmpA-like PG-binding domain. (puentelelievre2025evolutionandstructural pages 13-15)

These features align with the domain annotations you provided from UniProt/InterPro/Pfam (MotB-like N-terminus + plug + OmpA-like/PG-binding domain), supporting that Q88DC3 is the canonical stator B subunit.

1.3 Subcellular localization (where MotB acts)

MotB is an inner-membrane stator component whose periplasmic region extends outward to bind peptidoglycan. In mechanistic models, stator recruitment/activation involves extension or repositioning of MotB’s periplasmic domain to reach the peptidoglycan layer, thereby anchoring the stator and enabling productive torque generation. (velezgonzalez2024rotationofthe pages 1-2)

1.4 Mechanism (what MotB does)

A current mechanistic view supported by recent reviews and primary studies is:
1. MotA/MotB assemble as an ion-channel stator module; stator units can dynamically exchange between the motor and a membrane pool. (nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 8-9)
2. The plug region helps keep the channel inactive until correct assembly at the rotor; upon motor recruitment, conformational change is coupled to “unplugging” and initiation of ion flow. (velezgonzalez2024rotationofthe pages 1-2)
3. Ion flow (typically H+ for MotAB-type stators) is coupled to torque generation through interactions with rotor components (often discussed in terms of stator–C-ring/FliG coupling). (velezgonzalez2024rotationofthe pages 1-2)
4. Stator incorporation/stability is coupled to the energetic state: in E. coli, stator incorporation depends on the proton motive force, and collapse of PMF can drive stator dissociation; other species show analogous dependence on their coupling ion (e.g., Na+ for sodium stators). (nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 8-9)

2) Recent developments and latest research (emphasis on 2023–2024)

2.1 Dynamic stator assembly and coupling to ion motive force (review synthesis, 2024)

A 2024 review summarizes that MotA/MotB stators assemble around the rotor one unit at a time and that MotB-containing stators exchange even during rotation; critically, incorporation and dissociation can be modulated by the availability of the coupling ion motive force (PMF in H+-driven motors). (Nakamura & Minamino, Nov 2024, Biomolecules, https://doi.org/10.3390/biom14121488) (nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 8-9)

2.2 Plug-gated activation and peptidoglycan anchoring (primary literature, 2024)

A 2024 primary study (not in Pseudomonas putida but directly on MotB-family stator biology) describes MotB as containing a plug helix that interacts with the membrane to keep the channel inactive, and proposes activation upon stator recruitment when MotB’s periplasmic domain extends to allow peptidoglycan anchoring, concomitant with unplugging and motor function. (Vélez-González et al., Mar 2024, PLOS ONE, https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0298028) (velezgonzalez2024rotationofthe pages 1-2)

Direct KT2440 motB-only knockout phenotypes were not available in the retrieved evidence. Nonetheless, multiple flagella system loss phenotypes quantify why MotB-dependent torque generation matters physiologically:
- KT2440 typically carries 5–7 polar flagella (background trait). (martinez‐garcia2014themetaboliccost pages 1-4)
- Deletion of a ~70 kb flagellar region (PP4329–PP4397, ~1.1% of the genome) produced an aflagellate, non-motile strain (unable to swim; no protruding flagella by TEM). (martinez‐garcia2014themetaboliccost pages 4-7, martinez‐garcia2014themetaboliccost pages 1-4)
- Surface properties shifted: hydrophobicity (MATH score) decreased from 59.3 ± 12.1 (WT) to 36.4 ± 11.5 (Δflagellar region), P < 0.0001. (martinez‐garcia2014themetaboliccost pages 4-7)
- Metabolic/redox reallocation consistent with an energetic burden of motility: ATP/ADP ratio 1.3-fold higher and NADPH/NADP+ ratio 1.2-fold higher in the non-flagellated strain (in excerpted results), along with improved paraquat survival in multiple media. (martinez‐garcia2014themetaboliccost pages 7-10)

These results do not isolate MotB’s effect from other flagellar genes, but they provide quantitative, strain-relevant evidence that disabling the flagellar system (of which MotB is an essential torque-generating component) can measurably alter energy allocation, surface interactions, and stress tolerance. (martinez‐garcia2014themetaboliccost pages 4-7, martinez‐garcia2014themetaboliccost pages 7-10)

4) Current applications and real-world implementations (MotB relevance)

MotB is not typically deployed as a standalone “application gene,” but it is functionally central to flagellar motility, which is a practical determinant of:
- Surface colonization and adhesion/biofilm behaviors: KT2440 flagellar-region deletion increased adhesion persistence (remained adhered after multiple days in the reported assay) and altered surface physicochemical properties, illustrating how motility modules can be tuned to shift between planktonic dispersal and surface-associated states—an important consideration in environmental and industrial uses of Pseudomonas chassis strains. (martinez‐garcia2014themetaboliccost pages 4-7)
- Fitness trade-offs in bioprocessing/bioremediation chassis design: Quantified energetic/redox changes in the non-flagellated strain (e.g., increased ATP/ADP and NADPH/NADP+) provide a rationale for engineering reduced motility to reallocate resources toward production or stress tolerance when dispersal is not desired. (martinez‐garcia2014themetaboliccost pages 7-10)

5) Expert interpretation, consensus, and uncertainties

5.1 Consensus model supported by authoritative sources

Across contemporary synthesis and primary evidence, MotB-family proteins are understood as:
- A stator anchor and gate: anchoring stator units to peptidoglycan (OmpA-like PG-binding domain) and gating ion flow via a plug segment. (velezgonzalez2024rotationofthe pages 1-2, puentelelievre2025evolutionandstructural pages 13-15)
- A dynamic motor component: stators assemble/disassemble in response to energetic conditions (ion motive force) and possibly load, as summarized in 2024 review literature. (nakamura2024structureanddynamics pages 8-9)

5.2 What remains unclear specifically for Q88DC3 / PP_4904 in KT2440

From the retrieved, citable sources, the following items cannot yet be asserted as KT2440/Q88DC3-specific facts (they remain well-supported MotB-family inferences):
- The exact MotB plug residues/structural transitions in KT2440, and whether they differ from other pseudomonads.
- A clean genetic dissection of PP_4904 motB alone (motility speed/torque, stator occupancy, etc.).
- Definitive promoter-class assignment for PmotA (σ\u20754/FleQ vs FliA) based solely on the extracted text available here. (leal‐morales2022transcriptionalorganizationand pages 10-11)
