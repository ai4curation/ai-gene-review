Gene Description

phaG encodes a 3-hydroxyacyl-ACP:CoA transacylase (originally characterized as (R)-3-hydroxydecanoyl-ACP:CoA transacylase) that connects de novo fatty-acid synthesis to medium-chain-length polyhydroxyalkanoate (mcl-PHA) biosynthesis by supplying (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA monomers from ACP-bound fatty-acid-synthesis intermediates. The directionality and exact in vivo mechanism (ACP:CoA transacylase versus thioesterase coupled to an acyl-CoA ligase) have been debated, and deletion genetics in KT2440 show PhaG is a contributory but non-essential, partially redundant node in PHA monomer supply.

This activity connects de novo fatty-acid synthesis to medium-chain-length polyhydroxyalkanoate monomer supply.

Research report: phaG (PP_1408; UniProt O85207) in Pseudomonas putida KT2440

0) Target verification (critical identity check)

The target protein is PhaG from Pseudomonas putida strain KT2440 (also referenced as ATCC 47054/DSM 6125), encoded by PP_1408 and mapped to UniProt O85207. Multiple independent sources explicitly connect phaG ↔ PP_1408 and describe it as an enzyme acting on (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-ACP intermediates to supply precursors for medium-chain-length polyhydroxyalkanoate (mcl-PHA) biosynthesis (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 3-4, liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 13-16).

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 mcl-PHA monomer supply and the “PhaG link”

In pseudomonads, mcl-PHA is polymerized by PHA synthases using (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA monomers. A central metabolic challenge is how cells generate these CoA thioesters from either (i) β-oxidation intermediates when fatty acids are supplied or (ii) de novo fatty-acid synthesis (FAS) intermediates when “unrelated” substrates (e.g., sugars, glycerol) are used.

PhaG is widely described as the enzyme that links de novo FAS to PHA by converting (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-ACP → (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA (i.e., an ACP:CoA transacylase/transferase activity), thereby creating direct PHA-synthase substrates from ACP-bound FAS intermediates (liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 13-16).

1.2 Competing mechanistic models: transacylase vs thioesterase

A major theme in the literature is that PhaG’s dominant in vivo activity has been debated:

  • Model A (transacylase/transferase): PhaG transfers the (R)-3-hydroxyacyl group from ACP to CoA, providing (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA in a single step (liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3, yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 3-4).
  • Model B (thioesterase-like route): Heterologous data in E. coli have been interpreted as PhaG functioning (at least in that context) like a 3-hydroxyacyl-ACP thioesterase, generating free 3-hydroxy fatty acids that then require activation by an acyl-CoA ligase (e.g., AlkK/PP_0763) to re-form CoA thioesters (wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 85-88, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 16-19).

A current practical view is that PhaG is a “connector enzyme” at the ACP/CoA boundary of lipid metabolism, and apparent activity can depend on host background, competing thioesterases, and availability of CoA-ligation/processing enzymes (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 3-4, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 16-19).

2) Enzymatic function and substrate specificity (best-supported)

2.1 Primary function in P. putida KT2440 metabolism

Across KT2440-focused sources, PhaG is consistently positioned as enabling flux from FAS to PHA precursors by acting on ACP-bound (R)-3-hydroxyacyl intermediates and supplying PHA monomers (liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 13-16).

The chain-length window associated with PhaG-mediated monomer supply is consistent with mcl products (classically C6–C14 range in PhaG-centered descriptions of mcl-PHA) (zheng2005molecularcloningand pages 1-2).

2.2 Evidence supporting transacylase function (ACP→CoA transfer)

A high-impact metabolic engineering study leveraging KT2440 PhaG (PpuPhaG; PP_1408) reported metabolite profiles consistent with ACP:CoA transacylase function: in engineered E. coli, PpuPhaG overexpression supported production of 170 mg/L C7–C13 methyl ketones with no detectable 3-hydroxy methyl esters, which the authors interpret as evidence that PpuPhaG “functions primarily as a 3-hydroxyacyl ACP:CoA transacylase” (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 3-4). Strains lacking PhaG overexpression produced only trace methyl ketones (<1 mg/L) (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 3-4).

This work also frames an energetic rationale: using a transferase/transacylase step saves one ATP per product relative to the thioesterase + acyl-CoA synthetase route (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 2-3).

2.3 Evidence supporting thioesterase-like behavior (ACP hydrolysis) in heterologous contexts

A detailed recombinant study (focused on reconstructing PHA pathways in E. coli) reported that expressing phaG led to secretion of ~30 mg/L free 3-hydroxy fatty acids (notably 58% 3-hydroxydecanoic acid and 42% 3-hydroxyoctanoic acid), interpreted as consistent with thioesterase function (wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 85-88). In the same study, high PHA formation required an acyl-CoA ligase (PP_0763) along with phaG and a PHA synthase: recombinant E. coli accumulated 4.1 ± 1.3% CDW PHA (about 69 ± 21 mg/L) in the phaG+ligase+phaC configuration (wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 85-88). The authors also reported ~220-fold higher phaG transcription in KT2440 under nitrogen-limited PHA-producing conditions (wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 81-85, wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 85-88).

An authoritative review centered on P. putida PHA engineering summarizes this conflict: PhaG was initially described as a transferase linking FAS to PHA, but heterologous phenotypes (e.g., secretion of 3-hydroxydecanoate and dependence on AlkK) motivated the alternative thioesterase-like interpretation (mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 16-19).

3) Biological process, pathway context, and in vivo physiology

3.1 Pathway role: connecting de novo FAS and PHA accumulation

A 2023 mechanistic reassessment of PHA monomer supply in KT2440 explicitly describes PhaG as an enzyme that “directly links the fatty acids de novo synthesis and PHA accumulation” by converting (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-ACP → (R)-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA (liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3).

3.2 Functional redundancy in monomer supply (important current insight)

The same 2023 study provides strong evidence that PhaG is contributory but not essential for PHA accumulation in KT2440 because monomer supply is redundant:

  • When three (R)-specific enoyl-CoA hydratase homologs were deleted, the strain still accumulated PHA up to 10.7% CDW.
  • Additional phaG deletion caused a further 1.8-fold decrease in PHA content but did not abolish PHA accumulation (liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3).

This indicates that (i) PhaG is part of the monomer supply network and (ii) KT2440 can route carbon to PHA through other enzymes/pathways when PhaG is absent (liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3).

3.3 Regulation: nitrogen limitation and carbon-source dependence

Nitrogen limitation/high C:N is repeatedly associated with phaG induction and PHA accumulation.

  • Transcriptomics in KT2440 during mcl-PHA production showed carbon-source dependent timing of phaG expression and clear induction linked to nitrogen depletion: on sodium gluconate, maxima for multiple pha genes occurred at 24 h when ammonium was fully consumed; on oleic acid, nitrogen depletion induced phaF and phaG with phaG peaking at 41 h (mozejkociesielska2018transcriptomeremodelingof pages 4-6).
  • In KT2440, phaG transcription was reported as ~220-fold higher under low-nitrogen, PHA-producing conditions versus high nitrogen (wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 81-85, wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 85-88).
  • A related P. putida strain (CA-3) encodes a PhaG >99% identical to KT2440 PhaG; in CA-3, maximal phaG expression occurred only under nitrogen limitation and coincided with PHA accumulation, supporting the nitrogen-dependent regulation model in closely related systems (oleary2005geneticcharacterizationof pages 1-2).

4) Subcellular localization and site of action

None of the retrieved sources provides direct experimental localization (e.g., microscopy, fractionation) of KT2440 PhaG (PP_1408). However, all mechanistic descriptions place its activity on ACP-bound intermediates of cytosolic fatty-acid synthesis, implying a cytosolic functional context (mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 13-16, liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3). This should be treated as inference from substrate context rather than a measured localization claim.

5) Current applications and real-world implementations

5.1 Metabolic engineering beyond PHAs: medium-chain oleochemicals

A major modern application of KT2440 PhaG is as a tool enzyme to channel ACP-bound intermediates to CoA-linked pools for downstream product formation.

A 2022 metabolic engineering study used PpuPhaG (PP_1408) to implement an “acyl-ACP:CoA transacylase strategy” enabling production of several medium-chain products in engineered E. coli, reporting titers up to 1.1 g/L C8–C14 free fatty acids, 1.5 g/L C7–C15 methyl ketones, and 1.1 g/L C8–C16 fatty alcohols (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 2-3). This strategy is positioned as energetically favorable because it can avoid ATP costs associated with free-fatty-acid activation (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 2-3).

These outcomes represent a real-world synthetic-biology implementation of the “PhaG link,” extending its utility from biopolymer monomer supply to broader oleochemical biomanufacturing (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 2-3).

5.2 mcl-PHA process relevance in KT2440

While the retrieved KT2440-centric evidence is mechanistic and regulatory rather than industrial process-focused, phaG expression patterns are repeatedly used as biomarkers/targets for improving mcl-PHA synthesis under nitrogen limitation and varied carbon sources (mozejkociesielska2018transcriptomeremodelingof pages 4-6).

6) Expert opinions and synthesis (authoritative analysis)

  1. Connector enzyme consensus: Across reviews and mechanistic studies, PhaG is consistently characterized as a key connector between de novo fatty acid synthesis and PHA metabolism (mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 16-19, liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3).
  2. Mechanistic ambiguity remains: A high-impact engineering study argues metabolite signatures support transacylase function for PpuPhaG (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 3-4), while other recombinant reconstructions and reviews summarize evidence compatible with thioesterase + ligase coupling (wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 85-88, mezzina2021engineeringnativeand pages 16-19).
  3. Redundancy is now central (2023): Deletion analyses demonstrate that even removing multiple monomer-supplying enzymes including PhaG does not eliminate PHA accumulation, pointing to a robust, redundant network and suggesting that “single-gene” interventions may shift yields but rarely abolish phenotype (liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3).

7) Key statistics and data highlights (from recent studies)

  • PhaG deletion effect in KT2440: phaG deletion produced an additional 1.8-fold decrease in PHA content (in a background already lacking three hydratases) but did not abolish PHA accumulation (Feb 2023; AMB) (liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3).
  • Residual PHA despite monomer-supply deletions: triple hydratase deletion strain still accumulated PHA up to 10.7% CDW (Feb 2023; AMB) (liu2023βoxidation–polyhydroxyalkanoatessynthesisrelationship pages 1-3).
  • Nitrogen regulation: phaG transcription ~220-fold higher under low N vs high N (KT2440; reported in a recombinant-focused study measuring KT2440 expression) (wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 81-85, wang2012investigationanddevelopment pages 85-88).
  • Transacylase-strategy metabolite signature: 170 mg/L C7–C13 methyl ketones and <1 mg/L in controls; 55 mg/L C8–C12 3-hydroxy methyl esters in a specific engineered context (Aug 2022; Nat Commun record) (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 3-4).
  • Engineered titers enabled by PhaG-based strategy: up to 1.1 g/L free fatty acids, 1.5 g/L methyl ketones, and 1.1 g/L fatty alcohols (Aug 2022; Nat Commun record) (yan2022metabolicengineeringstrategies pages 2-3).

8) Evidence gaps and recommended next steps (annotation confidence)

  • Direct enzymology in KT2440 (kinetics, substrate preference, directionality) is not present in the retrieved full-text evidence set. The literature repeatedly refers to conversion between ACP- and CoA-linked 3-hydroxyacyl species, but specific kcat/Km values for KT2440 PhaG were not captured here.
  • Subcellular localization of PhaG is not directly measured in the retrieved corpus; current localization is inferred from ACP/FAS substrate context.

Summary

PhaG (PP_1408; UniProt O85207) in P. putida KT2440 is best annotated as a central lipid-metabolism connector that supplies mcl-PHA monomers from de novo fatty-acid synthesis via reactions at the ACP/CoA interface. Its regulation is tightly linked to nitrogen limitation and carbon-source regime. Modern 2023 deletion genetics highlight metabolic redundancy in monomer supply, while contemporary engineering work uses KT2440 PhaG as a modular enzyme to route ACP-bound carbon into CoA-linked pools for both PHA and non-PHA oleochemical production.
