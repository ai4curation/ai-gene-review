## Research report: **groEL (GroEL/Cpn60/Hsp60)** in *Escherichia coli* K-12 (UniProt **P0A6F5**)

### 0) Target verification (critical disambiguation)
The literature summarized here matches the UniProt target **P0A6F5** from *Escherichia coli* K-12: **GroEL**, a **group I chaperonin (Hsp60/Cpn60)** that forms a **double-ring tetradecamer** and functions with the co-chaperonin **GroES (Hsp10/Cpn10)** to assist protein folding in the **cytosol** (gardner2023structuralbasisof pages 1-2, wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2). The described architecture (two heptameric rings), ATPase-coupled folding cycle, and domain organization (equatorial/intermediate/apical) are consistent across foundational and recent sources (gardner2023structuralbasisof pages 1-2, xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3).

### 1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

#### 1.1 What GroEL is
GroEL is the archetypal bacterial **chaperonin** (Hsp60 family): a large, barrel-shaped complex that binds **non-native polypeptides** and, together with GroES, provides a protected environment that promotes productive folding and prevents aggregation (ranson1998chaperonins. pages 1-2, xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3).

**Structural definition.** GroEL assembles as a **tetradecamer** (two back-to-back **heptameric** rings), forming a central cavity (gardner2023structuralbasisof pages 1-2, xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3). Each GroEL subunit comprises three major domains: (i) an **equatorial** domain (ATP binding/hydrolysis and ring-ring contacts), (ii) an **apical** domain (binding of substrate and GroES), and (iii) an **intermediate** hinge domain that couples nucleotide state to apical motions (gardner2023structuralbasisof pages 1-2, wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2).

#### 1.2 Core biochemical activity and enzymatic classification
GroEL is an **ATPase** whose ATP-driven conformational cycle powers capture, encapsulation, and release of folding substrates in cooperation with GroES (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3, ranson1998chaperonins. pages 1-2). In mechanistic terms, ATP binding and hydrolysis act as both **energy input** and **timer** for the encapsulation cycle (ellis2001molecularchaperonesinside pages 1-2, xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3).

Although UniProt annotates EC **5.6.1.7**, the retrieved primary sources in this run chiefly describe GroEL as an ATP-driven folding machine and do not explicitly restate this EC number in the extracted passages; the functional description remains unambiguous from structural/biochemical evidence (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3, gardner2023structuralbasisof pages 1-2).

#### 1.3 “Anfinsen cage” and conformational switching
A central concept in GroEL biology is that GroES capping converts GroEL into a **folding chamber** (“cage”) in which folding proceeds without aggregation (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3, ranson1998chaperonins. pages 1-2). A key mechanistic feature is a nucleotide- and GroES-dependent **remodeling of the cavity**: upon ATP and GroES binding, the cis-ring undergoes major conformational changes that create an **enlarged chamber** and change the environment from **hydrophobic binding surfaces** to a more **hydrophilic** interior conducive to folding (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3, saibil2002thechaperoninfolding pages 3-4). 

#### 1.4 Two-stroke cycle and inter-ring allostery
GroEL operates as an alternating-ring (“two-stroke”) machine with **negative cooperativity between rings**, such that events in one ring regulate the other (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3, saibil2002thechaperoninfolding pages 3-4). Foundational work quantified a typical cycle as providing ~**15 s** for folding at the cost of **7 ATP** molecules (per cis ring) (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3), and Ellis describes a similar ~15 s timing at 25°C linked to ATP hydrolysis and GroES residence (ellis2001molecularchaperonesinside pages 1-2).

### 2) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

#### 2.1 2024: In situ chaperonin cycle and stoichiometry in *E. coli*
A major 2024 advance is **direct visualization in cells** by cryo-electron tomography. Wagner et al. (Nature, **Aug 2024**, https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-07843-w) quantified GroEL–GroES states in *E. coli* and found that **~55–70%** of GroEL complexes are **asymmetrical** (GroES on one ring; “bullet” EL–ES1), with the remainder in symmetrical (“football” EL–ES2) states (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2). They also detected **substrate** bound to the **free ring** of the asymmetrical complex (defining a substrate-acceptor state), and visualized **encapsulated substrate** in a folded-like state prior to release into the cytosol (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2). Figure-level quantification in the same work shows ~**60:40** EL–ES1:EL–ES2 at 37°C and ~**70%** asymmetric complexes under heat stress (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction media 7e90e690).

#### 2.2 2023: Cryo-EM snapshots of substrate progression through the cycle
Gardner et al. (PNAS, **Dec 2023**, https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2308933120) provided a structural basis for how substrates progress through the bacterial chaperonin cycle. They describe capture via hydrophobic apical interactions and ATP-driven steps that drive chamber expansion (roughly **doubling cavity volume**) to accommodate substrates up to **~60–70 kDa** (gardner2023structuralbasisof pages 1-2). A notable mechanistic insight is an **asymmetric 4:3 subunit arrangement** in a substrate-bound ring, consistent with GroES recruitment while some subunits maintain substrate contacts—helping explain how encapsulation can occur without premature loss of the client (gardner2023structuralbasisof pages 1-2).

#### 2.3 2023: Updated view of the in vivo GroEL client repertoire and obligate substrates
A 2023 review focused on *E. coli* clients (Taguchi & Koike-Takeshita, Frontiers in Molecular Biosciences, **Feb 2023**, https://doi.org/10.3389/fmolb.2023.1091677) synthesizes large-scale client discovery and emphasizes that GroEL/GroES is a key hub preventing aggregation and enabling folding for a substantial client set (taguchi2023invivoclient pages 1-2). Quantitatively, it summarizes: 
- a chaperone-free translation dataset covering **3,173 proteins** (>70% of the proteome), with **~1/3** aggregated in the absence of chaperones (taguchi2023invivoclient pages 2-3);
- an estimate that **~250** proteins were identified in GroEL–GroES complexes in mass spectrometry studies, and that **~80 proteins** have been identified as **Class IV** obligate clients (taguchi2023invivoclient pages 2-3);
- reclassification work in which ~**60%** of Class III candidates required GroE in vivo and became bona fide Class IV clients (taguchi2023invivoclient pages 3-4).

### 3) Gene product function, biological processes, and cellular localization

#### 3.1 Primary molecular function: ATP-dependent chaperonin folding assistance
GroEL’s primary role is to assist folding of **non-native cytosolic proteins** through repeated cycles of binding, ATP-driven conformational switching, GroES-mediated encapsulation, and timed release (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3, ranson1998chaperonins. pages 1-2). Recent in situ evidence indicates substrates can be observed **encapsulated** and subsequently released into the **cytosol** in a folded state (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2).

#### 3.2 Substrate scope and specificity (what proteins does it act on?)
GroEL is not sequence-specific in the classical enzyme sense; instead it recognizes **non-native conformations** and exposes hydrophobic patches typical of folding intermediates (gardner2023structuralbasisof pages 1-2, saibil2001allosteryandprotein pages 1-4). However, the in vivo client repertoire is structured:
- Many studies support that GroEL/GroES assists folding of roughly **~10%** of cytosolic/newly synthesized proteins (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2, wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunctiona pages 33-36).
- The client set includes both **interactors** (transiently bound) and a smaller set of **obligate clients** that require GroEL/GroES to fold properly in vivo (taguchi2023invivoclient pages 3-4, taguchi2023invivoclient pages 2-3).
- Obligate clients are typically **aggregation-prone**, enriched for certain folds (e.g., TIM barrels), and generally constrained to sizes that fit the cage (often **<~70 kDa**) (taguchi2023invivoclient pages 3-4).

**Essential client examples.** In one curated Class IV set, six named GroE-dependent essential proteins include **DapA, ASD, MetK, FtsE, HemB, KdsA** (taguchi2023invivoclient pages 3-4).

#### 3.3 Cellular localization
The dominant functional localization of GroEL is **cytosolic**: GroEL–GroES complexes were visualized in situ in *E. coli* and shown to release folded substrates into the **cytosol** (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2). 

### 4) Expert synthesis and authoritative mechanistic interpretations

#### 4.1 How GroEL promotes folding (expert mechanistic consensus)
Foundational structural reviews describe GroEL/GroES as a folding machine that couples **ATP binding/hydrolysis** to: (i) substrate capture by hydrophobic apical sites; (ii) GroES capping and release of substrate into an expanded chamber; (iii) a switch to a more hydrophilic interior; and (iv) timed release coordinated by inter-ring allostery (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3, saibil2002thechaperoninfolding pages 3-4). The “two-stroke” analogy—one ring processing while the other loads/unloads, coordinated by negative cooperativity—captures the core allosteric design (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3).

Recent structural work extends this view by resolving intermediate states that show how substrates may remain partially engaged while GroES is recruited, suggesting a physically choreographed sequence of binding sites during encapsulation rather than a single abrupt release event (gardner2023structuralbasisof pages 1-2).

#### 4.2 Essentiality and network role in proteostasis
GroEL/GroES is repeatedly described as uniquely important for bacterial viability, and the 2023 review calls GroE the only indispensable chaperone for *E. coli* viability (taguchi2023invivoclient pages 2-3). Proteome-scale observations that large fractions of proteins aggregate without chaperones and that a substantial subset of proteins require GroE for folding highlight why GroEL is central in proteostasis (taguchi2023invivoclient pages 2-3, taguchi2023invivoclient pages 3-4).

### 6) Current applications and real-world implementations

#### 6.1 Recombinant protein production in *E. coli*
Co-expression of GroEL/ES (often with other chaperones like Trigger Factor) is widely used to reduce inclusion bodies and increase soluble recovery of difficult proteins.

- **Thermus thermophilus DNA polymerase in BL21(DE3):** combined GroEL/ES + Trigger Factor co-expression reduced insoluble fraction and increased total protein concentration from **3.7019 mg/mL** (no chaperone) to **3.9258 mg/mL** (GroEL/ES+TF) in the reported workflow (Maksum et al., **Jan 2024**, https://doi.org/10.7324/japs.2024.152923) (maksum2024theeffectsof pages 3-5).
- **Vaccinia virus F13 (p37) in BL21(DE3):** GroEL/ES + Trigger Factor co-expression enabled production and recovery of GST-F13 with reported yields of **~50–80 mg/L** after purification/refolding (Merkuleva et al., **Dec 2024**, https://doi.org/10.3390/biotech13040057) (merkuleva2024theeffectsof pages 8-11).

#### 6.2 In-cell structural biology and method development
GroEL is also a major target for cutting-edge imaging/structural methods because of its size, abundance, and well-defined states. For example, in situ cryo-ET has been used to quantify GroEL–GroES state populations directly in *E. coli* (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2), and GroEL has been used as a test specimen for single-protein diffraction method development (Ekeberg et al., **Jan 2024**) though detailed functional annotation relies on the chaperonin-focused works above.

### 7) Visual evidence (in situ chaperonin states)
Wagner et al. provide a figure illustrating both the morphological distinction between asymmetrical (“bullet”) and symmetrical (“football”) GroEL–GroES complexes and the condition-dependent quantification of their abundance (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction media 7e90e690). This image supports the claim that asymmetrical EL–ES1 predominates in vivo and that environmental conditions (e.g., heat stress) shift the population.

### 8) Summary functional annotation (for databases)
**Protein:** GroEL (Chaperonin 60; Hsp60/Cpn60) 

**Primary function:** ATP-dependent molecular chaperone that, with GroES, promotes folding of non-native cytosolic proteins by binding folding intermediates, encapsulating them in a chamber whose properties are remodeled by ATP/GroES binding, and releasing clients in a timed, alternating-ring cycle (xu1998groelgroesstructureand pages 1-3, saibil2002thechaperoninfolding pages 3-4).

**Localization:** Cytosol; chaperonin complexes and folded-substrate release observed in situ in *E. coli* cells (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2).

**Biological processes:** Proteostasis, folding of newly synthesized proteins, recovery from stress; supports folding for a defined client set (~10% cytosolic proteome, with a subset obligately dependent) (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2, taguchi2023invivoclient pages 2-3).

**Complex/partners:** GroEL tetradecamer + GroES heptamer; in vivo populations include both EL–ES1 and EL–ES2, with EL–ES1 typically more abundant (wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction pages 1-2, wagner2024visualizingchaperoninfunction media 7e90e690).
