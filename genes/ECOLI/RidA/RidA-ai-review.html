Gene Description

RidA (formerly YjgF) is a 2-iminobutanoate/2-iminopropanoate deaminase (EC 3.5.99.10) in E. coli that accelerates the release of ammonia from reactive enamine/imine intermediates generated by PLP-dependent enzymes such as threonine dehydratase (IlvA). RidA is a cytoplasmic homotrimer (PMID:10595546) and its primary evolved function is detoxification of reactive intermediates in amino acid metabolism, particularly in the isoleucine biosynthetic pathway. Importantly, RidA acquires chaperone holdase activity ONLY when post-translationally modified by HOCl (hypochlorous acid) via N-chlorination of lysine and arginine residues (PMID:25517874). Untreated RidA has NO chaperone activity. This conditional chaperone function is a secondary, stress-activated moonlighting function and not the core evolved role of RidA.

Core Functions

RidA deaminates reactive enamine/imine intermediates from PLP-dependent enzymes, particularly threonine dehydratase (IlvA), converting 2-iminobutanoate to 2-oxobutanoate and 2-iminopropanoate to pyruvate. This detoxification function supports isoleucine biosynthesis and protects against reactive intermediate toxicity.

Research report: Escherichia coli K-12 ridA (YjgF; UniProt P0AF93)

0) Mandatory target verification (gene/protein identity)

The target protein is RidA (historical name YjgF) from Escherichia coli K-12, UniProt P0AF93, annotated as 2-iminobutanoate/2-iminopropanoate deaminase (enamine/imine deaminase; EC 3.5.99.10). The literature retrieved and synthesized here explicitly discusses RidA/YjgF proteins as members of the Rid (YjgF/YER057c/UK114) family of enamine/imine deaminases involved in control of reactive 2-aminoacrylate (2AA) stress, consistent with the UniProt description (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 5-7, irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 7-8, fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 1-3).

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 RidA as a “metabolite damage-preemption” enzyme

RidA is now widely regarded as a metabolite damage-preemption enzyme: it accelerates the hydrolytic deamination of reactive enamine/imine intermediates produced by pyridoxal-5′-phosphate (PLP)-dependent enzymes, preventing these intermediates from persisting long enough to damage other enzymes (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 8-9, irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 22-23).

A central concept is “2-aminoacrylate (2AA) stress”. 2AA is an enamine (in tautomeric equilibrium with an imine form sometimes referred to as 2-iminopropanoate) that can escape from its generating PLP enzyme and covalently inactivate other PLP-dependent enzymes by modifying the PLP cofactor (“scars” on target enzymes) (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 8-9, irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 7-8).

1.2 What RidA catalyzes (reaction definition)

The best-supported physiological substrates for RidA are the IlvA-generated enamines:
- 2AA generated from serine, and
- 2-aminocrotonate (2AC) generated from threonine
(irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 5-7, fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 1-3).

RidA accelerates their conversion to the corresponding stable keto acids plus ammonia:
- 2AA → pyruvate + NH3, and
- 2AC → 2-ketobutyrate + NH3
(irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 5-7).

Although these reactions can occur spontaneously in water, RidA’s biological value is that spontaneous decay can be too slow relative to intracellular fluxes and diffusion, allowing reactive intermediates to persist long enough to cause damage (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 5-7, irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 8-9).

2) Biochemical function, mechanism, and substrate specificity

2.1 Physiological substrate generation (IlvA-dependent)

RidA function is tightly linked to PLP-dependent dehydratases, especially IlvA (serine/threonine dehydratase), which can generate 2AA (from serine) or 2AC (from threonine) (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 5-7, fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 1-3).

2.2 Quantitative biochemical effects (what RidA changes)

Direct kinetic characterization is challenging because 2AA is highly labile, but quantitative in vitro effects have been reported. In a synthesis of the RidA paradigm, addition of RidA increased:
- 2-ketobutyrate formation from IlvA + threonine by approximately ~2-fold at physiological pH and ~4–5-fold at pH 9.5, and
- pyruvate formation from serine-derived intermediates by approximately ~2.2-fold at pH 9.5
(irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 5-7).

A key parameter illustrating why chemistry and biology diverge is the reported ~1.5 s half-life of free 2AA; even this short lifetime can be sufficient for damage if production is high and the metabolite can access vulnerable PLP enzymes (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 22-23).

2.3 Structural/active-site features (current model)

RidA proteins are typically homotrimers, with the functional active site formed in an inter-subunit cleft, consistent with a mechanism dominated by substrate and water positioning rather than a classic catalytic residue (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 7-8, fulton2025maintainingarobust pages 23-27).

Conserved residues emphasized in E. coli numbering include Gly31, Asn88, Arg105, and Glu120, with Arg105 highlighted as particularly predictive/essential for deaminase activity in RidA-like subfamilies (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 7-8, fulton2025maintainingarobust pages 23-27).

3) Cellular localization (where RidA acts)

The functional model requires RidA to access free (diffusible) enamine/imine intermediates released from PLP enzymes, implying a soluble intracellular (cytosolic) localization rather than membrane association or secretion (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 8-9, irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 7-8).

4) Pathway context and biological processes in E. coli

4.1 RidA integrates with PLP-dependent amino acid metabolism

The RidA paradigm places ridA within a broader set of PLP-dependent reactions (e.g., dehydratases and multiple PLP enzymes that can become off-target “victims” of 2AA), positioning RidA as a general protective factor for amino-acid and central metabolism under conditions that elevate serine/threonine dehydratase side chemistry (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 8-9, irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 22-23).

4.2 E. coli-specific redundancy: RidA and TdcF

A key recent development is explicit E. coli evidence that RidA function is partially redundant with a paralog, TdcF. In coculture-based fitness assays, ridA and ridA tdcF mutants showed reduced competitive fitness under serine perturbation, with the double mutant generally more compromised, supporting overlapping roles in moderating reactive intermediate stress (fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 3-6, fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 6-9).

5) Recent developments (prioritized 2023–2024)

5.1 2024: RidA contributes to competitive fitness and isoleucine flux in E. coli

A 2024 study (Fulton et al., Oct 2024, Microbial Cell, DOI: 10.15698/mic2024.10.837, URL: https://doi.org/10.15698/mic2024.10.837) used coculture experiments to expose subtle phenotypes. It concludes that RidA contributes to fitness in both S. enterica and E. coli, and presents evidence in E. coli for a role in moderating flux toward isoleucine biosynthesis while reducing 2AA stress (fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 1-3).

Quantitatively, the same study reports E. coli IlvE (branched-chain aminotransferase) activity measurements showing little difference among wild type and mutants under some tested conditions (nmol 2KMV/mg protein): WT 130 ± 6, ridA 140 ± 20, tdcF 163 ± 15, ridA tdcF 131 ± 9 in minimal medium; in minimal + serine (5 mM), WT 132 ± 4 vs ridA 110 ± 4 (fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 6-9, fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute media 9bffa396). The study emphasizes that fitness defects can occur even when canonical biochemical damage readouts are subtle in E. coli (fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 3-6, fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 6-9).

5.2 2024: RidA as a practical tool in metabolic engineering

A 2024 metabolic engineering study (Nie et al., Jan 2024, Microbial Cell Factories, DOI: 10.1186/s12934-023-02282-0, URL: https://doi.org/10.1186/s12934-023-02282-0) implemented ridA overexpression in E. coli to improve a synthetic bioconversion route to phenylpyruvate (and then L-phenylalanine).

Key quantitative outcomes:
- With ridA overexpression, phenylpyruvate titer reached 5.0 g/L (30.1 mM) within 24 h.
- Conversion of benzaldehyde → phenylpyruvate increased to 77%.
- The authors report ridA overexpression improved phenylpyruvate production by 16.3%.
(nie2024systematicengineeringenables pages 4-6)

These results support a real-world implementation where RidA is used as a “metabolite stress relief” component in engineered pathways containing PLP enzymes and reactive imine/enamine intermediates (nie2024systematicengineeringenables pages 4-6).

6) Expert opinions and authoritative synthesis

Authoritative reviews emphasize that RidA’s function was difficult to establish because (i) the key substrate is short-lived, (ii) the reaction can occur spontaneously, and (iii) phenotypes are often conditional and depend on metabolic network architecture. Nonetheless, the current expert consensus is that RidA proteins are broadly conserved enamine/imine deaminases whose dominant role is preventing damage by reactive enamines/imines (especially 2AA) and thereby maintaining metabolic robustness (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 5-7, irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 8-9).

7) Statistics and data highlights (recent + foundational)

  • 2AA half-life ~1.5 s (a key parameter explaining measurement challenges) (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 22-23).
  • RidA boosts observed keto-acid production rates from IlvA-derived intermediates by ~2-fold at physiological pH (and higher at alkaline pH in vitro), supporting catalytic quenching beyond spontaneous hydrolysis (irons2020ridaproteinsprotect pages 5-7).
  • E. coli IlvE activity values show subtle or condition-dependent differences among ridA/tdcF genotypes in 2024 experiments (fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute pages 6-9, fulton2024ridaproteinscontribute media 9bffa396).
  • In metabolic engineering, ridA overexpression yielded 5.0 g/L phenylpyruvate and 77% conversion, with a 16.3% improvement versus without ridA in that system (nie2024systematicengineeringenables pages 4-6).

ridA (yjgF) encodes a cytosolic, homotrimeric RidA-family enamine/imine deaminase that quenches reactive enamine/imine intermediates (notably 2-aminoacrylate/2-iminopropanoate from serine and 2-aminocrotonate from threonine, generated by IlvA), converting them to stable keto acids (pyruvate or 2-ketobutyrate) and ammonia. Functional Summary Review

The BioReason functional summary describes RidA as:

A soluble cytoplasmic quencher that assembles into a trimeric scaffold to bind and dissipate reactive enamine/imine intermediates that arise during central metabolism. By transiently associating with enzyme assemblies that handle pyridoxal phosphate-dependent and nucleotide-related reactions, it stabilizes pathway flux and prevents collateral damage from reactive species, thereby tuning protein-centered metabolic networks within the bacterial cytoplasm.

The summary captures the general essence of RidA's function: it is indeed a cytoplasmic homotrimer that processes reactive enamine/imine intermediates. The connection to PLP-dependent enzymes is correctly made. However, there are issues:

  1. Molecular function mischaracterization: The thinking trace infers "protein binding" (GO:0005515) as the primary molecular function, claiming the trimeric fold "relies on protein-protein contacts." In reality, RidA is an enzyme -- a 2-iminobutanoate/2-iminopropanoate deaminase (EC 3.5.99.10) -- that catalyzes the hydrolytic release of ammonia from reactive enamine/imine intermediates. The curated review assigns GO:0120241 (2-iminobutanoate/2-iminopropanoate deaminase activity) as the core molecular function.

  2. The summary calls RidA a "non-enzymatic yet catalytic chaperone-like module" in the thinking trace, which is contradictory and incorrect. RidA has clear deaminase catalytic activity.

  3. Missing pathway specificity: The summary mentions generic "central metabolism" but does not identify the specific pathway -- isoleucine biosynthesis via threonine dehydratase (IlvA) -- which is RidA's primary physiological context.

  4. Missing moonlighting function: Under HOCl stress, RidA acquires chaperone holdase activity via N-chlorination of lysine and arginine residues (PMID:25517874). This conditional chaperone function is not mentioned.

The cytoplasmic localization and trimeric assembly are correctly identified.

Comparison with interpro2go:

Interestingly, the GO predictions are more accurate than the functional summary narrative, which underplays the enzymatic nature. The narrative and GO predictions appear somewhat disconnected.

Notes on thinking trace

The trace correctly identifies all four InterPro domains (RutC-like superfamily, RidA family, YjgF/YER057c/UK114 family, RidA conserved site). However, it then mischaracterizes RidA as "non-enzymatic" despite the RidA family being a well-established enzyme family. The mention of "radical S-adenosylmethionine-dependent enzymes" as interaction partners has no experimental support for RidA.
