## Research report: Functional annotation of **dnaK** (UniProt **P0A6Y8**) in *Escherichia coli* K-12

### 1) Target verification (mandatory)
The gene symbol **dnaK** in this report refers specifically to *Escherichia coli* (strain K-12) **Chaperone protein DnaK**, the canonical bacterial **Hsp70** family member, with UniProt accession **P0A6Y8**. This identity is explicitly tied to UniProt P0A6Y8 in mechanistic work focused on the *E. coli* DnaK–GrpE system, and the functional context (Hsp70 chaperone; cochaperones DnaJ and GrpE; NBD+SBD architecture) matches the UniProt description. (maqtedar2026thenucleotideexchange pages 15-20, mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 1-2)

### 2) Key concepts, definitions, and current understanding

#### 2.1 Core molecular function: ATP-dependent Hsp70 chaperone
**DnaK is an ATP-dependent molecular chaperone** that binds short, degenerate peptide motifs in client proteins and uses an ATP-driven allosteric cycle to switch between low- and high-affinity client-binding states. (mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 1-2, bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 4-7)

**Domain architecture (definitions)**: DnaK is composed of (i) an N-terminal **nucleotide-binding domain (NBD)**, (ii) a conserved **interdomain linker**, and (iii) a C-terminal **substrate-binding domain (SBD)** that includes a β-sandwich peptide-binding pocket (**SBDβ**), an α-helical “lid” (**SBDα**), and a flexible/disordered C-terminal segment. (mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 2-4, mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 1-2)

#### 2.2 Chaperone cycle and cochaperones (DnaJ/GrpE)
**Nucleotide-coupled substrate binding**: In the ATP-bound state, DnaK adopts a more open conformation with **low substrate affinity and fast exchange**, whereas the ADP-bound state favors **high-affinity substrate capture** (often described as lid-closed/trapped substrate). (bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 4-7, melero2015modulationofthe pages 1-2)

**Cochaperone roles**:
- **DnaJ (Hsp40/J-domain protein)**: stimulates DnaK ATPase activity and participates in substrate delivery/targeting. (bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 4-7, bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 7-9)
- **GrpE (nucleotide exchange factor; NEF)**: accelerates ADP release from DnaK, promoting ATP rebinding and thereby enabling substrate release/turnover; GrpE also participates in regulating DnaK allostery. (bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 4-7, rossi2024newinsightsinto pages 1-2)

**Quantitative enzymology (representative)**: substrate alone can stimulate DnaK ATPase activity by **~2–10-fold**, while **DnaJ + substrate** can synergistically enhance ATPase stimulation by **>1000-fold** in reported mechanistic summaries. (bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 4-7)

#### 2.3 Substrate specificity and proteome-wide scope
DnaK binds **short hydrophobic motifs** (commonly described as ~7-residue cores, frequently Leu-enriched and flanked by basic residues). Such motifs are common: one estimate reports an average frequency of **~1 potential DnaK site per 36 residues**, implying that **~98%** of *E. coli* proteins harbor at least one potential DnaK-binding segment. (bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 4-7)

Proteome-scale computational analysis further supports broad surveillance: predicted DnaK- and DnaJ-binding motifs occur across nearly all proteins, with **DnaJ sites predicted ~2× as often as DnaK sites** and many predicted to overlap. (srinivasan2012molecularchaperonesdnak pages 1-2)

#### 2.4 Cellular localization and biological processes/pathways
**Localization**: DnaK is primarily a **cytoplasmic** chaperone. In a large pull-down/MS interactome compilation, **~80%** of identified DnaK interactors were cytoplasmic, while the remainder included membrane/periplasm-associated proteins. (bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 7-9)

**Biological processes**: DnaK is a central hub in bacterial proteostasis, functioning in de novo folding, stress-denatured protein handling, and aggregate management (often in cooperation with other chaperones such as ClpB). (mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 1-2, hoskins2025hsp90dnakand pages 1-2)

**Heat-shock regulation pathway (RpoH/σ32)**: DnaK has a regulatory role in heat shock control circuits; in particular, deletion of dnaK causes pronounced stress phenotypes and commonly selects for suppressors in **rpoH (σ32)**, consistent with DnaK being part of a feedback mechanism controlling heat shock transcriptional programs. (mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 2-4, mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 1-2)

**Physiological importance**: ΔdnaK strains show marked temperature-related growth defects (reported restricted growth in the range of **~20–35°C**) and filamentation. (mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 2-4)

### 3) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

#### 3.1 2024 structural/mechanistic update: DnaK–GrpE complex in *E. coli*
A major *E. coli*-specific advance is the 2024 **Journal of Biological Chemistry** paper: Rossi et al., “New insights into the structure and function of the complex between the *Escherichia coli* Hsp70, DnaK, and its nucleotide-exchange factor, GrpE” (publication date **Jan 2024**, https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbc.2023.105574). The work synthesizes structural modeling/solution evidence to revise how **GrpE opens the DnaK nucleotide-binding cleft**, emphasizing movement/rotation of NBD **subdomain IIB** as part of the nucleotide exchange mechanism and reporting a GrpE thermosensor-relevant melting temperature (**~48°C**). (rossi2024newinsightsinto pages 1-2)

The paper notes that a previously used GrpE **G122D** mutation yields a weak complex and lacks functional NEF/refolding activity, motivating re-evaluation of mechanistic models based on that variant. (rossi2024newinsightsinto pages 1-2)

**Image-supported mechanistic claim**: structural overlays and models in the 2024 JBC paper depict subdomain IIB rotation (reported as **14°** in an overlay context) and a model with a more open nucleotide-binding cleft upon GrpE binding. (rossi2024newinsightsinto media 7fa2413d)

#### 3.2 2023 computational analysis of allosteric signaling (DnaK vs BiP)
A 2023 study used molecular dynamics and mutual information metrics to compare allosteric signaling features between *E. coli* DnaK and human BiP, aiming to identify residues contributing to conformational coupling between ligand-binding sites and cochaperone/substrate interactions. This supports ongoing mechanistic refinement of Hsp70-family allostery using simulation-based network approaches. (jiang2025mechanismsofassembly pages 23-24)

#### 3.3 2024: infection-relevant stress roles of bacterial DnaK (enteric bacteria)
A 2024 **PLOS Biology** study (Apr 2024; https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.3002560) reported that *Salmonella* DnaK can promote survival under infection-relevant stress (cytoplasmic Mg2+ starvation) by **reducing protein synthesis**; the paper describes specific survival/protein-synthesis assay conditions and genetic requirements. While this is not *E. coli* K-12 directly, it highlights a potentially broader bacterial DnaK function linking proteostasis to translational control under host-like stress conditions. (chan2024chaperonehsp70helps pages 23-25)

### 4) Current applications and real-world implementations

#### 4.1 DnaK as an experimental workhorse in protein folding/disaggregation assays
The DnaK/DnaJ/GrpE system is widely used as a **reconstituted in vitro chaperone machine**. For example, mechanistic work on DnaK functional tuning and cochaperone coupling uses **denatured luciferase refolding/reactivation assays** and peptide-binding assays to quantify effects of cochaperones and mutants. (rossi2024newinsightsinto pages 1-2, melero2015modulationofthe pages 1-2)

DnaK also participates in multi-chaperone systems; biochemical characterization of its collaboration with *E. coli* Hsp90 (Hsp90Ec) includes binding and activity synergy measurements, supporting DnaK’s role as a remodeling hub rather than an isolated foldase. (kravats2017interactionofe. pages 10-15)

#### 4.2 Antimicrobial/anti-biofilm targeting concepts
DnaK is an explored antibacterial target, including via **peptide antagonists** and inhibitor screening concepts. A 2024 microbiology study on an SMR peptide reported dose-dependent inhibition of *Staphylococcus aureus* biofilm formation via targeting DnaK, with “significant inhibition” at **18 µM, 36 µM, and 72 µM** (organism differs from *E. coli*, but the target concept is DnaK-directed anti-biofilm activity). (huang2024smrpeptideantagonizes pages 12-12)

### 6) Expert synthesis and interpretation (authoritative analysis grounded in evidence)

1. **Primary molecular role**: The collected evidence supports DnaK as a central ATP-driven chaperone that binds exposed hydrophobic segments and—through DnaJ-accelerated ATP hydrolysis and GrpE-driven nucleotide exchange—controls client binding lifetimes and folding trajectories. The large magnitude of cochaperone-dependent stimulation (>1000× with DnaJ+substrate in one summary) emphasizes that DnaK’s physiological behavior is primarily a **cochaperone-programmed machine**, not a standalone ATPase. (bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 4-7)

2. **System-level role**: Proteome-wide motif predictions and interactome/aggregation datasets support the interpretation of DnaK as a **global proteostasis hub** whose client set is broad enough that impairing DnaK/DnaJ reveals widespread aggregation and stress sensitivity. (bhandari2015prokaryoticsystemsbiology pages 7-9, srinivasan2012molecularchaperonesdnak pages 1-2)

3. **Regulatory coupling**: The repeated observation that ΔdnaK strains acquire suppressors in rpoH/σ32 implies that DnaK activity is integrated into feedback control of heat-shock gene expression; this provides a mechanistic explanation for why perturbation of DnaK can produce strong pleiotropic phenotypes (filamentation; temperature sensitivity) that are downstream of transcriptional imbalance and proteostasis collapse. (mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 2-4, mayer2021thehsp70chaperonemachines pages 1-2)

4. **What is new in 2024**: The 2024 JBC work strengthens the mechanistic linkage between GrpE binding and NBD structural remodeling (subdomain IIB movement opening the nucleotide cleft), while embedding this in a thermosensing framework (GrpE coiled-coil stability around ~48°C). This provides a more structurally explicit basis for nucleotide exchange and suggests routes by which GrpE binding can propagate allosteric signals beyond the immediate nucleotide pocket. (rossi2024newinsightsinto pages 1-2, rossi2024newinsightsinto media 7fa2413d)
