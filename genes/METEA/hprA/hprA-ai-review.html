Gene Description

hprA encodes hydroxypyruvate reductase (EC 1.1.1.29), also known as glycerate dehydrogenase (GDH) or HPR-A, a NAD(P)H-dependent oxidoreductase that plays a central role in the serine cycle of methylotrophy. The enzyme catalyzes the reversible reduction of 3-hydroxypyruvate (produced by serA) to D-glycerate, which is the second step of the serine cycle. HprA can utilize both NADH and NADPH as electron donors and functions as a homodimer in the cytoplasm. Beyond its role in the serine cycle, HprA may also participate in C2 metabolism by interconverting glyoxylate and glycolate (glyoxylate reductase activity). The enzyme belongs to the D-isomer specific 2-hydroxyacid dehydrogenase family and contains NAD(P)-binding Rossmann-fold domains. HprA has been extensively characterized biochemically, with protein sequencing and kinetic studies confirming its function as a key enzyme in formaldehyde assimilation via the serine pathway.

Core Functions

HprA catalyzes the NAD(P)H-dependent reduction of 3-hydroxypyruvate to D-glycerate, the second step of the serine cycle for formaldehyde assimilation. This reaction follows the serA-catalyzed oxidation of 3-phosphoglycerate and produces glycerate that is subsequently phosphorylated by gck. The enzyme functions as a homodimer in the cytoplasm and can utilize both NADH and NADPH as electron donors. HprA plays a central role in carbon assimilation during methylotrophic growth, channeling one-carbon units from methanol through the serine cycle into cellular biomass. The enzyme may also have glyoxylate reductase activity, potentially participating in C2 metabolism by interconverting glyoxylate and glycolate.
