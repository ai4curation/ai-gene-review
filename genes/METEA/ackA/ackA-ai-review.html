Gene Description

ackA encodes acetate kinase (EC 2.7.2.1), also known as acetokinase, which catalyzes the reversible Mg2+-dependent phosphorylation of acetate to acetyl phosphate using ATP as the phosphate donor. This is the first step of the two-step ack-pta pathway for acetate assimilation: acetate is first converted to acetyl phosphate by AckA, and then pta (phosphate acetyltransferase) converts acetyl phosphate to acetyl-CoA. The enzyme belongs to the acetokinase family and functions as a homodimer in the cytosol. AckA can use either Mg2+ or Mn2+ as a metal cofactor, though Mg2+ is preferred. In methylotrophs, this pathway may be involved in overflow metabolism when acetate accumulates, or in scavenging environmental acetate as an alternative carbon source. The reversibility of the reaction also allows AckA to function in ATP generation from acetyl phosphate during overflow metabolism. The ack-pta pathway provides a metabolic route connecting acetate to central metabolism via acetyl-CoA, enabling utilization of C2 compounds for growth and energy production.

Core Functions

AckA catalyzes the ATP- and Mg2+-dependent reversible phosphorylation of acetate to acetyl phosphate, the first step of the two-step ack-pta pathway for acetate assimilation. This reaction enables M. extorquens to utilize acetate as an alternative carbon source by converting it to acetyl-CoA (via pta), which can then enter central metabolism. The enzyme functions as a homodimer in the cytosol and can use either Mg2+ or Mn2+ as metal cofactor. The reversibility of the reaction also allows AckA to function in ATP generation from acetyl phosphate during overflow metabolism when acetyl-CoA accumulates. The ack-pta pathway represents an important metabolic flexibility mechanism, allowing the organism to both assimilate environmental acetate and manage metabolic overflow of C2 compounds.
