Gene Description

Wee1 is the prototypical dose-dependent negative regulator of mitotic entry in fission yeast and the founding member of the WEE1 protein kinase subfamily. It is a dual-specificity protein kinase (acting on both serine and tyrosine residues; EC 2.7.10.2) whose principal physiological substrate is the cyclin-dependent kinase Cdc2 (Cdk1). Wee1 phosphorylates Cdc2 on the inhibitory residue Tyr15, keeping the Cdc2/cyclin complex inactive until cells reach a threshold size; the antagonistic phosphatase Cdc25 removes this phosphate to trigger the G2/M transition. Loss of Wee1 causes premature mitosis at roughly half the normal cell size (the "wee" small-cell phenotype). Wee1 localizes predominantly to the nucleus and spindle pole body, where it can act on Cdc2, and is recruited in transient bursts to medial cortical Cdr2 nodes, where the upstream SAD-family kinases Cdr1 and Cdr2 phosphorylate and inhibit it in a cell-size-dependent manner. Beyond canonical G2/M size control, Wee1 serves as the shared effector that enforces cell-cycle delay downstream of the DNA-damage (Chk1), DNA-replication (Cds1), and cytokinesis checkpoints, contributes to stable kinetochore-microtubule attachment in mitosis, and (together with the related kinase Mik1) restrains premature meiotic divisions.

Core identity / function

Wee1 is the prototype dose-dependent negative regulator of mitotic entry (G2/M transition) in fission yeast, the founding member of the WEE1 kinase subfamily. It is a dual-specificity (Ser + Tyr) protein kinase whose key substrate is the cyclin-dependent kinase Cdc2 (Cdk1): it phosphorylates Cdc2 on the inhibitory residue Tyr15, holding Cdk1 inactive until cells reach the threshold size for division. The opposing phosphatase Cdc25 removes this phosphate to drive mitosis. Loss of wee1 causes premature mitosis at half the normal cell size ("wee" small-cell phenotype).
