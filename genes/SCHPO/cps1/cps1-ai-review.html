Gene Description

SPAC24C9.08 (O13968) is an M20A family metallopeptidase in S. pombe with predicted carboxypeptidase activity. The protein contains characteristic M20 peptidase domains including the catalytic domain (IPR002933) and dimerization domain (IPR011650), with a predicted di-zinc catalytic center. It localizes to the vacuole as a type II single-pass membrane protein. The gene is named "cps1" suggesting homology to S. cerevisiae carboxypeptidase S (CPS1/yscS), but this assignment should be treated cautiously: (1) the gene symbol "cps1" in S. pombe literature also refers to bgs1, a completely different glucan synthase; (2) the precise biochemical activity of SPAC24C9.08 has not been experimentally determined; (3) while S. cerevisiae CPS1 is a well-characterized Gly-Xaa carboxypeptidase, the M20A subfamily has diverged to include enzymes with non-peptidase activities (e.g., mammalian PM20D1 which functions as an N-fatty-acyl-amino acid synthase/hydrolase). Without direct biochemical characterization, the precise enzymatic function remains uncertain.

Introduction and Summary

The Schizosaccharomyces pombe gene SPAC24C9.08, also known as cps1, encodes a vacuolar carboxypeptidase S (SpCPS), a zinc-dependent metallopeptidase belonging to the M20A family of peptidases [PomBase-SPAC24C9.08-database]. This enzyme functions as a carboxypeptidase in the vacuolar lumen, where it participates in the terminal degradation of proteins and peptides by cleaving amino acids from the C-terminus of polypeptide substrates. The protein is synthesized as a type II transmembrane precursor that transits through the secretory pathway and is delivered to the vacuole via the multivesicular body (MVB) sorting pathway, where it is processed to its mature, soluble form [hecht-2014-vacuole-abstract].

Critical Note on Gene Symbol Ambiguity: The gene symbol "cps1" in S. pombe literature can be confusing, as it is also used as an alias for bgs1 (SPBC19G7.05c), which encodes the 1,3-Œ≤-glucan synthase catalytic subunit involved in cell wall synthesis and cytokinesis. The protein described in this report‚Äîthe vacuolar carboxypeptidase S encoded by SPAC24C9.08‚Äîis a fundamentally different enzyme from the glucan synthase [PomBase-SPAC24C9.08-database].

Carboxypeptidase S was first identified in S. cerevisiae by Wolf and Weiser in 1977 using a carboxypeptidase Y-deficient mutant (prc1-1) [wolf-1977-cpsy-abstract]. They observed that a double mutant lacking carboxypeptidase Y but auxotrophic for leucine could still grow on the dipeptide benzyloxycarbonylglycylleucine (Cbz-Gly-Leu) as a sole nitrogen source, indicating the existence of a second carboxypeptidase. Using a new peptidase test, they biochemically confirmed this second enzyme and named it carboxypeptidase S (yscS). Subsequent genetic studies characterized CPS-deficient mutants and demonstrated that the absence of both carboxypeptidases Y and S does not affect mitotic growth, but prevents cells from utilizing certain exogenous peptides as nitrogen sources [wolf-1981-cps-mutants-abstract].

Protein Structure and Domain Architecture

SpCPS belongs to the M20A subfamily of metallopeptidases, which is part of the larger M20 family within clan MH [MEROPS-M20-database]. The protein contains several characteristic domains including the peptidase M20 catalytic domain (IPR002933), the peptidase M20 dimer domain (IPR011650), the bacterial exopeptidase dimerization domain (IPR036264), and the M20A-specific domain (IPR047177) [PomBase-SPAC24C9.08-database].

The structural architecture of M20 family members is well characterized. Each monomer consists of a catalytic domain with an Œ±Œ≤Œ± sandwich fold that harbors a bimetallic active site center, and an oligomerization domain (also called the satellite or lid domain) that covers the active site and mediates homodimerization [MEROPS-M20-database]. The oligomerization domain contributes an invariant arginine residue that interacts with the alpha-carboxylate of the amino acid substrate at the S1' site, helping to orient substrates in the active site. The structure adopts a homodimeric configuration where hydrogen bonds between helices in the lid domain form the dimer interface.

The M20 family employs a "co-catalytic" mechanism involving two zinc ions per monomer at the active site center [MEROPS-M20-database]. In the S. cerevisiae ortholog, the enzyme is synthesized as a ~64 kDa precursor containing a transmembrane sequence spanning amino acids 20-40 that anchors it to the ER membrane [hecht-2014-vacuole-abstract]. Following glycosylation, mature forms of 74 or 77 kDa are produced, differing by one carbohydrate residue [spormann-1992-biogenesis-abstract].

SpCPS has four lysine residues (K16, K19, K32, and K33) in its putative N-terminal cytoplasmic domain, which are potential sites for ubiquitination required for MVB sorting [iwaki-2007-escrt-abstract]. This is consistent with findings in S. cerevisiae where K8 of the N-terminal cytoplasmic domain is ubiquitinated.

Catalytic Activity and Substrate Specificity

As a zinc-dependent metallocarboxypeptidase, SpCPS depends on Zn¬≤‚Å∫ ions for catalytic function [hecht-2014-vacuole-abstract]. The enzyme hydrolyzes terminal or penultimate peptide bonds at the carboxy termini of peptides, releasing single amino acid residues [SGD-CPS1-database]. Based on studies of the S. cerevisiae ortholog CPS1 (YJL172W), the enzyme exhibits partially overlapping substrate specificity with carboxypeptidase Y (CPY) and demonstrates preference for glycine and leucine residues at the P1 site [hecht-2014-vacuole-abstract]. CPS contributes approximately 60% of vacuolar activity for hydrolyzing specific synthetic dipeptides.

The catalytic mechanism employed by M20 family members involves the following steps: (1) before substrate binding, a bridging water molecule is positioned between two Zn¬≤‚Å∫ ions and spatially close to the carboxylate group of the catalytic glutamate; (2) upon substrate binding, the enzyme undergoes a conformational change; (3) the catalytic glutamate acts as a general base, promoting nucleophilic attack of the metal-bound water on the substrate carbonyl carbon; (4) the carbonyl oxygen binds in an "oxyanion binding hole" formed by Zn1 and an imidazole group of histidine, polarizing the carbonyl group; (5) this leads to a tetrahedral intermediate that subsequently decays to products after proton transfer [MEROPS-M20-database]. The transition from an open to a closed enzyme conformation may be associated with formation of the catalytically competent active site.

Subcellular Localization

SpCPS is localized to the vacuolar lumen, the primary degradative compartment of yeast cells [SGD-CPS1-database][hecht-2014-vacuole-abstract]. The yeast vacuole is analogous to the mammalian lysosome and performs multiple functions including metabolite storage, pH and ion homeostasis, and protein degradation. The vacuole harbors at least seven distinct proteases in S. cerevisiae, including two carboxypeptidases and two aminopeptidases [hecht-2014-vacuole-abstract].

Trafficking Pathway: From Biosynthesis to Vacuolar Delivery

The intracellular trafficking of SpCPS represents one of the best-characterized biosynthetic cargo pathways through the endosomal system and multivesicular body (MVB) pathway. Unlike soluble vacuolar hydrolases such as carboxypeptidase Y (Cpy1), which require the Vps10 receptor for sorting from the trans-Golgi network (TGN) to the prevacuolar endosome (PVE), Cps1 is a transmembrane protein that reaches the PVE in a Vps10-independent manner [yanguas-2019-gga-abstract][iwaki-2006-vps10-abstract].

The trafficking of Cps1 from the TGN to the vacuole involves several key steps and molecular players. Carboxypeptidases Y and S, along with the Vps10 receptor and the vacuolar ATPase subunit Vph1, follow the carboxypeptidase Y (CPY) pathway from the TGN to the PVE [yanguas-2019-gga-abstract]. Using S. pombe quantitative live-cell imaging, biochemical, and genetic analyses, Yanguas and colleagues demonstrated that collaboration between the GGA (Golgi-localized, Gamma-ear-containing, ARF-binding) adaptors Gga22 and Gga21, and between Gga22 and the endosomal epsin Ent3, is required for efficient Cps1 exit from the TGN and its sorting in the PVE en route to the vacuole [yanguas-2019-gga-abstract]. These monomeric clathrin adaptors facilitate trafficking events of Cps1 in different organelles.

MVB Sorting and Ubiquitin-Dependent Regulation

A critical aspect of CPS trafficking is its sorting into the intralumenal vesicles of multivesicular bodies, which is required for delivery to the vacuolar lumen rather than the limiting membrane. The MVB sorting pathway provides a mechanism for delivering transmembrane proteins into the lumen of the lysosome/vacuole [katzmann-2004-rsp5-abstract]. For CPS and other biosynthetic cargoes, MVB sorting is controlled by protein ubiquitination.

The ubiquitin ligase Rsp5 (a HECT-domain E3 ligase) appends K63-linked polyubiquitin chains on protein cargo, which promotes MVB trafficking [katzmann-2004-rsp5-abstract]. Katzmann and colleagues identified that a point mutation in Rsp5 (rsp5-326) specifically impairs ubiquitin modification of precursor carboxypeptidase S (pCPS) while preserving normal ubiquitination of other substrates. This established that ubiquitin modification acts in cis as a signal for the sorting of cargoes into the MVB pathway. Multiple Rsp5 domains participate in cargo modification: the C2 domain aids membrane recruitment, WW domains facilitate protein-protein interactions, and the HECT domain catalyzes ubiquitin transfer to target substrates [katzmann-2004-rsp5-abstract].

The ubiquitinated cargo is then recognized by the endosomal sorting complexes required for transport (ESCRT). ESCRT-0 (the Vps27p/Hse1p complex) recognizes ubiquitinated cargo via three UIM (ubiquitin-interacting motif) domains and subsequently recruits ESCRT-I, ESCRT-II, and ESCRT-III [iwaki-2007-escrt-abstract]. Together, these ESCRT complexes drive cargo incorporation into and formation of intralumenal vesicles within MVBs.

ESCRT Machinery and Class E Vps Proteins in S. pombe

The class E vps genes are largely conserved between S. cerevisiae, S. pombe, and mammals [iwaki-2007-escrt-abstract]. In S. pombe, Iwaki and colleagues demonstrated that the sst4/vps27 and sst6 genes are essential for proper vacuolar sorting of carboxypeptidase Y and the MVB marker Ub-GFP-CPS [iwaki-2007-escrt-abstract]. Disruption of various other class E vps homologues also resulted in defects in sorting of CPY and Ub-GFP-CPS. This study was the first to demonstrate that the MVB pathway functions in S. pombe and that the roles of class E Vps proteins in MVB sorting are conserved.

The S. pombe Sst4/Vps27 protein contains VHS, FYVE, and UIM domains, similar to its S. cerevisiae counterpart [iwaki-2007-escrt-abstract]. Additionally, S. pombe possesses a mammalian AMSH homologue (Sst2) that functions as a class E Vps protein. Sst4p (Vps27 homolog) is required for efficient transport of the fluorescent dye FM4-64 and Ub-GFP-CPS fusion protein to the vacuole, confirming its role in both endocytic membrane trafficking and anterograde sorting of biosynthesized proteins into MVBs.

Vacuolar Processing and Maturation

Once delivered to the vacuole, the precursor form of CPS (pCPS) must be processed to generate the mature, soluble enzyme (mCPS). In S. cerevisiae, the carboxypeptidase yscS precursor molecules are delivered to the vacuole in a membrane-bound form via the secretory pathway [spormann-1992-biogenesis-abstract]. After assembly into the vacuolar membrane, proteinase yscB (Prb1) cleaves the precursor molecules to release soluble carboxypeptidase yscS forms into the lumen of the vacuole. Similarly, in S. pombe, CPS is processed by proteinase B and released into the vacuolar lumen [hecht-2014-vacuole-abstract].

Mature carboxypeptidase S appears soluble in the vacuolar lumen, while precursor proteins accumulate at the vacuolar membrane [spormann-1992-biogenesis-abstract]. An N-terminal hydrophobic domain mediates membrane binding in the precursor form. The enzyme is synthesized as two active high molecular mass precursor forms which are converted to the mature forms with a half-time of approximately 20 minutes.

Biological Role and Cellular Functions

As a vacuolar hydrolase, SpCPS participates in the terminal degradation of proteins and peptides within the vacuolar lumen. The vacuole serves as the major degradative organelle of yeast cells, breaking down substrates delivered by autophagy, endocytosis, and biosynthetic pathways [hecht-2014-vacuole-abstract]. The proteolytically produced amino acids are recycled for maintenance of cellular functions, particularly during nutrient starvation conditions.

Studies in S. cerevisiae have revealed several phenotypes associated with CPS1 deletion: decreased growth rate, leucine auxotrophy, elevated nicotinamide sensitivity, increased heat sensitivity, reduced filamentous growth, and decreased competitive fitness [SGD-CPS1-database]. Expression of CPS1 is induced under low-nitrogen conditions, suggesting a role in amino acid recycling during nutrient limitation. Overexpression results in a slow growth phenotype.

The S. cerevisiae CPS1 gene is involved in 97 total interactions: 42 physical interactions (primarily identified through affinity capture-MS) and 55 genetic interactions (predominantly negative genetic interactions) [SGD-CPS1-database]. These extensive interaction networks suggest that CPS integrates with multiple cellular processes.

Relationship to Other Vacuolar Proteases

The yeast vacuole contains at least two carboxypeptidases with partially overlapping substrate specificities [hecht-2014-vacuole-abstract]. While carboxypeptidase Y (CPY/Prc1) is a serine protease, carboxypeptidase S (CPS/Cps1) is a zinc metallopeptidase belonging to a distinct protein family. Both enzymes contribute to the hydrolysis of C-terminal amino acids from peptide substrates in the vacuolar lumen, but they differ in their catalytic mechanisms, structural organization, and trafficking requirements.

In S. pombe, the vacuolar protease network includes Isp6, a starvation-specific serine protease that is essential for autophagy-dependent protein degradation during nitrogen starvation [nakashima-2006-isp6-abstract]. Studies have shown that isp6 disruption mutants grow normally under standard conditions but are defective in large-scale protein degradation during nitrogen starvation and exhibit infertility under nutrient-poor conditions. Isp6 and its paralog Psp3 are required for processing autophagy-delivered cargo in the vacuole. SpCPS likely works in concert with these endopeptidases, with Isp6 and Psp3 providing initial cleavages and carboxypeptidases like SpCPS and Cpy1 completing the breakdown of peptide products to free amino acids. This coordinated action is critical for amino acid recycling during nutrient starvation and for supporting nitrogen-dependent developmental processes such as mating and sporulation in fission yeast.

Open Questions

Several aspects of SpCPS biology remain incompletely understood:

  1. Precise substrate specificity: While the S. cerevisiae ortholog shows preference for Gly and Leu at the P1 site, the full substrate specificity profile of SpCPS has not been systematically characterized. Understanding whether SpCPS has similar or distinct preferences would clarify its functional role.

  2. Regulation of expression and activity: CPS1 expression is induced under low-nitrogen conditions in S. cerevisiae, but the transcriptional and post-translational regulatory mechanisms controlling SpCPS expression and activity in S. pombe are not well characterized.

  3. Functional redundancy with other vacuolar peptidases: The extent to which SpCPS function overlaps with or complements other vacuolar proteases, particularly during autophagy and nitrogen starvation, remains to be fully elucidated.

  4. Role in specific physiological processes: While cps1 null mutants in S. cerevisiae show phenotypes related to sporulation and stress responses, the specific physiological roles of SpCPS in S. pombe sporulation, mating, and stress adaptation have not been directly investigated.

  5. Three-dimensional structure: No experimental structure of SpCPS or the S. cerevisiae ortholog CPS1 is currently available. Structural determination would provide insights into substrate specificity determinants and catalytic mechanism.

  6. Ubiquitination sites and regulation: While four lysine residues (K16, K19, K32, K33) in the N-terminal cytoplasmic domain are potential ubiquitination sites, the specific residue(s) modified and the regulatory consequences of this modification in S. pombe remain to be determined.

  7. Evolution and functional conservation: While SpCPS is clearly a homolog of S. cerevisiae CPS1, detailed evolutionary analysis of the M20A peptidase subfamily across fungi and the degree of functional conservation would be valuable.

While the broad-specificity endoproteases such as Pep4 and the serine carboxypeptidase Prc1 initiate the degradation process by making internal cleavages and removing propeptides from zymogenic forms of vacuolar enzymes, the exopeptidases including Cps1 complete the degradation process by sequentially removing amino acids from peptide products[14][26]. Studies in S. cerevisiae demonstrate that the zinc metallopeptidase Cps1 specifically participates in the final degradation steps of autophagy, as deletion of CPS1 results in accumulation of partially degraded autophagic material and defects in the recovery of cellular amino acid pools during starvation[14][26]. Although direct experimental evidence for Cps1 function in S. pombe autophagy remains limited, the functional conservation of autophagy machinery across yeast species strongly suggests that the S. pombe Cps1 carboxypeptidase plays an analogous role in degrading autophagic cargo[51].

The regulation of Cps1 activity and expression is coupled to nutrient availability and cellular stress conditions that trigger autophagy. During nitrogen starvation and other nutrient-limiting conditions that induce autophagy, cells activate the autophagy machinery and increase the expression of autophagy-related proteins[26][51]. Concurrently, the vacuolar proteolytic system, including Cps1, is upregulated to enhance the capacity for cargo degradation and amino acid recycling[26]. The increased vacuolar proteolytic activity supports the synthesis of additional autophagic machinery proteins such as Atg8, which are required for continued autophagic flux[26]. Thus, Cps1 and the broader vacuolar proteolytic system are integral to the cellular strategy of nutrient recycling during periods of metabolic stress, enabling survival and maintenance of essential biosynthetic processes when external nutrient availability is limited[26][51].

Comparative Analysis with Homologous Carboxypeptidases

The M20 family of metallopeptidases exhibits substantial diversity in structure and substrate specificity despite sharing a conserved catalytic mechanism and active site architecture[13][20][27]. This diversity is exemplified by members of the M20 family that catalyze distinct reactions including carboxypeptidase activity (removal of C-terminal amino acids), dipeptidase activity (hydrolysis of dipeptides), and aminopeptidase-like activities (removal of N-terminal amino acids)[11][42]. Crystal structures and biochemical characterization of M20 family members have revealed that variations in the three-dimensional arrangement of the catalytic and regulatory lid domains allow for modulation of active site geometry, thereby enabling different substrate specificities and reaction types to be catalyzed by structurally related enzymes[13][20][27].

In eukaryotes, vacuolar carboxypeptidases similar to Cps1 have been identified in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Arabidopsis thaliana, and mammalian cells, where they perform analogous proteolytic functions in lysosomal and vacuolar degradation pathways[14][17][26]. The S. cerevisiae Cps1 protein, also known as a zinc metallopeptidase carboxypeptidase, shares significant sequence identity and functional homology with the S. pombe SPAC24C9.08 Cps1 protein[14][26]. Both proteins are vacuolar zinc-dependent carboxypeptidases that participate in the final stages of proteolytic degradation and autophagy[14][26]. The conservation of these proteins across distantly related yeast species indicates that vacuolar zinc metallopeptidases represent an ancient and essential component of the eukaryotic proteolytic system[17][26].

The plant protein AtCPY, the Arabidopsis thaliana ortholog of carboxypeptidase Y, similarly functions as a vacuolar serine carboxypeptidase involved in protein degradation[17]. Interestingly, misfolded forms of AtCPY are recognized by the ER quality control system (ERAD pathway) and targeted for proteasomal degradation, demonstrating that even vacuolar proteases themselves are subject to proteolytic quality control mechanisms[17]. Mammalian carboxypeptidases such as the glutamate carboxypeptidase II (also known as N-acetylated-alpha-linked acidic dipeptidase 2), a member of the M28 metallopeptidase family, show functional analogies to M20 family carboxypeptidases and participate in the cleavage of terminal amino acids from substrates in specialized cellular compartments[8]. The structural and functional conservation of carboxypeptidase catalysis across these organisms underscores the fundamental importance of exopeptidase-mediated degradation in cellular protein turnover and metabolic regulation.

Distinction from the Bgs1/Cps1 Œ≤(1,3)-Glucan Synthase

An important clarification must be made regarding nomenclature: the gene SPAC24C9.08 encoding the vacuolar carboxypeptidase Cps1 represents a distinct gene from the bgs1 gene (also historically referred to as cps1 in earlier literature), which encodes a Œ≤(1,3)-glucan synthase essential for cell wall synthesis and cytokinesis[25][31][37][40][49]. This nomenclatural distinction reflects the evolutionary history of Schizosaccharomyces pombe genetics research, wherein the cps1 designation was originally assigned to the chlorpropham-supersensitive mutant that was later recognized to be a mutation in the bgs1 gene[19][22][57]. The bgs1/cps1 gene encodes a large integral membrane protein that catalyzes the synthesis of linear Œ≤(1,3)-glucan, the major structural polymer of the primary septum formed during cytokinesis[25][31][37][40][49].

The bgs1/cps1 Œ≤(1,3)-glucan synthase is a completely distinct protein from the SPAC24C9.08-encoded vacuolar carboxypeptidase discussed in this report. Whereas the Bgs1/Cps1p glucan synthase is an integral membrane protein involved in biosynthetic processes (specifically cell wall polymer synthesis), the SPAC24C9.08 Cps1 is a soluble vacuolar protease involved in catabolic processes (protein degradation)[37][40][49][52]. The Œ≤(1,3)-glucan synthase Bgs1/Cps1p contains multiple transmembrane domains characteristic of polymerase and synthase enzymes, whereas the carboxypeptidase Cps1 (SPAC24C9.08) contains domains characteristic of soluble metalloproteases[37][40][49]. These proteins have entirely different functions in cellular physiology and respond to different regulatory signals. Mutations in the bgs1/cps1 glucan synthase result in cell wall integrity defects, hypersensitivity to cell wall-damaging agents, and cytokinetic defects[19][22][25][31][40][57], whereas mutations in the SPAC24C9.08 carboxypeptidase would be expected to impair protein degradation and autophagy, though such mutations have not been extensively characterized.

The historical conflation of these two genes under the cps1 nomenclature underscores the importance of careful gene symbol verification when consulting older literature, particularly from the pre-genomic era when gene names were often based on phenotypic characterization of mutants rather than biochemical characterization of gene products[19][22][49][57]. Modern genomic databases such as PomBase and UniProt have clarified this nomenclatural situation by assigning systematic ORF names (SPAC24C9.08 for the vacuolar carboxypeptidase) and precise functional annotations based on sequence homology and domain architecture[1][5][24][46].

Current Characterization Status and Functional Inference

The SPAC24C9.08-encoded Cps1 carboxypeptidase is currently designated as "uncharacterized" in the primary UniProt database entry, reflecting the limited experimental characterization of this specific protein in the peer-reviewed literature[5]. However, the functional role of this enzyme can be reliably inferred from multiple lines of evidence: its classification within the well-characterized M20A peptidase family, its vacuolar localization, its structural homology to characterized vacuolar carboxypeptidases in other organisms, and the known roles of related carboxypeptidases in autophagy and protein degradation[1][5][11][13][26][42].

The peptidase M20 family belongs to the broader clan MH of metallopeptidases and is defined by characteristic active site residues and structural features that collectively define the carboxypeptidase reaction mechanism[11][42][44]. Members of this family have been extensively studied through structural biology, biochemistry, and genetic approaches, providing a comprehensive understanding of how M20 carboxypeptidases achieve their catalytic activity and recognize substrates[13][20][23][27]. Given that the SPAC24C9.08 Cps1 protein contains all of the conserved active site residues and structural domains characteristic of M20 carboxypeptidases, it is highly probable that this protein catalyzes zinc-dependent hydrolytic removal of amino acids from peptide substrates[1][5][44].

The vacuolar localization of Cps1, as indicated by sequence-based targeting predictions and database annotations, is consistent with a role in vacuolar protein degradation and autophagy[1][5][24][51][54]. The vacuole in Schizosaccharomyces pombe, like the vacuoles in other fungi and the lysosomes in mammalian cells, is the primary site of controlled proteolysis in the cell[26][51][54]. The compartmentalization of proteolytic enzymes such as Cps1 within the vacuole allows for extensive protein degradation without risking damage to cytoplasmic proteins and cellular structures[26][51][54]. Thus, the combination of M20 carboxypeptidase characteristics and vacuolar localization strongly suggests that Cps1 functions as a vacuolar exopeptidase participating in the final stages of protein degradation pathways including autophagy[1][5][26][51].

The precise kinetic parameters, substrate specificity, and in vivo substrates of the SPAC24C9.08 Cps1 protein remain to be experimentally determined through biochemical and genetic approaches. Future work employing direct enzymatic assays with purified recombinant protein, site-directed mutagenesis of proposed active site residues, and genetic analysis of cps1 deletion or catalytic mutant strains would provide detailed information about the specific substrates recognized by this enzyme, its preference for particular amino acids at the P1 position, and its quantitative contribution to vacuolar protein degradation capacity relative to other carboxypeptidases in S. pombe[26][51].

Integration into the Vacuolar Proteolytic System

The S. pombe Cps1 carboxypeptidase functions as part of a sophisticated multi-enzyme proteolytic system within the vacuole. Beyond Cps1, the vacuolar lumen contains numerous other proteolytic enzymes including endoproteases (Pep4, an aspartic protease analogous to yeast protease A), serine carboxypeptidases (Prc1 and potentially other serine carboxypeptidases), and additional metallopeptidases[14][26][51]. The different proteases in this system have complementary specificities: the endoproteases make internal cleavages within proteins, generating peptide intermediates; the broad-specificity carboxypeptidases remove residues sequentially from peptide termini; and specialized carboxypeptidases remove specific amino acids depending on their substrate specificity[14][26][51]. This compartmentalization and functional diversification of proteolytic activities ensures complete degradation of proteins to free amino acids[14][26][51].

The activation of vacuolar proteases requires removal of inhibitory propeptides, a process that is tightly regulated and coordinated to ensure that proteolytic activity is maintained only when appropriate[14][26]. The initial activation of Pep4 (protease A) is rate-limiting, as this enzyme subsequently activates other zymogenic proteases within the vacuole by removing their inhibitory propeptides[14][26]. The coordinated activation and balanced activity of multiple proteases ensures efficient protein degradation while preventing excessive proteolysis that might damage essential vacuolar proteins[14][26]. The regulation of Cps1 activity through its propeptide-mediated zymogenic storage represents one mechanism by which vacuolar proteolytic capacity is regulated in response to the availability of substrates (proteins requiring degradation)[14][26].

Conclusion and Significance

The Cps1 protein encoded by the SPAC24C9.08 gene in Schizosaccharomyces pombe represents a vacuolar zinc-dependent metallopeptidase belonging to the M20A family of carboxypeptidases[1][5][24]. Although currently designated as uncharacterized in primary databases, the functional role of Cps1 can be reliably inferred from its sequence homology to well-characterized M20 family carboxypeptidases, its vacuolar localization, and the known roles of related enzymes in autophagy and protein degradation[1][5][11][13][26][42]. This enzyme participates in the terminal phase of vacuolar proteolysis, sequentially removing amino acids from peptide substrates generated by prior endoprotease activity, thereby generating free amino acids that support cellular metabolism during both nutrient-replete and nutrient-starved conditions[26][51][54].

The characterization of vacuolar carboxypeptidases such as Cps1 contributes to the broader understanding of autophagy and cellular homeostasis, processes that are fundamental to cell survival and that are dysregulated in human diseases including cancer, neurodegeneration, and metabolic disorders[26][51]. The use of Schizosaccharomyces pombe as a model system for studying autophagy and protein degradation has yielded important insights into the molecular mechanisms of these processes that are highly conserved from yeast to humans[51]. Future detailed characterization of the SPAC24C9.08 Cps1 carboxypeptidase through biochemical and genetic approaches would provide specific information about its substrate specificity, kinetic parameters, and quantitative contribution to vacuolar proteolysis in fission yeast. Such studies would not only advance our understanding of this specific enzyme but would also contribute to the broader goal of understanding how cells achieve complete and controlled protein degradation through the orchestrated action of multiple classes of proteolytic enzymes functioning within distinct cellular compartments. The distinction between the vacuolar carboxypeptidase Cps1 (SPAC24C9.08) and the Œ≤(1,3)-glucan synthase historically called Cps1/Bgs1p exemplifies the importance of careful nomenclature and highlights how genomic and proteomic approaches have clarified the functional roles of distinct yeast gene products that were previously confused under overlapping nomenclature.
