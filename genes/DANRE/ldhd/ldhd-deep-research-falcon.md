## Research Report: Functional Annotation of **ldhd** (UniProt **F1QXM5**) in *Danio rerio* (zebrafish)

### Scope and gene/protein identity verification
The target protein is **zebrafish ldhd (UniProt F1QXM5)**, annotated as **mitochondrial D-lactate dehydrogenase (LDHD)**. This identity is supported by direct zebrafish loss-of-function data showing **stereospecific accumulation of D-lactate (but not L-lactate)** when **ldhd** is disrupted, consistent with the expected role of mitochondrial LDHD in D-lactate clearance. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 3-4)

### 1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

#### D-lactate vs L-lactate
Lactate exists as two stereoisomers, **L-lactate** and **D-lactate**. In vertebrates, L-lactate is abundant and is primarily handled by NAD-dependent cytosolic LDH isoforms (LDHA/LDHB), whereas **D-lactate** is usually present at low concentrations and is mainly produced by (i) **intestinal bacterial metabolism** and (ii) **endogenous methylglyoxal/glyoxalase metabolism**, which yields D-lactate as a downstream product. Clinical literature emphasizes that D-lactate is typically low in healthy humans (reported range **5–20 μmol/L**), but can rise markedly when clearance is impaired. (kwong2021humand‐lactatedehydrogenase pages 1-2)

#### What “mitochondrial D-lactate dehydrogenase (LDHD)” means
In vertebrates, LDHD refers to a **mitochondrial flavoprotein** that **oxidizes D-lactate to pyruvate**. Importantly, mammalian LDHD is not the classical NAD-dependent “LDH” family (LDHA/LDHB), but a distinct enzyme class with different cofactor and mechanism. (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 1-2)

### 2) Primary biochemical function of zebrafish ldhd (reaction, specificity, cofactors)

#### Catalyzed reaction and substrate specificity
The conserved vertebrate function of LDHD is **oxidation of D-lactate to pyruvate**. In zebrafish, the essential in vivo readout is that **ldhd loss-of-function increases D-lactate levels while L-lactate remains unchanged**, demonstrating stereospecificity for D-lactate metabolism. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 3-4, monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 5-6)

Beyond D-lactate, vertebrate LDHD acts more broadly on **D-2-hydroxyacids**. In human cases associated with LDHD variants, additional metabolites such as **D-2-hydroxyisovaleric acid and D-2-hydroxyisocaproic acid** can be elevated, and zebrafish data in the same study are consistent with LDHD participating in the metabolism of D-lactate and related D-2-hydroxyacids. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 5-6, monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 2-3)

#### Cofactors and mechanism (best current mechanistic evidence; 2023)
The most detailed mechanistic/structural characterization comes from mammalian LDHD (mouse), which is strongly informative for zebrafish functional annotation because zebrafish genetics show the same directionality (D-lactate accumulation on loss). Mouse LDHD is:

* **FAD-containing (flavoprotein):** purified enzyme has the yellow color and absorbance features expected for FAD; a reported FAD:protein molar ratio is **0.73 ± 0.04**. (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 1-2)
* **Mn2+-dependent:** activity depends on divalent metal ions and is highest with Mn2+ (optimal **30–200 μM** Mn2+). (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 2-3)
* A **general dehydrogenase for hydrophobic D-2-hydroxyacids**, with no activity on corresponding L-isomers or on D-2-hydroxyacids with hydrophilic moieties. (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 1-2, jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 2-3)

Kinetic parameters reported for oxidation of **D-lactate → pyruvate** by mouse LDHD include **KM = 122.0 ± 4.5 μM** and **kcat = 61.0 ± 0.6 min−1** (with Vmax **1.22 ± 0.01 μmol·min−1·mg−1**). (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 2-3)

Structural evidence supports a mechanism in which substrate binding and catalysis are coordinated by a positively charged pocket and a hydrophobic pocket; key residues coordinate Mn2+, and hydride transfer occurs to the flavin. (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 6-7)

### 3) Subcellular localization and mitochondrial physiology

#### Mitochondrial localization
Vertebrate LDHD is consistently described as **mitochondrial** and associated with the **mitochondrial inner membrane**. Clinical/biochemical discussions cite mitochondrial uptake and metabolism of D-lactate and place the enzyme within mitochondrial metabolism rather than cytosolic glycolysis. (kwong2021humand‐lactatedehydrogenase pages 6-7)

A 2024 mitochondrial physiology study explicitly notes that LDHD is known to be associated with the mitochondrial inner membrane, and uses permeabilized-cell assays designed to preserve mitochondrial bioenergetics. (to2024pmfseqahighly pages 3-4)

#### Electron acceptor / coupling to respiration (major advance; 2024)
A key open question historically was the physiological electron acceptor for mammalian LDHD. A 2024 study provided direct evidence that **human LDHD transfers electrons from D-lactate to cytochrome c**, thereby supporting mitochondrial membrane polarization. (to2024pmfseqahighly pages 1-2, to2024pmfseqahighly pages 4-5)

Experimentally, LDHD knockout (A375 and HepG2) cells showed a **blunted mitochondrial membrane potential (ΔΨm) response to D-lactate**, while retaining responses to ascorbate/TMPD (which donates electrons directly to cytochrome c), consistent with LDHD acting upstream of cytochrome c reduction. (to2024pmfseqahighly pages 3-4)

In vitro biochemical characterization in the same work quantified LDHD kinetics for cytochrome c reduction with **kcat ~103 min−1** and **KM ~112 μM** (reported with uncertainty in the paper). (to2024pmfseqahighly pages 4-5)

### 4) In vivo functional evidence in zebrafish (loss-of-function and rescue)

The central zebrafish functional evidence comes from a zebrafish **ldhd** knockout allele (**sa15623**) that disrupts an essential splice site and yields an **ldhd−/− line that is adult viable and fertile**. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 6-7)

At larval stages, maternal-zygotic **ldhd−/−** embryos were reported to have **elevated D-lactate** while **L-lactate remained unchanged**, and **no obvious morphological abnormalities** were observed at early developmental time points examined. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 3-4, monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 5-6)

Causality and conservation were tested by mRNA rescue: microinjection of **wild-type human LDHD mRNA** restored D-lactate toward baseline, whereas mRNAs encoding patient-associated LDHD variants failed to rescue, supporting that the zebrafish phenotype reflects LDHD enzymatic loss-of-function rather than background effects. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 3-4)

### 5) Pathway context: D-lactate production and “metabolite repair”

A key pathway framing is that D-lactate is not only microbe-derived, but can also arise endogenously from central carbon metabolism via the **methylglyoxal (MG) / glyoxalase pathway**. LDHD is discussed as part of the broader concept of **metabolite repair**, in which enzymes prevent accumulation of side-products and damaged metabolites; in this view, mitochondrial LDHD clears D-lactate and potentially other D-2-hydroxyacids. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 4-5, kwong2021humand‐lactatedehydrogenase pages 6-7)

### 6) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

#### 2023: Structural and biochemical definition of LDHD substrate scope
A 2023 Nature Communications study established LDHD as an **Mn2+-dependent FAD enzyme** with broad activity toward hydrophobic D-2-hydroxyacids, and provided multiple structures of the enzyme with FAD/Mn2+ and ligands, substantially strengthening the mechanistic basis for substrate specificity and interpretation of pathogenic variants. (Publication date: Oct 2023; URL: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-023-42456-3) (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 1-2, jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 2-3)

#### 2023: Human genetic expansion—LDHD variants and systemic phenotypes
A 2023 Rheumatology paper linked rare recessive LDHD variants to **early-onset (juvenile) gout**, with homozygotes having significantly altered urate handling and higher D-lactate (blood and urine) compared with non-homozygotes in at least one family analysis. (Publication date: Apr 2023; URL: https://doi.org/10.1093/rheumatology/kead118) (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 9-10)

#### 2024: Direct demonstration of LDHD → cytochrome c electron transfer
A 2024 Nature Metabolism study provided direct biochemical and functional evidence that human LDHD transfers electrons from D-lactate to **cytochrome c**, establishing a concrete respiratory-chain connection and explaining LDHD-dependent mitochondrial membrane polarization in permeabilized cells. (Publication date: Feb 2024; URL: https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-024-00994-0) (to2024pmfseqahighly pages 1-2, to2024pmfseqahighly pages 4-5)

### 7) Current applications and real-world implementations

#### Clinical diagnostics / differential diagnosis of D-lactate elevation
Human genetics and biochemical phenotyping indicate that LDHD loss-of-function is a cause of **chronically elevated D-lactate** in plasma/urine, expanding differential diagnoses beyond classic short-bowel D-lactic acidosis. In the 2019 Nature Communications report, one patient’s mean urinary D-lactate excretion was reported as **1686 mmol/mol creatinine**, with plasma D-lactate **0.7 mM**, and the report notes that overt D-lactic acidosis is typically considered at plasma D-lactate **>3 mmol/L**. (Publication date: Apr 2019; URL: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-019-09458-6) (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 1-2)

These findings support real-world use of **D-lactate measurements** (blood/urine) and potentially additional D-2-hydroxyacid profiles as part of the diagnostic workup for unexplained D-lactate elevations. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 2-3, jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 9-10)

#### Model organism implementation (zebrafish)
Zebrafish ldhd knockout provides an in vivo system for mechanistic validation of human variants (via mRNA rescue) and for studying D-lactate homeostasis. The reported absence of gross early developmental abnormalities while showing a strong biochemical phenotype suggests feasibility for metabolic/pathway experiments without confounding early lethality. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 3-4, monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 6-7)

### 8) Expert interpretation and authoritative analysis

* **Mechanistic synthesis (2023):** The 2023 structural/biochemical study interprets LDHD as a mitochondrial FAD/Mn2+-dependent dehydrogenase with a binding-site architecture that explains why it acts on D-2-hydroxyacids with hydrophobic moieties and not on L-isomers or hydrophilic D-2-hydroxyacids. This provides a principled basis for functional annotation of vertebrate LDHD orthologs, including zebrafish ldhd. (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 1-2, jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 6-7)
* **Resolution of electron-acceptor uncertainty (2024):** Prior clinical literature emphasized that association of D-lactate dehydrogenase with the respiratory chain was known in lower organisms but incompletely characterized in mammals. The 2024 mitochondrial physiology paper addresses this gap by directly demonstrating electron transfer to cytochrome c and linking this to mitochondrial membrane polarization. (kwong2021humand‐lactatedehydrogenase pages 7-8, to2024pmfseqahighly pages 4-5)

### 9) Summary of key quantitative statistics (selected)

* **Zebrafish phenotype and experimental design:** maternal-zygotic **ldhd−/−** embryos show elevated D-lactate; D-/L-lactate were quantified at **3 dpf** using pooled batches of embryos with LC–MS/MS methods; rescue by wild-type human LDHD mRNA but not patient variants. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 3-4, monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 5-6)
* **Human biochemical values (examples from clinical genetics):** urinary D-lactate excretion mean **1686 mmol/mol creatinine** and plasma D-lactate **0.7 mM** in one report; D-lactic acidosis threshold cited as **>3 mmol/L**. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 1-2)
* **Mouse LDHD kinetics for D-lactate:** **KM ~122 μM**, **kcat ~61 min−1** (assay conditions described in the primary paper). (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 2-3)
* **Human LDHD kinetics for cytochrome c reduction (2024):** **kcat ~103 min−1**, **KM ~112 μM**. (to2024pmfseqahighly pages 4-5)

## Conclusion (functional annotation for DANRE ldhd; UniProt F1QXM5)
Collectively, the strongest evidence supports annotating zebrafish **ldhd (F1QXM5)** as a **mitochondrial FAD-dependent D-2-hydroxyacid dehydrogenase** whose primary physiological role is **oxidation of D-lactate to pyruvate**, with high stereoselectivity for D- over L-lactate. In zebrafish, genetic loss of ldhd causes **in vivo accumulation of D-lactate** without major early morphological defects in reported assays, and the biochemical phenotype is rescuable by wild-type LDHD. (monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 3-4, monroe2019identificationofhuman pages 5-6)

Modern vertebrate mechanistic work (2023–2024) further indicates that LDHD is **Mn2+-dependent**, can act on multiple **hydrophobic D-2-hydroxyacids**, and in humans can **transfer electrons from D-lactate to cytochrome c** to support mitochondrial membrane polarization—positioning LDHD at the interface of D-lactate clearance and mitochondrial redox physiology. (jin2023lactatedehydrogenased pages 2-3, to2024pmfseqahighly pages 1-2, to2024pmfseqahighly pages 4-5)
