Gene Description

DarB is a phage-encoded DNA methyltransferase homolog from bacteriophage P1 that must be injected into host cells to be functional. This 512-residue protein contains a Type II methyltransferase TaqI-like domain and SAM-dependent methyltransferase fold. As noted by the colleague who suggested this gene, DarB exemplifies phage proteins that require delivery into the host cell for activity, likely functioning in DNA modification to protect phage DNA from host restriction systems or to regulate gene expression during the phage life cycle. The protein is encoded as part of the phage genome but must be transferred to the host cytoplasm during infection to carry out its methylation function.

Key Findings

DNA Hypermodification System

  • 7-deazaguanine modifications: Replaces guanine in phage DNA
  • Creates hypermodified DNA resistant to restriction enzymes
  • Essential for evading bacterial defense systems
  • Part of queuosine-like modification pathway

Molecular Function

  1. Amidotransferase activity:
  2. Transfers ammonia to deazaguanine precursor
  3. Uses glutamine as nitrogen donor

  4. Coupled to GTP hydrolysis

  5. Biosynthetic pathway:

  6. Works with DarA (7-cyano-7-deazaguanine synthase)
  7. Produces preQ0-like intermediate

  8. Modified base incorporated during replication

  9. Host evasion strategy:

  10. Modified DNA invisible to restriction enzymes
  11. Escapes CRISPR targeting
  12. Resistant to host nucleases

Evolutionary Significance

  • Found in diverse bacteriophages
  • Convergent evolution with archaeal modifications
  • Related to queuosine tRNA modification
  • Arms race with bacterial immunity
