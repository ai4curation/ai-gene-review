## Research report: Human KRAS (UniProt P01116) — functional annotation and translational landscape

### 0) Target verification (mandatory)
The UniProt accession **P01116** corresponds to **human KRAS** (K-Ras; Ras family small GTPase) with a canonical **G-domain (~residues 1–166)** and a C-terminal **hypervariable region (HVR)** containing the **CAAX** membrane-targeting motif. This matches the Ras-family small GTPase described across authoritative reviews and mechanistic summaries, and no evidence here indicates a different gene/protein target (wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof pages 1-2, uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 2-4, yang2024rassignalingin pages 1-2).

### 1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

#### 1.1 KRAS is a small GTPase molecular switch (core biochemical function)
KRAS is a membrane-associated **small GTPase** that functions as a **binary molecular switch**, cycling between an **inactive GDP-bound** state and an **active GTP-bound** state (uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 2-4, wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof pages 1-2). In the GTP-bound form, KRAS engages downstream effector proteins to drive signaling; inactivation occurs through **GTP hydrolysis** (intrinsic to KRAS but strongly accelerated by GAPs) to regenerate GDP-bound KRAS (takacs2020theeffectsof pages 1-3, uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 2-4).

**Structural/functional motifs.** The KRAS G-domain contains the **P-loop**, **Switch I**, and **Switch II** regions, which coordinate nucleotide binding/hydrolysis and alter conformation to control binding to effectors and regulators (takacs2020theeffectsof pages 1-3, prajapati2025structuralinsightsregulation pages 2-3, uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 2-4). Common oncogenic hotspots occur at **codons 12, 13, and 61**, which are located in/near these functional regions and commonly reduce GAP-mediated inactivation (takacs2020theeffectsof pages 1-3, prajapati2025structuralinsightsregulation pages 2-3).

#### 1.2 Regulation by GEFs and GAPs
In normal signaling, KRAS activation is promoted by **guanine nucleotide exchange factors (GEFs)** such as **SOS1**, which catalyze GDP release and allow GTP loading, whereas KRAS inactivation is accelerated by **GTPase-activating proteins (GAPs)** such as **NF1/neurofibromin** (uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 6-8, tang2025krastargetedtherapiesin pages 2-3, uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 2-4).

A common upstream axis is RTK→GRB2→SOS1. For example, **EGF/EGFR** signaling recruits **GRB2**, which recruits **SOS1** to promote KRAS GDP→GTP exchange (uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 6-8). The protein tyrosine phosphatase **SHP2 (PTPN11)** is highlighted as an enabling node for RTK-to-SOS1 signaling and thus KRAS activation, motivating SHP2 as a combination target in KRAS-driven cancers (uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 6-8, tang2025krastargetedtherapiesin pages 2-3).

#### 1.3 Primary effector pathways and biological role
Activated KRAS drives multiple downstream pathways. Canonical effectors include:
- **RAF→MEK→ERK (MAPK)** signaling (takacs2020theeffectsof pages 1-3, uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 6-8, wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof pages 1-2)
- **PI3K→AKT→mTOR** signaling (takacs2020theeffectsof pages 1-3, uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 6-8)
- **RalGDS→RalA/B** signaling (takacs2020theeffectsof pages 1-3, wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof pages 1-2)

Broader “signalome” coverage includes **RAC/PAK** and **PKC/PLC**-linked signaling branches (takacs2020theeffectsof pages 1-3). A recent (2024) Molecular Cell review emphasizes the centrality of RAF and PI3K as RAS effectors and treats RAS-effector engagement as a structured, biophysically definable interaction space rather than a purely linear cascade (mozzarelli2024; retrieved but not evidence-extracted beyond metadata in this run).

### 2) Subcellular localization and post-translational processing (where KRAS acts)
KRAS function is tightly coupled to **membrane localization**, especially at the **inner leaflet of the plasma membrane**, where it organizes signaling through nanoscale clustering/dimerization.

#### 2.1 CAAX processing enables membrane attachment
KRAS contains a C-terminal **CAAX** motif that undergoes sequential processing: **prenylation** (most commonly farnesylation), **AAX proteolysis**, and **carboxyl methylation**, enabling membrane association (baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 1-3, wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof media 79aa27a2).

- **Farnesyl transferase (FTase)** attaches a farnesyl (C15) group to the CAAX cysteine (baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 1-3, wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof media 79aa27a2).
- After prenylation, the **AAX** residues are removed by **RCE1** (Ras converting enzyme 1) (baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 1-3, wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof media 79aa27a2).
- The newly exposed prenylcysteine is **methylated by ICMT**, increasing hydrophobicity and aiding stable membrane association (baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 1-3, wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof media 79aa27a2).

If farnesylation is blocked, KRAS can undergo **alternative geranylgeranylation**, which historically undermined early farnesyltransferase inhibitor approaches in KRAS-driven cancers (baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 1-3).

**Visual evidence.** The CAAX processing steps and subsequent membrane targeting are schematized in Figure 2 of Wang et al. (Acta Pharmacol Sin, Oct 2021) (wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof media 79aa27a2).

#### 2.2 Isoform-specific targeting: KRAS4A vs KRAS4B
KRAS is expressed as two splice isoforms, **KRAS4A (189 aa)** and **KRAS4B (188 aa)**, with different HVR features that alter trafficking and membrane anchoring (uniyal2025krasmutationsin pages 2-4).

- **KRAS4A** can be **palmitoylated** (e.g., at C180), which substantially enhances plasma membrane targeting and supports Golgi↔plasma membrane cycling via acylation/deacylation (wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof pages 3-4, baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 3-4).
- **KRAS4B** lacks palmitoylation and instead contains a **polybasic lysine-rich region** that binds acidic inner-leaflet lipids (notably phosphatidylserine), making electrostatic interactions a principal determinant of plasma membrane localization (kewagamang2024therelationshipbetween pages 21-25, haidar2021pastandfuture pages 5-7, wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof pages 3-4).

KRAS4B localization can be modulated by phosphorylation at **S181** in the polybasic region, which reduces positive charge and promotes dissociation/redistribution to intracellular membranes (ER and other endomembranes) (wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof pages 3-4, baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 4-6).

#### 2.3 Spatial organization: nanoclusters and dimerization
Beyond “being at the membrane,” KRAS signaling is spatially organized. A 2020 review notes KRAS signals from the plasma membrane in **nanoclusters** and can form **homodimers**, and that **membrane localization and nanoclustering/dimerization are required for efficient RAF activation** (baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 3-4, baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 4-6). Trafficking regulators such as **PDE6δ** bind farnesylated KRAS4B and facilitate cytosolic sequestration/transport to and from membrane compartments; blockade of this interaction disrupts plasma membrane localization (baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 3-4, haidar2021pastandfuture pages 5-7).

### 3) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

#### 3.1 KRAS has transitioned from “undruggable” to allele-selective druggable (G12C)
A 2024 review in *Journal of Hematology & Oncology* summarizes why KRAS was historically “undruggable” (high GTP affinity, limited pockets), and highlights a breakthrough: covalent inhibitors that bind **Cys12** in **KRASG12C** and occupy a **cryptic switch-II pocket** in the GDP-bound (OFF) state, enabling direct mutant-selective inhibition (yang2024rassignalingin pages 1-2). This review also notes that **sotorasib (AMG510)** and **adagrasib (MRTX849)** were approved beginning in **2021** for **KRASG12C-mutant NSCLC** (yang2024rassignalingin pages 1-2).

#### 3.2 2024: KRASG12D targeting with MRTX1133 (pancreas-focused)
A 2024 *Clinical Cancer Research* review emphasizes that **~93% of PDAC** harbor KRAS mutations and identifies **G12D (~42%)** as the most common allele (wei2024asmallmolecule pages 1-3). It highlights **MRTX1133** as a **non-covalent, selective KRASG12D inhibitor** developed by structure-based design, with potent preclinical activity and a **phase I/II clinical trial** initiated for KRASG12D-mutant solid tumors including PDAC (trial initiation date noted as March 20, 2023 within the review) (wei2024asmallmolecule pages 1-3).

#### 3.3 2023–2024: combination therapy proof in KRASG12C colorectal cancer
A key clinical demonstration of rational combination therapy is **dual KRASG12C + EGFR blockade** in metastatic colorectal cancer (mCRC). In a phase 1–2 trial reported in *NEJM* (Jan 2023), **adagrasib + cetuximab** improved outcomes versus adagrasib monotherapy in heavily pretreated KRASG12C mCRC:
- ORR **19%** with adagrasib monotherapy vs **46%** with adagrasib + cetuximab
- median PFS **5.6 months** (mono) vs **6.9 months** (combo)
- grade 3–4 treatment-related AEs **34%** (mono) vs **16%** (combo)
(NCT05054725 chunk 1).

These results align with the mechanistic concept that EGFR-driven upstream signaling can rapidly reactivate pathway flux in CRC, making EGFR co-inhibition a rational strategy (NCT05054725 chunk 1).

#### 3.4 2024: resistance mechanisms to KRASG12C inhibitors
A 2024 review focused on resistance to KRASG12C inhibitors describes major categories of resistance:
- **On-target** secondary KRAS mutations that prevent inhibitor binding
- **Off-target/bypass** mechanisms (e.g., activating alterations in NRAS, BRAF, RET; or tumor suppressor loss such as PTEN)
- Phenotypic changes including **EMT** and **histologic transformation**
It emphasizes that multiple mechanisms can co-exist clinically and that non-genomic resistance remains under-characterized, motivating comprehensive sampling at resistance (Frontiers in Oncology, Sep 2024) (NCT05054725 chunk 1).

### 5) Expert opinions / analysis (authoritative syntheses)
Several themes recur across high-level reviews and clinical evidence:

1. **Membrane biology is not incidental; it is enabling.** Reviews emphasize that KRAS requires CAAX processing and isoform-specific “second signals” (palmitoylation or polybasic electrostatics) to achieve plasma membrane localization and to form signaling-competent nanoclusters/dimers, implying that localization/trafficking modifiers remain valid indirect strategies alongside direct inhibitors (baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 1-3, wang2021posttranslationalmodificationof pages 3-4, haidar2021pastandfuture pages 5-7, baranyi2020krasprenylationas pages 3-4).

2. **Allele selectivity is central to both efficacy and therapeutic index.** The 2024 *J Hematol Oncol* review frames the feasibility of KRAS targeting as arising from mutant-selective pockets and covalent chemistry (switch-II pocket in GDP-state KRASG12C), while acknowledging toxicity risks of wild-type pathway suppression and feedback complexity (yang2024rassignalingin pages 1-2).

3. **Combination therapy is becoming a rule rather than exception.** The NEJM 2023 mCRC study provides empirical evidence that rational upstream co-targeting (EGFR) can markedly improve response to KRASG12C inhibition in CRC, supporting a broader combination paradigm to counter pathway reactivation/bypass (NCT05054725 chunk 1).

4. **Resistance is expected and heterogeneous.** 2024 resistance-focused review literature highlights that both on-target evolution and off-target bypass can co-occur, motivating longitudinal sampling and ctDNA-informed adaptive strategies (NCT05054725 chunk 1).
