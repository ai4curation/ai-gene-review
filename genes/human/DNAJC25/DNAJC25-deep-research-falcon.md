## Protein Identity and Classification

DNAJC25 (UniProt Q9H1X3) is DnaJ homolog subfamily C member 25 in *Homo sapiens*. Based on UniProt annotation, the protein contains:
- DnaJ domain (IPR001623)
- DNAJC25-like domain (IPR044632  
- J-domain superfamily (IPR036869)
- Pfam DnaJ domain (PF00226)

DNAJC25 belongs to the DNAJC25 family within the DNAJC subfamily of J-domain proteins (also called HSP40 family).

## Inferred Molecular Function

Based on its J-domain protein family membership, DNAJC25 is inferred to act as a molecular co-chaperone for HSP70. J-domain proteins stimulate HSP70 ATPase activity to facilitate protein folding and quality control. DNAJC25 does not have intrinsic enzymatic activity but would modulate HSP70 function. **Specific client proteins for DNAJC25 have not been experimentally identified.**

## Subcellular Localization

DNAJC25 is reported to localize to the cytoplasm based on literature discussing DNAJC subfamily members.

## Disease Relevance: Hepatocellular Carcinoma

The primary experimental finding for DNAJC25 comes from cancer research. Guo et al. (2023) identified DNAJC25 as a substrate of RNF149 E3 ubiquitin ligase. The abstract states DNAJC25 was identified as a "new substrate" of RNF149, which "promotes HCC progression through its E3 ubiquitin ligase activity." RNF149-mediated ubiquitination leads to DNAJC25 degradation, contributing to hepatocellular carcinoma progression.

Wu et al. (2025) cited this work, noting that RNF149 "can accelerate the progression of hepatocellular carcinoma by ubiquitinating the substrate" DNAJC25.

These findings suggest DNAJC25 may act as a tumor suppressor.

## Evolutionary Conservation

Comparative studies indicate HSP40/J-domain genes are evolutionarily conserved. Yan et al. (2021) studying hsp40 genes in Japanese flounder reported these genes "were conservative during evolution and were functional-constrained."

## Current Knowledge Status

Kaida and Iwakuma (2021) in their review note that "DNAJC25 is relatively a new member" of the HSP40/J-domain protein family, acknowledging the limited literature on this specific protein.
