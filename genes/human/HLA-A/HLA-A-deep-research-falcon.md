## Research Report: Human **HLA-A** (UniProt **P04439**) — Functional Annotation and 2023–2024 Research Update

### 0) Target verification (gene/protein identity)
The UniProt accession **P04439** corresponds to the human **HLA-A** protein, described as the **HLA class I histocompatibility antigen, A alpha (heavy) chain**, a member of the classical **MHC class I family** (heavy chain) that forms a surface peptide–MHC-I complex with **β2-microglobulin (B2M)** and short peptides for immune surveillance. This matches the canonical definition of classical HLA-I heavy chains encoded by **HLA-A, HLA-B, and HLA-C**. (wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 1-2, margulies2023chaperonefunctionin pages 1-2)

---

### 1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

#### 1.1 What HLA-A is
**HLA-A** encodes a **polymorphic MHC class I heavy (α) chain** (≈46 kDa), which is displayed on the surface of (virtually) all nucleated cells as part of a **trimeric complex** consisting of: (i) the HLA class I heavy chain, (ii) **β2-microglobulin**, and (iii) a bound peptide typically **~8–11 amino acids** in length. (margulies2023chaperonefunctionin pages 1-2, olson2023majorhistocompatibilitycomplex pages 1-3, liu2023molecularcharacteristicsfunctional pages 2-5)

#### 1.2 Domain architecture and peptide-binding groove
The heavy chain contains three extracellular domains (**α1, α2, α3**). The **α1/α2 domains form the peptide-binding groove** (including the C-terminal anchoring pocket, commonly discussed as the F pocket), and the **α3 domain** is Ig-like and lies membrane-proximal, adjacent to β2-microglobulin. (margulies2023chaperonefunctionin pages 3-5, liu2023molecularcharacteristicsfunctional pages 2-5, wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 1-2)

#### 1.3 Primary molecular function
The primary function of HLA-A is to present intracellularly derived peptides (self or pathogen/tumor derived) at the **plasma membrane** for scanning by **T-cell receptors (TCRs)** on **CD8+ T cells**, thereby enabling antigen-specific cytotoxic responses against infected or malignant cells. (wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 1-2, wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 2-4)

#### 1.4 Cellular localization and trafficking (where the protein acts)
HLA-A heavy chains are synthesized and assembled in the **endoplasmic reticulum (ER)**, undergo quality control and peptide editing/loading, then traffic through the **Golgi** to the **cell surface**. MHC-I complexes can also recycle through endosomal compartments, and endolysosomal sites can support alternate peptide loading in some contexts. (olson2023majorhistocompatibilitycomplex pages 1-3, massa2023interferonsandresistance pages 4-5)

---

### 2) Mechanistic pathway context: antigen processing and peptide loading

#### 2.1 Canonical MHC-I antigen processing pathway (HLA-A as the presenting molecule)
A consensus mechanistic picture from recent reviews is:
1) **Endogenous proteins** are degraded by the **proteasome** and other cytosolic peptidases into peptides. (massa2023interferonsandresistance pages 4-5, wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 2-4)
2) Peptides are transported into the ER by **TAP1/TAP2**. (massa2023interferonsandresistance pages 4-5, wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 2-4)
3) **ERAP1/ERAP2** trim longer peptides to optimal lengths for MHC-I binding (often summarized as 8–10 aa). (massa2023interferonsandresistance pages 4-5, wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 2-4)
4) The MHC-I heavy chain assembles with **β2-microglobulin** and is stabilized/edited by the **peptide-loading complex (PLC)**, including **calnexin**, **calreticulin**, **ERp57**, and **tapasin**; tapasin and TAPBPR act as peptide editors/chaperones. (massa2023interferonsandresistance pages 4-5, olson2023majorhistocompatibilitycomplex pages 1-3, margulies2023chaperonefunctionin pages 3-5)
5) Properly loaded **peptide–HLA-A–β2m** complexes exit the ER, traffic through the **trans-Golgi**, and are displayed at the **plasma membrane** for CD8+ T-cell recognition; peptide-deficient complexes are unstable and are retained/edited. (massa2023interferonsandresistance pages 4-5, olson2023majorhistocompatibilitycomplex pages 1-3)

A pathway schematic (in cancer context) summarizing these steps and major regulatory layers is shown in **Wu et al., 2023**, Figure 1. (wu2023targetingmhcimolecules media 43d05aed)

### 3) Recent developments (prioritizing 2023–2024)

#### 3.1 Structural/chaperone and cell biology refinements (2023)
Recent reviews emphasize a refined mechanistic understanding of **peptide editing** and the roles of **tapasin/TAPBPR** and ER chaperones in stabilizing partially empty MHC-I, favoring high-affinity peptides that support stable surface display—central to HLA-A function because only stable peptide-loaded complexes efficiently reach the plasma membrane. (margulies2023chaperonefunctionin pages 1-2, olson2023majorhistocompatibilitycomplex pages 1-3, margulies2023chaperonefunctionin pages 3-5)

#### 3.2 Immunopeptidomics and improved prediction/deconvolution (2024)
A major 2024 development is the scale and maturity of **mass spectrometry immunopeptidomics** datasets and the rise of deep learning models trained on them to predict peptide presentation.

**ImmuneApp (Xu et al., Nature Communications, Oct 2024)** reports large-scale training and deconvolution resources for HLA-I presentation modeling, enabling improved epitope and neoepitope prioritization across many alleles (including HLA-A allotypes). Key reported statistics include: **349,650** mono-allelic ligands spanning **149 HLA-I alleles**, analysis of **216** multi-allelic samples, and deconvolution/assignment of **835,551** ligands to **104** HLA-I alleles; the derived immunogenicity component is reported to have **2.1-fold higher PPV** than comparator models in their evaluation. (xu2024immuneappforhlai pages 2-3, xu2024immuneappforhlai pages 1-2)

A complementary example of contemporary immunopeptidomics mapping is **Braun et al. (Nature Communications, Aug 2024)**, which identified **248 unique** HLA class I/II SARS-CoV-2 peptides across antigens (e.g., N=71, E=12, Nsp8=73) and functionally tested T-cell reactivity for **56** detected peptides; they report that **>50%** of viral peptides were previously unpublished. (braun2024mappingtheimmunopeptidome pages 1-2)

#### 3.3 Tumor immune escape via antigen presentation loss (2023–2024)
Loss or reduction of MHC-I antigen presentation (including HLA-A) remains a central mechanism of immune escape and resistance to T-cell-based immunotherapies.

A 2024 Trends in Immunology review provides quantitative examples of genetic lesions in ICB-resistant disease: **homozygous B2M loss** was observed in **3/69 melanoma** cases and **1/14 NSCLC** cases in the cited resistant cohorts, while **MHC-I heavy-chain mutations** are described as low-frequency (**<4% pan-cancer**). (wang2024targetingmhciinhibitory pages 1-3)

Mechanistically, disruption can occur at multiple layers: heavy chain or B2M defects, peptide generation/loading machinery defects (e.g., TAP, ERAP, CALR, tapasin/TAPBP), transcriptional regulation (e.g., NLRC5/CITA; IFN/JAK/STAT), epigenetic repression, or increased degradation/removal from the surface. (wang2024targetingmhciinhibitory pages 1-3, wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 2-4)

### 4) Current applications and real-world implementations

#### 4.1 HLA-A-restricted adoptive T cell therapies (clinical)
HLA-A (especially **HLA-A*02:01**) is a common restricting allele used for engineered TCR therapies against intracellular antigens.

**NY-ESO-1 TCR-T (TAEST16001) in HLA-A*02:01 soft tissue sarcoma (Pan et al., Cell Reports Medicine, Aug 2023)**:
- **N=12** treated. (pan2023phase1clinical pages 1-3)
- **Overall response rate (ORR)**: **41.7%**; **median PFS**: **7.2 months**; **median duration of response**: **13.1 months**. (pan2023phase1clinical pages 1-3)
- Safety signals primarily cytopenias from lymphodepletion; **CRS** occurred in **2/12 (17%)** (both grade 2) and **neurotoxicity 0/12** in the excerpted outcomes. (pan2023phase1clinical pages 4-5)

**MAGE-A4 TCR-T (afamitresgene autoleucel; “afami-cel”) in HLA-A*02+ solid tumors (Hong et al., Nature Medicine, Jan 2023)**:
- Screening: **854** HLA-A*02-eligible screened; **225** were MAGE-A4+. (hong2023autologoustcell pages 1-2)
- Treated cohort: **mITT N=38** (ITT 63 enrolled). (hong2023autologoustcell pages 1-2)
- **ORR**: **24% (9/38)** overall (all partial responses); synovial sarcoma subgroup **44% (7/16)** vs **9% (2/22)** other tumors. (hong2023autologoustcell pages 1-2)
- **Median duration of response**: **25.6 weeks** overall and **28.1 weeks** for synovial sarcoma. (hong2023autologoustcell pages 1-2)
- **CRS**: **55%**, predominantly low grade (reported as 90% grade ≤2). (hong2023autologoustcell pages 1-2)
- Mechanistic/biologic note: exploratory analyses suggest tumor infiltration and an **IFN-γ-driven mechanism of action**. (hong2023autologoustcell pages 1-2)

These trials demonstrate direct clinical translation of HLA-A biology: the therapeutic receptor is HLA-restricted, and durable tumor control depends on intact tumor antigen processing/presentation capacity and stable pMHC display. (wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 2-4, wang2024targetingmhciinhibitory pages 1-3)

#### 4.2 Immunopeptidomics to support vaccine and target discovery (translational)
Immunopeptidomics is increasingly positioned as a practical discovery tool for mapping actionable peptides and informing therapeutic design (vaccines, TCR-based therapies, peptide-centric CARs). A 2024 review highlights examples where thousands of peptides are detected and subsequently filtered to a smaller set of high-confidence HLA-binding candidates; it also reports a proteogenomics aggregation scale of **~1,100 patients across 11 cancer types** in a referenced CPAC-related resource. (pongcharoen2024immunopeptidomicsinthe pages 8-9, pongcharoen2024immunopeptidomicsinthe pages 9-11)

### 5) Expert opinions and analysis (authoritative sources)

#### 5.1 Why HLA-A is central but also a key failure point in therapy
Recent expert reviews converge on the view that MHC-I antigen presentation is a bottleneck for immunotherapy: T-cell recognition depends on stable pMHC display, so tumors can evade immunity by downregulating any step in antigen processing/loading or by losing B2M/HLA expression. (wang2024targetingmhciinhibitory pages 1-3, wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 1-2, wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 2-4)

#### 5.2 Strategy direction suggested by 2023–2024 literature
Two complementary 2023–2024 directions emerge:
1) **Improve measurement and prediction** of what HLA-A presents (immunopeptidomics + ML/deconvolution), enabling better neoantigen selection and mechanistic biomarker development. (xu2024immuneappforhlai pages 2-3, xu2024immuneappforhlai pages 1-2)
2) **Therapeutically address MHC-I loss**—either by restoring/inducing antigen presentation (e.g., via IFN pathway modulation, epigenetic reprogramming) or by targeting inhibitory pathways that keep tumors “MHC-I low,” as proposed in recent cancer-focused reviews. (wang2024targetingmhciinhibitory pages 1-3, wu2023targetingmhcimolecules pages 2-4)
