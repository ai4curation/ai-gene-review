Gene Description

CYCS encodes human cytochrome c, a small (105 aa, ~12 kDa) soluble heme-containing electron carrier protein of the mitochondrial intermembrane space. Cytochrome c has two well-established, evolutionarily conserved functions: (1) In the mitochondrial electron transport chain, it shuttles single electrons from Complex III (cytochrome bc1, via its cytochrome c1 subunit) to Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase), accepting electrons via its covalently bound heme c group. (2) In apoptosis, upon mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP) triggered by pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family members (BAX/BAK), cytochrome c is released into the cytosol where it binds Apaf-1 to form the apoptosome, activating caspase-9 and triggering the downstream caspase cascade. The heme c prosthetic group is covalently attached via thioether bonds to Cys14 and Cys17 (mature numbering Cys15 and Cys18 in UniProt), with His18 and Met80 as axial ligands to the iron atom. Gain-of-function mutations in CYCS (e.g., G42S) cause thrombocytopenia type 4 (THC4) by enhancing apoptotic activation in the megakaryocyte/platelet lineage. Cytochrome c is one of the most extensively studied proteins in biochemistry and a classic example of a dual-function (moonlighting) protein.

Core Functions

Cytochrome c is a small soluble heme-containing electron carrier protein that shuttles single electrons from Complex III (cytochrome bc1) to Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase) in the mitochondrial intermembrane space. The protein accepts electrons from the cytochrome c1 subunit of Complex III via its covalently bound heme c group (Fe3+ -> Fe2+) and donates them to cytochrome c oxidase (Fe2+ -> Fe3+). This electron transfer function is essential for oxidative phosphorylation and cellular respiration. The heme c prosthetic group is covalently attached via thioether bonds to Cys15 and Cys18, with His18 and Met80 as axial iron ligands. Cytochrome c is loosely associated with the inner mitochondrial membrane surface via electrostatic interactions and cardiolipin binding, operating within the IMS.

  CYCS encodes human cytochrome c, a small (105 aa, ~12 kDa) soluble heme-containing
  electron carrier protein of the mitochondrial intermembrane space. Cytochrome c has two
  well-established, evolutionarily conserved functions: (1) In the mitochondrial electron
  transport chain, it shuttles single electrons from Complex III (cytochrome bc1, via its

  permeabilization (MOMP) triggered by pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family members (BAX/BAK),

  Cys15 and Cys18 in UniProt), with His18 and Met80 as axial ligands to the iron atom.
  Gain-of-function mutations in CYCS (e.g., G42S) cause thrombocytopenia type 4 (THC4) by
  enhancing apoptotic activation in the megakaryocyte/platelet lineage. Cytochrome c is
  one of the most extensively studied proteins in biochemistry and a classic example of a
  dual-function (moonlighting) protein.
