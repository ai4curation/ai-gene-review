# Manual literature synthesis for human GLIPR1L2 (Q4G1C9)

## Scope and evidence boundary

The configured automated deep-research providers were unavailable on 2026-07-18 (Falcon/Edison HTTP 402; Perplexity-lite HTTP 401). This manual synthesis uses the reviewed human UniProt record and four repository-cached primary papers. Direct human evidence is limited to gene discovery, splice forms, p53-responsive transcription, expression, and sequence-derived membrane architecture. The only causal vertebrate experiment deletes the three-gene mouse Glipr1l1–3 interval together, so it cannot assign a positive function specifically to Glipr1l2.

## Protein architecture and expression

Human GLIPR1L2 encodes a 344-aa CAP/CRISP-family protein. UniProt places an SCP/CAP domain at residues 58–192 and predicts a hydrophobic transmembrane helix at residues 254–274, followed by an acidic, disordered C-terminal tail. Six named UniProt isoforms differ substantially at their termini; the original discovery paper reported five splice forms. [PMID:16714093, "GLIPR1L1 has two and GLIPR1L2 has five differentially spliced isoforms."]

The discovery study classified GLIPR1L2 as a p53 target and detected expression in several tissues, including prostate and bladder. Current reviewed metadata reports highest expression in testis. [PMID:16714093, "Like hRTVP-1, GLIPR1L1 and GLIPR1L2 are p53 target genes."] [PMID:16714093, "GLIPR1L2 is expressed in different types of tissues, including prostate and bladder."] These findings describe regulation and tissue context, not the biochemical activity of the encoded protein.

## Localization evidence

The transmembrane helix makes the generic membrane annotation defensible. The precise membrane, orientation, and isoform dependence are not resolved. In particular, the canonical UniProt entry does not annotate a cleaved signal peptide or topology, and no focused human experiment demonstrates an extracellular pool. The N-terminal CAP domain could be extracellular or luminal in a suitable single-pass topology, but that structural possibility is not enough to call the protein active in the extracellular region. The family-level extracellular IBA should therefore remain undecided pending direct evidence.

## Reproductive evidence and paralog distinction

Glipr1l2/GLIPR1L2 is highly expressed in mouse and human testis and epididymis. [PMID:40572022, "Glipr1l1, Glipr1l2, Izumo4, Slc22a16, Spmip2, Tex51, Tmco2, Triml1, and Triml2 are highly expressed in mouse and human testis and epididymis"] A mouse 10,138-bp deletion removed Glipr1l1, Glipr1l2, and Glipr1l3 together. The triple-knockout males retained normal fecundity, testicular and epididymal histology, sperm morphology, and motility. [PMID:40572022, "Glipr1l1‐3 KO males exhibited no anomalies in their testis weight, testis and epididymis histology, or sperm morphology and motility"] This rules out an indispensable collective role under standard mating conditions but cannot reveal an individual GLIPR1L2 activity or exclude subtle/redundant functions.

The mechanistic sperm evidence in this gene family belongs to GLIPR1L1. Mouse GLIPR1L1 is remodeled during germ-cell maturation and resides with IZUMO1 in sperm-surface complexes; its deletion perturbs acrosomal exocytosis, IZUMO1 redistribution, and in-vitro fertilization. [PMID:31672133, "At least two of these complexes contain IZUMO1 in partnership with GLI pathogenesis-related 1 like 1 (GLIPR1L1)."] Neither that paper nor the earlier GLIPR1L1 study assays GLIPR1L2. Shared CAP architecture therefore does not justify transferring the specialized sperm molecular-adaptor role to GLIPR1L2.

## Curation-ready conclusion

GLIPR1L2 is an isoform-rich, testis-enriched single-pass membrane CAP-family protein whose direct molecular activity and physiological process remain unknown. Generic membrane localization is supported. Extracellular activity remains plausible but unverified, and the PAINT molecular-adaptor IBA is an unsafe paralog transfer from mouse Glipr1l1. No positive reproductive-process or p53-pathway annotation should be inferred from expression, transcriptional induction, or the nonessential triple-cluster knockout phenotype.
