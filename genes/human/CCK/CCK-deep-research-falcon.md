## Research report: Human **CCK** (cholecystokinin) functional annotation (UniProt **P06307**)

### 0) Target verification (critical identity check)
The UniProt accession **P06307** corresponds to **human cholecystokinin (CCK)**, produced as **preprocholecystokinin (preproCCK)** and processed to multiple bioactive peptides (e.g., CCK-58/33/22/8/5), consistent with membership in the **gastrin/cholecystokinin family** and the expected peptide-hormone biology. This matches the user-provided UniProt description (CCK precursor with multiple processed products) and aligns with contemporary review and primary literature descriptions of the human CCK locus and peptides. (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 3-6, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologya pages 1-3, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 1-3)

### 1) Key concepts, definitions, and current understanding

#### 1.1 What CCK is (molecular function and products)
**CCK is a secreted peptide signaling precursor**, not an enzyme or transporter. The **CCK gene** encodes **preproCCK**, which is post-translationally cleaved and amidated into multiple mature peptides including **CCK-58, CCK-33, CCK-22, CCK-8, and CCK-5**. (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 3-6, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologya pages 1-3, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 1-3)

A central structure–function concept is **tyrosyl O-sulfation** at the **7th residue from the C-terminus**, which strongly increases affinity for **CCK1R/CCKAR** (but is less important for CCK2R/CCKBR). (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 3-6, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologya pages 3-6)

#### 1.2 Where CCK is made and where it acts (cellular and subcellular localization)
CCK is produced by **intestinal enteroendocrine I-cells** (classically duodenum/jejunum) and by **neurons/glia in the CNS**; production is also noted in **thyroid C-cells** in some contexts. CCK is synthesized as a precursor in regulated secretory cells and released extracellularly as a peptide hormone/neuropeptide. (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 3-6, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologya pages 3-6)

At its sites of action, CCK binds **cell-surface GPCRs** (**CCK1R/CCKAR** and **CCK2R/CCKBR**) located on target cells (e.g., **vagal afferents, gallbladder smooth muscle, pancreatic acinar cells, gastric mucosa, and CNS neurons**). (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 3-6, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologya pages 3-6)

#### 1.3 Receptors, ligand preferences, and signaling pathways
Two primary receptor subtypes mediate CCK biology:

- **CCK1R (CCKAR; “CCK-A receptor”)**: preferentially binds **sulfated CCK** peptides and couples mainly to **Gq → PLC → IP3/Ca2+** (with context-dependent additional coupling described). It is expressed in tissues relevant to digestion and satiation including **gallbladder smooth muscle, pancreatic acinar cells, and vagal afferents**. (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 3-6, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologya pages 3-6)

- **CCK2R (CCKBR; “CCK-B/gastrin receptor”)**: recognizes the shared **C-terminal tetrapeptide** motif of **CCK and gastrin** with sulfation relatively less critical; enriched in **brain and gastric mucosa**; also signals largely via **Gq/PLC** with downstream engagement of MAPK/PI3K-related pathways depending on context. (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 3-6, li2025advancesinradiopharmaceuticals pages 4-6)

Physiologically, CCK1R activation supports **gallbladder contraction**, **pancreatic enzyme secretion**, **slowing of gastric emptying**, and **satiety signaling (notably via vagal afferents)**. (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 3-6, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologya pages 3-6)

### 2) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

#### 2.1 Receptor structural biology and membrane–lipid modulation (2024)
A 2024 *PLOS Biology* study showed that **membrane cholesterol** can change **CCK1R conformational dynamics** and thereby influence ligand binding and G-protein coupling; importantly, static cryo-EM structures may not capture the dynamic events driving altered stimulus–response coupling. The work provides deposited structural resources (PDB/EMDB) and quantitative binding kinetics. (harikumar2024cholesteroldependentdynamicchanges pages 1-2, harikumar2024cholesteroldependentdynamicchanges pages 5-8)

Quantitatively, they report high-resolution cryo-EM structures of cholesterol-sensitivity variants of human CCK1R in complex with **CCK-8** (2.51–2.59 Å), and fluorescence-polarization kinetic parameters such as **Kon ~2.4×10^8 M^-1 min^-1, Koff ~1.5 min^-1, pKi ~8.1** for WT CCK1R (with variant-specific changes). (harikumar2024cholesteroldependentdynamicchanges pages 5-8)

Interpretation: these findings support a modern view that **CCK1R pharmacology is state- and membrane-context dependent**, which is important for designing drugs that preserve satiety effects while minimizing adverse GI effects (e.g., nausea/colic), a theme also emphasized in clinical/translational reviews. (harikumar2024cholesteroldependentdynamicchanges pages 5-8, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 6-9)

#### 2.2 Enteroendocrine cell (EEC) single-cell atlases and sensors controlling CCK secretion (2024)
A 2024 single-cell atlas of mouse enteroendocrine cells profiled **20,006 EECs** and identified **10 major clusters**, reinforcing that many EECs co-express multiple hormones and sensory GPCR repertoires. A notable mechanistic finding is that **Mc4r was expressed in 55% of Cck-expressing cells in the upper small intestine**, and **MC4R agonism stimulated CCK secretion** in primary intestinal cultures, providing functional validation of a gut-intrinsic melanocortin-to-CCK secretory axis. (smith2024singlecelltranscriptomicatlas pages 1-2)

Complementing this, a 2024 analysis of the human Gut Cell Atlas highlighted that in primary human intestine, **CCK expression is highest in I cells** (with broader multi-hormone co-expression), and that only a small subset of cells in large atlases are annotated EECs (e.g., **~430,000 intestinal cells with 830 EECs** in the referenced dataset), emphasizing the rarity of the CCK-producing population in unbiased tissue sampling. (hysenaj2024singlecelltranscriptomeanalysis pages 4-5, hysenaj2024singlecelltranscriptomeanalysis pages 5-8)

A 2024 *Science* paper outlines a strategy for **deep transcriptomic profiling** of human EECs (notably using CD200 as a pan-EEC marker) and functional sensor interrogation via receptor loss-of-function and ligand-induced secretion assays, framing a drug-discovery approach focused on **stimulating endogenous EEC secretion** rather than only administering hormone mimetics. While the excerpt does not provide CCK-specific sensor knockouts or quantitative CCK secretion outputs, it is authoritative context for current human EEC sensor mapping approaches relevant to regulating hormones including CCK. (beumer2024descriptionandfunctional pages 1-2, beumer2024descriptionandfunctional pages 2-4)

#### 2.3 CNS circuit-level functional insights (2024)
A 2024 *Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience* study directly tested the function of **CCK-expressing neurons in the ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH)** using DREADD silencing and diphtheria toxin-mediated ablation. Acute chemogenetic inhibition reduced firing and increased food intake (e.g., daily intake increased from **3.125±0.135 g** to **3.693±0.143 g** with CNO 3 mg/kg), while chronic ablation caused progressive weight gain, altered meal patterns (larger meals), and impaired glucose homeostasis (e.g., fasting glucose **8.475±0.50 vs 6.520±0.55 mmol/L**). (eftychidis2024cholecystokininexpressingneuronsof pages 7-9, eftychidis2024cholecystokininexpressingneuronsof pages 11-12)

Interpretation: alongside classical peripheral I-cell CCK satiety signaling, these data indicate that **central CCK-expressing neuronal populations can be causal regulators of feeding behavior and glucose regulation**, supporting a dual peripheral–central CCK axis in energy homeostasis. (eftychidis2024cholecystokininexpressingneuronsof pages 9-11, eftychidis2024cholecystokininexpressingneuronsof pages 11-12)

### 3) Current applications and real-world implementations

#### 3.1 Established diagnostic implementation: sincalide-stimulated cholescintigraphy (HIDA)
In clinical nuclear medicine, **sincalide** (synthetic **CCK-8**) is used to provoke gallbladder contraction during HIDA scans to estimate **gallbladder ejection fraction (GBEF)**. A detailed protocol summarized in a 2024 clinical review specifies **IV sincalide 0.02 µg/kg infused over 60 minutes**, with a commonly used normal cutoff **GBEF ≥38%**; shorter 30-minute protocols have reported cutoffs around **~29%**, but standardization remains an active issue and reporting should include dose and timing. (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologyb pages 3-6, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologya pages 3-6)

#### 3.2 Emerging oncology imaging/radiotheranostics: CCK2R targeting
**CCK2R is overexpressed in >90% of medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) cells**, supporting a receptor-targeted nuclear medicine strategy. A 2024 first-in-human dosimetry study of **[68Ga]Ga-DOTA-CCK-66** in **6** MTC patients reported injection of **169±19 MBq**, serial imaging (10 min, 1 h, 2 h, 4 h), and an effective dose of **4.5±0.9 mSv** per 150 MBq; lesions were detected in **2/6** patients, with highest organ absorbed doses in bladder wall and stomach. (viering2024biodistributionandradiation pages 1-2, viering2024biodistributionandradiation media de24c944)

A complementary 2024 *Journal of Nuclear Medicine* report described preclinical development and proof-of-concept human imaging (n=2) of **DOTA-CCK-66**, including quantitative receptor affinity (**IC50 3.6–6.0 nM**) and tumor uptake metrics in vivo that support clinical translation. (gunther2024preclinicalevaluationof pages 1-2)

In the clinical trial pipeline, an actively recruiting study is evaluating **68Ga-DOTA-CCK-FS PET/CT** for diagnostic performance in MTC and other CCK2R-positive tumors (planned enrollment **30**, typical activity **~1.8–2.2 MBq/kg**, imaging **~60±5 min** post-injection). (NCT07138716 chunk 1, NCT07138716 chunk 2)

#### 3.3 Therapeutic modulation: CCK receptor antagonism (proglumide)
A Phase 1/2 clinical trial is evaluating **proglumide**, a CCK receptor antagonist, for **chronic pancreatitis**. The protocol includes **400 mg orally three times daily**, with an open-label lead-in and a randomized, triple-masked pseudo-crossover component, with **estimated enrollment 32** and endpoints including safety labs and pain measures (PROMIS Pain Interference; numeric pain scales) and pancreatic MRI. (NCT05551858 chunk 1, NCT05551858 chunk 2)

### 4) Expert opinions and analysis (authoritative synthesis)

#### 4.1 Consensus functional role (primary function)
Across modern reviews and mechanistic studies, the most supportable “primary function” annotation for human CCK is:

- **Secreted peptide-hormone/neuropeptide precursor** whose processed peptides coordinate **digestive secretion and motility** and contribute to **meal termination/satiety**, primarily via **CCK1R** on peripheral targets (gallbladder, pancreas, vagal afferents) and via **CCK2R/CCK1R signaling in the CNS** depending on circuit. (alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 3-6, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biologya pages 3-6)

#### 4.2 Translational trajectory
Recent receptor structural biology (e.g., cholesterol-dependent dynamics) argues that clinically useful CCK1R drugs may require **context-aware or biased signaling designs** rather than simple orthosteric agonism/antagonism, to decouple satiety from adverse GI effects. (harikumar2024cholesteroldependentdynamicchanges pages 5-8, alhussnaiy2024cholecystokinin(cck)biology pages 6-9)

The strongest near-term real-world translation is currently seen in **CCK2R-targeted imaging/theranostics**, where quantitative dosimetry and early patient feasibility studies are already available and recruiting trials are underway. (viering2024biodistributionandradiation pages 1-2, NCT07138716 chunk 1)
