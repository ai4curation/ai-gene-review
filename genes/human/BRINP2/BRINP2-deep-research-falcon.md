## Comprehensive research report: Human **BRINP2** (UniProt **Q9C0B6**; gene **BRINP2**; synonyms **FAM5B/DBCCR1L2/KIAA1747**)

### Executive summary
BRINP2 encodes a BRINP-family, neuronal MACPF-superfamily protein with an N-terminal signal peptide and a soluble (non-transmembrane) architecture, implying extracellular secretion rather than a classical membrane receptor/ion channel function. Current experimental evidence most strongly supports roles in (i) neuronal-developmental expression programs and (ii) suppression of cell-cycle progression when overexpressed in neural stem/progenitor contexts. In vivo, Brinp2 knockout mice show hyperactivity and sex-specific growth/behavior phenotypes, supporting neurodevelopmental relevance. However, **direct biochemical mechanism, binding partners/receptors, and definitive subcellular localization of endogenous human BRINP2 remain unresolved**, and recent computational receptor deorphanization failed to identify likely receptors for BRINP2. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 3-4, terashima2010analysisofthe pages 1-2, berkowicz2016micelackingbrinp2 pages 6-7, danneskioldsamsøe2023rapidandaccurate pages 13-17)

### 1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

#### 1.1 Gene/protein identity verification (mandatory)
The target gene symbol **BRINP2** corresponds to **BMP/retinoic acid-inducible neural-specific protein 2** and is also widely referenced as **FAM5B** in the literature; it belongs to the **BRINP family (BRINP1–3)** discussed together with astrotactins as part of the **neuronal MACPF gene family**. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 1-2, berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 3-4)

#### 1.2 What “MACPF superfamily” implies for BRINP2 function
MACPF (Membrane Attack Complex/Perforin) proteins are defined by a conserved fold used in pore-forming and membrane-interaction biology in immune effectors and other systems. Reviews emphasize that vertebrate BRINPs are **neurodevelopmentally expressed MACPF proteins**, but that their **molecular mechanism is not yet established** in the way immune MACPFs are. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 1-2, berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 6-7)

#### 1.3 Domain architecture and secretion model
A key review summarizes BRINP family architecture as containing an **N-terminal cleavable signal peptide**, a **MACPF domain**, and a **putative IGF-like domain**, followed by a cysteine-rich C-terminus; importantly, BRINPs are **predicted to lack transmembrane domains**, implying they are **soluble/secreted glycoproteins** rather than membrane-embedded receptors/transporters. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 3-4)

Visual evidence for this model (schematic domain map including BRINP2) is available from the extracted figure. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe media 5e61d041)

### 2) Expression, localization, and regulation

#### 2.1 Developmental and regional expression (nervous system)
Rodent Brinp2 expression is reported as **detectable from embryonic ~E11/E11.5**, with postnatal expression **peaking around P10–P14/P14** and becoming regionally restricted in adult brain. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 6-7, berkowicz2017theroleof pages 52-55, berkowicz2016micelackingbrinp2 pages 1-2)

Adult regional patterns described in detailed summaries include enrichment in **cortex (layers II–IV)**, **hippocampus**, **cerebellar Purkinje cell layer**, and **olfactory bulb granule cell layer**, with little/no expression in brainstem in those datasets. (berkowicz2017theroleof pages 52-55, berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 7-7)

#### 2.2 Cell-type bias and lineage specificity
In ES-cell derived neural stem cell models, BRINP2 (with BRINP1/3) is induced with neuronal differentiation conditions and is largely lost with astrocytic differentiation, consistent with a neuron-associated developmental program. (terashima2010analysisofthe pages 1-2)

#### 2.3 Retinoic acid (RA) and BMP inducibility: context-dependent regulation
Although the family name reflects BMP/RA-inducibility, BRINP2 regulation appears context-dependent.

* In mouse ES-derived neural stem cells differentiating to neurons in the presence of **retinoic acid (RA) and BDNF**, BRINP-family transcripts (including BRINP2) are induced, peaking at **day 3**. (terashima2010analysisofthe pages 1-2)
* In superior cervical ganglion (SCG) neurons, Brinp2 was reported **not** to be induced by BMP/RA and instead decreased during differentiation (present in undifferentiated neurons). (berkowicz2017theroleof pages 52-55)

#### 2.4 Subcellular localization: limited and inconsistent evidence
A key limitation for functional annotation is that **endogenous BRINP2 protein localization has not been established** in the cited literature summaries; reported localization patterns for BRINP2 are based on **C-terminal EGFP-tagged overexpression constructs**, with cytoplasmic localization in PC12 cells but intra-nuclear/ER localization in NIH3T3 cells, which could reflect artifacts, cell-type differences, or trafficking changes. (berkowicz2017theroleof pages 52-55)

### 3) Primary function: what BRINP2 does (evidence-based)

#### 3.1 Enzymatic/transport function?
No evidence in the retrieved sources supports BRINP2 being an enzyme, transporter, or canonical receptor with a known substrate/ligand. Instead, BRINP2 is treated as a secreted MACPF-domain protein with unclear extracellular interaction partners. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 3-4, danneskioldsamsøe2023rapidandaccurate pages 13-17)

#### 3.2 Cell-cycle restraint in neural stem/progenitor contexts
The most direct functional evidence for BRINP2-family activity is suppression of cell-cycle progression when overexpressed.

In proliferating mouse ES cell-derived neural stem cells, baseline cell-cycle distribution was reported as **69.2% G1, 26.1% S, 7.7% G2**. Overexpression of BRINP family members (including BRINP2 by study design) reduced the **S-phase fraction from 26.1% to 11.8–13.8%** and abolished detectable **G2-phase** cells, consistent with strong cell-cycle arrest/slowdown. (terashima2010analysisofthe pages 1-2)

A separate synthesis notes that overexpression of Brinp2 (with Brinp1/3) reduced G1→S transition in non-neuronal lines, although it cautions that overexpression artifacts may confound interpretation. (berkowicz2017theroleof pages 52-55)

#### 3.3 In vivo phenotypes support neurodevelopmental relevance
In knockout mouse models, Brinp2 loss is associated with behavioral phenotypes consistent with neurodevelopmental disorders.

Key quantitative findings include:
* **Male Brinp2−/− mice** show reduced body mass from ~7 weeks onward and ~**10% lower mass at week 12**, and increased velocity in the elevated plus maze consistent with **hyperactivity** (without increased open-arm preference). (berkowicz2016micelackingbrinp2 pages 6-7)
* **Female Brinp2−/− mice** showed normal weight and improved rotarod performance in the cited excerpt. (berkowicz2016micelackingbrinp2 pages 6-7)
* Viability/cohort metrics: in a breeding context, Brinp2−/− mice were obtained at **9/50 (22.5%)**, with **mean litter size 6.4 ± 2.9 (SD)**; triple knockouts were rare/poorly viable (**3/51; 5.9%**) across three breeders over 5 months. (berkowicz2016micelackingbrinp2 pages 4-6, berkowicz2016micelackingbrinp2 pages 6-7)

### 4) Pathways and interaction partners: current evidence and expert analysis

#### 4.1 Putative extracellular signaling—receptors remain unknown
A 2023 AlphaFold-based ligand–receptor “deorphanization” study screened orphan secreted ligands against a receptor library and used a **penalized ipTM > 0.6** heuristic threshold for candidate interactions. The study explicitly states: **“For BRINP2 and FGL2 we find no likely receptors.”** This is important negative evidence, suggesting that if BRINP2 signals extracellularly, it may do so through receptors not represented in the library, via multi-component complexes, via non-single-pass receptors, or via non-receptor mechanisms (e.g., ECM interactions). (danneskioldsamsøe2023rapidandaccurate pages 13-17)

#### 4.2 Expert synthesis: neurodevelopmental MACPFs with unresolved mechanism
Authoritative review literature frames BRINP proteins as neurodevelopmentally expressed MACPF-family members and highlights genetic links between BRINP/ASTN loci and neuropsychiatric/neurodevelopmental disorders, while acknowledging that BRINP molecular roles are not yet as mechanistically nailed down as those for astrotactins in neuronal migration. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 1-2, berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 6-7)

### 5) Disease associations, applications, and real-world implementations

#### 5.1 Neurodevelopmental and psychiatric genetics (candidate gene role)
Expert syntheses place BRINP2 at the **ASTN1/BRINP2 locus (1q25.2)** implicated by human genetics in neurodevelopmental phenotypes, including ADHD and CNVs reported in neurodevelopmental disorder cohorts. A key caveat repeatedly emphasized is that ASTN1 and BRINP2 are adjacent, so CNV/GWAS attribution is not clean. (berkowicz2017theroleof pages 55-60, berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 6-7)

A structured disease-association platform (Open Targets) reports GWAS-based genetic association evidence linking BRINP2 to **schizophrenia**, **autism spectrum disorder**, **major depressive disorder**, **cataract**, and **blood coagulation disease**; these are primarily association-level and require direct inspection of the cited GWAS papers for effect sizes and mechanistic interpretation. (OpenTargets Search: -BRINP2)

**Real-world implementation:** at present, BRINP2’s most common applied use is in **variant interpretation and gene prioritization** pipelines (CNV/GWAS annotation) rather than direct clinical testing or therapeutic targeting. (berkowicz2017theroleof pages 55-60, OpenTargets Search: -BRINP2)

#### 5.2 Cancer/transcriptomic models (exploratory biomarker context)
A 2024 study of **Helicobacter pylori infection in gastric cancer** identified BRINP2 among seven “HP-associated genes” and validated upregulation by RT-qPCR in **SGC-7901** cells infected with two H. pylori strains (MOI 100:1, 24 h), reporting **P < 0.05** for BRINP2 upregulation (fold-change for BRINP2 not provided in the excerpt). BRINP2 also appeared as a hub gene in a PPI network derived from survival-associated genes; the reported network size was **82 nodes and 265 edges**. (ou2024helicobacterpyloriinfection pages 10-12, ou2024helicobacterpyloriinfection pages 7-10)

**Real-world implementation:** this type of evidence supports BRINP2 as a candidate marker within research-stage prognostic/immune-correlation modeling, not as a clinically validated biomarker. (ou2024helicobacterpyloriinfection pages 10-12, ou2024helicobacterpyloriinfection pages 7-10)

### 7) Limitations and evidence gaps (important for functional annotation)

1. **Human BRINP2-specific mechanistic studies are scarce** in the retrieved corpus; much of the functional and expression inference comes from mouse systems and BRINP-family generalization. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 1-2, terashima2010analysisofthe pages 1-2)
2. **Endogenous BRINP2 localization** is not well established; existing localization observations are primarily from overexpression constructs with conflicting localization patterns. (berkowicz2017theroleof pages 52-55)
3. **Receptor/interaction partners** remain unidentified; computational screening in 2023 found no likely receptors, which narrows hypotheses but does not resolve mechanism. (danneskioldsamsøe2023rapidandaccurate pages 13-17)
4. Many disease links are **association-level** (GWAS/CNV) with locus ambiguity due to proximity to **ASTN1**, requiring careful fine-mapping and functional follow-up. (berkowicz2017neurodevelopmentalmacpfsthe pages 6-7, OpenTargets Search: -BRINP2)
