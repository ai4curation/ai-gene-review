Gene Description

ATF4 (Activating Transcription Factor 4) is a bZIP transcription factor that serves as the central effector of the Integrated Stress Response (ISR). Under basal conditions, ATF4 translation is suppressed by upstream open reading frames (uORFs) in its 5'UTR. Upon cellular stress (ER stress, amino acid deprivation, oxidative stress, mitochondrial stress), phosphorylation of eIF2alpha by stress-sensing kinases (PERK, GCN2, HRI, PKR) leads to preferential translation of ATF4. In the nucleus, ATF4 heterodimerizes with other bZIP transcription factors (CHOP/DDIT3, C/EBP family members, CREB, ATF3) via its leucine zipper domain and binds to cAMP response elements (CRE) and C/EBP-ATF response elements (CARE/AARE) to activate transcription of stress-responsive genes involved in amino acid metabolism (ASNS, SLC7A11), redox homeostasis, autophagy, and apoptosis. ATF4 also plays non-stress roles in osteoblast differentiation and bone development.

Core Functions

ATF4 is a bZIP transcription factor that binds to cAMP response elements (CRE) and C/EBP-ATF response elements (CARE/AARE) as homo- or heterodimers to activate transcription of stress-responsive genes including ASNS, CHOP/DDIT3, GRP78/BiP, TRB3, ATF3, and genes involved in amino acid metabolism and redox homeostasis.

Molecular Function:
DNA-binding transcription activator activity, RNA polymerase II-specific

ATF4 forms heterodimers with other bZIP transcription factors via its leucine zipper domain, including CHOP/DDIT3, C/EBP family members (CEBPB, CEBPG), CREB1, ATF1, ATF3, and other bZIP partners. The choice of dimerization partner determines target gene specificity and whether the outcome is adaptive or pro-apoptotic.

Molecular Function:
protein heterodimerization activity

The specific ATF4 heterodimer that forms substantially influences both the set of target genes activated and the magnitude of transcriptional response[11][18][28]. For instance, ATF4-CHOP heterodimers are particularly effective at activating pro-apoptotic genes during prolonged ER stress[11][18], while ATF4-C/EBPŒ≤ heterodimers preferentially regulate amino acid metabolic genes during nutrient stress[9]. ATF4-ATF3 heterodimers were found to bind and upregulate the pro-apoptotic protein NOXA/PMAIP1[10][18].

The CREB3L2-ATF4 heterodimer has recently emerged as particularly important in neurodegenerative disease contexts, specifically in Alzheimer's disease pathogenesis[26]. In this pathway, amyloid-beta 42 (AŒ≤42), an early trigger of Alzheimer's disease pathogenesis, potentiates the heterodimerization of CREB3L2 with ATF4[26]. Using a novel chemogenetic methodology termed ChIPmera (developed to resolve the genomic binding patterns of specific dimeric transcription factors), researchers demonstrated that the CREB3L2-ATF4 heterodimer regulates a transcription network that interacts with approximately half of the genes differentially expressed in Alzheimer's disease, including genes associated with Œ≤-amyloid and tau neuropathologies[26]. Importantly, this heterodimer triggers characteristic cellular features of Alzheimer's disease including tau hyperphosphorylation, a primary driver of neurodegeneration in the condition[26].

Interaction with Co-regulatory Proteins

Beyond heterodimerization with other bZIP transcription factors, ATF4 physically interacts with several non-bZIP proteins that modulate its function[11][28][57]. The pseudokinase TRIB3 directly interacts with ATF4 and inhibits its transcriptional activity, as well as that of CHOP and C/EBPŒ≤[18][28]. The chromatin architecture regulator CTCF has been identified as an ATF4-interacting protein, and this interaction may confer additional plasticity to the genome to facilitate large-scale reorganization of the transcriptome during stress[18][28]. In neurons, the DISC1 (disrupted in schizophrenia 1) protein binds ATF4 and inhibits its transcription factor activity by disrupting ATF4 dimerization and DNA-binding capability[21][28]. This interaction appears particularly important for regulating synaptic plasticity and long-term memory, as DISC1 acts as a regulator of synaptic functions by preventing ATF4-mediated transcription[21]. The DAPK3 (death-associated protein kinase 3) protein also interacts with ATF4, with evidence suggesting that ATF4 dimerization with DAPK3 prevents DAPK3 homodimerization and thereby inhibits its kinase activity-dependent apoptosis induction[18].

Cellular Localization and Nuclear Dynamics

Subcellular Distribution and Nuclear Trafficking

ATF4 functions exclusively as a nuclear transcription factor, requiring proper trafficking to the nucleus to execute its transcriptional roles[14][17][21]. A nuclear localization sequence (NLS) has been identified within the C-terminal region of the ATF4 protein[14]. Early observations suggested that endogenous ATF4 undergoes rapid translocation to the nucleus following synthesis during stress conditions[14]. However, subsequent experimentation revealed that ATF4 trafficking to the nucleus does not require additional signals from the integrated stress response pathway itself‚Äîectopically expressed ATF4 protein accumulates predominantly in the nuclear compartment even in the absence of stress and without activation of the eIF2Œ± phosphorylation cascade[14]. This indicates that the NLS within ATF4 is sufficient for constitutive nuclear localization, and that the stress-responsive element in ATF4 regulation is primarily translational rather than translocation-dependent[14]. The nuclear localization of ATF4 appears to occur rapidly, as most newly synthesized ATF4 protein localizes to the nucleus within minutes of synthesis[14]. ATF4 associates with chromatin and exists within protein-containing complexes and nuclear speck structures, indicating that it maintains stable interactions with other transcriptional and regulatory machinery[17][24][28].

Nuclear Dynamics During Stress

During amino acid starvation or ER stress, ATF4 binds to the promoters and enhancers of target genes with kinetics that reflect the stress intensity and duration[9]. ChIP analysis demonstrated that ATF4 binding to the ASNS gene promoter peaks at approximately 4-6 hours of amino acid starvation, with binding gradually declining between 4 and 24 hours as feedback regulatory proteins including ATF3, C/EBPŒ≤, and CHOP accumulate and compete for binding sites[9]. Circadian regulation represents an additional layer of ATF4 nuclear dynamics‚ÄîATF4 binds in a circadian time-dependent manner to CRE elements in the promoters of the core clock component PER2 and the serotonin transporter SLC6A4, periodically activating their transcription in a time-of-day-dependent manner[2][21][24].

Role in Cellular Adaptation and Metabolic Processes

Amino Acid Metabolism and Synthetic Capacity

ATF4 serves as the master regulator of amino acid biosynthesis and cellular amino acid homeostasis during nutrient stress[2][8][9]. During amino acid deprivation, ATF4 upregulates expression of amino acid biosynthetic enzymes and amino acid transporter proteins to increase intracellular amino acid availability[2][8][9]. The asparagine synthetase (ASNS) gene represents the paradigmatic example, with ATF4 binding to AARE sequences and robustly activating transcription in response to amino acid limitation[9]. In amino acid-deprived cancer cells or tumors in nutrient-poor microenvironments, ATF4-dependent ASNS upregulation maintains cellular asparagine levels, enabling continued protein synthesis and cell survival[9][35]. Similarly, ATF4 directly activates transcription of amino acid transporter genes from multiple transport systems, increasing the cellular capacity to capture available amino acids from the extracellular environment[2][42]. The ATF4-dependent amino acid transporter reprogramming has been particularly well-characterized in the context of tryptophan deprivation, where ATF4 upregulates expression of SLC1A5, SLC7A11, and SLC1A4 to enhance transport of glutamine, cystine, and other amino acids that can compensate for tryptophan limitation[39].

Oxidative Stress Response and Redox Homeostasis

Beyond amino acid metabolism, ATF4 is crucial for cellular adaptation to oxidative stress through upregulation of antioxidant genes and redox-balancing pathways[2][15][21]. ATF4 transcriptionally activates the glutathione biosynthetic pathway by inducing expression of multiple enzymes including cystathionine Œ≥-lyase (CTH) and glutamate-cysteine ligase catalytic subunit (GCLC)[15]. Glutathione (GSH), the product of these biosynthetic pathways, serves as the critical reducing substrate for glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4), the key enzyme protecting cells from ferroptotic cell death[15][41]. Additionally, ATF4 regulates expression of system xc‚àí amino acid transporters (SLC7A11 and SLC3A2) that mediate cystine/glutamate exchange, thereby maintaining cystine uptake which is required for glutathione synthesis[2][15][39]. This multifaceted ATF4-dependent regulation of redox homeostasis represents a critical adaptation to oxidative stress, linking amino acid transport, amino acid biosynthesis, and antioxidant enzyme expression into a coordinated transcriptional program.

Autophagy and Mitochondrial Function

ATF4 orchestrates the induction of autophagy during amino acid starvation and ER stress through direct transcriptional activation of multiple autophagy-related genes (ATG genes)[8]. The eIF2Œ±-ATF4 pathway directs a comprehensive autophagy gene transcriptional program by binding to AARE sequences in the promoters of genes including ATG9, ATG12, ATG101, and many others encoding components of the autophagosome biogenesis, elongation, and function machinery[8]. ATF4 and CHOP function cooperatively to increase transcription of the autophagy adaptor protein p62/SQSTM1, with both transcription factors binding AARE sequences in the p62 promoter[8]. The ATF4-dependent induction of autophagy during nutrient deprivation represents an adaptive response allowing cells to degrade intracellular components for amino acid recycling and energy production, supporting survival during prolonged nutrient limitation.

During ER stress, ATF4 also plays a critical role in regulating mitochondrial function and bioenergetics[55]. The PERK-eIF2Œ±-ATF4 axis specifically increases expression of supercomplex assembly factor 1 (SCAF1, also designated COX7A2L), which promotes formation of respiratory chain supercomplexes and enhances mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation[55]. This ATF4-mediated increase in mitochondrial energetic capacity appears particularly important for providing the ATP required for active unfolded protein response and protein synthesis recovery during ER stress[55]. Additionally, ATF4 activates transcription of mitochondrial chaperone proteins including the mitochondrial heat shock protein 70 (HSPA9) and proteases such as the AAA+ protease LON, which enhance mitochondrial protein quality control and proteostasis capacity[40]. Some ATF4 target genes localize to the mitochondria and regulate apoptosis signaling, including the chaperone DNAJA3/TID1 which exists in both long and short isoforms with opposing roles in apoptosis regulation[18].

Tissue-Specific and Developmental Functions

Bone Development and Osteoblast Differentiation

ATF4 plays indispensable roles in skeletal development and bone homeostasis through its expression in osteoblasts[20][50][53][56][59]. Global ATF4 deletion in mice results in severe osteopenia, impaired osteoblast terminal differentiation, reduced osteocalcin (OCN) expression, and decreased type I collagen synthesis[20][50]. The Atf4 null mouse skeleton exhibits a characteristically smaller size due to reduced cartilage growth and delayed ossification center formation[20]. Conditional genetic approaches revealed that ATF4 operates through distinct cell-autonomous functions in both chondrocytes and osteoblasts. In chondrocytes, ATF4 regulates expression of Indian hedgehog (Ihh), a paracrine signaling molecule that controls proliferation of prechondrogenic cells and stimulates osteoblast differentiation in the adjacent perichondrium[20]. Atf4 null chondrocytes exhibit reduced Ihh expression and impaired hedgehog signaling, leading to secondary defects in osteoblast differentiation and bone collar formation[20]. Through selective overexpression of Atf4 in chondrocytes of otherwise Atf4-null mice, the full bone formation phenotype can be rescued, demonstrating that chondrocyte-derived Atf4 dominates in controlling early osteogenesis during skeletal development[20].

In mature osteoblasts, ATF4 functions as a critical regulator during terminal differentiation and matrix mineralization[50][53][56][59]. ATF4 acts as a substrate for the ribosomal S6 kinase RSK2 (encoded by RPS6KA3), and this phosphorylation substantially enhances ATF4 transactivation activity and promotes expression of osteoblast-specific genes including osteocalcin (BGLAP), osteopontin (SPP1/IBSP), alkaline phosphatase (ALP), and type I collagen (COL1A1)[50][53]. Importantly, ATF4 post-transcriptionally regulates the synthesis of type I collagen, the main constituent of the bone matrix, through mechanisms that remain to be fully elucidated[50][53]. The cooperation between ATF4 and the master osteogenic transcription factor RUNX2 represents a key regulatory axis, with RUNX2 promoting expression of ATF4, which then acts downstream to amplify osteoblast differentiation signals[50][53].

ATF4 and the FOXO1 transcription factor cooperate in osteoblasts to regulate glucose homeostasis, with implications extending far beyond bone itself[56][59]. Both FoxO1 and ATF4 suppress osteocalcin activity through upregulating expression of protein tyrosine phosphatase (encoded by Esp), which inactivates osteocalcin through carboxylation[56][59]. This FoxO1/ATF4-mediated suppression of osteocalcin bioactivity decreases insulin secretion from pancreatic Œ≤-cells and reduces insulin sensitivity in liver, adipose tissue, and muscle[56][59], thereby increasing blood glucose levels and decreasing glucose tolerance[56][59]. Genetic models demonstrate that FoxO1 and ATF4 cooperate synergistically in this regulatory pathway‚Äîcompound heterozygous FoxO1^+/‚àí^;Atf4^+/‚àí^ mice with single allele deletions of both genes show metabolic phenotypes similar to complete knockouts of either factor alone[56]. This reveals an elegant cross-talk between bone and systemic energy metabolism, with osteoblast-expressed ATF4 contributing critically to whole-body glucose homeostasis.

Lens Development and Maintenance

ATF4 is essential for the survival and function of the avascular lens, which relies entirely on diffusion for nutrient delivery and depends critically on amino acid and glutathione availability[45][48]. Atf4 null embryos exhibit severe lens defects starting in late embryonic development, with complete lens disintegration observed by birth due to epithelial and/or fiber cell apoptosis[45][48]. The mechanism underlying this developmental requirement involves ATF4's role in regulating nutrient transport and biosynthesis genes critical for lens cell survival in its avascular environment[45]. RNA-sequencing analysis of Atf4 null lenses revealed enrichment for genes involved in nutrient transport, amino acid biosynthesis, and tRNA charging[45]. These changes result in marked reductions in lens free amino acid and glutathione levels, leading to diminished extractable lens protein and ultimately lens disintegration[45]. The lens epithelial cells in Atf4 null lenses show dramatically reduced proliferation rates‚Äîfewer than 20% of null lens epithelial cells are actively cycling compared to over 50% in wildtype lenses[45]. This demonstrates that ATF4, through its integrated stress response functions, is critical for mediating lens cell adaptation to the metabolic constraints imposed by avascularity and maintaining the optical transparency necessary for vision.

Hematopoiesis and Immune Function

ATF4 plays important roles in hematopoietic stem cell (HSC) homeostasis and immune cell development[23][32]. ATF4 deficiency leads to aging-like attrition of adult hematopoietic stem cells, characterized by functional defects including reduced repopulating and self-renewal capacities and myeloid differentiation bias[23]. ATF4-deficient HSCs show phenotypic expansion with increased frequencies of long-term HSCs, reminiscent of aged hematopoietic stem cell populations, indicating that ATF4 normally acts to prevent HSC aging-related alterations[23]. This ATF4-dependent regulation of HSC aging involves control of mitochondrial reactive oxygen species production through targeting HIF1Œ±[23]. Mechanistically, loss of ATF4 in HSCs leads to elevated mitochondrial ROS production, compromising the self-renewal and repopulating capacity of these cells[23]. ATF4 also regulates CD4+ T cell immune responses through metabolic reprogramming, controlling the expression of genes involved in amino acid intake, mTORC1 activation, protein translation, and de novo amino acid synthesis[32]. ATF4-deficient CD4+ T cells show defects in redox homeostasis, proliferation, differentiation, and cytokine production[32]. ATF4-deficient mice mount reduced Th1 but elevated Th17 immune responses and develop more severe experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE), a mouse model of multiple sclerosis[32]. These findings establish ATF4 as a critical regulator of both innate hematopoietic stem cell function and adaptive immune cell differentiation and function.

Role in Cancer and Metabolic Reprogramming

Amino Acid Metabolism in Cancer

ATF4 is frequently upregulated in cancer cells and tumors, and emerging evidence indicates that ATF4 plays complex roles in cancer progression through regulation of amino acid metabolism and metabolic reprogramming[22][35]. In numerous cancer types, ATF4-mediated upregulation of amino acid biosynthetic enzymes including asparagine synthetase and phosphoserine aminotransferase 1 (PSAT1) enables cancer cells to synthesize amino acids de novo when exogenous supply is limited[22][35]. This ATF4-dependent amino acid biosynthetic capacity is particularly important in nutrient-poor tumor microenvironments where certain amino acids become limiting. PSAT1 represents a critical downstream target of ATF4 in estrogen receptor-negative breast cancer, where ATF4-dependent PSAT1 upregulation enhances cell proliferation through the GSK3Œ≤/Œ≤-catenin/cyclin D1 signaling pathway[22]. In gastric cancer, ATF4 has been found to participate in cancer initiation and progression through multiple mechanisms, including direct activation of the Sonic Hedgehog pathway (through binding to the SHH promoter) and upregulation of CHAC1 glutathione-specific Œ≥ glutamylcyclotransferase to degrade glutathione and mediate ferroptosis[22].

Metabolic Adaptation and Ferroptosis Resistance

Paradoxically, while ATF4-mediated amino acid biosynthesis can support tumor growth in certain contexts, loss of ATF4 can increase susceptibility to ferroptosis and impair tumor development in others[22]. This dual role likely depends on the specific metabolic context and the particular cancer type. In hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), ATF4 directly targets SLC7A11 (encoding the xc‚àí amino acid transporter) to sustain glutathione synthesis and prevent ferroptosis-dependent cell death[22]. Therefore, ATF4 ablation could increase HCC susceptibility to ferroptosis and potentially impair tumorigenesis[22]. In glioblastoma, ATF4 modulates metabolic reprogramming, integrated stress response, and ferroptosis regulation to promote disease progression[22]. ATF4 can promote ferroptosis-mediated cell death and tumor angiogenesis in glioma, effects that are attenuated by xCT (SLC7A11) inhibition and can be further abrogated by the ferroptosis inducer erastin[22]. Thus, ATF4 ablation has been proposed as a promising target for suppressing glioma growth and vasculature by sensitizing glioma cells to ferroptosis[22].

Metabolic Cooperation and One-Carbon Pathway

In castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC), loss of the protein kinase C iota/lambda (PKCŒª/Œπ) activates mTORC1 to induce an ATF4-mediated transcriptional program, leading to metabolic adaptation of CRPC cells to elevate the flux through the one-carbon pathway[22]. This metabolic reprogramming driven by PKCŒª/Œπ loss and ATF4 activation appears to provide selective growth advantages to CRPC cells. These findings demonstrate that ATF4 functions at the hub of a complex metabolic reprogramming network in cancer, and that the outcome (tumor promotion or suppression) depends on the interplay between ATF4 activation and other metabolic signaling pathways operating in the specific cancer type.

Clinical Significance and Disease Associations

Neurodegenerative Diseases

Recent research has highlighted ATF4's role in Alzheimer's disease pathogenesis through its interaction with CREB3L2, as discussed above[26]. The CREB3L2-ATF4 heterodimer transcriptionally regulates genes involved in cellular proteostasis and protein trafficking, and dysregulation of this axis may contribute to the protein aggregation and tau hyperphosphorylation characteristic of Alzheimer's disease[26]. Additionally, ATF4 has been implicated in other neurodegenerative conditions through its role in regulating synaptic plasticity and long-term memory[21][28]. ATF4 normally acts as a repressor of synaptic plasticity, inhibiting the induction and maintenance of long-term memory through its interaction with DISC1, which disrupts ATF4 dimerization and DNA binding[21][28]. This repressive role of ATF4 in memory formation appears to be a conserved function, as ATF4 in C. elegans (where the IMPACT protein regulates ATF4) has been shown to promote lifespan extension[4][21].

Skeletal Disorders and Bone Disease

The critical role of ATF4 in osteoblast differentiation and bone homeostasis places it at the center of several skeletal disorders[50][53]. Mutations or dysregulation of ATF4 or its upstream regulators can lead to impaired bone formation and osteopenia[50][53]. The discovery that ATF4 cooperates with FOXO1 to regulate glucose homeostasis through osteoblasts has opened new perspectives on the skeletal contributions to metabolic diseases including obesity and diabetes[56][59]. Understanding ATF4 regulation in osteoblasts may provide new therapeutic opportunities for treating metabolic disorders through bone-targeted interventions.

Metabolic Diseases

ATF4's role in regulating glucose homeostasis through osteoblast expression indicates that ATF4 dysregulation may contribute to metabolic diseases[56][59]. The discovery of insulin signaling-dependent ATF4 upregulation through GSK3 inhibition suggests that ATF4 may serve as a link between nutrient sensing (via insulin signaling) and stress response pathways[11][28][52]. This positioning of ATF4 at the interface of nutrient sensing and stress responses may make it an important regulator in metabolic disease contexts where cells experience combined nutritional and metabolic stress.

Conclusion

Activating Transcription Factor 4 (ATF4) represents a masterfully evolved cellular regulatory hub that coordinates diverse aspects of mammalian physiology ranging from acute cellular stress adaptation to long-term developmental processes and systemic metabolic homeostasis. The intricate molecular mechanisms underlying ATF4 function‚Äîincluding its sophisticated translational control through upstream open reading frames, its context-dependent heterodimerization with numerous bZIP partners, its extensive post-translational modifications, and its selective target gene activation‚Äîexemplify how cells achieve remarkable specificity and plasticity in gene regulation using relatively simple molecular components. ATF4's dual functionality as both a basal metabolic regulator essential for normal development and tissue homeostasis, and a stress-responsive transcription factor capable of both cell survival and cell death decisions, demonstrates the evolutionary success of this protein as a central molecular decision point in cellular physiology. The extensive research into ATF4 function has revealed its involvement in virtually every major cellular and organismal process including amino acid metabolism, oxidative stress response, autophagy, apoptosis, development, hematopoiesis, immune function, bone homeostasis, energy metabolism, and disease pathogenesis. Future research directions include clarifying the precise molecular mechanisms underlying ATF4's role in specific diseases, understanding how ATF4 coordinates with other stress-response pathways at the systems level, developing therapeutic strategies to modulate ATF4 activity for disease treatment, and elucidating the full spectrum of ATF4 target genes and their functional relationships. The continuing discovery of new ATF4 interaction partners, target genes, and regulatory modifications indicates that despite decades of research, fundamental aspects of ATF4 biology remain to be uncovered, promising continued insights into how this elegant transcription factor orchestrates cellular adaptation and survival.
