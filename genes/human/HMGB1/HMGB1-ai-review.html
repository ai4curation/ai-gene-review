Gene Description

HMGB1 is a 215-amino acid non-histone chromatin protein containing two HMG-box DNA-binding domains (A-box and B-box) and an acidic C-terminal tail. It has dual functions: (1) Nuclear - binds and bends DNA, modulates chromatin architecture, regulates transcription, and participates in DNA repair (BER, MMR, NER) and V(D)J recombination; (2) Extracellular - acts as a danger-associated molecular pattern (DAMP)/alarmin released from necrotic or activated cells, signaling through TLR4, TLR2, RAGE, and CXCR4 (via CXCL12 heterocomplex). Its activity is redox-regulated: all-thiol HMGB1 is chemotactic, disulfide HMGB1 (C23-C45 bond) induces cytokine production, and fully oxidized HMGB1 is inactive.

Core Functions

HMGB1 binds and bends DNA through its two HMG-box domains (A and B). DNA bending is essential for its function as demonstrated by mutant studies showing that HMGB1 incapable of DNA bending fails to activate target promoters (PMID:19223331). The protein can bend DNA to form circles as small as 66 bp (PMID:8339930).

Molecular Function:
DNA binding, bending
Directly Involved In:
DNA topological change
Cellular Locations:
nucleus

HMGB1 acts as a transcriptional coregulator by binding transcription factors (p53, p73, NF-Y, TBP) and modulating their DNA binding activity. It can both positively and negatively regulate transcription depending on cellular context (PMID:8006019, PMID:19223331, PMID:11748232).

Molecular Function:
DNA-binding transcription factor binding
Directly Involved In:
positive regulation of transcription by RNA polymerase II
Cellular Locations:
nucleus

Extracellular HMGB1 acts as a DAMP/alarmin released from necrotic or activated cells, signaling through TLR4, TLR2, RAGE, and CXCR4 (via CXCL12 heterocomplex) to promote inflammation and chemotaxis. The all-thiol redox form is chemotactic, the disulfide form induces cytokine production, and the fully oxidized form is inactive.

Identity verification and core definitions
- Gene/protein identity: HMGB1 (HGNC:4983), UniProt P09429, Homo sapiens. A 215‚Äìamino-acid, non-histone chromatin protein of the HMGB family with two HMG-box DNA-binding domains (A-box ~aa9‚Äì79, B-box ~aa95‚Äì163) and an acidic C-terminal tail. Contains nuclear localization signals NLS1 (aa28‚Äì44) and NLS2 (aa179‚Äì185). Functions as an architectural chromatin factor in the nucleus and a prototypical alarmin/DAMP extracellularly (structure-function and domain mapping supported in 2024‚Äì2020 sources) (yuan2024hmgb1asan pages 1-3, lv2024multiplefunctionsof pages 1-2, datta2024highmobilitygroup pages 1-2, starkova2023structureandfunctions pages 1-2). Primary functions and localization
- Nuclear: Binds and bends DNA, modulates nucleosome structure, assists transcription, replication, and DNA damage repair; interacts with histones (including H1) and can facilitate nucleosome sliding and access (datta2024highmobilitygroup pages 1-2, starkova2023structureandfunctions pages 1-2). URL: https://doi.org/10.3390/cells13231946; https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms24098334.
- Cytoplasmic/organelle: Shuttles to cytoplasm (regulated by PTMs), localizes to mitochondria/lysosomes; interacts with Beclin-1 to promote autophagy; participates in inflammasome/pyroptosis pathways (datta2024highmobilitygroup pages 1-2, passali2025hmgb1asa pages 2-4). URL: https://doi.org/10.3390/cells13231946; https://doi.org/10.3390/jcm14155392.
- Extracellular: Released passively by necrotic cells or actively via non-classical secretion; acts as a DAMP/alarmin engaging TLR2/4, RAGE, or forms a chemotactic heterocomplex with CXCL12 to signal via CXCR4 (passali2025hmgb1asa pages 2-4, yuan2024hmgb1asan pages 1-3, fan2024hmgb1rageaxisin pages 1-2). Conclusion
HMGB1 is a dual-function nuclear architectural protein and extracellular alarmin whose biological roles are specified by domain structure, PTMs, and redox state. Recent advances clarify: (i) endogenous proteolytic control via complement C1s generates a potent antagonist fragment; (ii) nuclear mechanisms by which HMGB1 enhances chromatin dynamics even in the presence of linker histone H1; and (iii) mass-spec approaches that quantify redox isoforms in tumors, exposing actionable CXCL12/HMGB1‚ÄìCXCR4 signaling. Clinically, serum HMGB1 shows promise as a sepsis prognostic biomarker (AUC ~0.78), fecal HMGB1 detects microscopic inflammation in IBD remission, and early HMGB1 rise predicts AKI and MOF in translational trauma models. Therapeutically, direct HMGB1 inhibition (e.g., glycyrrhizin), use of antagonist fragments (A‚Äëbox/F3), RAGE inhibition, and disruption of the CXCL12/HMGB1 heterocomplex represent leading strategies, with further work needed to standardize isoform-specific assays and validate interventions in clinical trials (lorvellec2023hmgb1cleavageby pages 13-14, palone2016fecalhmgb1reveals pages 1-2, pirani2024tumorcellsexpress pages 1-2, jiang2024correlationofserum pages 2-4, yang2024anearlyhmgb1 pages 1-2).
