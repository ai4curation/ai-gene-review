# Manual literature synthesis for human GLIPR1L1 (Q6UWM5)

## Scope and evidence boundary

The configured automated deep-research providers were unavailable on 2026-07-18 (Falcon/Edison HTTP 402; Perplexity fallback HTTP 401). This manual synthesis uses the repository-cached UniProt record and six primary papers. Direct human evidence establishes sequence, isoforms, precursor processing, and strong male-reproductive-tract expression. Functional and detailed localization assignments are inferred primarily from mouse and bovine orthologs.

## Biological synthesis

Human GLIPR1L1 encodes a 242-aa member of the CAP/CRISP superfamily. The protein has an N-terminal signal peptide, an extracellular CAP domain, a predicted C-terminal GPI-anchor signal, and two splice isoforms. GLIPR1L1 is strongly testis enriched; a later study also reports expression in human testis and epididymis. [PMID:16714093, "GLIPR1L1 is testis-specific"] [PMID:40572022, "are highly expressed in mouse and human testis and epididymis"]

Mouse experiments provide the clearest functional mechanism. GLIPR1L1 occupies high-molecular-weight sperm complexes containing IZUMO1, and reciprocal immunoprecipitation confirms their interaction. The complex is maturation dependent and is associated with sperm membrane rafts. After acrosomal exocytosis, GLIPR1L1 loss sharply reduces redistribution of IZUMO1 across the sperm head, reduces progesterone-induced acrosome reaction, and lowers in-vitro fertilization efficiency without measurably reducing zona-pellucida binding. [PMID:31672133, "Collectively, these results confirm that IZUMO1 and GLIPR1L1 form a protein complex within male germ cells"] [PMID:31672133, "IZUMO1 was relocated throughout the sperm head in 85% of sperm, compared to 21% of acrosome-reacted Glipr1l1−/− sperm"]

These results support a nonenzymatic adaptor or organizer role: GLIPR1L1 helps assemble or reposition an IZUMO1-containing sperm-surface complex during acrosome remodeling and thereby supports optimal sperm–egg fusion. The evidence does not establish GLIPR1L1 as a direct fusogen. The source paper notes that its precise molecular function remains unknown and that complex composition is incomplete.

Bovine studies independently establish GLIPR1L1 on the sperm plasma membrane and in lipid rafts, with a GPI anchor, and identify it in CD9-positive epididymal microvesicles. [PMID:22552861, "GliPr1L1 was associated with the sperm plasma membrane preparation"] [PMID:23785420, "P25b, GliPr1L1, and MIF were highly expressed in CD9-positive microvesicles"] The epididymosome route should not automatically be transferred to human because mouse and bovine orthologs use different routes to acquire their sperm-surface pool.

## Strength and limitations

The case for conserved male-reproductive function is strengthened by human testis enrichment, orthology, shared CAP-family architecture, a human signal peptide and predicted GPI anchor, and concordant mouse/bovine sperm-surface localization. However, direct experiments on endogenous human GLIPR1L1 localization, IZUMO1 interaction, acrosome remodeling, and fertilization are absent.

The phenotype is modulatory rather than essential. Mouse single-gene deletion compromises acrosome reaction and IVF but leaves natural litter size unchanged. A later deletion of all three neighboring Glipr1-like genes also leaves natural fertility, sperm morphology, and motility intact. [PMID:31672133, "Glipr1l1 knockout males sired litters of equivalent size to that of WT control males"] [PMID:40572022, "depletion of all three genes does not significantly affect male fertility"] Thus GLIPR1L1 participates in optimal acrosomal/fertilization performance but is neither an indispensable male-fertility gene nor a demonstrated direct membrane-fusion catalyst.

## Curation-ready conclusion

The core inferred human activity is molecular adaptor activity at acrosomal/sperm-head membrane rafts as part of an IZUMO1-containing adhesion complex, directly contributing to IZUMO1 localization during the acrosome reaction and supporting sperm–egg plasma-membrane fusion. Extracellular, plasma-membrane, membrane-raft, and acrosomal-vesicle locations are well supported by orthology and human sequence features. The sperm head-tail coupling apparatus is a credible additional orthologous location. Epididymosome localization and sperm–zona binding should remain contextual rather than core human assertions.
