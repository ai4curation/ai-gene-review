Gene Description

PRDX4 encodes peroxiredoxin-4, a typical 2-Cys thioredoxin-dependent peroxidase that reduces hydrogen peroxide and organic hydroperoxides. It is cotranslationally translocated to and retained mainly in the endoplasmic reticulum, with a smaller cytoplasmic fraction that has been linked to redox modulation of NF-kappaB signaling. Its core annotation is thioredoxin-dependent peroxiredoxin/peroxidase activity in peroxide detoxification and redox homeostasis, not generic protein binding.

Comprehensive Functional-Annotation Research Report: Human PRDX4 (Peroxiredoxin-4; UniProt Q13162)

0) Target identity verification (critical disambiguation)

The UniProt accession Q13162 corresponds to Homo sapiens PRDX4, whose recommended name is Peroxiredoxin-4 (PrxIV), a typical 2-Cys peroxiredoxin that is primarily localized to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and can also be secreted/extracellular. This identity is directly supported by a 2024 human proteomics study listing PRDX4 = Q13162 (giuseppe2024changesinducedby pages 8-10) and by a PRDX4-focused review describing human PRDX4/Prx4 as an ER-localized typical 2-Cys peroxiredoxin and discussing its specific isoforms and ER targeting (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 1-2, thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4).

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Peroxiredoxins and “typical 2-Cys” peroxiredoxins

Peroxiredoxins (PRDXs) are thiol-based peroxidases that reduce peroxides using catalytic cysteine residues. PRDX1–4 are classified as typical 2-Cys PRDXs, forming an intersubunit disulfide between the peroxidatic cysteine (Cp) and resolving cysteine (Cr) during catalysis (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 1-2, karpenko2021peroxiredoxins—theunderratedactors pages 2-3).

1.2 What makes PRDX4 distinct among human PRDXs

A distinguishing feature emphasized in the PRDX4 literature is its ER luminal localization (often described as the only peroxiredoxin isoform primarily localized to the ER) and its coupling to oxidative protein folding in the secretory pathway, with the additional property that it can appear in extracellular compartments (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 1-2, thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4).

2) Molecular function: enzymatic reaction, mechanism, and substrate specificity

2.1 Enzymatic activity and reaction chemistry

PRDX4 catalyzes peroxide reduction through the canonical typical 2-Cys cycle: the peroxidatic cysteine is oxidized by H2O2 to cysteine sulfenic acid (Cp–SOH); then the resolving cysteine from the partner subunit attacks to form an intersubunit disulfide, while H2O2 is reduced to water (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4). A review further notes that peroxiredoxins (including PRDX4) reduce not only hydrogen peroxide but also other peroxides such as alkyl hydroperoxides and peroxynitrite (family-level statement) (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 1-2). Experimentally, PRDX4 is clearly reactive to both H2O2 and tert-butyl hydroperoxide, as used in a mechanistic cell/biochemistry study (elko2021oxidationofperoxiredoxin4 pages 10-11).

2.2 Electron donors and recycling partners (thioredoxin/PDI-linked redox systems)

After PRDX4 forms the intersubunit disulfide, it must be reduced to return to the active peroxidatic form. For PRDX4, reduction/recycling can proceed via thioredoxin (Trx), glutathione (GSH), protein disulfide isomerase (PDI), and other ER oxidoreductases (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4). In the ER, PRDX4 is described as functionally integrated with PDI-family proteins, where PRDX4 transfers oxidizing equivalents/disulfides to PDI family members to support oxidative protein folding (moilanen2021novelregulatorymechanisms pages 50-53, ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 1-2).

2.3 Kinetics and quantitative enzymology

A PRDX4-focused review reports a second-order rate constant for PRDX4-mediated H2O2 reduction of 2.2 × 10^7 M⁻1·s⁻1, and contrasts this with much slower reactions attributed to GSH and Trx in the cited comparison, as well as a lower activity reported for another ER antioxidant (GPx8, 95 M⁻1·s⁻1) (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4). These values support PRDX4 as a highly efficient ER peroxide scavenger.

2.4 Hyperoxidation, inactivation, and functional switching (peroxidase ↔ chaperone)

At higher peroxide levels, PRDX4 can become hyperoxidized (e.g., to sulfinic and sulfonic acid forms), leading to loss of peroxidase activity; the sulfinic form can be repaired by sulfiredoxin (Srx) (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4). A 2023 review of ER stress/oxidative stress emphasizes that high H2O2 can induce superoxidation (hyperoxidation) of PRDX4, thereby inactivating it and contributing to a ROS–ER stress feedback loop (ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 5-7).

3) Cellular localization and where PRDX4 acts

3.1 ER luminal targeting and retention versus secretion

PRDX4 is localized mainly to the endoplasmic reticulum, enabled by an N-terminal region that supports translocation into the ER lumen (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4). Despite lacking the canonical KDEL ER-retention motif, PRDX4 ER localization is attributed to interactions with PDI and ERp44; perturbations of these components (e.g., ERGIC-53 knockdown, chemical chaperone exposure) can increase PRDX4 secretion, while ERp44/PDI overexpression can suppress secretion (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4). PRDX4’s presence in the extracellular environment is consistent with its reported secretion (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4, elko2021oxidationofperoxiredoxin4 pages 11-12).

3.2 Isoforms and compartment-specific implications

A testis-associated isoform, Prx4t, is described as lacking the N-terminal signal peptide and therefore localized to the cytosol rather than the ER (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4). The PRDX4/Prx4 isoform context is important for interpreting expression/localization data in tissue-specific studies.

4) Pathways and biological processes: oxidative protein folding and ER redox homeostasis

4.1 Integration into oxidative folding pathways (ERO1–PDI axis and PRDX4)

Oxidative protein folding in the ER involves PDI-family members introducing disulfides into nascent proteins. A central oxidase, ERO1, re-oxidizes PDI and in doing so generates H2O2 as a byproduct (thapa2022essentialrolesof pages 2-4). A major contemporary view is that ER-resident peroxidases (including PRDX4) use this peroxide product to both detoxify it and to re-oxidize PDIs, thereby coupling peroxide consumption to disulfide-bond formation (ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 1-2). More broadly, PRDX4 is described as an ER-resident antioxidant and oxidative folding enzyme acting in cyclic redox pathways with PDIs (ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 1-2).

4.2 ERO1-independent H2O2 supply and PRDX4-mediated folding

A mechanistic primary study shows that PRDX4 can sustain disulfide bond formation in the absence of ERO1 and provides evidence for an ER-localized, ERO1-independent source of H2O2 that fuels PRDX4-mediated disulfide bond formation (konno2015ero1independentproductionof pages 1-2). This supports PRDX4 as a flexible component of ER redox homeostasis, not solely dependent on ERO1-derived peroxide.

4.3 ER stress/UPR linkage: redox feedback loops that involve PRDX4

A 2023 review links the unfolded protein response (UPR) to oxidative stress via CHOP-driven induction of ERO1, increasing H2O2 production during oxidative folding. In this framing, high H2O2 can inactivate PRDX4 (hyperoxidation), helping sustain a vicious cycle of ER stress, Ca2+ dysregulation, and mitochondrial ROS amplification (ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 5-7).

5) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

5.1 2023: Systems-level framing of PRDX4 within ER stress and oxidative stress

Ong & Logue (Antioxidants, April 2023) synthesize evidence that PRDX4 participates in oxidative protein folding by using ERO1-generated H2O2 to help recycle PDIs and maintain ER redox balance, and they cite the ER as a major ROS source (reported as ~25% in their review) (ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 1-2). They also emphasize PRDX4 hyperoxidation/inactivation under high H2O2 in the context of ER-stress amplification (ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 5-7). URL: https://doi.org/10.3390/antiox12050981 (ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 1-2).

5.2 2024: PRDX4 in cancer-redox discussions as a compensatory ER oxidoreductase

Chen et al. (Journal of Experimental & Clinical Cancer Research, March 2024) review the ERO1α–PDI axis in cancer and explicitly list PRDX4 among oxidoreductases/peroxireductases that can compensate for ERO1α-dependent oxidative folding. They further discuss that inhibition of ERO1α can impair oxidative folding of some pro-tumoral proteins in cancer, suggesting backup systems (including PRDX4) may be insufficient/dysfunctional in tumor contexts (chen2024biologicalmechanismsand pages 3-4, chen2024biologicalmechanismsand pages 4-6). URL: https://doi.org/10.1186/s13046-024-02990-4 (chen2024biologicalmechanismsand pages 1-3).

A 2024 proteomic study of extracellular vesicles (EVs) from glioblastoma stem cells (Cells, March 2024) identifies PRDX4 (Q13162) as upregulated in EVs after P2X7 receptor stimulation (BzATP). 6) Current applications and real-world implementations

6.1 Biomarker-oriented measurements in secreted/extracellular compartments

PRDX4 is described as a secretable protein and has been investigated as a circulating/serum oxidative stress marker in the PRDX4 literature summarized in a mechanistic paper’s referenced context (elko2021oxidationofperoxiredoxin4 pages 11-12). The 2024 EV proteomics study provides an implementation example: PRDX4 is detectable and significantly modulated in tumor-derived EVs (giuseppe2024changesinducedby pages 8-10).

6.2 Targetable ER redox dependencies in cancer biology (expert synthesis)

Cancer-focused reviews emphasize ER oxidative folding systems (ERO1α/PDI axis) as potential targets and explicitly recognize PRDX4 as part of the redundant ER peroxide-handling/oxidative folding network (chen2024biologicalmechanismsand pages 3-4, chen2024biologicalmechanismsand pages 4-6). This supports the interpretation that PRDX4 may influence tumor cell fitness under ER stress and oxidative folding demands, and that tumor-specific failures of compensatory redox pathways could create vulnerabilities.

7) Expert opinions and authoritative synthesis

7.1 PRDX4 as a node coupling peroxide detoxification to productive oxidative folding

An authoritative 2023 review argues that enzymes like PRDX4 (and related ER peroxidases) are not merely “antioxidants,” but are integrated into oxidative folding cycles by using ERO1-derived peroxide as an electron acceptor to recycle oxidized PDI states (ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 1-2). This conceptual model positions PRDX4 at the intersection of redox detoxification and secretory pathway proteostasis.

7.2 PRDX4 in ER stress–oxidative stress feedback loops

The same review frames PRDX4 inactivation by hyperoxidation as a mechanism that can exacerbate ER stress under high ROS, contributing to a feed-forward loop involving CHOP→ERO1→H2O2 and mitochondrial ROS amplification (ong2023unfoldingtheinteractions pages 5-7). This highlights that PRDX4’s redox state can shift cells between adaptive and maladaptive outcomes during proteostatic stress.
