CC   -!- FUNCTION: Electroneutral antiporter that mediates the transport of
CC       adenyl nucleotides through the inner mitochondrial membrane. Originally
CC       identified as an ATP-magnesium/inorganic phosphate antiporter, it also
CC       acts as a broad specificity adenyl nucleotide antiporter. By regulating
CC       the mitochondrial matrix adenyl nucleotide pool, could adapt to
CC       changing cellular energetic demands and indirectly regulate adenyl
CC       nucleotide-dependent metabolic pathways (PubMed:15123600,
CC       PubMed:22015608). In vitro, a low activity is also observed with guanyl
CC       and pyrimidine nucleotides (PubMed:15123600). May play a role in
CC       protecting cells against oxidative stress-induced cell death, by
CC       buffering calcium levels in the mitochondrial matrix through the
CC       formation of calcium-phosphate precipitates (PubMed:22015608,
CC       PubMed:29100093). {ECO:0000269|PubMed:15123600,

CC   -!- ACTIVITY REGULATION: Activated by an increase in cytosolic calcium
CC       levels that induces a conformational change of the N-terminal
CC       regulatory domain, uncapping the channel and allowing transport
CC       (PubMed:22015608, PubMed:24332718, PubMed:26164100). When calcium
CC       levels are high, the amphipathic alpha-helix of the linker region is
CC       bound to a cleft in the N-terminal regulatory domain, allowing
CC       substrate transport by the carrier domain (PubMed:28350015). When
CC       calcium levels drop, the cleft closes and the amphipathic alpha-helix
CC       is released to bind to the carrier domain, inhibiting substrate
CC       transport (PubMed:28350015). Inhibited by bathophenanthroline,
CC       mersalyl, p-hydroxymercuribenzoate, bromcresol purple and tannic acid
CC       (PubMed:15123600). {ECO:0000269|PubMed:15123600,

CC   -!- DOMAIN: The regulatory N-terminal domain/NTD, formed of two pairs of
CC       fused calcium-binding EF-hands, binds calcium in the mitochondrial
CC       intermembrane space and regulates the antiporter activity of the

CC       terminal domain is intrinsically disordered and binds to the
CC       transmembrane domain, inhibiting the transporter activity. Binding of
CC       calcium leads to a major conformational change and abolishes the
CC       interaction with the transmembrane domain and the inhibition of the
CC       transporter activity (PubMed:24332718). {ECO:0000269|PubMed:24332718}.
