## Protein identity, topology, and expression

Human SYNPR (UniProt Q8TBG9) encodes synaptoporin, a 265-residue, four-pass membrane protein with a MARVEL domain and a cytoplasmic C-terminal tail. UniProt describes it as an "Intrinsic membrane protein of small synaptic vesicles" and calls channel activity only probable and by similarity [file:human/SYNPR/SYNPR-uniprot.txt, "Intrinsic membrane protein of small synaptic vesicles. Probable vesicular channel protein (By similarity)."].

The human cDNA study identified two splice variants and brain-restricted expression across the tested panel [PMID:12974474, "It has two splicing variants"; PMID:12974474, "specifically expressed in the brain"]. No evidence reviewed here assigns distinct functions to the two protein isoforms.

## Experimentally supported localization

The original rat study found that synaptoporin copurified with small synaptic vesicles [PMID:2206533, "copurifies with small synaptic vesicles"]. A later comparative study used immunoelectron microscopy to confirm synaptoporin/synaptophysin-II on synaptic vesicles and found that it is an N-glycosylated membrane protein in a heteromultimeric vesicle-membrane complex [PMID:8229211, "Immunoelectron microscopy confirmed that syp II is localized to synaptic vesicles"; PMID:8229211, "part of a heteromultimeric complex in synaptic vesicle membranes"]. These experiments are in rodents, but human SYNPR is highly conserved and the human study explicitly identifies the encoded product as a synaptic-vesicle membrane protein.

## Supported biological roles

Two separable roles are supported.

First, a 2024 family study directly tested synaptoporin in a heterologous reconstitution assay. Coexpression of synaptoporin with synapsin produced clusters of small vesicles, unlike several unrelated synaptic-vesicle membrane proteins. The paper concludes that this family has overlapping roles in synaptic-vesicle biogenesis, clustering, and acquisition of normal small vesicle size [PMID:38985768, "expression in COS7 cells of each of these three other proteins together with synapsin resulted in the formation of clusters of small vesicles similar in size to those generated by synaptophysin and synapsin coexpression"; PMID:38985768, "These results point to an overlapping role of these proteins in the biogenesis and clustering of SV"]. Quadruple-knockout mouse evidence establishes family redundancy rather than an indispensable SYNPR-only role.

Second, at hippocampal mossy-fiber to CA3 synapses, presynaptic synaptoporin was required for activity-dependent structural and functional homeostatic adaptation. Knockdown blocked mossy-fiber bouton and postsynaptic thorny-excrescence expansion after activity suppression, and rescue restored the response [PMID:23312519, "TE formation required presynaptic synaptoporin"; PMID:23312519, "Presynaptic synaptoporin was required for functional MF upregulation, and also provides instructive signals necessary for postsynaptic TE formation and/or growth"]. This is a circuit-specific role demonstrated in rodent neurons, not evidence that SYNPR globally regulates all synapses.

## Claims not supported as established molecular functions

The 1990 name "putative channel protein" arose from sequence homology and membrane topology. The cached paper does not report direct conductance, selectivity, flux, or reconstitution measurements for synaptoporin. Accordingly, no ion-channel molecular-function term should be assigned from this evidence.

The three GO `protein binding` rows come from proteome-scale human interactome maps. They establish high-throughput interaction context but do not define a specific molecular activity of SYNPR, and the generic GO term is uninformative. The biologically motivated synapsin relationship in PMID:38985768 should be tested directly at endogenous levels before assigning a specific binding activity.

## Synthesis

The best-supported core identity is a brain-enriched integral component of selected synaptic-vesicle membranes. Its supported roles are overlapping organization/clustering of small synaptic vesicles and a specialized contribution to mossy-fiber–CA3 homeostatic structural plasticity. Its intrinsic biochemical activity and the mechanism by which it controls vesicle size remain unresolved; the 2024 study explicitly frames this as an open question [PMID:38985768, "A critical open question to be addressed is how these tetraspanins may determine vesicle shape and whether they do so by an intrinsic property or by recruiting to vesicles other factors with broad expression in neurons and nonneuronal cells"].
