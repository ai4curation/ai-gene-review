### 1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

#### 1.1 Proglucagon as a prohormone precursor (domain concept)
Human **preproglucagon** is synthesized as a precursor containing an N‑terminal signal peptide followed by the **proglucagon** sequence encoding multiple peptide hormones. A recent authoritative synthesis describes preproglucagon as **180 amino acids** (20‑aa signal peptide + 160‑aa proglucagon) and emphasizes that **tissue-specific post‑translational processing** determines which mature peptides are produced. (gasbjerg2026proglucagonderivedpeptideshuman pages 5-6)

#### 1.2 Tissue-specific processing: PCSK2/PC2 vs PCSK1/PC1/3
A central concept for functional annotation of **GCG** is that **different prohormone convertases** cleave proglucagon in different cell types:
- In **pancreatic α-cells**, **PC2 (PCSK2)** predominates and processing mainly yields **mature glucagon** (plus GRPP and IP1). (gasbjerg2026proglucagonderivedpeptideshuman pages 5-6, gasbjerg2026proglucagonderivedpeptideshuman pages 6-7, koepke2025proglucagonprocessingin pages 51-55)
- In **intestinal L cells** and **brainstem preproglucagon neurons**, **PC1/3 (PCSK1)** predominates and generates **GLP‑1, GLP‑2, IP2, and glicentin**, with a fraction of glicentin further processed to **oxyntomodulin**. A focused discussion of human α-cells notes that intra-islet GLP‑1 production is reported, but the **precise protease(s)** mediating GLP‑1 generation in human α-cells remains **controversial**, with proposed roles for PC1/3 and possibly other enzymes (e.g., furin) in some contexts. ### 2) Molecular function, pathways, and localization

#### 2.1 Cellular localization and secretion route
Functionally, GCG encodes a **secreted prohormone** whose mature peptides are stored in and secreted from **dense-core secretory granules** in endocrine cells; mature peptides act as endocrine and paracrine ligands at class B GPCRs (e.g., GCGR, GLP‑1R, GLP‑2R). Tissue-specific processing is a defining feature of its biology. (gasbjerg2026proglucagonderivedpeptideshuman pages 5-6, koepke2025proglucagonprocessingin pages 51-55)

#### 2.2 Glucagon: primary biochemical role and signaling via GCGR
**Primary function.** Glucagon is described as a principal glucose-elevating hormone secreted by pancreatic α-cells that **acts primarily on the liver** to increase hepatic glucose production by stimulating **glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis** (and inhibiting glycogenesis). (macdonald2023metabolicmessengersglucagon pages 1-3)

**Receptor and second messenger pathways.** The glucagon receptor (**GCGR**) is a **class B GPCR** that couples mainly to **Gs and Gq**. In a recent review, GCGR signaling is described as:
- **Gs → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA/EPAC/CREB** (canonical glucagon signaling for metabolic gene programs and acute metabolic responses). (macdonald2023metabolicmessengersglucagon pages 1-3)
- **Gq → PLC → InsP3 → intracellular Ca2+ mobilization** (with more recent work highlighting hepatocyte Ca2+ signaling contributions to acute hepatic glucose production and convergent effects on CREB phosphorylation). (macdonald2023metabolicmessengersglucagon pages 1-3, zhang2023theregulationand pages 3-4)

**Physiological targets and roles beyond glycemia.** Reviews summarize glucagon actions extending into **lipid** and **amino-acid metabolism**, with glucagon suppressing de novo lipogenesis and promoting amino-acid-driven gluconeogenesis; sustained GCGR blockade or loss reduces hepatic amino-acid uptake/catabolism and is associated with **hyperaminoacidemia** and compensatory α-cell changes. (macdonald2023metabolicmessengersglucagon pages 1-3, macdonald2023metabolicmessengersglucagon pages 3-4)

**Regulation of secretion (α-cell physiology).** α-cells are electrically excitable, and glucagon secretion is regulated by glucose and nutrients through voltage-dependent ion channels and intracellular calcium; it is also strongly shaped by paracrine signals (insulin, somatostatin) and neural inputs. (macdonald2023metabolicmessengersglucagon pages 3-4, reed2024theregulationof pages 8-10)

#### 2.3 GLP‑1 (GCG-derived incretin): metabolic pathway role (evidence in corpus)
Within the retrieved corpus, GLP‑1 is emphasized chiefly as a gut/brain proglucagon-derived peptide generated by PC1/3 processing, with major therapeutic relevance through **GLP‑1 receptor agonism**. (gasbjerg2026proglucagonderivedpeptideshuman pages 5-6, gasbjerg2026proglucagonderivedpeptideshuman pages 6-7)
A Nature Metabolism review highlights that GLP‑1 exerts a clinically important **glucagonostatic** effect under normo-/hyperglycemia, potentially mediated directly at α-cells or indirectly via δ-cell somatostatin. (macdonald2023metabolicmessengersglucagon pages 6-8)

#### 2.4 GLP‑2: intestinotropic growth factor and GI physiology
GLP‑2 is described as an **intestinotropic (“proadaptive”)** peptide that promotes mucosal growth and absorptive function, with mechanisms that include effects on intestinal architecture and transport capacity. (gasbjerg2026proglucagonderivedpeptideshuman pages 24-25)
GLP‑2 also affects GI motility and secretions (e.g., reduced gastric acid secretion and slowed gastric/small-bowel emptying contributing to an ileal-brake phenotype), supporting increased mucosal contact time. (gasbjerg2026proglucagonderivedpeptideshuman pages 25-27)

### 3) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

#### 3.1 Evolving glucagon biology and α-cell pathophysiology
A 2023 Nature Metabolism review synthesizes modern views of glucagon as a metabolic messenger, emphasizing both its central hepatic role and the complexity of extrahepatic effects (some potentially indirect or pharmacological). It also highlights that therapeutic targeting of glucagon pathways includes both **GCGR antagonism** (to reduce hyperglycemia) and **GCGR agonism** as a component of multi-agonist anti-obesity strategies; importantly, it notes ongoing debate about the extent to which glucagon’s counter-regulatory role is indispensable given redundancy in other counter-regulatory systems. (macdonald2023metabolicmessengersglucagon pages 3-4, macdonald2023metabolicmessengersglucagon pages 6-8)

A 2023 Nutrients review further emphasizes the “multi-organ” view of glucagon (glucose, amino acids, lipids, energy expenditure, satiety) and discusses therapeutic innovation (co‑agonists and triple agonists) alongside safety signals observed with GCGR antagonism in humans. #### 3.2 Human outcome-trial evidence transforming the GCG-derived peptide therapeutics landscape (SELECT)
A major 2024 advance is cardiovascular- and kidney-outcome evidence for GLP‑1 receptor agonism in people with overweight/obesity and established CVD **without diabetes**:
- **SELECT (Nature Medicine, published online 25 May 2024)**: 17,604 participants randomized (8,803 semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly; 8,801 placebo). The prespecified 5‑component kidney composite occurred in **1.8% vs 2.2%** (HR **0.78**, 95% CI 0.63–0.96; P=0.02) over a median follow‑up of 182 weeks; the trial also previously reported a **20% reduction in MACE**. (https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-024-03015-5; May 2024) (colhoun2024longtermkidneyoutcomes pages 1-2, colhoun2024longtermkidneyoutcomes pages 3-4)

These trials are directly relevant to **GCG functional annotation** because they validate durable, clinically meaningful effects of pharmacologically augmenting **GLP‑1R signaling**, a pathway normally activated by the GCG-derived peptide GLP‑1.

### 4) Current applications and real-world implementations

#### 4.1 Therapeutic classes directly leveraging GCG-derived peptides

**GLP‑1 receptor agonists (GLP‑1RAs).** Semaglutide (2.4 mg weekly) in SELECT reduced MACE (HR 0.80) and improved kidney outcomes (HR 0.78 for a composite kidney endpoint) in people with obesity/overweight and CVD without diabetes, supporting use across the cardiovascular–kidney–metabolic spectrum. (colhoun2024longtermkidneyoutcomes pages 1-2, colhoun2024longtermkidneyoutcomes pages 3-4, lingvay2024semaglutideandcardiovascular pages 1-2)

**Dual GIP/GLP‑1 receptor agonist: tirzepatide.** A 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis (7 RCTs, n=4,795; 12–72 weeks) reported dose-dependent weight loss vs placebo with tirzepatide: mean differences in percent weight change of **−8.07% (5 mg), −10.79% (10 mg), and −11.83% (15 mg)**, corresponding to absolute MDs of **−7.5 kg, −11.0 kg, and −11.5 kg**, respectively; GI adverse events were most common and typically mild-to-moderate/transient. (https://doi.org/10.1007/s12020-024-03896-z; Jun 2024) (qin2024efficacyandsafety pages 1-2, qin2024efficacyandsafety pages 10-11)

**Triple agonists (GLP‑1/GIP/glucagon) and other multi-agonist strategies.** A 2024 kidney-focused review highlights the growing clinical pipeline, including **retatrutide** (triple agonist targeting GLP‑1, GIP, and glucagon receptors) and contextualizes expected multi-organ effects (weight loss, metabolic control, and potential organ protection). (abasheva2024glp1receptoragonists pages 1-2, abasheva2024glp1receptoragonists media 4d72c6ec, abasheva2024glp1receptoragonists media afb21f18)

**GLP‑2 analogs for short bowel syndrome (SBS): teduglutide and next-generation agents.** GLP‑2 analog therapy is a direct clinical implementation of the GCG-derived GLP‑2 pathway:
- A 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis (17 studies; search to 30 June 2023) reported adverse-event prevalences for teduglutide: GI side effects **~49%**, URTI **29%**, catheter-related infections **11%**, pyrexia **23%**, sepsis **22%**, with additional complication categories reported (hepatobiliary/pancreatic 11%, electrolyte imbalances 13%, dehydration 11%, etc.). It notes regulatory approvals (FDA 2012, EMA 2013) and emphasizes careful monitoring. (https://doi.org/10.12669/pjms.41.9.12604; Aug 2025) (liu2025safetyofteduglutide pages 1-2)
- A 2024 SBS meta-analysis reported efficacy signals including ~**5 L/week** reduction in parenteral nutrition volume and ~**360 kcal/day** reduction in PN calories, plus a one-third chance of weaning and a 72% chance of >20% PN volume reduction (heterogeneous evidence base). (https://doi.org/10.21203/rs.3.rs-4300748/v1; May 2024) (ahmad2024glp2agonistsreduce pages 1-5)

#### 4.2 Real-world/implementation considerations (access and monitoring)
A practice-oriented 2024 expert paper notes that ADA/EASD consensus reports categorize tirzepatide’s glucose-lowering and weight effects as “very high,” and discusses practical use as monotherapy when metformin is inappropriate or as add-on therapy; it also summarizes clinically important benefits observed in SURPASS (e.g., substantial weight loss, HbA1c reductions, improved insulin sensitivity and reduced insulin requirements in some contexts). (https://doi.org/10.1007/s13300-024-01587-6; May 2024) (vazquez2024useoftirzepatide pages 2-4)
