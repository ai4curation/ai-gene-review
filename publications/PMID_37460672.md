Bacteria and their viruses have coevolved for billions of years. This ancient 
and still ongoing arms race has led bacteria to develop a vast antiphage 
arsenal. The development of high-throughput screening methods expanded our 
knowledge of defence systems from a handful to more than a hundred systems, 
unveiling many different molecular mechanisms. These findings reveal that 
bacterial immunity is much more complex than previously thought. In this Review, 
we explore recently discovered bacterial antiphage defence systems, with a 
particular focus on their molecular diversity, and discuss the ecological and 
evolutionary drivers and implications of the existing diversity of antiphage 
defence mechanisms.

Introduction:  18 
Phages are viruses that infect bacteria and hijack the cell machinery to replicate. Phages possess 19 
two main types of reproduction cycles. During a lytic cycle, the phage nucleic acids are expressed, 20 
producing both the phage genome and proteins that will be assembled into new virions 1. At the end of a 21 
lytic cycle, the host is lysed, and progeny phages are released. During a lysogenic cycle, the phage genome 22 
is integrated into the host genome as a prophage and is transmitted across bacterial generations. Phages 23 
are extremely abundant and are thought to outnumber bacteria ten to one in the ocean 2 24 
.  Estimates suggest that phage infection could account for 20 to 40% of bacterial daily mortality. 25 
As such, phage infection represents a major evolutionary driver for bacteria 2.  26 
 In response to this strong evolutionary pressure , bacteria have developed an arsenal of anti-phage 27 
systems, which can be defined as single genes or groups of genes that provide to their host partial or full 28 
resistance against phages. The anti-phage arsenal of bacteria has long been thought to be limited to 29 
Restriction-modification (RM), CRISPR-Cas, and a few other systems with unknown mechanisms. 30 
However, the development of high throughput bioinf ormatic and experimental approaches (Box 1) has 31 
extended our knowledge of the anti-phage arsenal of bacteria to more than a hundred (Table 1), revealing 32 
an unsuspected wealth of diverse systems and mechanisms. 33 
 Whereas existing knowledge of antiphage mechanisms was before almost exclusively limited to 34 
two systems detecting and degrading invading nucleic acids, these discoveries uncovered unsuspectedly 35 
complex immune strategies and notably systems that produce small anti-phage molecules 3,4, systems 36 
that rely on intracellular signal transduction through production of signaling molecules 5–7, and systems 37 
that recognize conserved structural patterns of viral proteins to trigger immune response 8. These recent 38 
findings have made a compelling case to revisit existing paradigms on antiviral immunity in bacteria from 39 
a simplistic picture to a much more detailed one. 40 
 41 
 Early discovered defense systems were previously reviewed (see 9–11). Here, we will first describe the 42 
diversity of anti-phage mechanisms, focusing on the most recently discovered ones, and discuss the 43 
evolutionary drivers and the ecological consequences of this diversity.   44

I. Antiphage defense systems are highly diverse  46 
1. Genomic diversity 47 
 48 
The genomic features of anti-phage systems reflect their diversity. First, the repertoire of genes 49 
associated with phage defense includes diverse func tions and domains, comprising nucleases, helicases, 50 
proteases, kinases, ATPases, reverse transcriptases, et c. The variety of this repertoire seems in itself 51 
limited and comprises mostly nucleic acid interacting domains and mostly a few other domains (SIR2, TIR, 52 
and transmembrane domains for instance) (Supp Table 1). However anti-phage genes can be combined 53 
in unique ways to compose diverse defense systems. Certain defense systems are single-gene (AbiH, Lit, 54 
NixI, BstA…), while other systems can encode 5 genes or more (CRISPR-Cas, BREX, DISARM, Dnd, Ssp…).  55 
 56 

3 
 
 The abundances of different defense systems in bacteria vary greatly from one system to another. 57 
Analysis of the RefSeq database with DefenseFinder,  a tool developed to systematically detect defense 58 
systems in prokaryotic genomes, re veals that RM and CRISPR-Cas are encoded in respectively 84% and 59 
40% of bacterial genomes, while more than half of detected systems are found in less than 3% of bacterial 60 
genomes12 (Fig 1B).  The distribution of defense systems appears to be long-tailed 12,13, and contains a 61 
wealth of rare systems that are the main source of diversity in antiviral arsenals.    62 
 63 
Multiple defense systems can also be combined in unique ways into an antiviral arsenal that is specific to 64 
a given strain. Out of 21,364 fully sequenced bacterial genomes, 78% of them encode more than two 65 
defense systems 12 (Fig 1A), with important variations between strains. For instance, certain intracellular 66 
parasitic bacteria ( e.g. members of the Chlamydiae phylum, Fig 1B) harbor few or no defense systems, 67 
while other species encode more than 50 12,13.  68 
 69 
It is also notable that many of the defense systems encoded by bacteria can also be detected in archaea. 70 
Among the systems detected by DefenseFinder more than a third can be detected in archaea, showing 71 
strong conservation of antiviral immunity between prokaryotic kingdoms. 72

2. Mechanistic diversity  75 
 76 
 To target phages without harming the host, most defense systems combine two elements: a 77 
sensor that detects the infection, and an effector th at either targets the phage or kills the infected host  78 
before the phage can complete its reproduction cycle, effectively protecting the rest of the population 79 
from newly released virions 14. This specific mechanism is describe d as “Abortive infection” (Abi). The 80 
various mechanisms used by bacteria to sense and re solve phage infection, as well as the articulation 81 
between the two are described in the following part (Fig 2).  82 
a) Detecting phage infection: sensing modules and mechanisms of bacterial immunity  83 
Following the specific binding of a phage to its host bacteria during adsorption, the phage genome is 84 
injected into the host. Viral genes are then expresse d by the host cell machinery, and newly synthesized 85 
nucleic acids are packaged in/with the newly translated  phage proteins to produce mature virions. All of 86 
these steps can be sensed by the cell’s defense systems as cues of viral infection. 87 
 88 
Detecting invading nucleic acids 89 
Detection of invading nucleic acid is a widespread strategy to sense phage infection (Fig 2). As nucleic acid 90 
injection and synthesis are among the first steps of phage infection, this sensing mechanism allows for an 91 
early immune response. These sensors are linked to two types of mechanisms. 92 
 93 
First, certain systems recognize motifs that ar e modified in the host genome. For Restriction-94 
Modification (RM) systems, recognition of invading nu cleic acids relies on the modification of specific 95 
motifs in the bacterial genome. The effector domain is then triggered by the recognition of non-modified 96 
motifs (except for type IV RM systems which target  modified invading DNA, the host DNA being without 97 

4 
 
alterations) 15. Classically, this modification occurs through methylation 15. Some newly discovered 98 
defense systems, generally described as RM-like, also  rely on methylation of the host DNA to recognize 99 
invading nucleic acids. This is the case of BREX 16,17 and DISARM18, two systems for which the mechanisms 100 
remain to be elucidated. Other systems use different types of modifications of the host DNA. Notably, 101 
DndACDE 19,20 and SspABCD 21,22 are protein complexes that perform respectively double-stranded and 102 
single-stranded phosphorothioate modification of the host DNA, by replacing a non-bridging oxygen with 103 
a sulfur in the DNA sugar-phosphate backbone. Non-modified DNA is recognized by diverse effectors to 104 
mediate immune response. Similarly, the anti-plasmid  Dpd system inserts 7-deaz aguanine derivatives in 105 
the host DNA to distinguish self from non-self 23. 106 
 107 
Defense systems can also use a nucleic acid gu ide to specifically recognize complementary 108 
sequences. For instance, CRISPR-Cas  system relies on the processi ng by the Cas machinery of small 109 
fragments of the invading viral nucleic acids during phage infection that are integrated in the CRISPR array 110 
24. Thus, the CRISPR array provides it s host cell with a memory of past infections, making CRISPR-Cas the 111 
first reported bacterial adaptive immune system. The CRISPR array is transcribed and processed, providing 112 
mature guide RNAs that  allow the Cas machinery to specifically recognize their nucleic acid target 24. 113 
CRISPR-Cas systems are generally classified into two classes and six types based on the set of Cas genes 114 
they encode and the nature of their effector (single protein or complex) 24.  115 
 116 
Prokaryotic argonautes (pAgo) seem to rely also on  invading nucleic acid recognition to achieve phage 117 
defense. Argonaute proteins are found in all three doma ins of life. In eukaryotes, they are key players in 118 
RNA interference. In prokaryotes, shorter pAgos were shown to provide defense against phages in 119 
association with diverse known anti-phage effectors 25–27. In vitro, the pAgo subunit of pAgo-containing 120 
systems (either alone or in a comple x with ancillary proteins) uses nucleic acid guides to recognize and 121 
bind nucleic acid targets 26,27, suggesting that pAgos are the sensors of these systems. 122 
 123 
Finally, other systems might also detect invading nucleic acids through a mechanism independent of 124 
sequence recognition. The anti-plasmid Wadjet system for instance was recently  reported to recognize 125 
invading DNA through topology sensing and to cleave closed-circular DNA 28. Similarly, newly reported 126 
evidence suggests that certain CBASS systems could be activated through binding of a folded fragment of 127 
RNA, the secondary and/or tertiary structure of the RNA being essential for CBASS activation 29.  Sensing 128 
of characteristic structural featur es of newly injected phage DNA might also be used for anti-phage 129 
defense, although supporting evidence for such mechanism is currently lacking.  130

Sensing of phage protein  132 
If infection is not detected and resolved during the first stages of the phage reproduction cycle, phage 133 
protein synthesis will start. These proteins can be de tected by sensor modules, and  detection of viral 134 
proteins through direct binding appears to be a common strategy in defense systems. Interestingly, all 135 
the systems reported to detect phage proteins (Avs 8, Stk230, CapRel31, DSR25, PARIS32, RexAB33 and AbiZ34) 136 
function through an Abi mechanism, meaning that re cognition of the trigger phage protein ultimately 137 
activates a toxic effector (Fig 2). In the case of dete ction of proteins involved in progeny virion assembly 138 
(functional or structural), which tend to be synthesized late in the phage reproduction cycle, this could be 139 
explained by the difficulty of resolving phage infection at advanced stages of infection. Indeed, late stages 140 
of infection offer a reduced time window for reso lving phage infection and are characterized by the 141 
presence of many copies of phage proteins and nucle ic acids in the cell. Besides, many phages cause 142 
irreparable damage to the host and its genome during infection, and several authors report that the host 143 
does not necessarily die as a result of “abortive” systems’ activity, but rather because of the damage 144 

5 
 
caused by the phage prior to the activation of the system 35,36. It thus seems that the fitness-costly Abi 145 
defense strategies could be kept as a last resort to protect the rest of the population and intervene when 146 
the infection is too advanced to be contained, or when the phage is resistant to the other defense systems 147 
of the host. 148

In general, two types of proteins can trigger defense systems:  150 
 151 
Certain defense systems sense proteins with func tions that are essential for phage replication. 152 
This is the case of Avs (formerly AVAST) systems, which are found in up to 4% of fully sequenced bacterial 153 
genomes8,32,37. Two Avs enzymes, one from Salmonella enterica and one from E.coli were documented to 154 
recognize and bind viral terminases and portal protei ns, respectively, which are both necessary for viral 155 
replication 8. Other defense systems target structural proteins. For instance, the DSR system in Bacillus 156 
subtilis recognizes phage SPR infection by binding to its tail tube protein 25, while the CapRel system found 157 
in E. coli is activated by the binding of the major capsid proteins of diverse phages31.   158 
Viral proteins with important biological roles ar e submitted to strong selective constraints, and 159 
therefore recognizing them to trigger phage defense presents major advantages. First, it limits the 160 
possibility of escape through mutations of the phage protein. For instance, the Stk2 system found in 161 
Staphylococcus epidermidis recognizes the Pack protein of phiNM1. Stk2 escapees have a reduced fitness 162 
and encode mutated versions of Pack. Sequencing of the Stk2 escapees shows uncomplete DNA packaging 163 
in the virions, suggesting that Pack is essential to DN A replication or packaging  and that its mutation is 164 
deleterious to the escapee phages 30. Second, it allows for a potentially wide defense range, as such viral 165 
proteins are generally conserved throughout evolution. As an example, Avs sensing of viral proteins does 166 
not rely on sequence but rather on conserved stru ctural features, allowing the enzymes to recognize 167 
triggers with little sequence homology in viruses as distant as human herpesvirus 8.  168 
 169 
Proteins that are not essential to viral replicat ion but which have a role in inhibiting defense 170 
systems can also be the trigger of anti-phage systems. PARIS for instance is triggered by the Ocr protein 171 
encoded by certain phages, an anti-restriction protein that inhibits both BREX and RM systems 32.  172

Monitoring the integrity of the bacterial cell machinery  175 
During infection, the host machinery is rapidly hijacked to stop the production of bacterial 176 
components while forcing it to prod uce viral ones. This process is detected by the sensor modules of 177 
several defense systems. In most case s, this type of sensing could be described as undirect sensing of a 178 
phage protein.  179 
 180 
Such a strategy has been described in two systems that use a retron element as their sensor. 181 
Retrons are genetic elements composed of a specia lized reverse transcriptas e  and a non-coding RNA 182 
(ncRNA), which is processed by the reverse transcriptase to produce an RNA-DNA hybrid. In E. coli, the 183 
retron Ec48 was shown to have anti-phage activity. While the exact mechanism remains elusive the retron 184 
Ec48 was shown to monitor the activity of the Re cBCD complex, meaning that inhibition of RecBCD 185 
triggers the activity of the system 38. In Salmonella Typhimurium’s RcaT system, which also includes a 186 
retron sensor, two phage triggers were identified. Both of the triggers interfere with the RNA-DNA 187 
complex of the retron element, one by methylating it (Dam) and the other by degrading it (RecE) 39.  188 
Similarly, mutation of the phage protein gp5.7 a llows T7 phages to escape both the single-gene 189 
defense systems dCTP deaminase or dGTPase. As gp5.7 is responsible for shutting down σS-dependent 190 
host RNA polymerase transcription, this observat ion suggests that both dCTP deaminase and dGTPase 191 

6 
 
systems could sense transcription inhibition 40. The PrrC system on the other hand is activated when the 192 
phage T4 tempers with its E. coli host restriction endonuclease (EcoprrI). Indeed, the PrrC protein is bound 193 
to and inhibited by EcoprrI under normal conditions. However binding of the phage T4-encoded protein 194 
to EcoprrI during infection alleviates PrrC inhibition, triggering the immune response 41,42.  195 
Anti-phage Toxin-Antitoxin (TA) systems also monitor the host cell’s machinery’s integrity. These 196 
systems encode a stable toxic effector protein, which is inhibited by a labile RNA or protein antitoxin 197 
under normal conditions. During phage infection, the degradation of the labile antitoxin releases the 198 
toxin, killing the host.  In type III TA systems, whic h encode RNA antitoxins, phage hijacking of the host 199 
cell machinery and subsequent cessation of bacterial tr anscription results in the depletion of the labile 200 
antitoxin and release of the toxin inhibition. This strategy is used by both ToxIN 36 and AvcD 43.  A similar 201 
mechanism can be found in Type II TA RnlAB, where the RnlB antitoxin protein is a lot less stable than its 202 
cognate toxin, RnlA. Shutoff of the host transcriptio n during phage infection and degradation of RnlB by 203 
the host proteases results in RnlA activation 44.  204 
 205 
An interesting evolutionary consequence of this type of sensor is that they force the phages to a trade-off 206 
between taking over their host’s resources or escapi ng detection by anti-phage systems, potentially 207 
resulting in reduced fitness of the resistant phages.  208 
b) Activating downstream immune response: signal transduction in bacterial immunity  209 
Once viral infection is detected by the sensor module, the information must be transmitted to 210 
activate the effector module. Two cases can be distinguished:  Direct activation or Indirect activation 211 
through signaling molecules 212 
 213 
In a direct activation context, the sensor and effector modules physically interact. This is the case of all 214 
the single gene systems, in which the sensor and effector domains are found either on the same protein 215 
or fused, including Avs 8, CapRel 31, dGTPase and dCTP deaminase 40, Lit and PrrC 45, pAgo-Sir2 systems 216 
25,26. It is also the case of RM and RM-like systems, and of most CRISPR-Cas sy stems (except for Type III, 217 
see part 3.c), where the effector protein/protein complex directly recognizes foreign nucleic acid.  218 
 219 
In mechanisms involving indirect activation through signaling molecules, the sensor produces messenger 220 
molecules that activate the effector. This signal-transduction strategy was first discovered in CRISPR-Cas 221 
systems Type III. When recognizing its target RNA, the cyclase domain of the large Cas10 subunit 222 
polymerizes ATP into a cyclic oligoadenylate (cOA) species 46. The signaling molecule is then recognized by 223 
an ancillary toxic effector that mediates an Abi response 47. 224 
 CBASS systems constitute another type of signaling anti-phage systems. Upon infection, the 225 
cyclase (CD-Ntase) encoded by a CBASS system pr oduces cyclic-oligo-nucleot ides signaling molecules 226 
(cGMP-AMP, c-UMP-UMP, cCMP–UMP, cUMP–GMP, c-AMP-AMP, c-GMP-GMP, cAAA, cGAA…). These 227 
signaling molecules activate various anti-phage immune  effectors, for instance Cap4 endonucleases or 228 
Patatin-like phospholipases 48,49.  PYCSAR is similar to CBASS but produces the cyclic mononucleotides 229 
cCMP or cUMP 7. 230 
  Finally, in the Thoeris system, the sensor protein ThsB detects phage infection through an 231 
unknown mechanism, activating its TIR domain to pro duce a variant of cyclic ADP-ribose (v-cADP). This 232 
signal molecule binds to and activates the toxic effector of the system, ThsA 6,50.  233 
 234 
 The presence of intermediate signaling molecu les offers interesting possibilities. As their 235 
concentration gradually builds up during infection, the cell can tune its response via degradation or 236 
dilution. For example, ring nucleases have been show n to be associated with CRISPR type III and to 237 

7 
 
degrade the cOA molecules produced during infection, allowing an interruption of the Abi pathway before 238 
cell death if the infection is otherwise resolved 51. Conversely, signaling systems offer the possibility of 239 
signal amplification. For instance, the CRISPR type III encoded by the archaea Sulfolobus solfataricus 240 
produces 1000 signaling molecules per RNA molecule detected, which could allow for an early immune 241 
response 51.  242 
 Interestingly, the CD-Ntases of CBASS systems share distant but clear homology with cGas, a 243 
eukaryotic enzyme that performs immune signal transduction in the cGAS-STING antiviral pathway 48. 244 
Thoeris also resembles a eukaryotic immune pathway, as TIR-domain receptors in plants produce a similar 245 
signal molecule upon pathogen recognition, leading to  cell death via a mechanism reminiscent of Abi. 246 
Therefore, the discovery of signal transduction through messenger molecules for anti-phage defense 247 
opens fascinating parallels with eukaryotic immunity. 248 
c) Interrupting the phage reproduction cycle: effector modules of bacterial immunity   249 
 250 
Once infection is detected, several strategies are exploited by anti-phage systems to interrupt the phage 251 
reproduction cycle.  They mainly target three types of elements: nucleic acids, single nucleotides and the 252 
cell membrane. 253 
 254 
Nucleic acids degrading effectors  255 
 Systems degrading nucleic acids are very abundant in bacterial genomes 12 (Fig 1C). Two types of 256 
mechanisms exist: specific cleavage of viral nucleic acids or untargeted cleavage of both viral and host 257 
nucleic acids. 258 
 259 
Although they differ in their organization and mechanism, CRISPR-Cas Type I, II, and V all recognize and 260 
specifically cleave target phage DNA 52. Restriction Modification systems also specifically cleave phage 261 
DNA when recognizing unmodified motifs (or modified in  type IV Restriction systems). In the Restriction-262 
like systems Ssp and Dnd, effector modules DndFGH and SspE also cleave unmodified nucleic acids 19,22. 263 
The NixL nuclease, which is encoded on phage parasi tes and inhibits the Vibriophage ICP1, also appears 264 
to act through specific cleavage of ICP1 genome, although the exact mechanism behind self from non-self 265 
discrimination is not yet fully understood 53.    266 
 267 
Two types of CRISPR-Cas systems, Type III and Type VI,  can activate untargeted nucleic acids (DNA and/or 268 
RNA) cleavage by the nucleases associated to the system upon detection of invading nucleic acids, leading 269 
to either cell death or dormancy following infection 24. CBASS, Avs and Lamassu systems can also be found 270 
associated with endonuclease effectors 8,13,48,54–56. Among the Toxin-Antitoxin systems, both RnlAB and 271 
ToxIN have a RNAse toxin that non-specifically cleaves host and phage RNA upon phage infection 36,44. The 272 
Gabija system effector GajA has also been prop osed to function as a non-specific DNA nicking 273 
endonuclease when activated 57.  Finally, both PrrC and RloC respectively nick and cleave tRNALys 274 
molecules when activated 42,58. 275 
 276 
Generally, targeted nucleic acid degradation allows the host to resolve and survive phage infection, 277 
whereas untargeted nucleic acid degradation leads to an Abi mechanism. However, while untargeted DNA 278 
degrading systems offer little chance of resurrection to the infected cell, cleavage of RNA transcripts only 279 
leads to cell dormancy. Therefore, if the system’s tr igger is destroyed, the host can potentially recover 280 
from phage infection. For instance, cells can recover from the dormancy induced by Cas13 collateral 281 
RNAse activity in Type VI CRISPR systems if they also encode an RM system 59. 282

Some systems do not cleave nucleic acids but modify viral nucleic acids to achieve defense. For instance, 284 
in the DarTG system, the DarT toxin performs ADP-ribosylation of the phage DNA, which inhibits both viral 285 
DNA and RNA synthesis 35. Similarly, CapRel’s toxin domain pyrophosphorylates tRNAs, blocking 286 
translation 31. Systems like  Nhi, BREX, or DISARM seem to  target phage nucleic acids without cleaving 287 
them through a mechanism not yet deciphered 16,18,60.  288

Nucleotides modifying effectors 290 
Since phage replication requires an abundance of intracellular nucleotides to use as building 291 
blocks for the fast-replicating phage genome, certain bacterial anti-phage effectors directly target the 292 
host's single nucleotide pool.  293 
Depleting the host’s pool of nucleotides deprives the replicating phages of essential building 294 
blocks. Two classes of enzymes that degrade dNTPs have been described as effectors of anti-phage 295 
systems: dCTP deaminases and dGTPases, which respectively degrade dCTP and dGTP 40,43.  296 
Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD) is an essential coenzyme of many essential metabolic 297 
reactions and depletion of cellular NAD+ can cause growth arrest or cell death.  Thoeris, DSR, pAgo have 298 
effectors which use the NADase activity of their SIR2-domain effector to kill infected cells 6,25,26.  Pycsar 299 
and CBASS on the other hand have TIR domains which also kill the cell through NAD+ depletion 48,61. TIR 300 
domains are also associated with retrons and Avs, suggesting a similar mechanism 8,38. Finally, SEFIR 301 
domains can also be anti-phage effectors acting through NAD+ depletion 13. While RADAR was initially 302 
reported to function through deamination of its host cellular RNAs during phage infection 37,62, depletion 303 
of cellular ATP through adenosine deamination was re cently suggested to be involved in the antiphage 304 
activity of the system 63. 305 
 306 
Prokaryotic viperins are enzymes that can modify nucleotides triphosphates (NTP) substrates and remove 307 
the hydroxyl group at the 3 ′ carbon of the ribose, producing 3 ′-deoxy-3′,4′-didehydro (ddh) nucleotides. 308 
Viperins were shown to inhibit transcription by T7 RNA polymerase, probably because the viral 309 
polymerase incorporates ddh nucleotides in the nascent chain of the viral RNA. Ddh nucleotides would 310 
then act as chain terminators, causing premature termination of the RNA chain 64.  311

Membrane disrupting effectors 318 
 Many defense systems encode transmembrane pr oteins or membrane targeting proteins which 319 
kill the infected cell by disrupting its membrane. Se veral types of membrane-disrupting effectors can be 320 
distinguished. 321 
 322 
Bacterial Gasdermins (bGSDM) are generally found next to one or several proteases, which are 323 
activated during phage infection to cleave an inhibitory C-terminal peptide of the bGDSMs, releasing them 324 
of their auto-inhibition. Activated bGDSMs assemble and form large pores in the membrane, eventually 325 
killing the host 65. Interestingly, gasdermins are also known effectors of programmed cell death in 326 
mammals 65. Although the primary mode of action of most CRISPR systems is to interfere with nucleic 327 
acids, the Csx28 protein encoded by Type VI-B2 system forms pores in the membrane of infected cell upon 328 
target phage RNA detection by Cas13, resulting in  membrane depolarization. The presence of Csx28 329 
increases anti-phage defense by several orders of magnitude compared to CRISPR-Cas alone 66. Other 330 
systems relying on pore-forming effectors include RexAB 33, Pif 67,68, and probably Zorya which encodes 331 
homologs of proton channel-forming proteins54.  332 
 333 
Phospholipases are common CBASS effectors an d degrade the cell membrane’s phospholipids 334 
when activated 5,48. The archaeal pAgo found in Sulfolobus islandicus also encodes a toxic effector (Aga2) 335 
which binds anionic phospholipids and triggers membrane depolarization 27.  336 
 337 
Proteins containing TM domains have been shown to impair membrane integrity and lead to cell 338 
death in CBASS, PYCSAR, and Avs systems 7,8,48,69. The retron system Ec48 from E.coli also encodes a 339 
transmembrane-domain containing effector, which causes premature loss of membrane integrity during 340 
phage infection 38.  Intriguingly, the single-protein system AbiZ, which encompasses two transmembrane 341 
domains, teams up with phage-encoded holins or lysins to cause premature lysis 34.  342 
 343 
Overall, effectors that disrupt the membrane integr ity appear to be common in anti-phage defense 344 
systems and respond to phage infection by killing the infected host. 345 
 346 
 347 
From systems than monitor the host integrity to signaling systems and membrane degrading ones, 348 
the diversity of anti-phage sensors, signal transductors , and effectors is striking. Yet, the mechanisms of 349 
more than half (58%) of the reported anti-phage systems are not yet deciphered 12 (Fig 1C, Supp Table 1) 350 
and most of the 7000 gene families predicted to be anti-phage based on their prop ensity to be encoded 351 
next to known defense genes (Box 1) remain to be studied 37, suggesting the existence of many more 352 
systems and mechanisms. Importantly,  the articulation sensor-transductor-effector does not seem to 353 
apply to all defense systems. Notably, several systems described as chemical defense seem to 354 
constitutively produce small anti-phage molecules, as reported for aminoglycosides 3 and DNA 355 
intercalants 4.  356 
 357 
The diversity of strategies to resist phage infectio n goes beyond anti-phage systems. Other layers of 358 
anti-phage mechanisms exist, although they are not always considered to be strictly defense systems (Box 359 
2). These mechanisms can participate in determinin g the set of strains a given phage can successfully 360 
infect, i.e. its host range. For instance, mutating or masking the bacterial receptors that allow phages to 361 

10 
 
adsorb to the bacterial membrane prevents the inje ction of viral nucleic acids. In this review, we 362 
considered only defense systems that act intracellularly (meaning after phage infection) and which do not 363 
have housekeeping functions. 364

II. Drivers of anti-phage defense diversity  366 
The outstanding diversity of the defense strategies implemented by bacteria to resist phages has both 367 
ecological and evolutionary roots. Indeed , t he coevolution between bacteria and phages, as well as 368 
between bacteria and other Mobile Genetic Elements (MGEs), has driven the emergence and 369 
diversification of anti-phage systems for billions of ye ars. This phenomenon is facilitated by the genetic 370 
organization of anti-phage systems: the reservoir of anti-phage genetic elements (domains, sensors, 371 
effectors ...) is limited, but its elements can be combin ed in a multiplicity of novel associations to create 372 
defense systems.   373 
1. Coevolution between bacteria and phages drives the diversification of anti-phage systems  374 
Ecological studies of bacteria-phages interactions  in natural populations hint at a fast-paced 375 
coevolution. For instance, a ma trix of interactions of 195 Vibrio crassostreae  strains against 243 376 
vibriophages isolated over a 5-month time period show s that only 2.2% of the host-phage interactions 377 
tested are positive and that a given phage is more likely to infect a host isolated in a close space/time area 378 
70.  379 
 Behind the coevolution of phage and bacteria is  a continuous arm-race process between phages 380 
and bacteria. Notably, the diversity of anti-phage defense systems is mirrored by a similar diversity of anti-381 
defense systems encoded by phages (Box 3) 10,71,72. 382 
 383 
The selective pressure maintained by the continuo us emergence of escaper phages probably drives 384 
the fast acquisition and diversification of phage defe nse systems in bacteria. Reflecting the speed of loss 385 
and acquisition of defense systems in bacteria, the de fensive content of very closely related strains can 386 
be highly variable. For instance, in a set of 23 nearly clonal Vibrio lentus strains, defense elements were 387 
shown to account for 95% of the flexible region 73.   388 
 389 
 390 
 391 
 392 
2. Mobile genetic elements (MGE) and horizontal  gene transfer (HGT) promote diverse anti-393 
phage arsenals in bacteria 394 
Defense systems have a turnover rate signifi cantly higher than any other class of genes 74. The 395 
patchy distributions of most defense systems sugges t that horizontal gene transfer (HGT) is key in 396 
explaining this dynamic 12,13,75. Consequently, the defensive content of very closely related strains can be 397 
highly variable.  398 
 399 
Mobile Genetic Elements (MGE) are major cont ributors to defense systems’ HGT. Notably, 400 
prophages, integrative and conjugative elements (I CE), plasmids, and transposons often carry defense 401 
systems 32,76–81. Studies suggest that the majority of E. coli defense arsenal could be carried by MGEs 77,79,82. 402 
However, MGEs and the defense elements they encode  can take a toll on their host, both due to the 403 
resources their expression consumes and to the risk of autoimmunity 83. Due to their fitness cost, MGEs 404 

11 
 
are frequently lost by bacteria. Therefore, the st rong association between MGE and defense systems is 405 
probably responsible for their high gain-and-loss rate through HGT. As MGEs often seem to integrate at 406 
specific hotspots, it could also explain the existence of defense islands (Box 1) 32,79. The importance of HGT 407 
in the spread of bacterial defense systems has led to the hypothesis of a pan-immune system, where 408 
various systems are distributed and shared among bacterial communities, increasing the diversity of 409 
accessible systems while lowering their fitness cost 84. 410 
 411 
Certain systems appear to be ch aracteristic of certain types of MGEs. For instance, prophages 412 
often encode defense systems that protect the host they infect from other phages 85–87. Certain defense 413 
systems are almost always found on prophages, an d rarely on the chromosome or other types of 414 
MGEs12,79. For instance, BstA 88, NixI 53, RexAB 33, Ltp 89 and Lit 90 are characteristic of phages and 415 
prophages. On the other hand, some systems appear to  be frequently associated with plasmids, like TA 416 
systems for instance 9. Whether systems which are always encoded on MGEs and never by bacteria 417 
themselves can be considered anti-phage systems is still unclear, and questions the limits of the definition 418 
of a defense system (Box 2). 419 
 420 
Although MGE-encoded defense systems can be beneficial to their hosts, they are first and 421 
foremost selfish genetic elements. For instance, prophages encoding BtsA also encode an anti-BstA (aba) 422 
element that prevents autoimmunity and allows them to undergo their lytic cycle. Because of the selfish 423 
nature of MGEs, it has been proposed that the defense systems they encode could be mere weapons in 424 
the inter-MGE war 83,91. This hypothesis highlights how ecologic al conflicts between bacteria and MGEs, 425 
and between MGEs themselves, drive the diversification of defense systems under changing evolutionary 426 
pressures. 427

3. Modularity as a source of diversity 429 
 430 
The evolution and diversification of defense systems is  probably supported by their modularity. Both in 431 
terms of domains, sensors, and/or effectors, the diversification of defense systems seems driven by an 432 
evolutionary mix-and-match game played at different and imbricated scales. 433 
 434 
First, at the gene level, certain domains appear  to be co-opted by many defense systems. For 435 
instance, HEPN domains are common RNase effectors in nucleic acid degradin g systems like CRISPR-Cas 436 
Type III,  Prrc, RloC, RnlAB and ApeA 92 and were also found in defensive hotspots 32. Similarly, SMC 437 
domains are encoded by several systems, including Lamassu 13,54  and Wadjet 28, and are also found in 438 
defensive hotspots 32.   439 
 440 
At the system level, several studies suggest th at a given sensor module can activate multiple 441 
effector modules. For instance, the DNA-modifying module of the Dnd system (DndACDE) is not only 442 
found with the DNA-cleaving effector (DndFGH) but can also  be associated in archaea with   an effector 443 
module (PbeABCD) that does not degrade or cleave viral DNA but halts phage replication 19,20. Similarly in 444 
Ssp, DNA-modifying SspBCD can be associated with either SspE (DNA-nicking) or SspFGH (unknown 445 
mechanism) 21,22, suggesting additional mechanisms. Other examples are the endonucleases NucC, which 446 
is an effector of both CRISPR-Cas and CBASS systems 46, and Cap4, which can be an effector of both 447 
Lamassu and CBASS systems 93. 448 
Conversely, an effector module can also be activated by multiple sensor units. For instance, in 449 
genomes encoding a Thoeris system, the thsA effector gene is often found accompanied by several thsB 450 

12 
 
sensors. It appears that different ThsB proteins recognize different phages and all activate the same 451 
effector, widening the activity spectrum of the system 6.  In the Avs defense systems, the NLR sensor 452 
domain can be fused with diverse effector domains. Gao and colleagues created a chimeric system where 453 
the natural Mrr-like nuclease effector domain of E. coli’s Avs4 was swapped with the transmembrane 454 
domain effector domain from an Avs4 homolog encoded by Sulfurospirillum sp. The chimeric system 455 
retained its antiviral activity, further demonstrating the modularity of the Avs  systems8.  456 
It has also been proposed that domains and modules associated with defense systems can either 457 
evolve from or be domesticated into non-defensive ce llular processes, as it has reported for certain RM 458 
enzymes 91,94,95. Highlighting even more the modular nature of defense systems are the many newly 459 
discovered multi-effector or multi-sensor families of systems, such as CBASS, Lamassu, retrons and pAgos 460 
26,27,38,39,48.  461 
The co-optation of domains, sensors, and/or e ffectors, from other defense systems or other 462 
cellular pathways could allow the evolutio n of novel anti-phage mechanisms not de novo  but as a 463 
combination of existing modules.  464 
 465 
III. Impact of the diversity of anti-phage systems on phages and bacteria 466 
  467 
Defense systems have strong ecological repercussions, both on phage-bacteria interactions and on 468 
phage and bacteria separately. 469 
1. Impact of defense systems diversity on host range 470 
  471 
The impact of the diversity of anti-phage defense systems on host range is not fully understood yet. 472 
However, several tendencies have already emerged. 473 
First, the combination of different defense systems is often synergetic. On one hand, encoding several 474 
defense systems active against the same phage can increase the host resistance compared to the 475 
expression of a single system 70,82,96. On the other hand, most defense systems only defend against specific 476 
phages. For instance, 15 Pseudomonas aeruginosa systems were cloned and 11 of them were specific to 477 
one or two phage families when expressed in a P. aeruginosa host 97.The expression of several defense 478 
systems active against different phages can result in an increase of the resistance range, which can be 479 
equal or superior to the sum of the resistance profiles conferred by individual systems 81,97. 480 
Secondly, encoding diverse defense systems could hinder the emergence of resistant phages, as reported 481 
for instance for the combination of ToxIN and RexAB 98, BREX and RM systems  81, CRISPR and RM systems 482 
99.   483 
Overall, the diversity of anti-phage sensors and effectors could allow defense systems to target a 484 
wider range of phages while preventing the emergence of resistance. 485 
2. Defense systems shape microbial genomics 486 
 487 
 Defense systems also shape microbial genomics. They can impact horizontal gene transfer and shape the 488 
spread and fixation of mobile genetic elements in microbial populations in various ways. 489 
First, the diversity of defense systems impacts MGEs. Indeed, anti-phage defense systems can restrict not 490 
only phages but also other types of MGEs. As MGEs ca n bring beneficial elements to the host (antibiotic 491 
resistance genes for instance), this could pr ove detrimental to the host. For instance, in Vibrio cholerae 492 
the presence of genetic elements encoding defense systems was shown to limit the acquisition of two 493 

13 
 
beneficial MGEs 80, and the introduction of a CRISPR-Cas system in a Bacillus strain naturally lacking one 494 
resulted in reduced growth, sporulation, biof ilms, and adaptability to environmental stresses 100. 495 
Depending on their molecular mechanisms, defense systems are more or less permissive toward invading 496 
nucleic DNA, and therefore towards MGEs. For inst ance, systems that detect RNA can coexist with 497 
unexpressed foreign DNA. 498 
 499 
Second, the diversity of anti-phage mechanisms infl uences HGT in bacteria. For instance, “addictive” 500 
systems such as TA systems can participate in stabil izing MGEs in the bacterial population by killing any 501 
progeny cells that did not inherit the MGE encoding them. DNA degrading systems like RM could facilitate 502 
homologous recombination by introducing breaks in dsDNA 101. CRISPR-Cas systems have also been 503 
proposed to promote HGT, possibly by favoring homologous recombination between CRISPR spacers and 504 
phage protospacers 102,103. 505 
 506 
Although the impact of viral predation on microbial communities is probably still underestimated, several 507 
studies highlight the importance of phage-bacteria interactions on microbiomes and ecosystems 508 
2,104,105.Phages not only impact the species they target, but also indirectly impact competing species of the 509 
ecological niche. As an example, a studied showed that introduction of lytic phages in gut microbiome of 510 
a mouse model led to stable coexistence of the phag e and the targeted host, and had cascading indirect 511 
effects on non-targeted species, and ultimately impacted the gut metabolome 104. As defense systems 512 
shape phage-bacteria interactions and influence microbial genomics, they probably have major ecological 513 
implications that remain to be fully comprehended. 514 
Conclusion 515 
The diversity of anti-phage mechanisms in bacteria is striking and comprises unexpectedly 516 
complex immune strategies. Despite recent advances in our knowledge of anti-phage defense 517 
mechanisms in bacteria, many questions remain to be explored. In particular, very few systems have been 518 
characterized outside of classic model organisms, and future research on distant bacteria or archaea could 519 
provide fascinating insights and allow the discovery of novel mechanisms. In the past, the mechanistic 520 
richness of bacterial anti-phage defense has been the source of major biotechnological breakthroughs.  In 521 
fields as diverse as molecular biology, genome editing, and clinical diagnosis, systems like RM, CRISPR-Cas 522 
and retrons have provided invaluable tools, and many more applications are probably yet to be uncovered 523 
106–110. 524 
 525 
  526 
The giant leaps made in our understanding of an ti-phage defense systems have major ecological 527 
and evolutionary repercussions. These advancements compel us to shift our perspective towards an 528 
integrated understanding of the role of defense systems within a complex network of interactions, moving 529 
away from a simplistic view of how a single system impacts the interaction between a phage and its host. 530 
 531 
First, the overwhelming presence of abortive infection systems 8,26,43,62,69, despite their important fitness 532 
cost for the bacteria expressing them, demonstrates  that defense systems sh ould not be considered 533 
exclusively at the single-cell level, but rather at the population level. Reports on the anti-phage activity of 534 
certain molecules excreted by certain Actinobacteria  also support the importance of considering anti-535 
phage defense at the level of microbial communities. St udies suggest that real-lif e factors such as inter-536 
species competition 111 and spatial heterogeneity could affect the dynamics of anti-phage defense in 537 
bacteria 112. Yet, the role of anti-phage systems has rarely been explored outside of simple and controlled 538 
laboratory settings.  Notably, very few anti-phage sy stems have been studied in  their original host, and 539 

14 
 
studying anti-phage mechanisms in their native conditions will probably be key in a better understanding 540 
of anti-phage defense in nature.  541 
 542 
Secondly, the interplay between defense systems and the genetic elements that encode them 543 
appears far more complex than initially envisioned.  Defense systems’ diversity not only stems from an 544 
ongoing war between bacteria and MGEs, but also shap es the fate of all protagonists involved. From 545 
bacteria to plasmids, phages, prophages, and even phage satellites, all kinds of MGEs  can develop and/or 546 
hijack for their own benefit existing defense systems, anti-defense systems, and even anti-anti-defense 547 
systems. These findings challenge our perceptions of defense systems as weapons in a war between 548 
bacteria and phages and suggest that anti-phage defense systems can be envisioned as a toolbox used by 549 
both MGEs and bacteria to interfere with gene fl uxes, alongside notably anti -plasmids and viral anti-550 
defense systems 83. Investigating a potential continuum between anti-phage defense and HGT and/or 551 
MGE regulation will no doubt help refine current knowledge of microbial genomics. 552 
 553 
Lastly, from an evolutionary perspective, defense systems appear to be an excellent example of the 554 
concept of evolutionary tinkering developed by François Jacob in 1977, which argues that natural selection 555 
is akin to a tinkerer assembling existing objects, i.e. in this case defensive modules, until they combine 556 
into something useful 113. Interestingly, some defensive modules appear to be conserved across domains 557 
of life. As mentioned earlier, many antiviral syst ems can be conserved between Bacteria and Archaea. 558 
Maybe more surprisingly, both protein and domain  conservation between prokaryotic and eukaryotic 559 
immunity have also been reported,  with for instance Viperins 64, Gasdermins 65, but also TIR and  NLR-560 
domains 6,114. Several hypotheses could explain the conser vation of antiviral immune systems between 561 
prokaryotes and eukaryotes, one of them being that these systems were inherited from the last common 562 
ancestor(s) of prokaryotes and eukaryotes 115. It is also possible that some defense systems were acquired 563 
during later events of HGT between prokaryotes and ea rly eukaryotes.  Combining ideas of evolutionary 564 
tinkering and immune building blocks opens two fascinating research avenues: i) Delimiting what can be 565 
considered anti-phage building blocks, mapping them in chromosome genomes and studying their 566 
possible combinations might allow systematic explor ation of the diversity of anti-phage systems in 567 
bacteria 94. ii) The conservation of building blocks across domain of life could allow the discovery and 568 
understanding of eukaryotic immune mechanisms sharing homology with known anti-phage systems 50,116. 569

Box 1: High-throughput methods for prediction of anti-phage systems  1064 
The development of novel methods to uncover anti-phage systems in bacteria has drastically increased 1065 
the rate of defense systems discovery. The initial approach that revealed dozens of novel systems 1066 
(Approach A) relies on the fact that defense systems often colocalize with one another on the bacterial 1067 
genome 54,123. This observation is used to predict bioinfor matically genes involved in anti-phage defense 1068 
based on their tendency to be encoded close to known defensive genes. The genomic context of homologs 1069 
of the genes predicted to be defensive is then used to determine if they are single-gene systems or if they 1070 
work in association with others. The newly predicte d system is then validated experimentally. This 1071 
approach was fine-tuned to lead to the prediction of thousands of genes potentially involved in anti-phage 1072
