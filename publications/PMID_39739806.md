Spns1 mediates the rate-limiting efflux of lysophospholipids from the lysosome 
to the cytosol. Deficiency of Spns1 is associated with embryonic senescence, as 
well as liver and skeletal muscle atrophy in animal models. However, the 
mechanisms by which Spns1 transports lysophospholipid and proton sensing remain 
unclear. Here, we present a cryogenic electron microscopy structure of human 
Spns1 in lysophosphatidylcholine (LPC)-bound lumen-facing conformation. Notably, 
LPC snugly binds within the luminal-open cavity, where the molecular dynamics 
simulations reveal that LPC presents a propensity to enter between 
transmembrane-helices (TM) 5 and 8. Structural comparisons and cell-based 
transport assays uncover several pivotal residues at TM 5/8 that orchestrate the 
transport cycle, which are unique to Spns1. Furthermore, we identify a 
five-residue network that is crucial for proton-sensing by Spns1. Transference 
of these network residues to Spns2, a sphingosine-1-phosphate uniporter, causes 
the chimeric Spns2 to be low pH dependent. Our results reveal molecular insights 
into lysosomal LPC transport and the proton-sensing mechanism by Spns1.

## Full Text

The lysosome is not only an essential catabolic organelle but also a key signaling center that integrates environmental cues to control cell growth (1, 2). The lysosomal lumen contains various hydrolytic enzymes capable of breaking down different types of biomolecules, including lipids, proteins, nucleic acids, and carbohydrates. These precursor biological macromolecules are received through either endocytosis, phagocytosis, and/or autophagy and the subsequent by-products are exported to the cytosol for reutilization by the cell (3). Malfunctional degradation of lysosomal substrates and defects in transporting these metabolites out of lysosomes are characteristics of a class of diseases known as lysosomal storage diseases (LSDs) (4, 5).

Lysosomal lipids contribute to the structural integrity of lysosomal membranes, which play a critical role in maintaining the compartmentalization of lysosomal enzymes: preventing the leakage of hydrolytic enzymes into the cytoplasm, which could lead to cellular damage. Although many lysosomal transporters that export hydrophilic molecules have been identified (6), our knowledge on lysosomal lipid transporters remains limited. Given that the lysosome is an important organelle for the hydrolysis of complex sphingolipids and phospholipids, it is imperative to recycle these hydrolytic products, such as sphingosine and lysophospholipids, back to the cytoplasm (7, 8). While Niemann–Pick C1 protein has been demonstrated to transport cholesterol out of lysosomes (9, 10), only very recently have several groups identified that Spinster homolog 1 (Spns1), a homolog of the sphingosine-1-phosphate (S1P) transporter Spns2, functions as a lysosomal transporter to mediate the export of lysophospholipids to the cytosol for recycling (11–13). Spns1 is critical for cell survival under nutrient-limiting conditions and deficiency of Spns1 is linked to embryonic senescence and organ atrophy in animal models (14–16). Mutations in Spns1 also result in symptoms reminiscent of LSDs in humans, highlighting the physiological significance of this pathway in the lysosome (13).

Spns1 is a member of the solute carrier 63 (SLC63) family along with two other transporters (Spns2 and Spns3), all of which belong to the major facilitator superfamily (MFS) (17). MFS members all share a similar topology consisting of twelve transmembrane helices (TMs); however, they translocate a wide range of highly diverse substrates across biological membranes. Up to now, four MFS transporters in humans—Spns1, Spns2, Mfsd2a, and Mfsd2b—have been identified as being responsible for the deployment of amphiphilic lysolipids (12, 18). Spns2 and Mfsd2b are S1P transporters at the plasma membrane (19, 20), while Mfsd2a is a sodium-dependent lysophosphatidylcholine (LPC) transporter that is essential for the uptake of the LPC-containing omega-3 fatty acid docosahexaenoic acid across both the blood–brain and blood–retina barriers (21, 22). Recent structural studies suggested that Mfsd2a engages monoacyl lipid substrates by its outward-facing cavity on the plasma membrane and flip them from the outer leaflet to the inner leaflet in a sodium-dependent manner (23–26). Intriguingly, although Spns1 and Spns2 share a high sequence identity (54%), they have different cellular localizations, transport mechanisms, and substrate selectivity. While Spns2 is localized to the plasma membrane and transports S1P in a facilitated-diffusion manner (27), Spns1 is located on the lysosomal membrane and mediates lysophospholipid efflux depending on the proton gradient generated by the vacuolar H+-ATPase (11–13).

Although many structural studies have gleaned important insights into the transport mechanisms of Mfsd2a and Spns2 (23–29), the molecular determinants of how Spns1 uses the proton gradient to flip amphiphilic lysolipids between leaflets of the bilayer remain unknown. Here, we present the structure of human Spns1 in complex with an LPC substrate by single-particle cryoelectron microscopy (cryo-EM) uncovering the transport mechanism of Spns1-mediated lysophospholipid export from the lysosome.

The luminal opening surrounded by TMs 5 and 8 accommodates the polar head of LPC, with residues Y203 of TM5 and S322 of TM8 potentially making hydrophilic contacts. In this state, the acyl tail of LPC adopts a linear shape protruding out of the cavity in the luminal leaflet of the lipid bilayer and makes hydrophobic interactions with residues G200, L201, and I204 on TM8 and G326, L327, and C330 on TM8 (Fig. 2B). The outward-facing structure of mouse Mfsd2a shows a potential lipid-binding site situated in the extracellular leaflet between TM5 and TM8 (24). Notably, residue T202 on TM2 and N335 on TM8 of mouse Mfsd2a have been shown pointing toward this putative lipid binding cleft (24). However, these two residues are not conserved in Spns1. Intriguingly, when superimposing our LPC-bound Spns1 structure onto the mouse Mfsd2a structure, a clash emerges between the LPC head in our structure and residue F64 of mouse Mfsd2a (A80 in Spns1) explaining why LPC binds closer to the central cavity of Spns1 than that of mouse Mfsd2a (Fig. 2C). Comparing Spns1 to the structure of human Spns2, TM5 and TM8 of Spns2 pack tightly against each other (Fig. 2D), leaving no space to engage a lipid cargo between them. For Spns2, the entire S1P molecule is inside the transport cavity (27–29). These findings suggest that Mfsd2a and Spns1, but not Spns2, utilize a similar LPC binding mode to accommodate the substrate for the initiation of the transport cycle.

To validate the LPC-binding site in Spns1, we conducted a cell-based LPC transport assay. Mutations in Spns1 that introduce potential steric hindrance at the LPC-binding site, including G200L on TM5, and G326L on TM8, dramatically reduced the transport activity compared to that of Spns1WT (Fig. 2E and SI Appendix, Fig. S4). Moreover, we introduced two mutations (P196F and V337W) between TM5 and TM8 to abolish the transport ability of Spns1 without interfering with the LPC binding site. These two mutants, as well as the mutant Y203A, exhibited substantially decreased LPC transport activity (Fig. 2E).

Moreover, we also performed a lipid pull-down assay to test the interaction between LPC and Spns1. Our results show that Spns1 can efficiently bind to fluorescently labeled 12:0 LPC (NBD-LPC), and this binding is competitively inhibited by unlabeled LPC; while Spns1 with mutations at the lateral opening (Y203A, G200L, and G326L) exhibited significantly reduced binding to NBD-LPC, confirming the specificity of the interaction (Fig. 2F and SI Appendix, Fig. S5). Thus, these findings support our structural observations that the hydrophobic residues on TM5 and TM8 in Spns1 form a cleft for the translocation of the acyl chain of LPC (Fig. 2 E and F and SI Appendix, Fig. S4). In addition to TM5/8, TM2/11 may also be used as a potential gate for the ligand. To test this, we mutated two conserved residues, I105A and Y436A, on the interface between TM2 and TM11 (SI Appendix, Fig. S6A). Our results showed that these changes on TM2 and TM11 did not affect the expression, localization, or transport activity of Spns1, confirming the crucial role of TM5 and TM8 in the LPC transport (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 B–D).

We also employed molecular dynamics (MD) simulations to study how the surrounding residues interact with the LPC substrate (SI Appendix, Fig. S7). Calculated interaction energies of LPC with residues within a radius of 4 Å, including the mutated ones, indicate that Spns1WT is energetically more stable than G220L, Y203A, and G326L mutants. Thus, this finding further supports our structural observation on the LPC-binding site of Spns1.

To gain further insights into the transport process of LPC, we conducted MD simulations to monitor the transport of LPC in Spns1 (Fig. 3A). Our findings indicated that after 20 ns simulations, LPC exhibited a propensity to enter the luminal cavity via the TM5 and TM8 entrance (Fig. 3B). However, the position of LPC at 50 ns was similar to that at 200 ns, suggesting that it may require longer time or other factors for LPC to penetrate deeper into the cavity (Fig. 3B). By superimposing the snapshots of LPC at various time points during MD, we observed the headgroup of LPC entering the central cavity of Spns1, while its tail extends toward the membrane surface (Fig. 3C) uncovering the transport process of LPC. Structural comparison between the Spns1 model at the 10 ns and 200 ns highlights several significant alterations to the residues within the LPC-binding site, which include the positional shift of the S322 and L327 residues on TM5 and Y203 residue on TM8 as well as F77 residue on TM1 (Fig. 3D). Interestingly, we observed that the F77 and Y203 residues demonstrated notable shifts toward the binding site, facilitating the movement of LPC toward the C-domain. Conversely, the residues S322 and L327 moved away from the binding site, likely mitigating potential steric clashes with LPC. The movement of the side chains of key residues likely allows LPC to move slightly deeper to the transport cavity of Spns1.

The Mechanism of Proton SensingThe proton gradient across the lysosomal membrane is essential for lysosomal transporters like cystinosin (33), Sialin (34), and SLC7A14 (35) to transport their substrates. To investigate the proton sensing mechanism of Spns1, we systematically mutated the glutamate and aspartate residues on the luminal leaflet of TMs including E87 and D94 in the hydrophilic network as mentioned above as well as D211 and D321 (SI Appendix, Fig. S3A) and tested the transport activity of these variants. The results showed that mutating D94 or D211, but not E87 and D321 to alanine caused a reduction of Spns1-mediated LPC transport (Fig. 4C). In the inward-facing state, E87 and D94 are accessible to the solvent from the lumen (Fig. 4A). Mutation of D94A in Spns1WT or in the active E87A mutant abolished transport activity, highlighting the critical role of D94. Interestingly, these reductions in LPC transport activity of Spns1 were partially rescued by the D94N variant (Fig. 4C), suggesting that protonation of the side chain of D94 is important for transport activity. D94 is found to interact with R300 and S442 (Fig. 4A). Thus, we hypothesize that once D94 receives the proton, the salt bridge between D94 and R300 as well as the hydrophilic interaction between D94 and S442 are disrupted. This disruption results in the movement of TM7 and TM11 toward to the edge of Spns1 (SI Appendix, Fig. S3A), thereby generating salt bridges and hydrophilic bonds (e.g., D177-R415 and R121-D263) (SI Appendix, Fig. S3B) and facilitating the opening of the luminal transport cavity. Consistent with our structural observations, the mutation R300A, which disrupts the salt bridge between R300 and D94, almost abolished the transport activity of Spns1 (Fig. 4C). These results highlight the central role of D94 in proton sensing during the transport cycle of Spns1.Previous works showed that Spns2 functions as a uniporter to transport S1P in a concentration gradient-dependent manner (27, 36). To further dissect the proton sensing mechanism of the Spinster homologues, we generated a Spns2 variant Spns2Q130E/A140S to introduce a similar luminal proton sensor as observed in Spns1 (Fig. 4 A and B) and tested the S1P transport activity of this Spns2 variant in both neutral and low pH environments. Spns2Q130E/A140S rendered Spns2 to be a pH-dependent transporter for S1P (Fig. 4 D–F). Interestingly, the Spns2-specific inhibitor SLF1081851 (also referred to as 16d) can still abolish the transport activity of this Spns2Q130E/A140S variant (37). Inversely, when these residues in Spns1, E87, and S97, were respectively mutated to glutamine and alanine, Spns1E87Q/S97A exhibited LPC transport activity at both neutral and low pH (Fig. 4 G and H). Thus, these results demonstrate the importance of the luminal interaction network for the proton sensing during lysosomal export of lysophospholipids by Spns1.

DiscussionAs an MFS transporter, Spns1 likely operates according to a rocker-switch model, wherein the N- and C-domains undergo rocking motions during the transport cycle, facilitating the opening or closing of the central cavity. Our structural analysis reveals that LPC binds to a lateral opening formed by TM5–TM8, providing LPC access into the central cavity. This binding mode in the initial stage of the transport cycle resembles a previous finding in mouse Mfsd2a, which cotransports LPC/Na+ across the lipid bilayer. Furthermore, a structural comparison suggests that the inward-opening state of gallus Mfsd2a bound to LPC aligns well with the AlphaFold-predicted inward-opening conformation of Spns1 (SI Appendix, Fig. S3F). This alignment suggests a similar translocation path formed by the hydrophobic residues between TMs 5 and 8 for LPC transport in these two structural homologues, Mfsd2a and Spns1. Recent studies showed that LPC can enter Mfsd2a in both TMs 5/8 and TMs 2/11 (38) and the gate between TMs 2/11 seems to be more important for lipid entry to the cavity of Mfsd2a (39). However, our structure and functional studies indicate that TMs 5/8 in Spns1 is crucial for the transportation of LPC. It is noteworthy that Mfsd2a functions as a sodium-dependent symporter, with its sodium binding site positioned at the center of the protein. The entire LPC molecule is found to be inside the Mfsd2a cavity in the inward-facing conformations. The phosphorylcholine group of LPC has been proposed to participate in displacing the sodium ion to initiate the conformational changes in Mfsd2a.
