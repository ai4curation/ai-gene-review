Cell walls are critical structures of fungi, bacteria, and plants, providing 
mechanical strength, maintaining shape, and protecting cells from environmental 
stress. In the fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe, the α-glucan synthase 
Ags1 produces α-1,3-glucan chains essential for cell wall integrity, but how 
these chains are further assembled into mature polymers is not understood. Here, 
we identify two conserved glycosylphosphatidylinositol-anchored α-amylase-like 
enzymes, Aah1 and Aah3, which act redundantly as key contributors to α-glucan 
network formation. Cells lacking both enzymes exhibit severe growth and 
morphological defects, including rounded shape, delayed division, and cell 
clumping. Using solid-state NMR spectroscopy of intact cells, we show that the 
double mutant cell walls have dramatically reduced α-1,3-glucan and 
galactomannan content, with a compensatory increase in β-glucans driven by the 
activation of the cell integrity pathway. These changes correlate with cell wall 
thickening, increased rigidity, and reduced polymer mobility and hydration. We 
also uncover in vivo polymorphic forms of α- and β-glucans, some of which are 
selectively lost or gained in the mutant cells. Our data suggest that Aah1 and 
Aah3 function as GH13-family transglycosylases that collaborate nonredundantly 
with the α-glucan synthase to build a properly organized α-glucan matrix. These 
findings highlight a previously unrecognized layer of complexity in fungal cell 
wall biosynthesis and point to GH13-family enzymes as potential antifungal 
targets given that related enzymes are found in many fungi.

## Full Text

The survival of many organisms depends on the structural support and protection provided by the cell wall, which acts as a critical barrier against environmental stresses such as changes in osmolarity and temperature (1–3). For cell-walled organisms, including bacteria, fungi, and plants, maintaining the integrity of this barrier while simultaneously allowing for growth and division presents a fundamental biological challenge (2–6). How cells build a cell wall that provides both the rigidity needed for protection and the flexibility required for remodeling remains poorly understood.

The fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe is an ideal model organism to investigate this open question because it is genetically tractable, exhibits a well-defined growth pattern and shares conserved cell cycle regulation with multicellular organisms (7–11). Like plants and bacteria, S. pombe is encompassed by a cell wall that undergoes dynamic remodeling throughout the cell cycle to accommodate tip growth and medial fission (12). A further advantage of S. pombe is that its cell wall architecture is well characterized. Electron microscopy studies revealed that the cell wall is organized in three distinct layers: an outer layer rich in galactomannan (GM), a medial layer composed primarily of α-1,3-glucan, β-1,3-glucan and β-1,6-glucan (Fig. 1), and an innermost layer adjacent to the plasma membrane containing GM and a dense meshwork of glucans (12–17). Several of the enzymes that synthesize these polysaccharides are essential for vegetative growth, including the α-glucan synthase Ags1 and the β-glucan synthases Bgs1, Bgs3, and Bgs4 (18–21).

β-glucan synthases are well studied across fungi and other walled organisms, leading to many insights into their structure, function, and regulation (22–24). In contrast, α-glucan synthases remain less well understood, despite their critical biological roles. Notably, most α-glucan synthases, including S. pombe Ags1, contain two catalytic domains: a cytoplasmic glycosyltransferase 5 (GT5) domain and an extracellular glycoside hydrolase 13 (GH13) domain (21, 25, 26). Both domains are essential for Ags1 function (26, 27). It is hypothesized that the GT5 domain synthesizes a linear ~120 residue α-1,3-glucan chain from an α-1,4-glucan primer and these chains are extruded through the transmembrane channel (13, 21). Subsequently, the Ags1 GH13 domain is suggested to act as a transglycosylase that joins the ~120 residue α-glucan chains together to construct a ~260 α-glucan chain (Fig. 1) (13). The bifunctional nature of α-glucan synthases likely imparts unique regulatory and catalytic properties compared to β-glucan synthases, and several questions remain about how α-glucan synthases function including whether they cooperate with other membrane proteins to form the α-glucan matrix.

Intriguingly, S. pombe encodes seven other proteins that also contain GH13 “α-amylase-like” domains (28). While classical α-amylases hydrolyze α-1,4-glucans, S. pombe cell lysates and their cultured media have no detectable α-amylase activity (29). And, like Ags1, the GH13 domains of these GH13 domain-containing proteins lack key catalytic residues typical of hydrolytic activity (28, 30). Thus, they are suggested to act as transglycosylases, enzymes that cleave and rejoin glucan chains, suggesting roles in α-glucan remodeling (31, 32). The shared features with Ags1 raise the possibility that these α-amylase-like proteins act in concert with or in parallel to Ags1 to assemble or extend α-glucan chains in the cell wall.

Five of the seven S. pombe α-amylase-like proteins are glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI)-anchored, positioning them at the membrane-cell wall interface (28, 29, 33–35). Because cell wall–degrading enzymes are typically secreted into the extracellular space, the membrane-associated localization of this subset of GH13-domain proteins further emphasizes their potential a role in α-glucan chain elongation rather than degradation. In S. pombe, the deletion of Aah3 results in a modest change in cell shape but deletions of the other genes individually have no morphological consequence (29). Similar GPI-anchored GH13-domain proteins are found in other fungi, including human pathogens such as Aspergillus nidulans. In A. nidulans, deletion of the GH13 enzyme AmyD leads to an increase in α-1,3-glucan levels, though its precise molecular function is undefined (36). However, whether other GPI-anchored GH13 proteins impact α-glucan levels similarly is not known but their widespread presence suggests that they are commonly employed for shaping fungal cell walls and could represent potential targets for antifungal intervention.

In this study, we comprehensively examined the GPI-anchored α-amylase-like enzymes in S. pombe to clarify their functions and involvement in cell wall biosynthesis. We found that cells lacking two of the five, Aah1 and Aah3, have drastic defects in cell shape; they are spherical rather than rod-shaped and fail to physically separate following cytokinesis, resulting in clumps of cells. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) showed that aah1∆ aah3∆ cells have abnormally thick cell walls and lack the typical three-layered organization of wildtype cell walls. This phenotype is correlated with limited water permeability and significantly reduced cell wall dynamics on both micro- and nanosecond timescales, as shown by solid-state NMR spectroscopy (37). Determination of cell wall composition by solid-state NMR uncovered previously unrecognized polymorphic forms of polysaccharides within the S. pombe cell wall and revealed that aah1∆ aah3∆ cells have a significant reduction in α-1,3-glucan and GM content. There is also a compensatory increase in β-1,3-glucan content in comparison to wildtype cells due to activation of the cell wall integrity pathway. The phenotypes of aah1∆ aah3∆ cells are strikingly similar to cells with defective Ags1 GH13 domain function (27). However, overexpression of aah3 failed to rescue defective ags1 GH13 function and overexpression of ags1 failed to rescue aah1∆ aah3∆ cell phenotypes. Thus, we propose that Aah1 and Aah3 are required in addition to Ags1 for transglycosylating α-glucan chains to create the appropriately sized fibers needed to maintain proper cell shape and allow for cell separation following cytokinesis. Together, these findings provide insights into α-glucan biosynthesis, shared and distinct roles of GH13-containing α-amylase-like enzymes, and yeast cell wall formation.

If the elevated level of β-1,3-glucan content in aah1∆ aah3∆ cells was a compensatory adaptation to the dramatic reduction in α-glucan content, aah3∆ cells would be expected to rely on β-1,3-glucan synthetase function. Indeed, aah3∆ cells had a mild negative genetic interaction with an allele of bgs4 (cwg1-1) and a strong negative genetic interaction with an allele of bgs1 (cps1-191) (SI Appendix, Fig. S4B).The Cell Integrity Pathway (CIP) (12, 54), a signaling cascade involving the protein kinase C homologs, Pck1 and Pck2, and the mitogen-activated protein kinase homolog, Pmk1, increases glucan synthase activity and gene expression when S. pombe cells sense defects in their cell walls (26, 55, 56). To test whether the upregulation of β-glucans in aah1Δ aah3Δ cells could be through CIP signaling, we combined pck2Δ or pmk1Δ with aah1Δ or aah3Δ, crossed these strains with each other, and analyzed the resultant colonies after tetrad dissection. We found that pck2Δ aah1Δ aah3Δ and pmk1Δ aah1Δ aah3Δ combinations were synthetically lethal (SI Appendix, Fig. S4F). aah1∆ aah3∆ Cells Have Altered Cell Wall Composition and Rigidity.

Because Aah1 and Aah3 are predicted cell wall modifying enzymes, we used solid-state NMR to compare the cell wall composition and polysaccharide structure between wildtype and aah1∆ aah3∆ cells. This spectroscopic approach has not previously been applied to intact fission yeast and offers several technical advantages, including the ability to directly analyze cell walls in intact, natively hydrated cells (37, 45–47). Additionally, it provides spatial information on cell wall composition across different domains with distinct dynamics and enables the detection of polymorphic polysaccharide forms (48). Polymorphs, which are chemically identical but structurally distinct, commonly occur in cellular environments and can arise due to variations in conformation, hydrogen-bonding patterns, or ligand binding (48–53).

Initial 1D solid-state NMR screening of intact wildtype and aah1∆ aah3∆ cells revealed significant alterations in the polysaccharide content and the polysaccharide distribution across dynamically distinct domains of the S. pombe cell wall (Fig. 4A). The rigid cell wall phase is typically attributed to molecules packed with chitin microfibrils in other fungal species. In S. pombe, however, this phase is likely formed by tightly clustered molecules and is predominantly composed of β-1,3-glucan and α-1,3-glucan, with evidence of polymorphic forms of α-1,3-glucan present in the wildtype, exemplified by two carbon 1 peaks at 101 and 100 ppm (Aa1 and Ab1) (Fig. 4 A and B). In comparison, the aah1∆ aah3∆ mutant exhibited a significantly reduced α-1,3-glucan content, with the Aa1 peak diminished to one-third of its original intensity and the Ab1 peak completely absent (Fig. 4C). These α-1,3-glucans and β-1,3-glucans were also detected in the mobile phase originating from the surface and membrane-proximal layers, which are rich in highly dynamic GM and glycoproteins. The mobile phase additionally contained contributions from β-1,6-glucan and α-1,2- and α-1,6-linked mannose units in mannan (Fig. 4A).

The rigid domain, critical for the mechanical integrity of the cell wall, was examined in detail using 2D 13C–13C correlation experiments. The analyses resolved all carbon sites in two allomorphic forms of β-1,3-glucans and two structural variants of α-1,3-glucans, which constitute the exclusive components of the rigid phase (Fig. 4B). We also confirmed the complete absence of chitin in this fungus (SI Appendix, Fig. S3), consistent with other studies (14, 15). In the wildtype sample, strong signals corresponding to type-a polymorph and type-b polymorph of α-1,3-glucans were detected, as evidenced by their C1-C2 cross peaks (Aa1-2 and Ab1-2, Fig. 4C). Notably, type-b α-1,3-glucans were entirely absent in the aah1∆ aah3∆ mutant, consistent with 1D spectral observations (Fig. 4 A–C). Intensity analysis also revealed a significant decrease in type-a α-1,3-glucan content, from 31% in the wildtype to 11% in the mutant (Fig. 4D). Consequently, the overall α-1,3-glucan content declined markedly, from 44% in wildtype rigid cell walls to 11% in the aah1∆ aah3∆ mutant.

The substantial reduction in α-1,3-glucan content in aah1∆ aah3∆ cells and their phenotype were reminiscent of cells with defective ags1 function, specifically the commonly used ags1-664 mutant (40). We therefore wondered whether the mutation within ags1-664, which had not previously precisely determined, resided within the Ags1 GH13 domain (26, 27). Indeed, sequencing of ags1-664 revealed Y256F and R408H substitutions within the GH13 domain (SI Appendix, Fig. S4A), underscoring its essential function. Given that ags1, aah1, and aah3 all encode GH13 domain-containing proteins, we tested whether there were negative genetic interactions between ags1-664 and the α-amylase defective cells. As predicted for genes in a common pathway, a strong negative genetic interaction was observed between aah3Δ and ags1-664 (SI Appendix, Fig. S4B). To test whether the Ags1 GH13 domain and Aah3 GH13 domains are functionally redundant, we overproduced Aah3 or Ags1 in aah1∆ aah3∆ cells. While overproduced Aah3 rescued the morphological defects of aah1∆ aah3∆ cells, overproduction of Ags1 did not and overproduced Aah3 did not rescue ags1-664 cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 C and D). We conclude that Aah1 and Aah3 enzymes are not functionally redundant with Ags1, and cells require both types of GH13 domain enzymes to support α-glucan chain assembly.

The weak β-1,6-glucan signals observed in the rigid fraction of the wildtype cell wall such as H3-4 and H5-6 cross peaks (Fig. 4B), which accounted for only 2% (Fig. 4D), were absent in the aah1∆ aah3∆ mutant. This molecule was primarily detected in the mobile phase, as indicated by the strong carbon 3 (H3) peak at 76.5 ppm in the 1D 13C spectra that captured either mobile molecules or all molecules (Fig. 4A) and further supported by 2D spectra in subsequent sections. These findings suggest that a minor fraction of β-1,6-glucan, initially colocalized with rigid α-1,3-glucans and partially rigidified in the wildtype sample, shifted entirely to the mobile phase upon the depletion of α-1,3-glucans in the aah1∆ aah3∆ mutant.

This substantial loss of α-1,3-glucans in aah1∆ aah3∆ cell walls was accompanied by the emergence of a polymorphic form of β-1,3-glucans. DiscussionIn this study, we applied solid-state NMR spectroscopy to analyze the cell wall composition and structure of intact fission yeast cells and to define the role of GPI-anchored α-amylase-like enzymes in cell wall biosynthesis. We found that deletion of two such enzymes, Aah1 and Aah3, leads to a dramatic reduction of cell wall α-1,3-glucans and GMs and a compensatory increase in β-1,3-glucans. These compositional shifts were reflected in thickened cell walls and loss of the outermost GM layer, as confirmed by imaging. Morphologically, aah1∆ aah3∆ cells were round, failed to separate after cytokinesis, and formed clumps. Notably, the aah1∆ aah3∆ phenotypes that severely compromised cell growth only emerged upon deletion of both genes, suggesting functional redundancy between these enzymes. This raises the possibility that, in other fungi, the contributions of similar GH13-domain proteins to cell wall architecture may be masked by overlapping activities and could explain why such enzymes have not been more broadly implicated in α-glucan remodeling.Solid-state NMR enables the direct characterization of the chemical composition and physical properties of intact cell walls. It has driven conceptual advances in understanding cell wall architecture across a diverse range of fungal species, including Aspergillus (66, 73–77), Cryptococcus (78–81), Candida (82), Schizophyllum (83–85), as well as Rhizopus and Mucor species (86). The application of this technique to S. pombe resulted in findings concordant with previous studies that analyzed cell wall composition of purified S. pombe cell wall material using various methods (87–91). Further, the spatial information obtained aligns with results from techniques such as immuno-electron microscopy (15, 16, 87, 92). This study also expands our understanding of the S. pombe cell wall with several findings, including insights into the distinct structural roles of various polysaccharides in cell wall construction. Additionally, we identified distinct polymorphic forms of α- and β-glucans in the wildtype wall, each with specific spatial organization and hydration profiles. These forms were altered or lost in the aah1∆ aah3∆ mutant, with β-glucans expanding in abundance and structural variability. In this study, we applied solid-state NMR spectroscopy to analyze the cell wall composition and structure of intact fission yeast cells and to define the role of GPI-anchored α-amylase-like enzymes in cell wall biosynthesis. We found that deletion of two such enzymes, Aah1 and Aah3, leads to a dramatic reduction of cell wall α-1,3-glucans and GMs and a compensatory increase in β-1,3-glucans. These compositional shifts were reflected in thickened cell walls and loss of the outermost GM layer, as confirmed by imaging. Morphologically, aah1∆ aah3∆ cells were round, failed to separate after cytokinesis, and formed clumps. Notably, the aah1∆ aah3∆ phenotypes that severely compromised cell growth only emerged upon deletion of both genes, suggesting functional redundancy between these enzymes. This raises the possibility that, in other fungi, the contributions of similar GH13-domain proteins to cell wall architecture may be masked by overlapping activities and could explain why such enzymes have not been more broadly implicated in α-glucan remodeling.

Solid-state NMR enables the direct characterization of the chemical composition and physical properties of intact cell walls. It has driven conceptual advances in understanding cell wall architecture across a diverse range of fungal species, including Aspergillus (66, 73–77), Cryptococcus (78–81), Candida (82), Schizophyllum (83–85), as well as Rhizopus and Mucor species (86). The application of this technique to S. pombe resulted in findings concordant with previous studies that analyzed cell wall composition of purified S. pombe cell wall material using various methods (87–91). Further, the spatial information obtained aligns with results from techniques such as immuno-electron microscopy (15, 16, 87, 92). This study also expands our understanding of the S. pombe cell wall with several findings, including insights into the distinct structural roles of various polysaccharides in cell wall construction. Additionally, we identified distinct polymorphic forms of α- and β-glucans in the wildtype wall, each with specific spatial organization and hydration profiles. These forms were altered or lost in the aah1∆ aah3∆ mutant, with β-glucans expanding in abundance and structural variability. Our genetic data suggest that the increased β-glucan content of aah1∆ aah3∆ cell walls is required for their viability and that the increase is achieved via CIP activity. This supports a feedback model where increased β-glucan synthesis partially compensates for α-glucan loss, mirroring previous findings that Ags1 overproduction reduces β-glucan content (26). Elucidating the mechanism of these compensatory relationships will be a fruitful area of future study.

The significant reduction of α-glucans in the aah1∆ aah3∆ double mutant contrasts somewhat with findings regarding the A. nidulans GPI-anchored GH13 enzyme, AmyD. The deletion of AmyD leads to an accumulation of α-1,3-glucan (36), suggesting a function in limiting or remodeling glucan content rather than promoting its formation. These opposing effects underscore functional diversity among membrane anchored GH13-domain proteins across fungi and highlight the need to individually characterize each enzyme to uncover their specific role in cell wall assembly.

The striking resemblance of the aah1∆ aah3∆ phenotype to that of cells with defective Ags1 GH13 domain function suggests that Aah1 and Aah3 are essential for α-glucan chain assembly or modification (Fig. 7C). Our data indicate that these enzymes function nonredundantly with Ags1, possibly acting downstream to elongate or restructure glucan chains. GH13-domain enzymes commonly catalyze transglycosylation, and unlike hydrolases, Aah1 and Aah3 lack the active site histidine typically required for cleavage activity (30). This suggests that Aah1 and Aah3 belong in a subclass of GH13 enzymes like the Aspergillus niger GPI-anchored GH13 enzymes that do not hydrolyze but rather exhibit 4-α-glucanotransferase (disproportionation) (30), moving glucan units between chains to adjust polymer length.

Solid-State NMR Experiments.

NMR experiments were conducted on two Bruker Avance Neo spectrometers at MSU Max T. Roger facility, with 1H Larmor frequencies of 800 MHz (18.8 T) and 400 MHz (9.4 T). All experiments were collected using two 3.2-mm MAS HCN probes under 15 kHz MAS at 293 K. 13C chemical shifts were externally referenced to the adamantane CH2 peak at 38.48 ppm on the tetramethyl silane (TMS) scale. Typical radiofrequency field strengths, unless specifically mentioned, were 80 to 100 kHz for 1H decoupling, 62.5 kHz for 1H hard pulses, and 50 to 62.5 kHz for 13C. The experimental parameters are summarized in SI Appendix, Table S2.

ssNMR experiments were carried out using 1D and 2D experimental methods. 1D 13C spectra were measured using different polarization methods to selectively detect the rigid and mobile components (SI Appendix, Fig. S9). The rigid components were detected by 1D 13C cross-polarization (CP) using 1-ms contact time (103, 104). Mobile components were measured by 1D 13C direct polarization (DP) using short (2 s) recycle delays. Extending the recycle delays to 35 s allowed us to achieve quantitative detection of all molecules through the same 13C DP experiment. The most mobile and highly solvated molecules are identified using a J-coupling based refocused INEPT experiments (105). Resonance assignment of rigid molecules was achieved through the measurement of 2D 13C–13C 53-ms CORD homonuclear correlation spectra (106), which detect intramolecular cross-peaks. For mobile molecules, 2D DP refocused J-INADEQUATE spectra were measured to track through bond connectivity, with each of the four tau delays optimized to 2.3-ms (107). The resolved chemical shifts were compared with the values indexed in the Complex Carbohydrate Magnetic Resonance Database to validate the chemical nature of the carbohydrates (108). In addition to carbohydrate signals, protein signals were also detected, primarily in the mobile phase (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). The 13C chemical shifts of carbohydrate and proteins identified in S. pombe have been tabulated in SI Appendix, Table S3.

Water accessibility of polysaccharides was accessed using a water-edited 2D 13C–13C correlation experiment (70, 71). The experiment initiated with 1H excitation followed by a 1H-T2 filter (1.0-ms × 2 for the wildtype sample and 1.4-ms × 2 for the mutant), which eliminated 95% of carbohydrate signals but retained 70 to 80% of water magnetization (SI Appendix, Fig. S7). The magnetization of water was then transferred to polysaccharide using a 4-ms 1H mixing period and then transferred to 13C through a 1-ms 1H-13C CP for 13C detection. A 50-ms DARR mixing period was used for both the water-edited spectrum and a control 2D spectrum showing full intensity. The ratio between the intensities of the water-edited spectrum (S) and the control spectrum (S0) was quantified; these S/S0 ratios reflect the relative extent of hydration at different carbon sites. The intensities were prenormalized by the number of scans of each spectrum.

Polysaccharide dynamics were examined using 13C-detected 1H-T1ρ relaxation, which was also measured using a Lee-Goldburg (LG) spinlock sequence combined with LG-CP for suppressing 1H spin diffusion during both the spinlock and the CP contact. This allowed site-specific examination of the 1H-T1ρ relaxation of protons sites through detection of their directly bonded carbons. The decay of peak intensity was fit to both a single exponential function (SI Appendix, Table S4) and a double exponential function (SI Appendix, Table S5). In addition, 13C spin-lattice (T1) relaxation was measured, where the z-filter duration ranged from 0.1-μs to 12-s (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 and Table S5) (109). Each well-resolved peak was considered and the decay of its intensity was quantified as the z-filter time increased, and the data were fit to a single exponential equation to calculate the 13C-T1 relaxation time constant. The absolute intensity of each peak was prenormalized by the number of scans. All the spectra were analyzed using Topspin 4.3.0 and all the graphs were generated through OriginPro 9.0. Illustrative figures were prepared using Adobe Illustrator 2023 V27.2.0.

The molecular composition was estimated by analyzing the intensities of distinct carbohydrate signals observed in two-dimensional 13C spectra (86). Rigid molecules were analyzed using CORD spectra, while mobile molecules were evaluated using DP refocused J-INADEQUATE spectra. The estimation was based on peak volumes, with only well-resolved signals included in the analysis. The relative abundance of each polysaccharide was quantified by normalizing the summation of integrated peak volumes by their respective cross-peak counts. The SE for each polysaccharide was calculated by dividing the SD of the integrated peak volumes by the total cross-peak count. The overall SE was determined as the square root of the sum of the squared SE for all polysaccharides. To estimate the percentage error for each polysaccharide, the SE of the specific polysaccharide was divided by its average integrated peak volume. This value was then multiplied by the ratio of the polysaccharide’s SE to the total integrated peak volume, adjusted by its relative abundance.
