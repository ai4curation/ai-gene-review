# Crisp proteins and sperm chemotaxis: discovery in amphibians and explorations in mammals.
**Authors:** Burnett LA, Xiang X, Bieber AL, Chandler DE
**Journal:** Int J Dev Biol (2008)
**DOI:** [10.1387/ijdb.072545lb](https://doi.org/10.1387/ijdb.072545lb)

## Abstract

1. Int J Dev Biol. 2008;52(5-6):489-501. doi: 10.1387/ijdb.072545lb.

Crisp proteins and sperm chemotaxis: discovery in amphibians and explorations in 
mammals.

Burnett LA(1), Xiang X, Bieber AL, Chandler DE.

Author information:
(1)Molecular and Cellular Biology Program, School of Life Sciences, Arizona 
State University, Tempe, AZ 85287-4501, USA.

Crisp proteins appear to play multiple roles in the life history of sperm. One 
of these roles is to act as a sperm chemoattractant. Allurin, a 21 kDa Crisp 
protein rapidly released from the egg jelly of at least two frogs, X. laevis and 
X. tropicalis, elicits directed motility in both homospecific and heterospecific 
sperm. In X. tropicalis, allurin is coded for by the newly documented Crisp A 
gene. Recently, the observation that allurin can also elicit chemotaxis in mouse 
sperm raises the question of whether allurin-like proteins might act as sperm 
chemoattractants in mammals. Although an allurin gene has yet to be documented 
in mammals, Crisp proteins truncated post-translationally appear to exist in 
both the male and female reproductive tract of mammals.
