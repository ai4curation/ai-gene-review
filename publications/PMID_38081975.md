The packaging of DNA into chromatin in eukaryotes regulates gene transcription, 
DNA replication and DNA repair. ATP-dependent chromatin remodelling enzymes 
(re)arrange nucleosomes at the first level of chromatin organization. Their 
Snf2-type motor ATPases alter histone-DNA interactions through a common DNA 
translocation mechanism. Whether remodeller activities mainly catalyse 
nucleosome dynamics or accurately co-determine nucleosome organization remained 
unclear. In this Review, we discuss the emerging mechanisms of chromatin 
remodelling: dynamic remodeller architectures and their interactions, the inner 
workings of the ATPase cycle, allosteric regulation and pathological 
dysregulation. Recent mechanistic insights argue for a decisive role of 
remodellers in the energy-driven self-organization of chromatin, which enables 
both stability and plasticity of genome regulation - for example, during 
development and stress. Different remodellers, such as members of the SWI/SNF, 
ISWI, CHD and INO80 families, process (epi)genetic information through specific 
mechanisms into distinct functional outputs. Combinatorial assembly of 
remodellers and their interplay with histone modifications, histone variants, 
DNA sequence or DNA-bound transcription factors regulate nucleosome mobilization 
or eviction or histone exchange. Such input-output relationships determine 
specific nucleosome positions and compositions with distinct DNA accessibilities 
and mediate differential genome regulation. Finally, remodeller genes are often 
mutated in diseases characterized by genome dysregulation, notably in cancer, 
and we discuss their physiological relevance.

Introduction

In eukaryotes, ATP-dependent chromatin remodelling enzymes (“remodellers”) enable genome regulation and maintenance by actively shaping the packaging of DNA. DNA packaging into chromatin is a multi-level self-organization [G] process1 (FIG. 1a) that relies on continuous turnover mediated by energy-dependent enzymes such as remodellers, loop extrusion factors2, polymerases, DNA topoisomerases and histone and DNA modifiers. This turnover generates both stability and plasticity in cellular responses, e.g., following environmental cues or during development, as the energy-dependent enzymes, together with other nuclear factors, establish intricate input–output relationships. One major output of chromatin self-organisation is the regulation of DNA accessibility and thereby of genome activities, including transcription, DNA replication and DNA repair.

Remodellers have crucial roles in chromatin self-organization: their interactions with chromatin regulate how they couple chemical energy from ATP hydrolysis with mechanical movements of their own modules and consequently of the DNA they bind to (FIG. 1b). Thus, remodellers actively organize the first level of chromatin, the nucleosomes (FIG. 1b,c). Nucleosome core particles (NCPs) consist of 145–147 base pairs (bp) of DNA wound around a histone protein octamer3 (see also Supplementary Movie 4 in ref. 4). Linker DNA of variable length connects between NCPs, and the term nucleosome originally refers to NCP plus linker DNA. Nucleosomes are highly organized both in their positions along the DNA and in their composition of histone variants5 and posttranslational modifications6. This organization may be either obstructive or instructive for DNA-templated reactions. For example, nucleosomes may occlude transcription-factor binding sites and thus repress transcription7,8. Conversely, efficient replication requires specific positioning of nucleosomes around replication origins9, and specific histone variants and modifications characterize loci undergoing DNA repair10,11. The complexity and self-organizing stability and plasticity of chromatin states [G] provide a basis for epigenetic regulation [G], which is maintained without sustained signalling (thus functioning as “cellular memory”) and enables different functions to unfold from the same genome12. The central question we discuss here is how an interplay of factors (e.g. transcription factors, chromatin enzymes, RNA) and processes (e.g. remodelling, transcription, replication) provides the information that determines how specific chromatin states are established, maintained and interconverted. One classical answer invokes the histone code hypothesis13–15, which inspired seminal studies of “writer–reader–eraser” mechanisms forming histone and DNA modification patterns6. However, such patterns are linked to nucleosome organization [G]. The molecular mechanisms by which remodellers organize nucleosomes have emerged only recently. We now begin to understand how remodellers recognise nucleosome-related features such as DNA shape and mechanics [G], histone modifications or variants, neighbouring nucleosomes and non-histone factors (FIG. 1c), and process such signals in remodeller-specific manners into specific outputs. This activity makes remodellers true co-determinants of chromatin states and the functional outcomes of (epi)genetic regulation and not just catalysts of chromatin fluidity.

Each eukaryote harbors several remodellers, from monomers to multi-subunit complexes, which are subdivided into families16. The main families are denoted SWI/SNF, INO80, ISWI and CHD (Supplementary Table 1), according to features of their Snf2-type motor ATPases. Snf2-type ATPases directionally translocate DNA using chemo-mechanical cycles of ATP binding and hydrolysis (FIG. 1b). On nucleosomes, this translocation alters and breaks histone–DNA contacts, which catalyzes DNA mobilization around histones (“nucleosome sliding”), histone ejection, and exchange of histone variants or (un)modified histones.

A note on nomenclature. We designate multi-subunit remodeller complexes in all-capital letters, sometimes in combination with numbers, e.g., human BAF or yeast SWR1 complex (Supplementary Table 1). For metazoan species, individual subunits are also designated with capital letters, e.g., SMARCA2 is a Snf2-type ATPase subunit of BAF. For yeast subunits, only the initial letter is upper case, e.g., Snf2 is the ATPase subunit of the yeast SWI/SNF complex, which is the first identified remodeller17 that gave its name to a whole remodeller family. Remodeller nomenclature can be confusing as there are often overlaps between nomenclature levels, e.g., INO80 denotes both a remodeller family as well as particular remodeller complexes within this family, whereas Ino80 denotes the ATPase subunit of the yeast INO80 complex. For human and yeast nomenclature of remodeller families, complexes and their subunits see Supplementary Table 1.

In this Review, we discuss recent structural and mechanistic data that deepen our understanding of remodeller mechanisms and regulation. We define concepts of energy-driven nucleosome organization by remodellers, provide an overview of remodeller architectures, summarize shared features of the motor ATPase and discuss how remodellers recognise and process (epi)genetic information. Finally, we discuss mechanism-based insights into the consequences of remodeller mutations in cancer.

Often, arrays are not only regular with regards to distances between nucleosomes, but are also aligned or phased[G] relative to a reference point (a barrier [G]). A stereotypical nucleosome organization is found at the 5’ ends of active genes in most eukaryotes7,34 (FIG. 1a; FIG. 2b; Animation 1). The promoter region is devoid of nucleosomes and thus termed nucleosome free or nucleosome depleted region34–36 (NFR or NDR, respectively); it is flanked by the well-positioned so-called +1 nucleosome, the first nucleosome downstream of the transcription start site (TSS). Further downstream, +2, +3 etc. nucleosomes are regularly spaced over the gene body and form an array that is phased to the +1 nucleosome. Although this nucleosome organization is conducive to transcription, it can be generated in vivo prior to transcription37,38.At first, it seemed that DNA sequence-intrinsic preferences to bend around the histone octamer provide a sequence-encoded energy landscape that determines such nucleosome organizations39. However, it has become clear that DNA–histone interactions alone are not sufficient to establish spaced and phased organizations23,24, and that remodellers specify nucleosome positioning patterns. Remodeller-specific nucleosome positioning was observed in early in vitro experiments as some remodellers positioned single nucleosomes at the center of a short DNA fragment whereas other remodellers positioned it at the ends29,40. Also early on, ISWI41,42 and CHD43, and later also INO80 remodellers28,30,44, were found to equalize linker lengths (indicative of spacing activity) in poly-nucleosomal substrates in vitro with remodeller-specific differences in the resulting linker lengths43,45. Recently, remodeller-specific nucleosome spacing and array phasing was comprehensively demonstrated for whole genomes and with different barriers and at different nucleosome densities in vitro28 and through single or combined remodeller depletion(s) in yeast46–52 and human cells53. The remodeller features that co-determine nucleosome spacing and array phasing were termed “remodeller rulers”54 and they begin to be identified in structural terms28,54,55.An unexpected first case of entirely remodeller-specified nucleosome positioning was recently revealed for the yeast INO80 complex30,56. Through its remodelling mechanism, INO80 reads genomic DNA mechanical properties56,57, which differ from classical DNA-binding preferences of histone octamers, and translates such information into stable nucleosome positions30,58. This mechanism explains +1 nucleosome positioning at TSS by INO80 in absence of other factors30,58, but INO80 can also position nucleosomes synergistically with barrier factors58. Sequence-dependent remodelling was observed for the yeast remodel the structure of chromatin (RSC) complex30,36,59–61, which preferentially and directionally removes nucleosomes from poly(dA:dT) sequences, which are enriched in yeast promoters34,35, and for yeast Chd1, which also discriminates between poly(dA:dT) sequences and other DNA features during nucleosome positioning62,63, and is likely the case also for other remodellers64,65.The cooperation between remodellers and other chromatin and transcription factors is understood best in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, both from genome-wide in vitro reconstitution assays with purified factors28,30,58 and from in vivo studies35,46–49,51,52,61,66–72 (FIG. 2b; Animation 1). The SWI/SNF-family remodeller RSC disassembles nucleosomes from the promoter region. In some conditions, the other yeast SWI/SNF-family remodeller, which gave its name to this family — the SWI/SNF complex — is important for nucleosome disassembly, too60,71,73. INO80 (ref.30,58) or the ISWI-family ISW1a or ISW2 (ref.49,68) remodellers position the +1 nucleosome. SWR1 of the INO80-family catalyzes the exchange of histone dimers H2A–H2B with H2A.Z–H2B variant dimers74, especially at the +1 nucleosome75. The role of this dimers exchange is not well understood76, but it is highly conserved across eukaryotes and may be involved in TSS selection or transcription initiation, as may be the positioned +1 nucleosome in general77. ISW1a and possibly other remodellers with spacing activity align downstream nucleosomes into a regular genic array. This array is disturbed by passage of RNA polymerases33,52,78, which may generate irregular spacing and partially disassembled nucleosomes, for example hexasomes79,80, which are non-canonical nucleosomal particles [G] that lack one H2A–H2B dimer. The CHD-family member Chd1 particularly regenerates the regular array, but other remodellers, such as ISW1a or INO80, are likely involved too50–52,81. INO80 remodels hexasomes in vitro and is suggested to remodel transcription-induced hexasomes in vivo82, whereas ISW1b and Chd1 may resolve stacked di-nucleosomal particles83, which may arise from nucleosome collisions84.Nucleosome spacing and array phasing are also generated by remodellers at other functional sites (FIG. 2c), e.g. at mammalian CCCTC-binding factor [G] (CTCF) binding sites32,85 and at yeast replication origins9, where CTCF or the origin recognition complex, respectively, provide the reference points (barriers) for array phasing. DNA double-strand breaks also serve as barriers for remodellers with spacing activity in vitro28,58 and a range of remodellers are recruited to DNA damage repair sites10. It is not clear yet, which nucleosome organization is generated at repair sites in vivo and whether phased arrays have a role in DNA repair.Kinetic trapping of remodelling outcomesNucleosome organization by remodellers does not require continuous remodeller binding and activity, as the resulting nucleosome organization persists following dissociation of the remodeller: fourteen salt bridges between the DNA phosphates and the histones3 render nucleosomes largely kinetically trapped. By themselves, nucleosomes move along or leave the DNA or exchange histones at rather non-physiological time scales or extent, as shown by negative controls lacking remodeller or ATP in many in vitro remodelling assays, and without much effect of different genomic sequences86–89. Different nucleosome organizations correspond to rather similar energy levels, i.e., they occur to similar extents at equilibrium, but are separated by large activation energy barriers (FIG. 2d). Using ATP hydrolysis energy, chromatin-bound remodellers lower these activation energy barriers, alter the energy landscape and impose their own organization preferences, which leads to directional nucleosome dynamics and may even override nucleosome-intrinsic preferences. For example, ISWI remodellers remove nucleosomes from sequences that intrinsically favour nucleosome formation90 and onto A/T-rich sequences49, which intrinsically disfavor nucleosome formation36,39. Following the remodelling reaction, the remodeller-specified organization will be kinetically trapped and remain populated, although it may be not more or even less energetically favorable than other organizations. This way, remodellers generate specific nucleosome organizations that represent high-energy states, simply owing to the lowered entropy resulting from generation of one specific state out of many possible states (FIG. 2a), or as they deviate from intrinsically preferred states, and that are crucial for the proper conductance and regulation of DNA-templated processes, such as transcription initiation.In general, epigenetic regulation relies on a molecular memory of highly organized states and kinetic trapping is likely key at different levels of such regulation. Energy of activated metabolites, such as acetyl-CoA and S-adenosyl methionine (which are required for chromatin acetylation and methylation, respectively), becomes kinetically trapped in covalent C–N or C–C bonds of acetylated or methylated histones or DNA and is only released if the activation energy barrier is lowered by relevant enzymes, in this example deacetylases or demethylases. Nevertheless, it had remained often less clear, how energy-driven nucleosome reorganizations, which are the basis of chromatin acetylation and methylation patterns, contribute to epigenetic memory. Here, we propose that the interplay between remodeller activities, which generate kinetically trapped nucleosome organizations, and context-dependent chromatin modification patterns establishes and maintains epigenetic memory.Balanced interplay between remodellersAlthough kinetic trapping stabilizes epigenetic states without requiring continuous binding of all factors that generated these states, there is nonetheless continuous turnover of nucleosome positions and composition, also owing to polymerase passages, which necessitates continuous re-generation of chromatin states. Nucleosome organizations quickly decay upon ad hoc depletion of remodellers in vivo, which shows that a tightly regulated interplay between remodellers is continuously required for chromatin self-organization46,53,91–93. If one remodeller type is deficient owing to experimental procedures or to disease-causing mutations, other remodellers impose their specific organization preferences and may generate different, dysregulated chromatin states.As the hallmarks of promoter nucleosome organization can be generated in vitro by the mere combination of different remodellers with nucleosomes and barriers in a purified reconstitution system30, some coordination between remodellers is intrinsic to the remodeller machineries. For example, RSC disassembles nucleosomes preferentially at poly(dA:dT)-enriched promoters36,59, thereby overriding activities of other remodellers in these regions, and the generation of short linker DNA by ISW1a or Chd1 dominates over the generation of longer linkers by ISW2 (ref.28,50). Nonetheless, the targeting and regulated interplay of remodellers in vivo may also involve histone modifications or variants and recruitment by DNA-binding proteins or other chromatin features and is not yet well understood.Crosstalk with higher levels of chromatin organizationIt is also still unclear how the first level of chromatin organization, as shaped by remodellers, influences higher levels of chromatin organization. The remodeller-generated regular nucleosome arrays with different average linker lengths and punctuations by NFRs28,30,35,36,46–53,58,60,61,66–73 likely affect chromatin fiber packing as such32, and also the accessibility of binding sites for architectural factors such as CTCF53 and for transcription, replication or repair factors (FIG. 2c). These factors in turn regulate genome activities that modulate and are modulated by chromatin organization in poorly understood chicken-and-egg dependencies (FIG. 2b). Remodellers may also continuously energize chromatin organization in the context of chromatin fiber movements94 or LLPS in the nucleus. Remodellers were proposed to act as “molecular stir bars” that counteract LLPS95. By contrast, some remodeller subunits, such as human SS18 (ref.96), human ARID1 (ref.97)and yeast Taf14 (ref.98) (Supplementary Table 1) may undergo phase separation themselves and regulate remodelling. Different remodeller-generated linker lengths in nucleosome arrays modulate the intrinsic LLPS properties of chromatin fibers99. How remodellers are involved in or affected by LLPS requires clarification100.

In essence, remodellers “energize” chromatin by contributing to the generation of highly organized chromatin states (FIG. 2a,d).

Remodellers organize nucleosome positions and compositions

A prominent case of remodeller-specified organizations are the non-random nucleosome positions along genomes, such as the abundant arrays of regularly spaced nucleosomes [G]32,33. Often, arrays are not only regular with regards to distances between nucleosomes, but are also aligned or phased[G] relative to a reference point (a barrier [G]). A stereotypical nucleosome organization is found at the 5’ ends of active genes in most eukaryotes7,34 (FIG. 1a; FIG. 2b; Animation 1). The promoter region is devoid of nucleosomes and thus termed nucleosome free or nucleosome depleted region34–36 (NFR or NDR, respectively); it is flanked by the well-positioned so-called +1 nucleosome, the first nucleosome downstream of the transcription start site (TSS). Further downstream, +2, +3 etc. nucleosomes are regularly spaced over the gene body and form an array that is phased to the +1 nucleosome. Although this nucleosome organization is conducive to transcription, it can be generated in vivo prior to transcription37,38.

At first, it seemed that DNA sequence-intrinsic preferences to bend around the histone octamer provide a sequence-encoded energy landscape that determines such nucleosome organizations39. However, it has become clear that DNA–histone interactions alone are not sufficient to establish spaced and phased organizations23,24, and that remodellers specify nucleosome positioning patterns. Remodeller-specific nucleosome positioning was observed in early in vitro experiments as some remodellers positioned single nucleosomes at the center of a short DNA fragment whereas other remodellers positioned it at the ends29,40. Also early on, ISWI41,42 and CHD43, and later also INO80 remodellers28,30,44, were found to equalize linker lengths (indicative of spacing activity) in poly-nucleosomal substrates in vitro with remodeller-specific differences in the resulting linker lengths43,45. Recently, remodeller-specific nucleosome spacing and array phasing was comprehensively demonstrated for whole genomes and with different barriers and at different nucleosome densities in vitro28 and through single or combined remodeller depletion(s) in yeast46–52 and human cells53. The remodeller features that co-determine nucleosome spacing and array phasing were termed “remodeller rulers”54 and they begin to be identified in structural terms28,54,55.

An unexpected first case of entirely remodeller-specified nucleosome positioning was recently revealed for the yeast INO80 complex30,56. Through its remodelling mechanism, INO80 reads genomic DNA mechanical properties56,57, which differ from classical DNA-binding preferences of histone octamers, and translates such information into stable nucleosome positions30,58. This mechanism explains +1 nucleosome positioning at TSS by INO80 in absence of other factors30,58, but INO80 can also position nucleosomes synergistically with barrier factors58. Sequence-dependent remodelling was observed for the yeast remodel the structure of chromatin (RSC) complex30,36,59–61, which preferentially and directionally removes nucleosomes from poly(dA:dT) sequences, which are enriched in yeast promoters34,35, and for yeast Chd1, which also discriminates between poly(dA:dT) sequences and other DNA features during nucleosome positioning62,63, and is likely the case also for other remodellers64,65.

The cooperation between remodellers and other chromatin and transcription factors is understood best in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, both from genome-wide in vitro reconstitution assays with purified factors28,30,58 and from in vivo studies35,46–49,51,52,61,66–72 (FIG. 2b; Animation 1). The SWI/SNF-family remodeller RSC disassembles nucleosomes from the promoter region. In some conditions, the other yeast SWI/SNF-family remodeller, which gave its name to this family — the SWI/SNF complex — is important for nucleosome disassembly, too60,71,73. INO80 (ref.30,58) or the ISWI-family ISW1a or ISW2 (ref.49,68) remodellers position the +1 nucleosome. SWR1 of the INO80-family catalyzes the exchange of histone dimers H2A–H2B with H2A.Z–H2B variant dimers74, especially at the +1 nucleosome75. The role of this dimers exchange is not well understood76, but it is highly conserved across eukaryotes and may be involved in TSS selection or transcription initiation, as may be the positioned +1 nucleosome in general77. ISW1a and possibly other remodellers with spacing activity align downstream nucleosomes into a regular genic array. This array is disturbed by passage of RNA polymerases33,52,78, which may generate irregular spacing and partially disassembled nucleosomes, for example hexasomes79,80, which are non-canonical nucleosomal particles [G] that lack one H2A–H2B dimer. The CHD-family member Chd1 particularly regenerates the regular array, but other remodellers, such as ISW1a or INO80, are likely involved too50–52,81. INO80 remodels hexasomes in vitro and is suggested to remodel transcription-induced hexasomes in vivo82, whereas ISW1b and Chd1 may resolve stacked di-nucleosomal particles83, which may arise from nucleosome collisions84.

Nucleosome spacing and array phasing are also generated by remodellers at other functional sites (FIG. 2c), e.g. at mammalian CCCTC-binding factor [G] (CTCF) binding sites32,85 and at yeast replication origins9, where CTCF or the origin recognition complex, respectively, provide the reference points (barriers) for array phasing. DNA double-strand breaks also serve as barriers for remodellers with spacing activity in vitro28,58 and a range of remodellers are recruited to DNA damage repair sites10. It is not clear yet, which nucleosome organization is generated at repair sites in vivo and whether phased arrays have a role in DNA repair.

Kinetic trapping of remodelling outcomesNucleosome organization by remodellers does not require continuous remodeller binding and activity, as the resulting nucleosome organization persists following dissociation of the remodeller: fourteen salt bridges between the DNA phosphates and the histones3 render nucleosomes largely kinetically trapped. By themselves, nucleosomes move along or leave the DNA or exchange histones at rather non-physiological time scales or extent, as shown by negative controls lacking remodeller or ATP in many in vitro remodelling assays, and without much effect of different genomic sequences86–89. Different nucleosome organizations correspond to rather similar energy levels, i.e., they occur to similar extents at equilibrium, but are separated by large activation energy barriers (FIG. 2d). Using ATP hydrolysis energy, chromatin-bound remodellers lower these activation energy barriers, alter the energy landscape and impose their own organization preferences, which leads to directional nucleosome dynamics and may even override nucleosome-intrinsic preferences. For example, ISWI remodellers remove nucleosomes from sequences that intrinsically favour nucleosome formation90 and onto A/T-rich sequences49, which intrinsically disfavor nucleosome formation36,39. Following the remodelling reaction, the remodeller-specified organization will be kinetically trapped and remain populated, although it may be not more or even less energetically favorable than other organizations. This way, remodellers generate specific nucleosome organizations that represent high-energy states, simply owing to the lowered entropy resulting from generation of one specific state out of many possible states (FIG. 2a), or as they deviate from intrinsically preferred states, and that are crucial for the proper conductance and regulation of DNA-templated processes, such as transcription initiation.In general, epigenetic regulation relies on a molecular memory of highly organized states and kinetic trapping is likely key at different levels of such regulation. Energy of activated metabolites, such as acetyl-CoA and S-adenosyl methionine (which are required for chromatin acetylation and methylation, respectively), becomes kinetically trapped in covalent C–N or C–C bonds of acetylated or methylated histones or DNA and is only released if the activation energy barrier is lowered by relevant enzymes, in this example deacetylases or demethylases. Nevertheless, it had remained often less clear, how energy-driven nucleosome reorganizations, which are the basis of chromatin acetylation and methylation patterns, contribute to epigenetic memory. Here, we propose that the interplay between remodeller activities, which generate kinetically trapped nucleosome organizations, and context-dependent chromatin modification patterns establishes and maintains epigenetic memory.

This way, remodellers generate specific nucleosome organizations that represent high-energy states, simply owing to the lowered entropy resulting from generation of one specific state out of many possible states (FIG. 2a), or as they deviate from intrinsically preferred states, and that are crucial for the proper conductance and regulation of DNA-templated processes, such as transcription initiation.

In general, epigenetic regulation relies on a molecular memory of highly organized states and kinetic trapping is likely key at different levels of such regulation. Energy of activated metabolites, such as acetyl-CoA and S-adenosyl methionine (which are required for chromatin acetylation and methylation, respectively), becomes kinetically trapped in covalent C–N or C–C bonds of acetylated or methylated histones or DNA and is only released if the activation energy barrier is lowered by relevant enzymes, in this example deacetylases or demethylases. Nevertheless, it had remained often less clear, how energy-driven nucleosome reorganizations, which are the basis of chromatin acetylation and methylation patterns, contribute to epigenetic memory. Here, we propose that the interplay between remodeller activities, which generate kinetically trapped nucleosome organizations, and context-dependent chromatin modification patterns establishes and maintains epigenetic memory.

Balanced interplay between remodellersAlthough kinetic trapping stabilizes epigenetic states without requiring continuous binding of all factors that generated these states, there is nonetheless continuous turnover of nucleosome positions and composition, also owing to polymerase passages, which necessitates continuous re-generation of chromatin states. Nucleosome organizations quickly decay upon ad hoc depletion of remodellers in vivo, which shows that a tightly regulated interplay between remodellers is continuously required for chromatin self-organization46,53,91–93. If one remodeller type is deficient owing to experimental procedures or to disease-causing mutations, other remodellers impose their specific organization preferences and may generate different, dysregulated chromatin states.As the hallmarks of promoter nucleosome organization can be generated in vitro by the mere combination of different remodellers with nucleosomes and barriers in a purified reconstitution system30, some coordination between remodellers is intrinsic to the remodeller machineries. For example, RSC disassembles nucleosomes preferentially at poly(dA:dT)-enriched promoters36,59, thereby overriding activities of other remodellers in these regions, and the generation of short linker DNA by ISW1a or Chd1 dominates over the generation of longer linkers by ISW2 (ref.28,50). Nonetheless, the targeting and regulated interplay of remodellers in vivo may also involve histone modifications or variants and recruitment by DNA-binding proteins or other chromatin features and is not yet well understood.

Crosstalk with higher levels of chromatin organizationIt is also still unclear how the first level of chromatin organization, as shaped by remodellers, influences higher levels of chromatin organization. The remodeller-generated regular nucleosome arrays with different average linker lengths and punctuations by NFRs28,30,35,36,46–53,58,60,61,66–73 likely affect chromatin fiber packing as such32, and also the accessibility of binding sites for architectural factors such as CTCF53 and for transcription, replication or repair factors (FIG. 2c). These factors in turn regulate genome activities that modulate and are modulated by chromatin organization in poorly understood chicken-and-egg dependencies (FIG. 2b). Remodellers may also continuously energize chromatin organization in the context of chromatin fiber movements94 or LLPS in the nucleus. Remodellers were proposed to act as “molecular stir bars” that counteract LLPS95. By contrast, some remodeller subunits, such as human SS18 (ref.96), human ARID1 (ref.97)and yeast Taf14 (ref.98) (Supplementary Table 1) may undergo phase separation themselves and regulate remodelling. Different remodeller-generated linker lengths in nucleosome arrays modulate the intrinsic LLPS properties of chromatin fibers99. Remodeller mechanisms of functionAlthough early-on it was debated whether remodeller-specific outcomes are driven by fundamentally different mechanisms of function101,102, the Snf2-type motor ATPase domains of remodellers bind DNA in a highly similar fashion, supporting the view that similar DNA conformational changes or translocation steps underly the different larger-scale remodelling outcomes. In this section we discuss how the evolutionarily conserved DNA translocation mechanism is geared towards providing different remodelling outcomes.Modular assembly of remodeller complexesRemodeller-specific mechanisms emanate from the variety of remodeller architectures that likely evolved from an ancestral superfamily 2 (SF2) NTPase — a helicase or nucleic acid translocase103,104 (FIG. 3a). Like all SF2 NTPases, Snf2-type ATPases contain two approximately equally sized RecA-fold domains — RecA1 and RecA2 (related to the Escherichia coli recombination factor RecA) — that form a two-lobed structure, composed of an amino-terminus lobe (N-lobe) and a carboxy-terminus lobe (C-lobe), along with family-specific and protein-specific structural features (FIG. 3a). ATP-driven repositioning of the N-lobe and C-lobe relative to each other drives DNA translocation, binding–release or distortion of nucleic acid substrates (see Supplementary Movie 3 in ref. 4). The motor ATPase subunits of remodellers harbor such a Snf2-type ATPase domain, but they can also possess other regulatory and targeting domains, and serve as scaffold for the association with other scaffold or regulatory subunits (FIG. 3b). This assembly mode evolved at least eight remodellers in S. cerevisiae and at least 20 in humans, or even more considering combinatorial assembly of subunit isoforms29,105. Supplementary Table 1 provides an overview of yeast and human remodellers from the four most-studied remodeller families.SWI/SNF and INO80 remodellers are large (~1 MDa) complexes (of >10 subunits) that assemble in a modular fashion and share common subunits, such as monomeric nuclear actin and actin related proteins (ARPs) [G]106 (FIG. 3c; Animation 2; Animation 3). In yeast, actin and Arps assemble into an Arp module with the helicase-SANT-associated (HSA) domain of motor ATPase subunits such as Ino80 or Snf2 in yeast INO80 or SWI/SNF complexes, respectively107–109. SWI/SNF remodellers form a modular core scaffold at the N-terminus of their motor ATPase subunit (Supplementary Figure 1a). INO80 remodellers have a long insertion domain in the C-lobe of the motor ATPase, which, as shown in the structure obtained from the fungus Chaetomium thermophilum, is anchored in the AAA+ ATPase Rvb1–Rvb2 complex and functions as a scaffold for the assembly of the nucleosome binding core module110,111 (Supplementary Figure 1b). The role of its ATPase activity in the function and regulation of INO80 family remodellers is unclear. Some remodellers also incorporate histone modifying enzymes or transcription factors. For example, the human histone acetyltransferase complex TIP60 may have evolved by ‘fusion’ of a remodeller similar to the yeast SWR1 complex and a histone acetyl transferase similar to the yeast NuA4 complex112. The transcription factor YY1 is stably anchored within the ARP-module of human INO80 (ref.113,114).ISWI and CHD remodellers typically assemble into smaller complexes, but also exhibit combinatorial composition, in particular in multicellular eukaryotes. There are three ISWI remodellers in yeast, each with 1–3 non-catalytic subunits115. In humans, two different motor ATPases, SNF2L or SNF2H (also known as SMARCA1 or SMARCA5, respectively) assemble with diverse subunits into at least 16 different ISWI remodellers116. There is only one CHD remodeller in yeast (Animation 4), which functions as a monomer, but CHD remodellers also form large complexes in metazoans. For example, human CHD3, CHD4 and CHD5 are motor ATPases that function interchangeably in the >6 subunit nucleosome remodelling and deacetylase (NuRD) complex117,118. NuRD functions both as a remodeller and as a histone deacetylase at transcriptionally repressed chromatin regions119.There are also remodellers outside the four most-studied families, such as human ALC1 (also known as CHD1L; ref.120,121), yeast Fun30 (ref.122) alias human SMARCAD1 (ref.123), human ATRX124,125 and human members of the Cockayne syndrome B (CSB)–ERCC6 subfamily126–128. Although nucleosomes are the primary substrate of most remodellers, some have (also) other substrates, such as the human SWI/SNF-family BAF complexes, which are proposed to also remodel (nucleosome-bound) Polycomb complexes129,130 and transcription factors131. This function may be related to the ability of SWI/SNF remodellers to displace not only histone octamers, but also other DNA-bound proteins while they slide a nucleosome84,131–133. Finally, there are Snf2-type ATPases that remodel non-nucleosomal substrates, such as the yeast TATA binding protein (TBP)-removing Mot1 (ref.134–136) and the yeast Ssl2 and human XPB translocases of the general transcription factor TFIIH137,138.During the past three decades, use of methods including protein purification, genetic interaction analyses, chromatin mapping and, more recently, proximity-labelling began to establish a comprehensive map of context-dependent remodeller compositions and interactions139. Subunit deletion studies link this complexity to genome regulation140. For example, deletions of complex-specific subunits revealed that human BAF, PBAF and ncBAF complexes (FIG. 3c; Supplementary Table 1), all based on the same SWI/SNF-family motor ATPases BRM (SMARCA2) or BRG1 (SMARCA4), regulate distinct gene expression programs. Furthermore, exchange of the ARP subunit BAF53A with homologue isoforms is essential for the function of the human BAF complex in neurodevelopment141, whereas overexpression and corresponding stoichiometric incorporation of BAF53A into the BAF complex drives squamous cell carcinomas142. However, even in light of recent progress143, the biogenesis pathways underlying such (re)assemblies and the plethora of context-dependent regulatory mechanisms of remodellers within chromatin remain still largely unexplored.Structure–function anatomyThe recent breakthroughs in structural biology based on advances in cryo-electron microscopy (cryoEM) allowed the visualization of mono-nucleosomes in complex with individual Snf2-type motor ATPase domains144–147, single-subunit remodellers121,148,149 and even mega-Dalton assemblies of multi-subunit remodellers110,111,150–157 (FIG. 3c; Supplementary Figure 1). The available structures, especially of all four well-studied SWI/SNF, INO80, ISWI and CHD remodellers, mostly represent ground states, which lack ATP hydrolysis-driven alterations. Nevertheless, the structures establish, in conjunction with biochemical and single-molecule studies, a framework for understanding the mechanisms and regulation of nucleosome remodelling.Remodellers engage nucleosomes by forming multi-valent interactions with both histones and DNA (Supplementary Figure 1; FIG. 4a). Some interactions, including with the prominent H2A–H2B acidic patch [G] and histone elbow [G]158 features of core histones and with histone modifications, share common principles with interactions between nucleosomes and other chromatin-binding factors6,158. A DNA position relative to the histone octamer in the NCP is called super-helical location (SHL; FIG. 4a), with SHL0 being at the pseudo-twofold symmetry dyad axis [G]. During nucleosome sliding, entry DNA [G] is translocated by the remodeller around the histone octamer and emerges as exit DNA [G]. Relative to a bound remodeller, the SHL at the entry side is numbered as SHL-7 so that numbering increases in direction of DNA translocation towards the exit side. For most remodellers, the motor ATPase has its primary binding site around the histone-outward facing minor groove of SHL-2121,144–146,148–157,159 (FIG. 4a). Although the INO80 remodeller binds at SHL-6, it adopts there an analogous configuration110,111.Even an individual Snf2-type motor ATPase domain was not only visualized at SHL-2, but also at SHL-6 (ref.144), suggesting that a remodeller may switch between different modes of binding and remodelling106,110, which may be an elegant mechanism for context-dependent regulation of different remodelling activities. Indeed, such a switch was recently observed for INO80 binding of hexasomes: loss of one H2A–H2B dimer from the NCP leads to unwrapping of 46 bp of DNA, a 145 degrees spin rotation of the remodeller complex and recognition of an exposed H3–H4 surface160,161 (FIG. 5d). A similar scenario is also conceivable for SWI/SNF remodellers and other multi-valent chromatin-binding factors. Notably, such spin rotation may substantially change the recognition of regulatory histone modifications as their position relative to histone reader domains is changed. Overall, these studies illustrate how multi-modal binding and remodelling of (non-canonical) nucleosomes in a context-dependent manner may profoundly affect chromatin regulation.Remodellers comprise many DNA and histone binding elements, which specify, in conjunction with the Snf2-type motor ATPase domain, binding and remodelling of nucleosomes. Even single-subunit remodellers such as yeast Chd1 (Animation 4), engage nucleosomes through multivalent interactions, which include nucleosomal DNA contacts with the double chromodomain, unwrapping of exit DNA from the H2A–H2B surface by the C-terminal DNA binding domain and recognition of the H2A–H2B acidic patch by the N-terminal ChEX domain162. The latter may prevent a second remodeller from binding an already Chd1-bound nucleosome. In general, the activity of SWI/SNF, ISWI and INO80 remodellers crucially relies on acidic patch recognition158. A single remodeller complex exhibits an asymmetric mononucleosome-binding configuration, which can involve simultaneous recognition of the acidic patches of the two H2A–H2B dimers through different subunits (Animation 2; Animation 3). Binding of two remodeller complexes to a single nucleosome was reported163,164. Structural re-arrangements of subunits that bind acidic patches would be required to accommodate such binding by a remodeller dimer, yet this may also contribute to differential regulation of the two ATPase motors that prevents futile cycles of DNA translocation in opposing directions106,110. It is interesting to note that, in addition to the motor ATPase domain of Ino80, the yeast INO80 complex possesses ten more subunits that contain ATPase folds [G] — three Rvb1 proteins, three Rvb2, nuclear actin, Arp4, Arp5 and Arp8 (Supplementary Table 1) — yet it is a long-standing open question whether ATP binding and hydrolysis cycles by those ATPase-folds have any role in remodelling, especially in the case of nuclear actin, which is proposed to regulate the BAF complex165.INO80 and SWI/SNF remodellers not only adopt multiple configurations, but also contain large intrinsically disordered regions, which may become ordered in certain contexts. This deformation mechanism differs from related SF2 DNA/RNA helicases, which invoke insertion of “wedge” or “pin” elements to separate strands and gain processivity by translocating on the resulting single-strand DNA (ssDNA) or ssRNA167,168. In Snf2-type motors, the N-lobe and C-lobe of the motor bind both DNA strands (FIG. 4a), of which one strand is topologically equivalent to the single strand on which the ssDNA or ssRNA helicases translocate and is denoted the “tracking” strand (see below), whereas the other strand is called the “guide” strand144,148,169. Different nucleotide-binding states (apo, ADP or ATP) of nucleosome-bound Snf2-type motor ATPases represent snapshots of the translocation cycle147,155 (FIG. 4b). The ATP-bound structure is different from the apo and ADP-bound structures, which are relatively similar to each other and may resemble the same post-hydrolysis state of the motor147. The ADP and apo states both show a 1 nt bulge in DNA147,170, where DNA locally adopt an RNA-like A-form162 or is bent110,114, whereas the DNA bound to the ATP remodeller state shows a more canonical B-form (FIG. 4b). Thus, it is proposed that, upon ATP-hydrolysis, the motor ATPase reels in 1 nt of the tracking strand in a corkscrew motion while the guide strand probably follows this motion when the DNA relaxes into a B-form upon binding of the next ATP molecule147,162,171, but the corresponding structure is not determined yet. Nonetheless, this last step completing the DNA translocation cycle may proceed in a uni-directional manner through altered affinities at sub-sites [G] on the motor, similar to the ratchet movement of ssDNA or ssRNA translocating helicases147,172–174. Together, these data support the general notion that SF1 and SF2 helicases or translocases proceed in 1 bp per NTP steps that are triggered by the release of inorganic phosphate upon ATP hydrolysis147,172–174.From microscopic to macroscopic stepsIt still remains elusive, how individual DNA translocation steps lead to the various remodelling outcomes. Even for nucleosome sliding, which is less disruptive than other remodelling outcomes, it is not well understood how directional DNA translocation around the histone octamer is achieved171. In single-molecule experiments using Chd1, ISWI175,176 or INO80177,178, different time delays were observed when DNA enters and exits the NCP and also different sizes of larger steps of nucleosome sliding were observed biochemically178,179. These time delays and apparent step sizes may be caused by two non-mutually exclusive mechanisms: either the motor ATPase catalyzes DNA translocation through larger steps, e.g. through consecutive ATP-hydrolysis cycles without intermittent guide strand relaxation, or the nucleosome may buffer the transient accommodation of base pairs175,176. A DNA twist [G] defect in nucleosomal DNA can accommodate an additional base pair in NCP crystal structures3,180. In early experiments, DNA twist defects were proposed to facilitate nucleosome mobilization at elevated salt concentrations or temperatures181. The local A-form deformation generated by the motor ATPase resembles a DNA twist defect144,162. In the simplest case, a 1 bp DNA corkscrew motion may ‘microscopically’ steer the direction of DNA twist defects, which could then diffuse around the histone octamer (FIG. 5a). DNA twist defects may also accumulate within the nucleosome, before they reach the exit site through diffusion, and may thus explain a ‘macroscopic’ translocation step size of 3–4 bp176. As DNA–histone contacts are reformed following each microscopic step, twist defect diffusion may also explain nucleosome sliding by an individual Snf2-type motor domain144, which may transiently dissociate in between DNA translocation steps. Nevertheless, whether DNA is immediately pushed forward in this manner is not clear based on the available structures110,114,144,162. Several ATP hydrolysis steps could build up additional DNA strain, at the ATPase binding site or elsewhere110, or histones may absorb energy and undergo conformational changes, as suggested specifically for ISWI remodelelers182.Applying force: the motor–rotor–stator–grip mechanismIn general, and in analogy to electrical motors and ATP synthases, the mechanical action of remodellers can be conceptualized as interplay between a stator (scaffold) that holds in place a motor and a grip element so that the motor can processively apply force and mobilize the rotor106,110 (FIG. 5a). Some remodelling outcomes, for example histone exchange or octamer ejection, likely require energy from several ATP hydrolysis steps for breaking multiple or even all histone–DNA contacts. Moreover, they may require force generation at specific sites of DNA–histone interactions. The multi-subunit structures of INO80-family (including SWR1) and SWI/SNF-family remodellers suggest how such remodelling outcomes may occur106 (FIG. 5). The motor ATPase domain is held in place by a stator and a grip element, providing a fairly rigid scaffold that mechanically couples the motor ATPase to multi-valent nucleosome interactions including contacts with the histone octamer such as at the H2A-H2B acidic patch, and contacts with DNA. For C. thermophilum INO80, the motor ATPase translocates DNA towards the heterodimeric Arp5–Ies6 grip element, which may constrain the DNA110 (FIG. 5a,b,c). One bp step per ATP repeatedly applied against a grip by the motor ATPase overwinds and underwinds DNA towards the entry and exit direction, respectively58,110,183 (FIG. 4b). DNA twist defects and other types of DNA strain, which may break histone–DNA contacts, could accumulate. Once sufficient force is generated, the DNA strain could propagate past the grip element (e.g. through twist-defect diffusion) that could act as valve to prevent diffusive back-slippage, and lead to larger step movements177,178. Alternatively, DNA strain may be less transient and not lead to nucleosome sliding, but allow destabilization of histone contacts. For example, successive rounds of DNA translocation by INO80 at SHL-6 could lead to DNA underwinding where it is bound to H2A–H2B (FIG. 5b), whereas SWI/SNF and SWR1 activity at SHL-2 would lead to overwinding and underwinding at H2A–H2B and H3–H4 interfaces, respectively58,106. Despite the rigid scaffold, the function of motor–rotor–stator–grip architecture remains adaptable through multi-modal spin rotation, as recently demonstrated for the activity of INO80 on hexasomes160,161 (FIG. 5b,d). Remodeller mechanisms of function

Although early-on it was debated whether remodeller-specific outcomes are driven by fundamentally different mechanisms of function101,102, the Snf2-type motor ATPase domains of remodellers bind DNA in a highly similar fashion, supporting the view that similar DNA conformational changes or translocation steps underly the different larger-scale remodelling outcomes. In this section we discuss how the evolutionarily conserved DNA translocation mechanism is geared towards providing different remodelling outcomes.

Snf2-type motor ATPases reshape DNAAll Snf2-type motor ATPases of nucleosome remodellers bind the nucleosome on the opposite side of DNA compared to histones, suggesting that histone contacts are remodeled by deformation of DNA rather than by directly disrupting histone–DNA interactions (FIG. 4a). This deformation mechanism differs from related SF2 DNA/RNA helicases, which invoke insertion of “wedge” or “pin” elements to separate strands and gain processivity by translocating on the resulting single-strand DNA (ssDNA) or ssRNA167,168. In Snf2-type motors, the N-lobe and C-lobe of the motor bind both DNA strands (FIG. 4a), of which one strand is topologically equivalent to the single strand on which the ssDNA or ssRNA helicases translocate and is denoted the “tracking” strand (see below), whereas the other strand is called the “guide” strand144,148,169. Different nucleotide-binding states (apo, ADP or ATP) of nucleosome-bound Snf2-type motor ATPases represent snapshots of the translocation cycle147,155 (FIG. 4b). The ATP-bound structure is different from the apo and ADP-bound structures, which are relatively similar to each other and may resemble the same post-hydrolysis state of the motor147. The ADP and apo states both show a 1 nt bulge in DNA147,170, where DNA locally adopt an RNA-like A-form162 or is bent110,114, whereas the DNA bound to the ATP remodeller state shows a more canonical B-form (FIG. 4b). Thus, it is proposed that, upon ATP-hydrolysis, the motor ATPase reels in 1 nt of the tracking strand in a corkscrew motion while the guide strand probably follows this motion when the DNA relaxes into a B-form upon binding of the next ATP molecule147,162,171, but the corresponding structure is not determined yet. Nonetheless, this last step completing the DNA translocation cycle may proceed in a uni-directional manner through altered affinities at sub-sites [G] on the motor, similar to the ratchet movement of ssDNA or ssRNA translocating helicases147,172–174. Together, these data support the general notion that SF1 and SF2 helicases or translocases proceed in 1 bp per NTP steps that are triggered by the release of inorganic phosphate upon ATP hydrolysis147,172–174.

From microscopic to macroscopic stepsIt still remains elusive, how individual DNA translocation steps lead to the various remodelling outcomes. Even for nucleosome sliding, which is less disruptive than other remodelling outcomes, it is not well understood how directional DNA translocation around the histone octamer is achieved171. In single-molecule experiments using Chd1, ISWI175,176 or INO80177,178, different time delays were observed when DNA enters and exits the NCP and also different sizes of larger steps of nucleosome sliding were observed biochemically178,179. These time delays and apparent step sizes may be caused by two non-mutually exclusive mechanisms: either the motor ATPase catalyzes DNA translocation through larger steps, e.g. through consecutive ATP-hydrolysis cycles without intermittent guide strand relaxation, or the nucleosome may buffer the transient accommodation of base pairs175,176. A DNA twist [G] defect in nucleosomal DNA can accommodate an additional base pair in NCP crystal structures3,180. In early experiments, DNA twist defects were proposed to facilitate nucleosome mobilization at elevated salt concentrations or temperatures181. The local A-form deformation generated by the motor ATPase resembles a DNA twist defect144,162. In the simplest case, a 1 bp DNA corkscrew motion may ‘microscopically’ steer the direction of DNA twist defects, which could then diffuse around the histone octamer (FIG. 5a). DNA twist defects may also accumulate within the nucleosome, before they reach the exit site through diffusion, and may thus explain a ‘macroscopic’ translocation step size of 3–4 bp176. As DNA–histone contacts are reformed following each microscopic step, twist defect diffusion may also explain nucleosome sliding by an individual Snf2-type motor domain144, which may transiently dissociate in between DNA translocation steps. Nevertheless, whether DNA is immediately pushed forward in this manner is not clear based on the available structures110,114,144,162. Several ATP hydrolysis steps could build up additional DNA strain, at the ATPase binding site or elsewhere110, or histones may absorb energy and undergo conformational changes, as suggested specifically for ISWI remodelelers182.

Moreover, they may require force generation at specific sites of DNA–histone interactions. The multi-subunit structures of INO80-family (including SWR1) and SWI/SNF-family remodellers suggest how such remodelling outcomes may occur106 (FIG. 5). Alternatively, DNA strain may be less transient and not lead to nucleosome sliding, but allow destabilization of histone contacts. Despite the rigid scaffold, the function of motor–rotor–stator–grip architecture remains adaptable through multi-modal spin rotation, as recently demonstrated for the activity of INO80 on hexasomes160,161 (FIG. 5b,d). Such mechanism might be also involved in histone exchange and ejection reactions, but further studies are required to understand the proposed chemo-mechanical cycles. Regulation of motor activity

Motor ATPase activity is mainly regulated at three levels: stabilization of a “resting” versus “active” conformation and regulation of the DNA translocation cycle. Active hit-and-run regulationChromatin remodelling continuously maintains genome-wide nucleosome organization, yet it must also respond to diverse cellular signals in an acute and site-specific manner. For example, nucleosome positioning at gene promoters relies on continuous “pushing” and “pulling” in an interplay of different remodellers46 (FIG. 2b). Single-molecule tracking of remodellers in yeast236 and fluorescence (cross-)correlation spectroscopy in human cells237 revealed a high frequency of short-lived (sub-second to second) chromatin-remodeller interactions. Such fast kinetics were also observed for transcription factors238 and are consistent with a hit-and-run mechanism, by which nuclear factors may efficiently find their genomic target sites239. However, the classical 3D–1D diffusion model of transcription factors [G], driven by thermal fluctuations of weak DNA interactions, seems an unlikely mechanism for remodellers. Remodellers can engage nucleosomes with nanomolar affinities to conduct large scale reconfigurations, yet their nuclear concentrations are in the micromolar range, e.g., one RSC remodeller per 30 nucleosomes in yeast240 or one ISWI remodeller per 25 nucleosomes in Drosophila melanogaster embryos241. Consequently, remodellers could get trapped on chromatin at steady state conditions. Instead, an active remodeller release mechanism was identified, in which the motor ATPase catalyzes not only remodelling, but also remodeller release from chromatin236,242. Thus, the unbound remodeller pool can be continuously replenished for efficient 3D diffusion across the nucleus. The motor ATPase may enable also local 1D searches, e.g. through ATP-dependent movement along promoter DNA or across nucleosomes. We propose that the context-dependent regulation of each step of such an active target search adds an important, yet unexplored, layer to the input–output relationships provided by remodellers.

Roles of mutated remodellers in cancerMutations in remodeller genes are linked with many human diseases, especially cancers and neurological disorders243–246. Here, we provide a structural perspective that rationalizes how specific mutations lead to certain mechanistic defects. We focus on the human SWI/SNF family, as it is both structurally well characterized and identified as a major human tumor suppressor, which is mutated in ~20% of cancers247,248. Mutations in remodeller genes are linked with many human diseases, especially cancers and neurological disorders243–246. Conclusions and outlook

The fundamental role of chromatin remodellers in the energy-driven self-organization of chromatin states is now well established. Detailed mechanisms have recently emerged, providing a first glimpse of chromatin remodellers as information processing hubs. Through their chemo–mechanical action and allosteric regulation, remodellers co-determine energy-driven genome regulation mediated through the (epi)genetic chromatin landscape.

There certainly is still a lot to learn about remodeller function and regulation at individual nucleosomes, including about the directionality of DNA translocation, recruitment mechanisms, and histone exchange and ejection. However, it becomes increasingly clear that remodellers cooperate and communicate with other remodellers and with other NTP-dependent machines, such as polymerases, DNA repair factors and chromatin-loop extruders, which together drive energy-dependent chromatin dynamics on much larger scales than individual or few nucleosomes. The collective behaviour of factors within such a dynamic chromatin landscape remains largely unexplored, but is likely crucial for understanding how remodeller functions and specific nucleosome organizations affect, for example, gene expression.

The dynamics and roles of chromatin states can be studied across spatial and temporal scales using single-molecule high-resolution techniques in vivo and in vitro. Such methods may change our current notions of how recruitment, remodelling and release implement the hourglass model of chromatin remodelling, e.g. regarding processivity along chromatin, cooperation with other factors, role of DNA topology and 3D genome organization. Additional remodeller mechanisms and functions may emerge from studies of such comprehensive physiological substrates and contexts, revealing unanticipated principles of energy-driven chromatin self-organisation and genome regulation.
