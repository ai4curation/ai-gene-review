Sialic acids linked to glycoproteins and glycolipids are important mediators of 
cell and protein recognition events. These sugar residues are removed by 
neuraminidases (sialidases). Neuraminidase-1 (sialidase-1 or NEU1) is a 
ubiquitously expressed mammalian sialidase located in lysosomes and on the cell 
membrane. Because of its modulation of multiple signaling processes, it is a 
potential therapeutic target for cancers and immune disorders. Genetic defects 
in NEU1 or in its protective protein cathepsin A (PPCA, CTSA) cause the 
lysosomal storage diseases sialidosis and galactosialidosis. To further our 
understanding of this enzyme's function at the molecular level, we determined 
the three-dimensional structure of murine NEU1. The enzyme oligomerizes through 
two self-association interfaces and displays a wide substrate-binding cavity. A 
catalytic loop adopts an inactive conformation. We propose a mechanism of 
activation involving a conformational change in this loop upon binding to its 
protective protein. These findings may facilitate the development of selective 
inhibitor and agonist therapies.

INTRODUCTION

Most extracellular and cell surface proteins are glycosylated. In vertebrates, these glycans are often capped by terminal sialic acids, a type of anionic sugar monomer. The most common sialic acid in humans is N-acetylneuraminic acid. Because of their presence at the outermost edges of glycoproteins and glycolipids and their variety attained through multiple chemical modifications, sialic acids serve important roles in cell and protein recognition and modulation of signaling at the cell membrane (1). They are added to newly synthesized glycoproteins by sialyltransferases in the Golgi and can be removed by neuraminidases (sialidases).

There are four mammalian sialidases that differ in their subcellular localization and substrate preferences and, consequently, biological functions. Neuraminidase-4 (NEU4) is found on mitochondria and possibly lysosomes or endoplasmic reticulum, displays a broad substrate specificity, and participates in ganglioside (glycolipid) catabolism (2–6). NEU3 degrades gangliosides at the outer or inner leaflet of the plasma membrane (7–9). The cytosolic NEU2 is the only mammalian sialidase of known three-dimensional (3D) structure, but its physiological substrates are unclear (10, 11). The ubiquitously expressed NEU1 is the most abundant homolog, shares the least sequence similarity with its family members, and is active on oligosaccharides, glycopeptides, and glycoproteins (1, 12–16). It is located in lysosomes and on the cell surface (12, 14, 15, 17, 18).

NEU1 is unique among all known mammalian, microbial, or viral sialidases, as it requires an accessory protein for activity—the protective protein cathepsin A (CTSA; PPCA) (12, 19–23). NEU1 and CTSA, along with β-galactosidase (GLB1), form a megadalton-sized lysosomal multienzyme complex (15, 21, 24–31). However, CTSA is sufficient for NEU1 activity, whereas GLB1 is not necessary (12, 21–24, 27, 32). Accordingly, NEU1 and CTSA can also associate into large complexes in the absence of GLB1 (21, 22). The role of CTSA is threefold: It facilitates the transport of NEU1 to lysosomes (33), protects it from proteolytic degradation (30), and was suggested to induce a conformational change in NEU1, enabling its enzymatic activity (12, 22, 30, 33).

The study of NEU1 was initially motivated by its involvement in two lysosomal storage disorders: sialidosis, caused by inherited genetic defects in NEU1 (16); and galactosialidosis, a combined GLB1 and NEU1 deficiency due to mutations in CTSA (20, 34). The pathogenesis of galactosialidosis is mostly caused by the lack of NEU1 activity stemming from the inability of CTSA to carry out its protective roles (15, 34, 35). These diseases have variable severity and age of onset, and are sometimes fatal.

Besides its catabolic activity in lysosomes, multiple additional functions of NEU1 were later found at the cell membrane via desialylation of glycans on various cell surface receptors and adhesion proteins (1). Desialylation of these proteins enables or, in some cases, inhibits ligand binding. Overall, as a positive regulator of inflammation and immune responses (1), NEU1 inhibition was proposed as a potential therapeutic approach for disorders including asthma (36), atherosclerosis (37–40), sepsis (41), immune thrombocytopenia (42), renal inflammation in lupus (43), neuroinflammation (44), pulmonary fibrosis (45), and cardiomyopathy (46, 47). On the other hand, generally increased tumor sialic acid levels and the associated invasiveness, chemoresistance, and immune evasion posit NEU1 activation as a potential strategy in cancer treatment (1, 48–50). Stimulation of NEU1 activity has also been suggested as potential approach to combating insulin resistance in type II diabetes (51) and for Alzheimer’s disease (52).

Design of selective NEU1 inhibitors (53, 54) and development of NEU1 and CTSA supplementation therapies, which are at the preclinical stage (35, 55–61), are hampered by an incomplete understanding of the enzyme’s properties. NEU1 forms oligomers and is prone to aggregation (16, 23, 58); its mode of membrane association is unknown; and its mechanism of activation by CTSA, unique among all sialidases, has not been characterized at the molecular level.

Here, we report the crystal structure of murine NEU1. The protein oligomerizes via two self-association interfaces, including a hydrophobic surface important for its activity. The enzyme displays a wide substrate-binding cavity and adopts an inactive conformation in a catalytic loop. On the basis of structural and biochemical analyses, we propose a mechanism of activation—binding of CTSA near this loop induces a conformational change in NEU1 that enables catalysis. These insights may facilitate development of NEU1 as a therapeutic target and expand our understanding of its cellular functions.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONNEU1 adopts a β-propeller fold devoid of transmembrane featuresWe produced NEU1 from multiple mammalian species in insect cells and obtained the best crystals with the murine homolog (86% sequence identity to human), allowing structure determination to a resolution of 3.5 Å (table S1). This level of detail enables positioning of the protein backbone and bulky side chains with confidence, but the orientations of many smaller side chains were modeled approximately.NEU1 consists of a single domain shaped as a six-bladed β-propeller, with each β sheet composed of four strands (Fig. 1A). This fold is typical of viral and bacterial sialidases (62, 63). NEU1 is part of the GH33 family of glycoside hydrolases (64), which comprises bacterial and eukaryotic enzymes (65–68) that release terminal sialic acids from glycans or transfer these sugar residues onto other glycans. This family contains the four mammalian sialidases, and the murine NEU1 crystal structure is most similar to that of human NEU2 (fig. S1A) (11) and, to a lesser extent, to the Aspergillus fumigatus sialidase (67). Despite the low sequence identity within this family, the β-propeller fold is highly conserved, with differences arising in the multiple connecting loops on the top and bottom faces. NEU1 shares 20 to 21% sequence identity with NEU2, NEU3, and NEU4, which are 41 to 45% identical to each other. The active site is located in a central cavity on one face of the propeller and is described later. Murine NEU1 contains four potential N-linked glycosylation sites; three glycans are clearly discernible in the structure and are conserved in the human homolog. However, its mode of association with membranes is unknown. It was proposed that the C-terminal Tyr-Gly-Thr-Leu segment corresponds to a YXXΦ motif, a trafficking signal recognized by the AP3 adapter complex (14, 17) and found on the cytoplasmic end of certain transmembrane proteins. As the NEU1 active site is facing the lysosome lumen, this implies the protein contains at least one transmembrane region. Recently, segments 139 to 159 and 316 to 333 of human NEU1 were suggested to span the membrane (69). In the murine NEU1 structure reported here, these residues form β strands at the core of the protein, and the C-terminal YGTL peptide does not substantially extend away from the catalytic domain (Fig. 1A). The crystal structure is therefore incompatible with a transmembrane arrangement of the mature, functional enzyme. NEU1 may instead associate with another protein that interacts with membranes. Alternatively, its 47-residue N-terminal signal peptide (SP) (30), which is atypically long among lysosomal enzymes, may be inefficiently cleaved, resulting in a population of luminal NEU1 attached to the membrane via an N-terminal signal anchor. All point mutations and enzymatic, oligomerization, and coprecipitation assays reported here were carried out with the human homolog based on the murine NEU1 structure.NEU1 displays a wide substrate-binding cavityAlthough the residues responsible for catalysis and stabilization of the sialate carboxyl group are conserved across the sialidase superfamily, amino acids that interact with the opposite side of the substrate, namely, its N-acetyl and glycerol moieties (Fig. 1B), are divergent (11). This likely stems from the diversity of sialic acids, which can bear modifications such as acetyl groups at several positions on the sugar backbone. Within the mammalian sialidase subfamily, there are differences in substrate preferences for modified sialic acids (72, 73), and the structure of murine NEU1 allows rationalizing these observations. In human NEU2, the glycerol moiety of the substrate contacts Glu218, Tyr179, and Tyr181, whereas in murine NEU1, Tyr179 is replaced by His214, Tyr181 is replaced by a threonine, and an additional residue (Asp257 that is a leucine in NEU2) establishes two hydrogen bonds with the glycerol group (Fig. 2A). Glu111 from NEU2 also interacts with this group but is located on a helix that is missing in NEU1. Mutation of His214 and Asp257 markedly reduced NEU1 activity (Fig. 2C). The cavity accommodating the N5-acetyl group is also substantially altered—lined by Asn132, Gly134, and the backbone of Leu133 in NEU1, or Met85 and Asn86 in NEU2.In general, the area around the substrate glycerol and N5-acetyl moieties is more widely open in NEU1 than in NEU2 (Fig. 2D), mainly due to length differences in three loops around the active site which cluster Glu111, Tyr181, and Gln270 around the sialic acid in NEU2. Notably, this is unrelated to the conformational switch of the loop bearing the catalytic Asp97 described earlier. This widely open active site explains why substrate O9-acetylation reduces activity by up to 90% in NEU2, NEU3, and NEU4 but only 50% in NEU1 (72, 73). NEU1 is also the least affected by presence of an N5-glycolyl group, which is slightly larger than N5-acetyl (73).These differences can be exploited for the development of inhibitors selective for NEU1 versus other mammalian or viral sialidases (53, 54). For example, sialate-based inhibitors with bulky substitutions on O9 or N5 improve potency and selectivity for NEU1, and these substituents were presumed to occupy a shared hydrophobic pocket (Fig. 2D) (53, 54). As previously reported (23), their binding affinity is weak, failing to reach saturation even at high CTSA concentration (Fig. 1C). The corresponding loop transitions from disordered to ordered upon substrate binding to NEU2 (11), but in other eukaryotic sialidases of the GH33 family, this loop is productively positioned even without substrate (65–68). This raises the possibility that CTSA may facilitate a conformational change in this loop in NEU1, as no other “inactive features” were identified in the NEU1 crystal structure. On the other hand, none of the mutants displayed activation by CTSA, in accordance with the idea that CTSA induces a conformational change possibly by specifically interacting with this NEU1 loop (Fig. 4D). Most mutations are predicted to affect the folding or stability of NEU1, and many others are possibly in this category (fig. S1B). The variants Arg78Cys, Arg341Gly, and Tyr370Cys disrupt substrate binding or catalysis directly and may not respond to exogenous CTSA. NEU1 is inactive in the absence of CTSA, probably to prevent degradation of sialylated glycans in the Golgi before reaching lysosomes. The multienzyme complex forms at acidic pH and likely contains CTSA dimers and NEU1 oligomers, and the contact surface(s) between these two proteins is distinct from their own self-association interfaces. Our results suggest that CTSA binds near or at a NEU1 catalytic loop and induces a conformational change in NEU1. This activation model remains to be confirmed by structural studies of the lysosomal multienzyme complex. The NEU1 crystal structure reported here will also help assessing mutations that occur in sialidosis and their responsiveness to CTSA supplementation and may assist the development of NEU1 replacement therapies. For example, an obstacle to the production of recombinant NEU1 is its propensity to aggregate; however, a point mutant in one of the self-association interfaces, designed to improve solubility, unexpectedly caused loss of activity. Last, the crystal structure will also facilitate the development of NEU1-selective inhibitors and probes by exploiting its comparatively wide substrate-binding cavity or possibly by targeting its inactive conformation. NEU1 activation or inhibition approaches have not yet reached clinical stages but may hold promise for the treatment of cancers and immune disorders.

The NEU1 oligomerization mode is unrelated to viral sialidase tetramers (62), disulfide-mediated NEU3 dimers (9), or the various lattice interfaces observed in NEU2 crystal structures (11, 75, 76), raising the possibility that NEU1 self-association may be required for complex formation with CTSA. Alternatively, either the beta or loop interface may first need to dissociate to bind to CTSA. To explore these possibilities, the ability of human NEU1 interface mutants to coprecipitate with CTSA was assayed. At acidic pH, concentrated isolated wild-type NEU1 slowly precipitates, whereas isolated CTSA remains soluble. It was found that coincubation of both proteins at high concentration at acidic pH results in rapid, approximately equimolar coprecipitation (Fig. 3D). This is not caused by unfolding of the proteins, as the same conditions greatly increase NEU1 enzymatic activity with CTSA. Rather, this aggregation may be representative of the high–molecular weight complexes observed in vivo (21, 22). The human NEU1 beta and loop interface mutants Leu57Asn and Ile249Tyr, although exhibiting less aggregation by design (fig. S3D), were nevertheless able to coprecipitate wild-type CTSA (Fig. 3D). This suggests that the NEU1 beta or loop self-oligomerization interfaces are distinct from the NEU1—CTSA contact surface(s) in their multienzyme complex. A partial overlap cannot be excluded, however, as only one mutant per interface was tested. This result also indicates that oligomerization of NEU1 is not required for its interaction with CTSA.

Neither of the two NEU1 self-association interfaces are in direct contact with its catalytic or substrate-binding residues, although the loop contact surface is within 13 Å of the putative substrate glycerol moiety. To determine whether NEU1 oligomerization is required for its activity, substrate hydrolysis by the interface mutants was assayed in presence or absence of CTSA. Unexpectedly, the human Ile249Tyr loop interface mutant was inactive in presence of CTSA and did not even display the low rate of CTSA-unassisted substrate hydrolysis (1%) observed in wild-type NEU1 (Fig. 3E). Possibly, ablation of the loop self-association interface results in local disruption of the protein that propagates to the substrate-binding site.

For enzymatic activity measurement, human NEU1 at 10 to 1000 nM was preincubated with human CTSA at up to 128 μM in buffer [20 mM sodium acetate (pH 4.6) and 100 mM NaCl] for 1 hour at 22°C. The substrate 4-methylumbelliferyl-N-acetyl-α-d-neuraminic acid (sodium salt; 4MU-NANA; Sigma-Aldrich, M8639) was added at 1 mM, followed by incubation at 37°C for 30 min. The reaction was stopped with 250 mM glycine (pH 10), and the fluorescence of the product 4-methylumbelliferone was measured at 365-nm excitation and 450-nm emission. Activity was quantified with a standard curve.

Purified NEU1 slowly precipitates at acidic pH, whereas isolated CTSA remains soluble. When both proteins are mixed at acidic pH, they rapidly coprecipitate, especially at high salt concentration. Human NEU1 at 4.4 mg/ml was incubated with human CTSA at 5.6 mg/ml (equimolar) in buffer [100 mM sodium acetate (pH 4) or sodium dl-malate (pH 5) and 500 mM NaCl] for 30 min at 22°C. The reaction was centrifuged at 21,000g for 10 min, and the pellet was solubilized with 8 M urea. Samples were incubated with SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) sample buffer containing dithiothreitol (DTT) at 95°C or without DTT at 22°C and analyzed by SDS-PAGE.
