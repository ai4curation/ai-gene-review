Abstract

The plant hormone jasmonic acid (JA) plays a key role in the environmental stress responses and developmental processes of plants. Although ATMYC2/JASMONATE-INSENSITIVE1 ( JIN1 ) is a major positive regulator of JA-inducible gene expression and essential for JA-dependent developmental processes in Arabidopsis thaliana , molecular mechanisms underlying the control of ATMYC2/JIN1 expression remain largely unknown. Here, we identify a mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) cascade, MAPK KINASE 3 (MKK3)–MAPK 6 (MPK6), which is activated by JA in Arabidopsis . We also show that JA negatively controls ATMYC2/JIN1 expression, based on quantitative RT-PCR and genetic analyses using gain-of-function and loss-of-function mutants of the MKK3–MPK6 cascade. These results indicate that this kinase unit plays a key role in JA-dependent negative regulation of ATMYC2/JIN1 expression. Both positive and negative regulation by JA may be used to fine-tune ATMYC2/JIN1 expression to control JA signaling. Moreover, JA-regulated root growth inhibition is affected by mutations in the MKK3–MPK6 cascade, which indicates important roles in JA signaling. We provide a model explaining how MPK6 can convert three distinct signals—JA, pathogen, and cold/salt stress—into three different sets of responses in Arabidopsis .

INTRODUCTION

Plants have the ability to cope with unfavorable environmental factors such as biotic and/or abiotic stress. When plants perceive such stresses, they immediately activate signaling machinery, including gene expression, to change their physiological status as a defense mechanism. One of the universal signaling module functions in response to such external stimuli is the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) cascade. The MAPK cascade is evolutionarily conserved among the eukaryotes and typically consists of three types of protein kinases, MAPK, MAPK kinase (MAPKK), and MAPKK kinase (MAPKKK) ( Chen et al., 2001 ). By responding to external stimuli, the MAPK cascade plays a critical role in gene expression, metabolism, cell death, proliferation, and differentiation in animals and yeast ( Widmann et al., 1999 ; Chen et al., 2001 ).

In plants, the MAPK cascade is also involved in various biotic and abiotic stress responses, hormone responses, cell division, and developmental processes ( Nakagami et al., 2005 ). Complete sequencing of the Arabidopsis thaliana genome has revealed that the MAPK family consists of >60 MAPKKK, 10 MAPKK, and 20 MAPK genes ( Ichimura et al., 2002 ). Despite the large number of MAPKs, only three (MPK3, MPK4, and MPK6) have been studied in detail. A biochemical analysis revealed that numerous biotic and abiotic stresses activate these MAPKs ( Nakagami et al., 2005 ). Recently, reverse genetic approaches have been used to elucidate the in planta function of these MAPKs. MPK4 is a negative regulator of systemic acquired resistance and a positive regulator of PDF1.2 expression through EDS1 and PAD4, essential components of salicylic acid–mediated defense responses ( Petersen et al., 2000 ; Brodersen et al., 2006 ). The substrate protein of MPK4 is MKS1 ( Andreasson et al., 2005 ). Lu et al. (2002) proposed that MPK3 plays a role in abscisic acid–induced postgermination growth arrest. Silencing of MPK6 compromised both gene-for-gene and basal resistance in Arabidopsis ( Menke et al., 2004 ). MPK6 and MPK3 appear to be required for ozone tolerance and reciprocally inhibit activity and duration ( Miles et al., 2005 ).

Several studies using the constitutive active form of Arabidopsis MAPKK have identified downstream MAPKs. For example, MKK4 and MKK5 can activate both MPK3 and MPK6 ( Asai et al., 2002 ; Ren et al., 2002 ). The 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid (ACC) synthases ACS2 and ACS6 are substrates for MPK6, regulated by MPK6 phosphorylation toward ethylene (ET) induction ( Liu and Zhang, 2004 ). This MKK4/MKK5–MPK3/MPK6 cascade also participates in the proposed microbe-associated molecular pattern signaling pathway containing MEKK1 as a MAPKKK ( Asai et al., 2002 ; He et al., 2006 ). Other approaches, such as yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) analysis and yeast complementation, suggest that MEKK1 functions upstream of the MKK1/MKK2–MPK4 cascade ( Ichimura et al., 1998 ; Mizoguchi et al., 1998 ). This cascade plays a role in the MEKK1–MKK2–MPK4/MPK6 cascade during cold and salt stress in Arabidopsis ( Teige et al., 2004 ). Several studies strongly suggest that MKK1 is also the upstream factor of MPK4 and mediates bacterial elicitor, wounding, and H 2 O 2 signals ( Huang et al., 2000 ; Matsuoka et al., 2002 ; Teige et al., 2004 ). Although these MAPKs are simultaneously activated by many types of stress, the presence of different phenotypes suggests that MAPK cascade regulation in plants could be much more complicated than it appears.

Jasmonic acid (JA) is an important regulator in response to environmental stresses such as ozone exposure, wounding, water deficit, and pathogen and pest attack ( Berger, 2002 ; Devoto and Turner, 2003 ). In Arabidopsis , mechanical and biotic wounding activates independent signaling pathways regulating different sets of target genes either at the injury or the systemic site ( Leon et al., 2001 ). JA induces systemic gene expression of VSP , JR1 , and Thi2.1 in response to wounding, but it is negatively regulated by the local synthesis of ET ( Rojo et al., 1999 ). However, JA and ET cooperate synergistically to activate basic pathogenesis-related (PR) proteins such as β -CHI , PR3 , and PDF1.2 ( Xu et al., 1994 ; Penninckx et al., 1998 ). Studies using protein kinase/phosphatase inhibitors have indicated that protein kinase activity may negatively regulate JA-induced systemic gene expression and positively regulate JA/ET-induced local gene expression ( Rojo et al., 1998 ). Transgenic tobacco ( Nicotiana tabacum ) that overexpresses WIPK, a tobacco MPK3 homolog, shows an increased JA level and constitutive expression of JA-inducible Proteinase inhibitor II ( Seo et al., 1999 ). In tobacco, however, JA activated neither SIPK, a tobacco MPK6 homolog, nor WIPK ( Kumar and Klessig, 2000 ). Thus, whether JA directly activates MAPK, leading to the induction of local and/or systemic response genes, remains unknown.

To date, six MAPKKs (MKK1, -2, -4, -5, -6, and -7) have been identified and analyzed in Arabidopsis ( Nakagami et al., 2005 ; Dai et al., 2006 ). MKK3 is one of the least characterized MAPKKs and has not yet been analyzed phenotypically or biochemically. Here, we describe the functional characterization of Arabidopsis MKK3, a group B MAPKK ( Ichimura et al., 2002 ) and a homolog of tobacco NPK2 ( Shibata et al., 1995 ). MKK3 has a unique domain composition with a C-terminal domain similar to that of nuclear transport factor 2 ( Ichimura et al., 2002 ). Biochemical analyses in planta and in vitro showed that MPK6 is a downstream target of MKK3. Microarray analysis revealed that MKK3 plays a role in JA signaling. Indeed, MPK6 was activated by JA, and this activation depended largely on the MKK3 manner. Loss-of-function and gain-of-function analyses revealed that the MKK3–MPK6 cascade negatively regulates ATMYC2 in both JA-dependent gene expression and inhibition of root growth. Our data indicate that this MKK3–MPK6 cascade is part of an important JA signal transduction pathway in Arabidopsis . Possible roles of the MKK3–MPK6 cascade in JA signaling are discussed.
