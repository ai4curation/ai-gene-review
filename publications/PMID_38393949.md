The mitochondrial inner membrane plays central roles in bioenergetics and 
metabolism and contains several established membrane protein complexes. Here, we 
report the identification of a mega-complex of the inner membrane, termed 
mitochondrial multifunctional assembly (MIMAS). Its large size of 3 MDa explains 
why MIMAS has escaped detection in the analysis of mitochondria so far. MIMAS 
combines proteins of diverse functions from respiratory chain assembly to 
metabolite transport, dehydrogenases, and lipid biosynthesis but not the large 
established supercomplexes of the respiratory chain, ATP synthase, or prohibitin 
scaffold. MIMAS integrity depends on the non-bilayer phospholipid 
phosphatidylethanolamine, in contrast to respiratory supercomplexes whose 
stability depends on cardiolipin. Our findings suggest that MIMAS forms a 
protein-lipid mega-assembly in the mitochondrial inner membrane that integrates 
respiratory biogenesis and metabolic processes in a multifunctional platform.

INTRODUCTION

Mitochondria contain membrane protein complexes that play crucial roles in respiration, ATP synthesis, protein biogenesis, and membrane organization. The systematic mapping of the mitochondrial proteome promoted the identification of a large number of mitochondrial proteins and functions.1–6 Mitochondria have been a major target of complexome analyses, strongly boosted by the establishment of mild approaches for lysis of the mitochondrial membranes and separation of native protein complexes, initially used for the characterization of respiratory complexes and supercomplexes.7–13 Together with in organello crosslinking and elucidation of high-resolution structures of numerous mitochondrial protein complexes, a systematic picture of the mitochondrial complexome is emerging. The structures, functions, and complexome organization of respiratory supercomplexes, F1Fo-ATP synthase, preprotein translocases, chaperone complexes, prohibitin scaffolds, and machineries for maintenance of membrane shape provide important insight into the molecular mechanisms and composition of mitochondria.2,14–33 These studies by a large number of mitochondrial researchers may have led to the view that the major protein assemblies of mitochondria have been identified and characterized. However, numerous mitochondrial proteins such as respiratory chain assembly factors and metabolite carriers have not been found as subunits of known mitochondrial protein complexes, although pull-down and crosslinking mass spectrometry studies reported interactions between some of the proteins.1,34–41 It is unknown whether these proteins just interact transiently or whether they may be part of not-yet-identified mitochondrial protein assemblies.

Here, we report that the mitochondrial inner membrane contains a mega-complex that has remained undetected to date. The complex of ~3 MDa combines factors operating in respiratory chain assembly, metabolism, and lipid biosynthesis and is termed mitochondrial multifunctional assembly (MIMAS). MIMAS goes substantially beyond the size of known mega-complexes of the yeast mitochondrial membranes and may form a protein-lipid platform for integrating biogenesis and metabolic processes of mitochondria.

RESULTSIdentification of a mega-complex in the inner mitochondrial membraneThe ADP/ATP carrier (AAC) and the cytochrome c oxidase (respiratory complex IV) assembly factor Rcf1 interact and can be crosslinked in the mitochondrial inner membrane,42,43 although they are not known to participate in similar functions. AAC is an abundant, highly conserved metabolite carrier, and Rcf1 is a conserved member of the hypoxia-induced gene domain 1 family.42,44–47 To investigate the context of this interaction, we performed two-dimensional (2D) gel analysis, where mitochondrial membrane protein complexes solubilized with the mild detergent digitonin were cleared of unsolubilized material and separated by blue native gel electrophoresis, followed by denaturing SDS-PAGE. Not only did AAC and Rcf1 co-migrate in the 1 MDa mass range of respiratory chain supercomplexes as reported,34,39,42,44,48 but we also observed a novel high-molecular-mass complex that contained both proteins, yet no respiratory chain complexes (Figure 1A). We identified this complex of ~3 MDa also in the mitochondrial complexome (MitCOM) dataset of the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae33 and termed it MIMAS (a giant in Greek mythology) (Figure 1B, top). MIMAS migrated in two peaks of ~3.3 and ~2.5 MDa, termed MIMAS-A and MIMAS-B, respectively, that were clearly separated from the large III2IV2 respiratory chain supercomplex (1 MDa) and the dimeric F1Fo-ATP synthase (1.2 MDa) (Figures 1B and S1). The MDa molecular mass range beyond the dimeric ATP synthase is rarely analyzed in standard native gel electrophoresis, explaining why the complex containing Rcf1 and AAC has escaped discovery so far. Remarkably, considerable fractions of the mitochondrial pools of Aac2 and Rcf1 migrated with MIMAS (Figures 1B and S2).We analyzed the MitCOM dataset further and discovered that a wide spectrum of respiratory chain assembly factors migrated with MIMAS (Figure 1C). These included early assembly factors for the Cox1 core subunit of cytochrome c oxidase such as Coa1 and Coa3, the chaperone Cox20 and the copper transfer protein Sco1 that both participate in Cox2 biogenesis (Figure 1C), as well as the late assembly factors of the Cox3 module Rcf2 and Rcf3 (Figure 1B, top).49–54 In line with recent structural studies that identified Rcf2 as a structural component of respiratory supercomplexes,55,56 a major fraction of it co-migrated with the respiratory III-IV supercomplexes (Figure 1B), while the remainder of Rcf2 migrated with MIMAS. In contrast, the assembly factor Coa657,58 preferentially migrated in the low-molecular-mass range, and only small amounts of Coa6 migrated in the MIMAS range (Figures 1C and 1D).We observed that not only Aac2 but also other metabolite carriers belonging to different protein families migrated in the MIMAS range, including the oxodicarboxylate carrier Odc1, the pyruvate carrier components Mpc1 and Mpc3, and the sideroflexin Fsf1 (Figure 1E).59–61 However, this is not a universal trait of inner membrane transporters since the mitochondrial ABC transporter Mdl162 displayed a distinct migration pattern. Due to their great abundance and significant presence in MIMAS,1,33 carrier proteins, particularly Aac2, are among the most abundant MIMAS components (Figure S2). 2D gel analysis of assembly factors and import of radiolabeled carrier precursors into isolated mitochondria (Figures 1D and 1F) fully supported the complexome data (further support by affinity purification is shown below). Thus, a wide range of respiratory chain assembly factors as well as metabolite carriers migrated in the high-molecular-mass MIMAS range.MIMAS includes metabolite dehydrogenases and phospholipid biosynthesis enzymesSince MIMAS contains a number of metabolite carriers, we expanded our line of inquiry to metabolite conversions that take place at the inner membrane. NADH oxidation in S. cerevisiae mitochondria is accomplished by type II NADH:ubiquinone oxidoreductases.63–67 Whereas the intermembrane space-facing NADH dehydrogenase Nde1 migrated in both MIMAS peaks A and B, the matrix-facing NADH dehydrogenase Ndi1 was not part of MIMAS but migrated in multiple peaks outside the MIMAS range (Figures 2A–2C and S1). In addition to Nde1, the D-lactate dehydrogenase Dld1 also migrated as part of MIMAS (MIMAS-A) (Figure 2A).The inner mitochondrial membrane harbors important steps in the biogenesis of the non-bilayer phospholipids phosphatidylethanolamine (PE) and cardiolipin, the mitochondrial signature phospholipid.68–71 The precursor of PE, phosphatidylserine, is imported from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and converted to PE by phosphatidylserine decarboxylase (Psd1). For the synthesis of cardiolipin, phosphatidic acid is imported from the ER and converted to cardiolipin via several biosynthetic and remodeling steps. We found that Psd1 as well as cardiolipin synthase Crd1 and cardiolipin transacylase Taz1 migrated in the MIMAS range (Figures 2D and 2E). In contrast, enzymes catalyzing early steps in cardiolipin biosynthesis such as Pgs1 and Tam41 mainly migrated outside the MIMAS range (Figure 2D). Thus, MIMAS includes respiratory chain assembly factors, metabolite carriers, dehydrogenases, and select phospholipid biosynthesis enzymes (Figure S2).To directly demonstrate the interaction of representatives of these protein classes by affinity purification, we performed pull-down experiments using Psd1 as bait (Figures 2F–2H). Psd1 co-isolated not only assembly factors such as Rcf proteins but also the enzymes Nde1 and Taz1. Importantly, we were able to isolate MIMAS as an intact complex, and we confirmed the presence of MIMAS components in the high-molecular-mass range of the eluate using blue native electrophoresis (Figures 2G and 2H). While both Nde1 and AAC migrate in several molecular mass populations, the Psd1His co-isolation specifically enriched the MIMAS pool of both proteins (Figures 2G and 2H). The mobility of MIMAS on blue native gels was not altered at digitonin:mitochondrial protein ratios from 4:1 to 20:1 or in the presence of 50–250 mM NaCl; MIMAS was moderately destabilized at ≥150 mM NaCl (Figures S3A and S3B). MIMAS was dissociated upon solubilization of mitochondria with the relatively mild but more delipidating detergent dodecylmaltoside or the harsher detergent Triton X-1008,72 (Figure S3C). Together with the PE dependence of MIMAS stability shown below, these findings support the view that MIMAS is a mega-complex whose stability involves lipid and ionic interactions.In addition to the in organello crosslinking of AAC to Rcf1,42,43 we performed crosslinking using the hetero-bifunctional reagent m-maleimidobenzoyl-N-hydroxysuccinimide ester in intact mitochondria and observed Nde1-specific crosslinking adducts for AAC (Figure 2I). In line with our findings, systematic crosslinking mass spectrometry studies of protein-protein interactions in yeast mitochondria also detected crosslinks between Aac2 and Nde1.36,37 We directly demonstrated the Nde1-AAC crosslinking by affinity purification of StrepNde1 under denaturing conditions and subsequent immunodecoration with α-AAC (Figure 2J). These results demonstrate the close proximity of MIMAS components in intact mitochondria. Thus, despite the variety of MIMAS components, the complex can be isolated biochemically and includes proteins that can be crosslinked in intact mitochondria.MIMAS integrity specifically depends on PEWe asked if known inner membrane mega-complexes are part of MIMAS. In addition to the III2IV2 respiratory supercomplex and the dimeric ATP synthase, the prohibitin complex is a large inner membrane complex of yeast mitochondria. Prohibitins form scaffolding complexes and are important for compartmentalizing phospholipids and proteins in the inner membrane.73–75 Prohibitins 1 and 2 tightly co-migrated with each other in the MitCOM dataset with a peak at 2.2 MDa and a profile shape that is clearly distinct from MIMAS (Figure 3A). Furthermore, MIMAS was not disrupted upon deletion of core components of the mitochondrial contact site and cristae organizing system that performs important roles in cristae architecture of the inner membrane76,77 (Figures S3D and S3E).The native inner membrane phospholipid composition and in particular the non-bilayer-forming lipids PE and cardiolipin are important for the stability and activity of numerous inner membrane complexes.70,71,78 Since MIMAS includes several enzymes involved in the biosynthesis of non-bilayer phospholipids, we analyzed the integrity of MIMAS in dependence on PE and cardiolipin. We used mitochondria from cells lacking either Psd1, which causes strongly reduced mitochondrial PE levels, or Crd1, resulting in cardiolipin deficiency.68,79,80 The stability of respiratory supercomplexes is known to depend on cardiolipin; in crd1Δ mitochondria, the supercomplexes are destabilized, whereas loss of Psd1 even slightly stabilizes respiratory supercomplexes compared to wild-type mitochondria (Figure 3B).70,71,80–84 Remarkably, the lack of Psd1, but not of Crd1, led to a strong destabilization of MIMAS complexes, shown for the various MIMAS components acting in respiratory assembly, metabolite transport, dehydrogenation, and phospholipid biosynthesis by blue native electrophoresis and 2D gel analysis (Figures 3B–3E). This was not due to decreased steady-state levels of these proteins (Figure S4A). The prohibitin complex, the F1Fo-ATP synthase, and the translocase of the outer membrane were largely unaffected in their stability in psd1Δ and crd1Δ mitochondria (Figures 3B and 3C). Thus, in contrast to other large mitochondrial membrane complexes, MIMAS specifically requires Psd1 and/or PE for its stability.To study if the protein Psd1 or its catalytic activity, i.e., the formation of PE, was required for MIMAS stability, we used the catalytically inactive mutant Psd1-S463A.85 Upon import into wild-type mitochondria, wild-type Psd1 and the Psd1 mutant protein were found in MIMAS (Figure 3F), indicating that the point mutation did not prevent the assembly of Psd1 into MIMAS. When expressed in psd1Δ cells, however, wild-type Psd1, but not Psd1-S463A, restored the formation of MIMAS (Figures 3G and S4B), demonstrating that the enzymatic activity of Psd1 was required for the integrity of MIMAS. We conclude that the integrity of MIMAS strongly depends on the phospholipid PE in contrast to other mitochondrial mega-complexes.Respiratory chain biogenesis factors at MIMASWe asked if further biogenesis steps of respiratory chain subunits may be related to MIMAS and observed that the inner membrane peptidase with the catalytic subunits Imp1 and Imp2 migrated at MIMAS (A+B) (Figures 4A and S1). The two subunits display distinct substrate specificities,86 but both are important for biogenesis of the respiratory chain. (1) Imp1 processes the complex IV core component Cox2.87 Thus, several factors required for Cox2 biogenesis interacted with MIMAS: the peptidase Imp1, the chaperone Cox20 that presents the Cox2 precursor to Imp1, and the copper transfer protein Sco1 (Figures 1C and 4A–4C).86,88–92 (2) Imp2 processes the complex III subunit cytochrome c1 (Cyt1).86 The covalent attachment of heme to Cyt1 occurs immediately before cleavage by Imp2.93 The Cyt1-specific heme lyase Cyt2 also co-migrated with MIMAS-A (Figures 4D and S1), revealing a migration of two Cyt1-specific biogenesis factors, heme lyase and peptidase, with MIMAS. (3) The second mitochondrial heme lyase Cyc3, which attaches heme to the electron carrier protein cytochrome c (Cyc1),94 also interacted with MIMAS (Figures 4B and 4D). Hemylation of the apocytochromes involves the heme reductase Cyc295,96 that similarly co-migrated with MIMAS (Figure 4D), whereas the mature cytochromes did not. Cyt1 is part of the respiratory supercomplexes, and cytochrome c is found in several smaller assemblies (Figures 1B and 4D). The finding that both heme lyases migrate at MIMAS solves a long-standing question: the proteins lack hydrophobic domains and have been reported to be associated with the mitochondrial inner membrane via an unknown mechanism97–99; their migration with MIMAS readily explains the localization at the inner membrane. (4) It has been reported that Rcf1 plays a role in the assembly of Cox13 into complex IV.42,44 Since Rcf1 is one of our founding components of MIMAS (Figures 1A and 1B), we asked if the assembly pathway of Cox13 may be related to MIMAS. Upon import of the radiolabeled precursor of Cox13 into mitochondria, the protein appeared not only in respiratory supercomplexes and monomeric complex IV but also in the high-molecular-mass MIMAS range (Figure 4E). Since mature Cox13 is not part of MIMAS (Figure 4D), we investigated if the Cox13 precursor at MIMAS represented a transient biogenesis intermediate. We imported the Cox13 precursor into isolated mitochondria, followed by reisolation of the mitochondria and chase of accumulated precursor. Blue native electrophoresis indeed demonstrated that MIMAS-localized Cox13 was of transient nature; it decreased over time in contrast to the Cox13 signal in mature complex IV and supercomplexes (Figure 4F).Rcf1 contains a conserved QRRQ motif that is involved in interaction with the Cox3 module.42,44,46,51,55,100 Garlich et al.43 showed that the R67A mutation of this motif leads to an almost complete loss of crosslinking of Rcf1 with AAC but enhanced respiratory chain interaction, whereas the Q61,71A mutant protein shows the opposite behavior, including enhanced interaction with AAC. Strikingly, we found that these conserved residues dictated Rcf1 interactions with the respiratory chain versus MIMAS. Whereas Rcf1-R67A was unable to bind MIMAS, Rcf1-Q61, 71A showed increased localization in MIMAS (Figure 4G). The MIMAS defective Rcf1-R67A mutant was indeed shown to display specific defects in complex IV assembly.43,51Taken together, these results support the view that the interaction between Cox13 and Rcf1 is initiated at MIMAS, followed by incorporation of Cox13 into complex IV. The mutant-specific shift in MIMAS localization and function of Rcf1 underscore our conclusion that MIMAS is not simply a large unspecific agglomerate but that specific point mutations in a protein with dual localization can direct it to or away from MIMAS.To investigate MIMAS components and interactors more systematically, we performed label-free mass spectrometric analysis of Psd1His/MIMAS eluates (Figure 4H; Table S1; STAR Methods). In parallel, we identified MIMAS candidates based on their MitCOM profiles33 by correlation analysis and manual curation and classified the stringent Psd1 interactors according to their profiles as well as their presence in the high-confidence yeast mitochondrial proteome1 (Figure 4I; Table S1). Almost all MIMAS components characterized in this study were recovered among the stringent Psd1 interactors (Figure 4H). Moreover, more than half of the Psd1 interactors displayed a MitCOM complexome profile of the MIMAS type, including major or moderate peaks in the MIMAS region (Figure 4I; Table S1). Additional MIMAS candidates identified with these approaches often belonged to the groups of mitochondrial carriers or respiratory chain biogenesis factors, consistent with the protein functions observed in MIMAS.

MIMAS includes metabolite dehydrogenases and phospholipid biosynthesis enzymes

Since MIMAS contains a number of metabolite carriers, we expanded our line of inquiry to metabolite conversions that take place at the inner membrane. NADH oxidation in S. cerevisiae mitochondria is accomplished by type II NADH:ubiquinone oxidoreductases.63–67 Whereas the intermembrane space-facing NADH dehydrogenase Nde1 migrated in both MIMAS peaks A and B, the matrix-facing NADH dehydrogenase Ndi1 was not part of MIMAS but migrated in multiple peaks outside the MIMAS range (Figures 2A–2C and S1). The inner mitochondrial membrane is extraordinarily rich in proteins and home to many central actors of mitochondrial physiology, including the oxidative phosphorylation machinery, protein translocases and biogenesis factors, metabolic enzymes, metabolite and ion transporters, architectural proteins, and quality control factors. Exemplifying the high protein density of the inner membrane, a recent study on quality control coupled to protein biogenesis found that a large number of proteins belonging to distinct processes were present in the vicinity of the mitoribosomal polypeptide tunnel exit and of the prohibitin scaffold.75 Interestingly, we show here that a subset of proteins found in these proximity networks, including several respiratory chain assembly factors and the dehydrogenases Nde1 and Dld1,75 are part of a specific mega-complex. The formation of a multifunctional mega-assembly like MIMAS could be one of the cellular strategies for the efficient organization of a proteinrich membrane.

We propose that MIMAS functions as a biogenesis and assembly platform of the mitochondrial inner membrane that brings metabolic processes as well as respiratory biogenesis and assembly factors into spatial and functional proximity by integrating them into a protein-lipid mega-assembly. MIMAS reveals a higher-level organization of mitochondrial proteins by integrating diverse functions into a large mega-complex.

QUANTIFICATION AND STATISTICAL ANALYSISThe MS data from WT and Psd1His affinity purifications (each n = 4) were analyzed in Spectronaut. For this, first a hybrid spectral library was construced by searching both DIA and DDA data against both an UniProt SwissProt yeast (release 2022_01_07, 6721 entries) and a common contaminants database117 (381 entries). Trypsin/P was selected as enzyme, with 2 missed cleavages allowed and peptide length restricted to 7–52 amino acids. Propionamide (C) was set as fixed, while Acetyl (Protein N-term) and Oxidation (M) as variable modifications. The library was then used to identify and quantify peptides/proteins in all DIA files using the following settings: XIC extraction window and mass tolerances were set to “Dynamic”, iRT calibration to “Automatic”, and all precursor & protein identification thresholds (Qvalue, PEP) to 0.01. Quantification precursor filtering was set to “Identified (Qvalue)”, Quantity MS level to “MS2” and Protein LFQ method to “Automatic”. Since a total number of eight samples was interrogated, this setting means that Spectronaut used the MaxLFQ-algorithm. Both imputation and cross-run normalization were disabled. Afterward, a report of all identified and quantified proteins was exported as tsv-file and further processed in R Statistical Software for differential expression (DE)-analysis. For this, protein abundances were first log2-transformed, followed by filtering out proteins flagged as contaminants as well as those identified with less than 3 abundance values in at least one experimental condition (WT, Psd1His). Missing values were imputed for each individual sample using random numbers drawn from a downshifted normal distribution (“MinProb”-method, MsCoreUtils, version 1.14.1). Differentially isolated proteins between Psd1His and WT were determined by a moderated t test applying linear modeling (limma trend, version 3.58.1). Limma also performs p value adjustment according to the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure to account for multiple hypothesis testing. Since His-affinity purifications at low salt concentration can lead to unspecific associations of proteins in variable amounts, whereas specific associations are highly reproducible, we applied stringent filter criteria for evaluation of the Psd1His/BN-eluates (termed Psd1 interactome). Proteins were declared significantly enriched when identified with more than 1 peptide in experimental condition “Psd1”, and exhibiting a log2 fold change >3 and an adjusted p value <0.002. The data from individual replicates as well as the statistical analysis results can be found in Table S1.

QUANTIFICATION AND STATISTICAL ANALYSIS

The MS data from WT and Psd1His affinity purifications (each n = 4) were analyzed in Spectronaut. For this, first a hybrid spectral library was construced by searching both DIA and DDA data against both an UniProt SwissProt yeast (release 2022_01_07, 6721 entries) and a common contaminants database117 (381 entries). Trypsin/P was selected as enzyme, with 2 missed cleavages allowed and peptide length restricted to 7–52 amino acids. Propionamide (C) was set as fixed, while Acetyl (Protein N-term) and Oxidation (M) as variable modifications. The library was then used to identify and quantify peptides/proteins in all DIA files using the following settings: XIC extraction window and mass tolerances were set to “Dynamic”, iRT calibration to “Automatic”, and all precursor & protein identification thresholds (Qvalue, PEP) to 0.01. Quantification precursor filtering was set to “Identified (Qvalue)”, Quantity MS level to “MS2” and Protein LFQ method to “Automatic”. Since a total number of eight samples was interrogated, this setting means that Spectronaut used the MaxLFQ-algorithm. Both imputation and cross-run normalization were disabled. Afterward, a report of all identified and quantified proteins was exported as tsv-file and further processed in R Statistical Software for differential expression (DE)-analysis. For this, protein abundances were first log2-transformed, followed by filtering out proteins flagged as contaminants as well as those identified with less than 3 abundance values in at least one experimental condition (WT, Psd1His). Missing values were imputed for each individual sample using random numbers drawn from a downshifted normal distribution (“MinProb”-method, MsCoreUtils, version 1.14.1). Differentially isolated proteins between Psd1His and WT were determined by a moderated t test applying linear modeling (limma trend, version 3.58.1). Limma also performs p value adjustment according to the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure to account for multiple hypothesis testing. Since His-affinity purifications at low salt concentration can lead to unspecific associations of proteins in variable amounts, whereas specific associations are highly reproducible, we applied stringent filter criteria for evaluation of the Psd1His/BN-eluates (termed Psd1 interactome). Proteins were declared significantly enriched when identified with more than 1 peptide in experimental condition “Psd1”, and exhibiting a log2 fold change >3 and an adjusted p value <0.002. The data from individual replicates as well as the statistical analysis results can be found in Table S1.
