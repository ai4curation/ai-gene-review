# Eukaryotic translation initiation factor 5A induces apoptosis in colon cancer cells and associates with the nucleus in response to tumour necrosis factor alpha signalling.
**Authors:** Taylor CA, Sun Z, Cliche DO, Ming H, Eshaque B, Jin S, Hopkins MT, Thai B, Thompson JE
**Journal:** Exp Cell Res (2007)
**DOI:** [10.1016/j.yexcr.2006.09.030](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yexcr.2006.09.030)

cells and associates with the nucleus in response to tumour necrosis factor 
alpha signalling.

Thompson JE.

Author information:
(1)Department of Biology, University of Waterloo, 200 University Ave. W.,

Eukaryotic translation initiation factor 5A (eIF5A) is thought to function as a 
nucleocytoplasmic shuttle protein. There are reports of its involvement in cell 
proliferation, and more recently it has also been implicated in the regulation 
of apoptosis. In the present study, we examined the effects of eIF5A 
over-expression on apoptosis and of siRNA-mediated suppression of eIF5A on 
expression of the tumour suppressor protein, p53. Over-expression of either 
eIF5A or a mutant of eIF5A incapable of being hypusinated was found to induce 
apoptosis in colon carcinoma cells. Our results also indicate that eIF5A is 
required for expression of p53 following the induction of apoptosis by treatment 
with Actinomycin D. Depiction of eIF5A localization by indirect 
immunofluorescence has indicated, for the first time, that the protein is 
rapidly translocated from the cytoplasm to the nucleus by death receptor 
activation or following treatment with Actinomycin D. These findings 
collectively indicate that unhypusinated eIF5A may have pro-apoptotic functions 
and that eIF5A is rapidly translocated to the nucleus following the induction of 
apoptotic cell death.
