# The octopus genome and the evolution of cephalopod neural and morphological novelties

## Abstract

Coleoid cephalopods (octopus, squid and cuttlefish) are active, resourceful predators with a rich behavioural repertoire. They have the largest nervous systems among the invertebrates. To investigate the molecular bases of cephalopod brain and body innovations, we sequenced the genome and multiple transcriptomes of the California two-spot octopus, Octopus bimaculoides. The core developmental and neuronal gene repertoire is broadly similar to that found across invertebrate bilaterians, except for massive expansions in protocadherins and C2H2 zinc-finger transcription factors. Extensive mRNA editing generates transcript and protein diversity. We identified hundreds of cephalopod-specific genes with elevated expression in specialized structures.

## Key Findings

- First octopus genome (O. bimaculoides) sequenced and assembled
- 2.7 Gb genome with approximately 33,000 protein-coding genes
- No evidence for whole-genome duplications
- Massive expansions in protocadherins and C2H2 zinc-finger transcription factors
- Extensive mRNA editing in neural genes
- Hundreds of cephalopod-specific genes identified
- Gene model OCBIM_22008529mg encodes the serotonin transporter (SERT/SLC6A4)
