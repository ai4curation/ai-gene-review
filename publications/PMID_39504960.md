The biological purpose of Integrator and RNA polymerase II (RNAPII) 
promoter-proximal pausing remains uncertain. Here, we show that loss of INTS6 in 
human cells results in increased interaction of RNAPII with proteins that can 
mediate its dissociation from the DNA template, including the CRL3ARMC5 E3 
ligase, which ubiquitylates CTD serine5-phosphorylated RPB1 for degradation. 
ARMC5-dependent RNAPII ubiquitylation is activated by defects in factors acting 
at the promoter-proximal pause, including Integrator, DSIF, and capping enzyme. 
This ARMC5 checkpoint normally curtails a sizeable fraction of RNAPII 
transcription, and ARMC5 knockout cells produce more uncapped transcripts. When 
both the Integrator and CRL3ARMC5 turnover mechanisms are compromised, cell 
growth ceases and RNAPII with high pausing propensity disperses from the 
promoter-proximal pause site into the gene body. These data support a model in 
which CRL3ARMC5 functions alongside Integrator in a checkpoint mechanism that 
removes faulty RNAPII complexes at promoter-proximal pause sites to safeguard 
transcription integrity.

Introduction

Promoter-proximal pausing is a key stage in the RNA polymerase II (RNAPII) transcription cycle on metazoan protein-coding genes1–3. Upon initiation and transcription of 20–60 nucleotides, RNAPII undergoes pausing which depends on its association with DSIF (5,6-dichloro-l-β-D-ribofuranosyl benzimidazole (DRB)-sensitivity inducing factor) and NELF (Negative Elongation Factor)4–8. Paradoxically, however, neither depletion of DSIF nor NELF substantially reduces RNAPII accumulation at this promoter-proximal pause site9,10. After formation of the paused elongation complex, RNAPII can take distinct paths. First, it may commit to synthesis of the full-length transcript and progress into the productive transcript elongation phase, which requires the pTEFb (positive transcription elongation factor b) kinase11–13. Alternatively, RNAPII may be prematurely terminated by Integrator, Restrictor, or other mechanisms14,15. Integrator associates with the paused elongation complex16 and brings about dissociation of early RNAPII elongation complexes through its RNA cleavage and protein phosphatase activities17–24. The extent of premature termination by Integrator is quite limited in most studies17,23,25, but it remains possible that the impact of Integrator is masked by other, functionally redundant mechanisms operating at the promoter-proximal pause15. Furthermore, the precise mechanism of the termination-versus-elongation decision at the promoter-proximal pause remains unclear.

Despite its genome-wide prevalence and seemingly fundamental nature, the biological role of promotor-proximal pausing is unclear. In specific cases, escape from the pause into productive elongation constitutes a rate-limiting step for transcription output. So, pausing can be a mechanism of transcription regulation, for example at the heat-shock genes, which exhibit high levels of RNAPII premature termination and promoter-proximal pausing at their promoters in the absence of gene activation26,27. Pausing at highly regulated genes28,29 has sometimes been used as basis for the hypothesis that the process functions as a major regulatory node for determining transcription output. However, promoter-proximal pausing is detected at most genes in human and Drosophila melanogaster cells6,10,30, including constitutive ‘housekeeping’ genes, which seems inconsistent with a function in regulating expression levels.

One possible explanation for the general nature of promoter-proximal pausing would be if it constitutes a quality control checkpoint which assures correct elongation complex assembly and capping of the nascent RNA 5’-end before release into productive elongation. Deposition of the 5’ mRNA cap occurs shortly after initiation, and recruitment of capping enzyme requires phosphorylation of serine 5 (Ser5) of the RPB1 C-terminal domain (CTD)31–34 and is stimulated by pausing factor DSIF35–37. The cap protects mRNA from 5’−3’ exonucleases and acts as a unique identifier for recruiting proteins involved in pre-mRNA splicing, poly-adenylation, nuclear export, and translation38. Already prior to the emergence of promoter-proximal pausing as a general regulatory step, an elongation checkpoint that helps ensure that capping occurs prior to release into processive elongation was suggested39–43, but no molecular components or mechanistic details of such a checkpoint have been identified. In the context of these different models for pausing and premature termination, we note that Integrator has been described both as an attenuator of transcription output17–19,21, and as a terminator of defective RNAPII complexes mediating a quality control process23. Interestingly in this respect, there is recent evidence that defects in DSIF, NELF or Integrator may indeed lead to release of RNAPII complexes which are transcriptionally compromised10,23,24,44.

In other circumstances that are unrelated to promoter-proximal pausing, ineffectual RNAPII is removed by poly-ubiquitylation and proteasomal degradation of the large RNAPII subunit, RPB11,45,46. This “last-resort” mechanism is invoked by transcriptional blockage and RNAPII arrest in response to UV-irradiation, chemical inhibition of transcription, or deficiencies in DSIF or TFIIS44,47–51. Such degradation of persistently arrested RNAPII may be important to prevent clashes between RNAPII and other chromatin-associated machineries such as DNA replication forks, which may ultimately result in genome instability.

Of the 15 core Integrator subunits, INTS6 has gained particular attention due to its function as a co-factor for protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A) and because the INTS6 gene is silenced in various cancers, making it a potential tumor suppressor21,52,53. By depleting INTS6, we observed a paused, Ser5-phosphorylated form of RNAPII at the beginning of genes, which can be removed by a mechanism involving poly-ubiquitylation and degradation of RNAPII via the Cullin 3-ARMC5 E3 ligase (CRL3ARMC5). Importantly, both INTS6 and ARMC5 are non-essential in HEK293 cells. However, their simultaneous loss greatly compromises cell growth and viability, and the 5’ peak of RNAPII density at most genes is dramatically diminished. Importantly, instead of being removed, poorly elongating RNAPII is dispersed into the gene body, suggesting that the promoter-proximal transcription checkpoint is greatly compromised. Our findings point to crucial, overlapping functions of Integrator and ARMC5 in the removal of defective RNA polymerase II transcription complexes at this checkpoint.

DISCUSSIONThe findings described in this study support the idea that a checkpoint operates at the promoter-proximal pause to ensure the correct assembly of a fully functional elongation complex with a capped transcript. Moreover, our data indicate that Integrator and the CRL3ARMC5 E3 ligase are executors of this checkpoint, which removes incompletely matured elongation complexes, either by dissociation and recycling (Integrator), or ubiquitin-mediated RPB1 degradation (CRL3ARMC5). While cell growth appears largely unaffected by the absence of either INTS6 or ARMC5, loss of both has severe consequences, indicating functionally important redundancy between these RNAPII removal pathways. We suggest that dynamic removal of defective RNAPII complexes occurs on a surprisingly large scale at the pause site and is crucial for transcription integrity.The order of events during initiation and promoter-proximal pausingPrevious data suggested that INTS6 and INTS8 are important to allow Integrator to associate with protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A), which counteracts phosphorylation of target proteins important for pausing and pause-release19,21,22,60,61,64. The data presented here show that INTS6 is also important for the association of Integrator with RNAPII in chromatin, such as at promoter-proximal pause sites. Given that previous structural characterization of Integrator with paused RNAPII complexes failed to show evidence for an interaction between INTS6 and RNAPII itself16,84, it seems obvious to speculate that the loss of a direct interaction between INTS6 and NELFB16 might instead underlie the change. However, the molecular mechanism underlying INTS6’s role in the association between Integrator and RNAPII at the promoter-proximal pause remains an interesting area for further study.Importantly, our data outline a revised, more detailed pathway for Integrator function and the precise timing of phosphorylation/dephosphorylation at or near the promoter. Using DRB-inhibition of pTEFb and mobility shift analysis of RPB1 in Western blots, we identified an interesting RNAPII intermediate, namely a paused and CTD serine5-phosphorylated form (RNAPII-PP), which is stabilized in INTS6-deficient cells. On an SDS-PAGE gel, RNAPII-PP migrates in-between RNAPII-O and RNAPII-A. We propose that this is due to exclusive phosphorylation of Ser5 residues, and that subsequent release from promoter-proximal regions and accompanying Ser2 phosphorylation is necessary for formation of the hyper-phosphorylated II-O form. Notably, paused RNAPII is also stabilized by ARMC5 loss and becomes even more abundant when both INTS6 and ARMC5 are absent.Analysis of the RNAPII-PP intermediate supports a model for the early events in the transcription cycle in which RNAPII, phosphorylated at CTD Ser5 by TFIIH during initiation, stops at the promoter-proximal pause site by association with DSIF and NELF. Capping enzyme recruitment and RNA capping ensue, as well as assembly of a functional elongation complex. pTEFb-mediated phosphorylation of DSIF, NELF, and of the RNAPII CTD then allows this elongation complex to escape the pause to transcribe the gene (Figure 7E, middle path, thick arrows). However, if capping or elongation complex assembly fails, then Integrator-PP2A instead de-phosphorylates CTD Ser5 and RNAPII is dissociated in a poorly understood process that involves Integrator-mediated transcript cleavage. CTD de-phosphorylation is important for RNAPII dissociation19, and enables the recycling of the dissociated RNAPIIA form for a new round of transcription (Figure 7E, lower path). ARMC5 provides an alternative pathway to such dissociation: the CRL3ARMC5 E3 ligase can ubiquitylate Ser5-phosphorylated RNAPII at the pause, causing its degradation (Figure 7E, upper path).We note that while this work was in progress, Lao et al reported that ARMC5 is part of an RNAPII E3 ligase containing CUL385, and that Aoi et al has shown that CUL3 is involved in RPB1 degradation triggered by loss of SPT544. However, neither of these works uncovered the underlying mechanisms delineated here. Whereas RNAPII ChIP-seq in mouse adrenal glands led to the conclusion that ARMC5 loss does not cause RNAPII stalling85, our eNETseq and PRO-seq experiments in HEK293 revealed a marked increase in RNAPII accumulation at the 5’ pause site in ARMC5 KO cells, presumably due to reduced turnover by ubiquitylation and degradation. Interestingly, in the adrenal cells investigated by Lao et al, the RPB1 pool was also enlarged upon ARMC5 mutation, which correlated with dysregulation of gene expression at the level of mRNA85. In contrast, in HEK293 cells, ARMC5 KO had only relatively minor effects on mRNA levels. The differences may be due to the different cell types employed and might help explain why ARMC5 deficiencies particularly affect adrenal glands.Two functionally redundant paths to RNAPII dissociation at the promoter-proximal pauseThe results reported here demonstrate a previously unsuspected redundancy between two highly active turnover mechanisms operating at the promoter-proximal pause. Only when both mechanisms are disabled is the full impact of RNAPII turnover evident. Thus, depletion of INTS6 alone has only rather limited effects on transcription as previously observed25, but when it is depleted in the absence of CRL3ARMC5 there is widespread and profound redistribution of RNAPII. Our results suggest that active turnover by CRL3ARMC5 normally masks the effect of Integrator, and when ARMC5 is deleted then Integrator takes over to terminate a large fraction of RNAPII at the promoter proximal pause.Paradoxically, concomitant inactivation of the turnover factors INTS6 and ARMC5 results in a loss of the ubiquitous peak of RNAPII at the promoter-proximal pause. We interpret this unexpected result to mean that the redundant turnover mechanisms of Integrator and CRL3ARMC5 actively evict many defective RNAPII complexes close to the promoter-proximal pause-site, and that when turnover is compromised those polymerases disperse over several kb into the gene body and no longer accumulate at a discrete position. The dramatic effect of disabling turnover by both INTS6 and ARMC5-dependent pathways were observed in cells that are not otherwise stressed. If, as suggested by our results, these mechanisms preferentially target defective elongation complexes, then the process of assembling a fully competent elongation complex would appear to be surprisingly inefficient under normal conditions. Alternatively, some correctly assembled elongationally competent complexes may also be subject to turnover by Integrator and CRL3ARMC5.We note that while Integrator and CRL3ARMC5 are two major effectors of turnover at the promoter-proximal pause, they are probably not the only ones. Comparison of RNAPII interactomes in the presence and absence of INTS6 revealed not only ARMC5, but also other proteins with a potential role in dissociating promoter-proximal RNAPII, such as TTF2, Senataxin, and PCF1115. Mass spectrometry analysisRaw mass spectrometry data were analyzed with MaxQuant (v1.6.15.0). Peak lists were searched against the human Uniprot FASTA database combined with 262 common contaminants by the integrated Andromeda search engine. False discovery rate was set to 1 % for both peptides (minimum length of 7 amino acids) and proteins. “Match between runs” (MBR) was enabled with a Match time window of 0.7, and a Match ion mobility window of 0.05 min. Relative protein amounts were determined by the MaxLFQ algorithm with a minimum ratio count of two. Statistical analysis of LFQ derived protein expression data was performed using the automated analysis pipeline of the Clinical Knowledge Graph 93. Protein entries referring to potential contaminants, proteins identified by matches to the decoy reverse database, and proteins identified only by modified sites, were removed. LFQ intensity values were normalized by log2 transformation and proteins with less than 70% of valid values in at least one group were filtered out. The remaining missing values were imputed using the MinProb approach (random draws from a Gaussian distribution; width = 0.2 and downshift = 1.8) 94 or with Mixed imputation using KNN.
