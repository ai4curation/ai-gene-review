Kim KY(1), Levin DE.

Author information:
(1)Department of Molecular and Cell Biology, Goldman School of Dental Medicine, 
Boston University, Boston, MA 02118, USA.

The Mpk1 MAP kinase of the cell wall integrity (CWI) signalling pathway induces 
transcription of the FKS2 gene in response to cell wall stress through a 
non-catalytic mechanism that involves stable association of Mpk1 with the Swi4 
transcription factor. This dimeric complex binds to a Swi4 recognition site in 
the FKS2 promoter. The Swi6 transcription factor is also required to bind this 
ternary complex for transcription initiation to ensue. In this context, the Mlp1 
pseudokinase serves a redundant function with Mpk1. We have identified three 
additional genes, CHA1, YLR042c and YKR013w, that are induced by cell wall 
stress through the same mechanism. We report on the behaviour of several 
promoter-lacZ reporter plasmids designed to detect cell wall stress 
transcription through this pathway.

Introduction

The cell wall of the budding yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae is required to maintain cell shape and integrity (Klis, 1994; Cid et al., 1995). The cell must remodel this rigid structure during vegetative growth, and during pheromone-induced morphogenesis. Wall remodeling is monitored and regulated by the cell wall integrity (CWI) signaling, which activates a MAP kinase cascade (reviewed in Levin, 2005). The MAP kinase cascade is a linear pathway comprised of Pkc1, a MEKK (Bck1), a pair of redundant MEKs (Mkk1/2) and a MAP kinase (Mpk1/Slt2). Mpk1 is a functional homolog of human ERK5 (Truman et al., 2006), a MAPK that is activated in response to growth factors as well as physical and chemical stresses (Abe, et al., 1996; Yan et al., 2001).

CWI signaling is induced in response to a variety of cell wall stressors. First, signaling is activated persistently in response to growth at elevated temperatures (e.g. 37–39°C; Kamada et al., 1995), consistent with the finding that null mutants in many of the pathway components display cell lysis defects only when cultivated at high temperature. Second, hypo-osmotic shock induces a rapid, but transient activation of signaling (Davenport et al., 1995; Kamada et al., 1995). Third, treatment with mating pheromone stimulates signaling at a time that is coincident with the onset of morphogenesis (Buehrer and Errede, 1997). Finally, CWI signaling is also stimulated by agents that interfere with cell wall biogenesis, such as the chitin antagonist Calcofluor white (Ketela et al., 1999), Congo Red, caffeine, or Zymolyase (de Nobel et al., 2000; Martin et al., 2000).

CWI signaling pathway induces activation of two known transcription factors. One of these is Rlm1 (Dodou and Treisman, 1997; Watanabe et al., 1997), which is activated through phosphorylation by Mpk1 (Jung et al., 2002). A second transcription factor that plays a role in CWI signaling is SBF (Madden et al., 1997; Baetz et al., 2001). SBF is a dimeric transcriptional regulator, comprised of Swi4 and Swi6, which is essential to normal regulation of G1-specific transcription (reviewed in Breeden, 2003). Swi4 is the sequence-specific DNA-binding subunit (CA/GCGAAA; Taylor et al., 2000), but Swi6 is required for binding to cell cycle-regulated promoters (Andrews and Moore, 1992; Baetz and Andrews, 1999; Sidorova and Breeden, 1993). Swi6 allows Swi4 to bind DNA at cell cycle-regulated promoters by relieving an auto-inhibitory intramolecular association of the Swi4 C-terminus with its DNA-binding domain. Additionally, Swi6 is the transcriptional activation component of SBF (Sedgwick et al., 1998).

That SBF has a second function related to CWI signaling was suggested with several findings. First, the cell lysis defect of an mpk1 mutant is suppressed by overexpression of Swi4 (Madden et al., 1997). Second, both swi4Δ and swi6Δ mutants are hypersensitive to Calcofluor White, supporting a role for SBF in cell wall biogenesis (Igual et al., 1996). Third, Mpk1 associates with SBF in vivo (Madden et al., 1997) and with Swi4 (but not Swi6) in vitro (Baetz et al., 2001). Fourth, Swi6 is phosphorylated in vivo and in vitro by Mpk1 in response to cell wall stress (Madden et al., 1997).

We found previously that CWI signaling drives expression of the FKS2 gene through SBF in a manner that is independent of the role of this factor in G1-specific transcription (Kim et al., 2008; Truman et al., 2009). Mpk1 and its pseudokinase paralog, Mlp1, which is also activated by the MEKs of the CWI signaling pathway, use a non-catalytic mechanism to activate transcription of FKS2 that is dependent on SBF and on activating signal. Activated (phosphorylated) Mpk1, or Mlp1, form a complex with Swi4 that associates with an SBF-binding site in the FKS2 promoter independently of Swi6 (Kim et al., 2008). In this context, Mpk1 (or Mlp1) relieves the auto-inhibitory Swi4 interaction by binding to a MAPK docking site on Swi4 that neighbors the C-terminal Swi6-binding site (Truman et al., 2009). Mutational ablation of the MAPK interaction site on Swi4 specifically blocks cell wall stress-induced FKS2 transcription, without interfering with the cell cycle functions of Swi4. Although Swi6 is not required for Swi4 to bind to the FKS2 promoter, it must still be recruited to the Mpk1-Swi4 complex to activate transcription.

Finally, we demonstrated recently that Mpk1 phosphorylation of Swi6 is a negative regulatory event that interferes with Swi6 entry to the nucleus (Kim et al., in press). Nucleocytoplasmic shuttling of Swi6 in response to CWI signaling is a biphasic process. First, upon activation of Mpk1, Swi6 is recruited to the nucleus in a manner dependent on formation of the Mpk1-Swi4 complex, but independent of Mpk1 catalytic activity. Swi6 subsequently exits the nucleus in response to phosphorylation by Mpk1 on Ser238, which interferes with the function of an adjacent nuclear localization signal recognized by the Kap120 β-importin.

Here, we identify three additional genes, CHA1 (YCL064c), YKR013w, and YLR042c, that are regulated through this cell wall stress transcriptional pathway. We also describe new lacZ reporter plasmids with promoter regions from these genes.

ResultsWe wished to identify additional genes whose regulation in response to cell wall stress was similar to that of FKS2. Two previous studies were useful toward this goal. García et al. (2004 (2009) determined the global transcriptional responses to cell wall stress induced by Congo Red and Zymolyase. Among the many genes whose expression was induced by cell wall stress, only a small number did not require the Rlm1 transcription factor. These included CHA1, FKS2, PHO89, YLR042c (García et al. 2004), and YKR013w (García et al. 2009). Each of these genes, except PHO89, contains at least one consensus site for Swi4 binding (C[A/G]CGAAA). Therefore, we constructed lacZ reporter plasmids bearing 1000 nucleotides 5’ to the translational start codon for each of these genes (except FKS2).The YKR013w promoter contains three putative Swi4-binding sites at positions −377, −361, and −209. The full-length YKR013w promoter fusion to lacZ expressed a high level of basal activity, which complicated detection of induced transcription in response to cell wall stress induced by elevated growth temperature (Figure 1). The high level of basal transcriptional activity is likely due to the periodic regulation of YKR013w through the cell cycle (Spellman et al., 1998) through one or more of its Swi4-binding sites active during G1 transcription. Therefore, we created additional reporters that separated these sites from one another. A reporter that carries the two 5’-most Swi4-binding sites, nucleotides −461 to −287 fused to the basal CYC1 promoter, behaved essentially like the full-length promoter, but displaying approximately half the basal activity with no detectable induction by cell wall stress (Figure 1). Therefore, we separated these sites from each other in two additional reporters. One of these contained the 5’-most Swi4 site (nucleotides −461 to −364); the other contained the middle Swi4 site (nucleotides −371 to −287). Both of these reporters displayed low basal expression, suggesting that both Swi4 sites are required for cell cycle regulation. Although the reporter bearing the Swi4 site at position −361 was responsive to cell wall stress, the reporter with the Swi4 site at position −377 was not, suggesting that there is an important functional distinction between these two Swi4 sites.The YLR042c promoter contains a single putative Swi4-binding site at position -179. Similarly, the CHA1 promoter contains a single putative Swi4-binding site at position -286. However, it also contains a heat shock responsive Hsf1-binding element at position -701 and an ER stress-responsive Hac1-binding element at position -200, which confounded transcriptional activation by cell wall stress in response to elevated temperature (not shown). To isolate the SBF-binding site from the other elements, we created a CHA1-lacZ reporter in which CHA1 nucleotides −500 to −263 were fused to the basal CYC1 promoter, as described previously for FKS2 (Kim et al., 2008). The PHO89-lacZ reporter was non-responsive to cell wall stress in our hands. Although the reporter we created possessed 1 kb of 5’ untranslated sequence, we did not find a consensus Swi4-binding site at any point within 2 kb of the translational start site for this gene. We cannot explain the discrepancy between our results and the microarray data (García et al. 2004). Therefore, we did not pursue PHO89 further.We compared the new promoter-lacZ reporters to an FKS2-lacZ reporter (Kim et al., 2008) for transcriptional induction in response to cell wall stress by increased growth temperature, or treatment with Congo Red, calcofluor white, or Zymolyase. All of these reporters were induced after 15 hours by all treatments, although there was variability among the reporters regarding relative induction by each stress (Figure 2). However, in general, elevated growth temperature was the strongest inducer for all of the reporters. In this regard, the CHA1-lacZ reporter showed the greatest relative increase (approximately 10-fold) at high temperature. The YLR042c-lacZ reporter was induced equivalently by heat stress, Congo Red, and calcofluor white, suggesting this as a good general reporter for cell wall stress.Induction by all cell wall stresses except elevated temperature was entirely dependent upon Mpk1, but independent of Mpk1 catalytic activity, because the catalytically inactive mpk1-K54R mutant behaved essentially like wild-type for all stresses. Induction was only partially dependent on the Mlp1 pseudokinase (Figure 2), a catalytically inactive paralog of Mpk1 (Kim et al., 2008). However, all induction was blocked in an mpk1Δ mlp1Δ double mutant, reflecting the partial overlap in function of Mpk1 and Mlp1 in this context. The greater dependence on Mpk1 over Mlp1 results from the fact that MLP1 is transcriptionally induced by Mpk1 through the Rlm1 factor (Jung and Levin, 1999; Jung et al., 2002). Therefore, in the absence of Mpk1, very little Mlp1 is present (not shown).Transcriptional induction of all of these reporters by cell wall stress was strictly dependent on both SWI4 and SWI6 (Figure 2), consistent with the presumptive use of the SBF-binding sites in these reporters through the non-catalytic mechanism described for FKS2 (Kim et al., 2008). Taken together, we conclude that all of these genes are induced in response to cell wall stress through a common mechanism that involves activation of Mpk1 and Mlp1 to engage Swi4 and Swi6.To assess the kinetics of reporter activation, we conducted a time course for activity in response to temperature stress at 39°C. We had determined previously that FKS2-lacZ reporters were activated slowly, with β-galactosidase activity continuing to increase for at least 15 hours after induction (Kim et al., 2008). This proved to be true for the new reporters as well (Figure 3). In all cases, reporter activity increased over time up to 15 hours of induction. This is in contrast to Rlm1-driven lacZ reporters, also activated by Mpk1, which typically reach a maximum level between two and four hours after thermal induction (Jung et al., 2002).

All of these reporters were induced after 15 hours by all treatments, although there was variability among the reporters regarding relative induction by each stress (Figure 2). However, in general, elevated growth temperature was the strongest inducer for all of the reporters. In this regard, the CHA1-lacZ reporter showed the greatest relative increase (approximately 10-fold) at high temperature. The YLR042c-lacZ reporter was induced equivalently by heat stress, Congo Red, and calcofluor white, suggesting this as a good general reporter for cell wall stress.

Induction by all cell wall stresses except elevated temperature was entirely dependent upon Mpk1, but independent of Mpk1 catalytic activity, because the catalytically inactive mpk1-K54R mutant behaved essentially like wild-type for all stresses. Induction was only partially dependent on the Mlp1 pseudokinase (Figure 2), a catalytically inactive paralog of Mpk1 (Kim et al., 2008). However, all induction was blocked in an mpk1Δ mlp1Δ double mutant, reflecting the partial overlap in function of Mpk1 and Mlp1 in this context. The greater dependence on Mpk1 over Mlp1 results from the fact that MLP1 is transcriptionally induced by Mpk1 through the Rlm1 factor (Jung and Levin, 1999; Jung et al., 2002). Therefore, in the absence of Mpk1, very little Mlp1 is present (not shown).

Transcriptional induction of all of these reporters by cell wall stress was strictly dependent on both SWI4 and SWI6 (Figure 2), consistent with the presumptive use of the SBF-binding sites in these reporters through the non-catalytic mechanism described for FKS2 (Kim et al., 2008). Taken together, we conclude that all of these genes are induced in response to cell wall stress through a common mechanism that involves activation of Mpk1 and Mlp1 to engage Swi4 and Swi6.

To assess the kinetics of reporter activation, we conducted a time course for activity in response to temperature stress at 39°C. We had determined previously that FKS2-lacZ reporters were activated slowly, with β-galactosidase activity continuing to increase for at least 15 hours after induction (Kim et al., 2008). This proved to be true for the new reporters as well (Figure 3). In all cases, reporter activity increased over time up to 15 hours of induction. This is in contrast to Rlm1-driven lacZ reporters, also activated by Mpk1, which typically reach a maximum level between two and four hours after thermal induction (Jung et al., 2002).

We presented three new lacZ reporter plasmids that all displayed identical behavior to that of an FKS2-lacZ reporter with regard to the genetic requirements for activation by cell wall stresses. For example, the CHA1-lacZ reporter displayed the greatest relative induction in response to heat stress. By contrast, the YLR042c-lacZ reporter appears to be the most generally responsive to all cell wall stresses tested.

It is very likely that additional genes regulated by the non-catalytic Mpk1 pathway have gone undetected because of the complexity of their promoters and the consequent overlap of various stress signals.
