Neurotransmission depends on synaptic vesicle (SV) exocytosis driven by soluble
N-ethylmaleimide-sensitive factor attachment protein receptor (SNARE) complex
formation of vesicular synaptobrevin/VAMP2 (Syb2). Exocytic fusion is followed
by endocytic SV membrane retrieval and the high-fidelity reformation of SVs.
Syb2 is the most abundant SV protein with 70 copies per SV, yet, one to three
Syb2 molecules appear to be sufficient for basal exocytosis. Here we demonstrate
that loss of the Syb2-specific endocytic adaptor AP180 causes a moderate
activity-dependent reduction of vesicular Syb2 levels, defects in SV
reformation, and a corresponding impairment of neurotransmission that lead to
excitatory/inhibitory imbalance, epileptic seizures, and premature death.
Further reduction of Syb2 levels in AP180(-/-)/Syb2(+/-) mice results in
perinatal lethality, whereas Syb2(+/-) mice partially phenocopy loss of AP180,
indicating that reduced vesicular Syb2 levels underlie the observed defects in
neurotransmission. Thus, a large vesicular Syb2 pool maintained by AP180 is
crucial to sustain efficient neurotransmission and SV reformation.
