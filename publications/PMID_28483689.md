# The C-terminus of ribosomal protein uS4 contributes to small ribosomal subunit biogenesis and the fidelity of translation.

Ribosomal protein uS4 is an essential ribosomal component involved in multiple 
functions, including mRNA decoding. Structural analyses indicate that during 
decoding, the interface between the C-terminus of uS4 and protein uS5 is 
disrupted and in agreement with this, C-terminal uS4 truncation mutants are 
readily isolated on the basis of their increased miscoding phenotypes. The same 
mutants can also display defects in small subunit assembly and 16S rRNA 
processing and some are temperature sensitive for growth. Starting with one such 
temperature sensitive Escherichia coli uS4 mutant, we have isolated temperature 
insensitive derivatives carrying additional, intragenic mutations that restore 
the C-terminus and ameliorate the ribosomal defects. At least one of these 
suppressors has no detectable ribosome biogenesis phenotype, yet still miscodes, 
suggesting that the C-terminal requirements for ribosome assembly are less rigid 
than for mRNA decoding. In contrast to the uS4 C-terminal mutants that increase 
miscoding, two Salmonella enterica uS4 mutants with altered C-termini have been 
reported as being error-restrictive. Here, reconstruction experiments 
demonstrate that contrary to the previous reports, these mutants have a distinct 
error-prone, increased misreading phenotype, consistent with the behavior of the 
equivalent E. coli mutants and their likely structural effects on uS4-uS5 
interactions.

1. Introduction

Early investigations into the decoding process identified ribosomal proteins uS4, uS5 and uS12 as key players involved in the ribosome’s discrimination between cognate and near-cognate tRNAs [1]. The advent of high resolution structures of ribosomes has provided structural interpretations of decoding and the roles of these proteins. These structural rearrangements on the 30S subunit include the disruption of interactions between ribosomal proteins uS4 and uS5 and the establishment of 16S rRNA-uS12 contacts. In support of this model, at least some of the mutations that influence decoding affect residues on the interface between proteins uS4 and uS5 [3]. In uS4, these alterations include amino acid substitutions as well as truncations that remove C-terminal residues, all of which are predicted to disrupt the uS4-uS5 interface (Fig.1). In addition to its role in decoding, ribosomal protein uS4 helps initiate the assembly of the small subunit by binding directly to 16S rRNA and at least some of the uS4 alterations that promote miscoding also display assembly defects [4]. uS4 is also a translational repressor [5] and is involved in transcription antitermination activities [6]. Consequently, mutations in uS4 can potentially have multiple effects on ribosome regulation, assembly and functionality.

A majority of the C-terminal uS4 mutants display increased miscoding [7]; one interpretation of this phenotype is that the altered ribosomes are predisposed to assume the closed conformation, even with near-cognate tRNAs, since the uS4-uS5 interface is already disrupted in the mutant ribosomes. However, at least some interface-disrupting uS4 mutants have an error-restrictive phenotype, which is difficult to reconcile with the open-closed model [8]. These uS4 mutations, isolated in Salmonella entericacarry a deletion of Val200 (ΔV200) or a truncation at Glu201 (E201*) [9]. Puzzlingly, the same E201* mutation was recently generated in E. coli in our laboratory and was shown to have an error-prone, ribosomal ambiguity (ram) phenotype [7]. In an effort to reconcile these discrepancies and to investigate the roles of the C-terminal region of uS4 in multiple ribosomal functions, we have isolated and characterized intragenic suppressors of an E. coli uS4 C-terminal deletion mutant and have reconstructed the ΔV200 and E201* mutations de novo both in E. coli and in S. enterica. We find that 16S rRNA processing, 30S biogenesis and translational fidelity are all affected by C-terminal truncations and that in contrast to earlier reports, the ΔV200 and E201* alterations in uS4 have a distinct error-prone phenotype in both bacteria. Moreover, in the course of our investigations, we found that two of the well-characterized uS4 C-terminal truncation mutants also carry unanticipated mutations affecting ribosomal protein uS17.

3. Results and Discussion3.1. Intragenic suppressors of uS4 C-terminal truncationsThe wild type uS4 protein is 205 amino acids long (numbering throughout omits the N-terminal methionine, which is removed [17]). The well-studied uS4 mutants D12, D14, and D16 [18–22] as well as the recently isolated FG2 mutant all have C-terminal truncations in uS4 as shown in Tables 1 and S1. D12 has a 5 base-pair deletion (C528 – A532; hereafter designated ‘Δ5’) in the rpsD gene that causes premature termination and generates a 179 amino acid protein. Strains D14 and FG2 have single base insertions of cytosine and thymine, respectively, after G535 (designated +C536 and +T536, respectively). These base insertions lead to premature termination and generate 181 amino acid long proteins. The last four amino acids in these truncated proteins are also altered. In D16, a G to A base change at G509 results in the conversion of TGG (Trp) at codon 169 to a TAG stop codon and generates a 168 amino acid long uS4 protein [21]. All the four C-terminal truncated mutants were reconstructed in MC361 and all displayed the prominent temperature sensitive phenotype previously reported for these mutants.In an effort to determine the contribution of the C-terminus of uS4 to distinct ribosomal functions, we have isolated and characterized temperature-insensitive derivatives of Δ5, a profoundly temperature sensitive uS4 truncation mutant that does not revert back to a wild type rpsD sequence. Streaking of overnight cultures of this truncation mutant (reconstructed in MC361) on LB plates at 42°C yielded multiple temperature-insensitive mutants. Of the 13 colonies analyzed, 9 carried intragenic mutations in the rpsD gene. Among these 9 mutants, 6 unique intragenic suppressors were uncovered (Tables 1 and S1), while 3 of the suppressor mutations were encountered more than once. The 4 remaining strains (Δ5rev6, Δ5rev9, Δ5rev11 and Δ5rev12) retained the original 5 bp deletion present in the starting Δ5 strain, but had no additional changes in rpsD. They thus carry extragenic suppressor mutations that render them temperature insensitive. Sequencing of rpsE, rpsL, prfA and prfB candidate genes encoding proteins uS5, uS12, RF1 and RF2, respectively, did not reveal any alterations and the identity of the extragenic suppressor mutations remains unknown.The relevant DNA and amino acid sequences of the 6 temperature insensitive mutants carrying intragenic changes are shown in Tables S1 and 1. In the original Δ5 mutant, the 5 bp deletion shifts the reading frame, leading to the synthesis of a shortened protein. In the intragenic suppressors, additional deletions of 1, 4 or 7 base pairs have the effect of restoring the original, zero reading frame and permit the synthesis of near-full-length proteins that are 2–4 residues shorter than wild type. In addition, in all the intragenic suppressor mutants, the regions of rpsD spanning the original 5 bp deletion and the sites of the suppressor mutations encode amino acids that differ from wild type uS4 (Table 1). The greatest number of changes is seen in Δ5rev4, which differs at 10 positions, followed by Δ5rev2, which differs at 8 positions. These amino acid substitutions and adjacent deletions all involve a small solvent-exposed loop (Fig.1). The intragenic suppressor mutants show varying degrees of growth recovery at 42°C (Fig. 2). Interestingly, Δ5rev1 and Δ5rev4 which show less robust growth at 42°C than the other suppressor mutants also grow poorly at 20°C.3.2. An altered uS17 protein is present in some uS4 mutant strainsRibosomal protein uS17 has been associated with neamine resistance and the restriction of miscoding in E. coli [23, 24]. In our efforts to identify the extragenic suppressors present in strains Δ5rev6, Δ5rev9, Δ5rev11 and Δ5rev12, the rpsQ gene was sequenced and all of these strains were found to carry a mutated rpsQ gene encoding a uS17 protein with an E62K alteration. Surprisingly, the same rpsQ mutation was present in the starting MC361 Δ5 strain used to isolate all the temperature insensitive suppressors. Moreover, even though we had reconstructed each of the intragenic suppressor mutants in MC361 using P1 transduction, 5 of these (Δ5rev1, Δ5rev2, Δ5rev3, Δ5rev4, and Δ5rev13) also carried the altered rpsQ gene, as did MC361 carrying rpsD (G509A). Only one reconstructed, temperature insensitive strain (Δ5rev5) as well as MC361 rpsD (+C536) and MC361 rpsD (+T536) had wild type rpsQ sequences. The rpsD and rpsQ genes are separated by approximately 7.5 kb and are thus co-transduced at high frequency by P1 phage. The origin of the rpsQ (E62K) mutation is unclear. The rpsD mutants Δ5, +C536 and G509A were isolated in the 1970s in separate experiments and in three independent strains, as suppressors of streptomycin dependence and were transferred by P1 transduction into a wild type strain, 017, at that time [10]. 3.4. A re-examination of the ΔV200 and E201* alterations in uS4 in E. coli and S. enterica

S. enterica and E. coli are closely related organisms and can even engage in intergeneric mating to form hybrids [29]. The finding by Björkman et al. [9] that the E201* truncation of Salmonella uS4 has an error-restrictive phenotype, in contrast to the error-prone phenotype of the same E. coli mutant is, therefore, surprising, given this relatedness and the high degree of conservation of the translation system in general. The amino acid sequences of uS5 proteins are identical in both organisms and the uS4 proteins differ at a single position (Y181 vs. F181). From a structural perspective, the error-prone phenotypes of the E201* and ΔV200 S. enterica uS4 mutants are also unexpected, since this region of the C-terminus of uS4 makes extensive contacts with uS5 and these are likely to be disrupted by the mutations, particularly the E201* truncation. To explore this discrepancy, we have regenerated the E210* and ΔV200 alterations in uS4 in both organisms using recombineering techniques.

The ΔV200 and E201* alterations do not affect rRNA processing or ribosome profiles in either E. coli or S. enterica and with the exception of the S. enterica E201* mutant which grows 1.5-fold slower than wild type, do not affect growth (data not shown). In lacZ readthrough assays, the ΔV200 and E201* alterations promote substantial levels of readthrough of UGA and UAG stop codons in both organisms, indicating that they have a distinct ram phenotype (Fig. 6). In addition, the E. coli wild type strain and both uS4 mutants all displayed identical responses to streptomycin (MIC = 25 mg/l). The wild type S. enterica LT2 strain had an MIC of 50 mg/l while the ΔV200 and E201* uS4 mutants had essentially identical MICs of 25 mg/l (since the MIC method involves two fold dilutions, two-fold differences in MIC are not considered significant). The latter result is in contrast to the results of Björkman et al. [9] who noted that their three error-restrictive S. enterica uS4 mutants (Q53L, ΔV200 and E201*) all had substantially increased streptomycin MICs.

Given the discrepancy between our results and those of Björkman et al. [9], we asked if the original error-restrictive S. enterica strains possibly carried additional mutations affecting ribosomal protein uS12. Sequencing of rpsL genes revealed that strains TH5459 (uS4 Q53L), TH5661 (uS4 ΔV200) and TH5665 (uS4 E210*) all had alterations in uS12, while other uS4 mutant strains from the same experiments (TH5602 (uS4 Q53P), TH5660 (uS4 I199N), TH5662 (uS4 K205N) TH6556 (uS4 K205T)), as well as the reconstructed E210* and delV200 strains generated by us during this work had wild type rpsL genes. The Q53L, ΔV200 and E201* uS4 mutants were originally isolated as suppressors of streptomycin-dependence and tellingly, the rpsL alterations in TH5459, TH5661 and TH5665 corresponded to the uS12 alteration used to select these uS4 mutants originally. Hence TH5459 and TH5661 had a G91D alteration in uS12 and TH5665 had a P90L change. It thus appears that the previously-reported error-restrictive and weak streptomycin resistance phenotypes associated with Q53L, ΔV200 and E201* in uS4 are the result of uS4-uS12 double-mutant combinations and not due to single uS4 alterations.
