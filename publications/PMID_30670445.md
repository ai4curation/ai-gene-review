Author information:
(1)Dana Farber/Boston Children's Cancer and Blood Disorders Center, Harvard
Medical School, Boston, MA.

Myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) typically presents in older adults with the
acquisition of age-related somatic mutations, whereas MDS presenting in children
and younger adults is more frequently associated with germline genetic
predisposition. Germline predisposition is increasingly recognized in MDS
presenting at older ages as well. Although each individual genetic disorder is
rare, as a group, the genetic MDS disorders account for a significant subset of
MDS in children and young adults. Because many patients lack overt syndromic
features, genetic testing plays an important role in the diagnostic evaluation.
This review provides an overview of syndromes associated with genetic
predisposition to MDS, discusses implications for clinical evaluation and
management, and explores scientific insights gleaned from the study of MDS
predisposition syndromes. The effects of germline genetic context on the
selective pressures driving somatic clonal evolution are explored. Elucidation
of the molecular and genetic pathways driving clonal evolution may inform
surveillance and risk stratification, and may lead to the development of novel
therapeutic strategies.
