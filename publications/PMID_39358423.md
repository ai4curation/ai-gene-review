Studies of proteins, found in one of the most stress-resistant animals 
tardigrade Ramazzottius varieornatus, aim to reveal molecular principles of 
extreme tolerance to various types of stress and developing applications based 
on them for medicine, biotechnology, pharmacy, and space research. Tardigrade 
DNA/RNA-binding damage suppressor protein (Dsup) reduces DNA damage caused by 
reactive oxygen spices (ROS) produced upon irradiation and oxidative stresses in 
Dsup-expressing transgenic organisms. This work is focused on the determination 
of structural features of Dsup protein and Dsup-DNA complex, which refines 
details of protective mechanism. For the first time, intrinsically disordered 
nature of Dsup protein with highly flexible structure was experimentally proven 
and characterized by the combination of small angle X-ray scattering (SAXS) 
technique, circular dichroism spectroscopy, and computational methods. Low 
resolution models of Dsup protein and an ensemble of conformations were 
presented. In addition, we have shown that Dsup forms fuzzy complex with DNA.

Introduction

Representatives of the phylum Tardigrada, due to their exceptional resistance to different forms of stress, are considered one of the most extremotolerant animals on Earth1. Studies of the tardigrade Ramazzottius varieornatus made possible the discovery of tardigrade-unique disordered proteins (TDPs), including the unique heat-soluble CAHS (cytosolic-abundant heat soluble), MAHS (mitochondrial-abundant heat soluble), SAHS (secretory-abundant heat soluble) protein families, and Dsup protein, which are associated with extreme stress tolerance2–6. In recent years, mechanisms of stress-protection by TDPs have been in focus of innovative research in genetics, molecular biology, cellular biology, and biophysics3,6–9. These proteins are promising for use in medicine, biotechnology, bioremediation, and space research, opening an intriguing possibility to create stress-resistant organisms, protective agents for radiation and chemotherapy, protein sensors and functional materials, stabilizers of biomolecules or components of pharmaceuticals, vaccines, and transplant material7,8,10–17.

The Damage suppressor protein (Dsup) (UniProt P0DOW4) of R. varieornatus was discovered in 2016 by Hashimoto et al. as a protein capable of protecting DNA from reactive oxygen spices (ROS) actively produced during irradiation and oxidative stress3,9,18, and the only ortholog of Dsup protein, titled Dsup-like (UniProt A0A1W0XB17), was found in tardigrade Hypsibius exemplaris9. Dsup-mediated DNA protection was observed in a number of non-tardigrade Dsup-expressing organisms: human cells HEK293T, plant Nicotiana tabacum, and animal Drosophila melanogaster3,17,19. Dsup protein consists of 445 amino residues (42.8 kDa), has hydrophilic properties, total positive charge, and, according to the sequence-based predictions, is a highly unstructured protein20. The protein contains the nuclear localization signal, binds to DNA without specificity to nucleotide sequence, and has a higher binding affinity to nucleosomes than to free DNA3,9. Furthermore, RNA-binding ability was demonstrated for Dsup protein17. C-terminal region of Dsup protein is essential for an effective binding to DNA and, in addition, a conserved region common to the HMGN nucleosome binding proteins and the Dsup protein was found9,21. In silico modeling predicts a tight electrostatic binding between Dsup and DNA and formation of an Dsup-DNA complex with intermolecular distance ~ 4 Å20, which putatively leads to a decrease in the accessibility of DNA to ROS and prevents subsequent DNA damage. The flexibility of IDPs leads to a variety of DNA binding patterns that range from rigid binding with partial folding of protein to the formation of highly dynamic fuzzy complexes with preserved high levels of disorder in the protein structure22–24. By binding to DNA, IDPs can regulate genome architecture, gene expression, and participate in molecular signaling2,25–29, thus the structural investigation of Dsup protein and Dsup-DNA complex might clarify the specific role of this protein in all of these processes.

Since IDPs exist in multiple conformations, this limits the study of the structure of such proteins and requires the use of special methods adopted for the study of ensembles of highly unstructured dynamic molecules, such as small-angle scattering techniques (X-ray and neutron), circular dichroism spectroscopy (CD), and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)30–32.

In this work, we performed for the first time structural analysis of Dsup protein, using small angle X-ray scattering (SAXS), circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy, and a series of computational approaches. We define an intrinsically disordered nature of Dsup protein in solution, its size, and shape, analyzed the effects of various buffers on the protein, describe several structural characteristics of the Dsup-DNA complex, as well as generate low resolution models and ensemble of conformations of this protein.

The study of unique disordered proteins of tardigrades provides not only fundamental data on the functions and mechanisms of action of these proteins, but also information on new principles of extreme stress tolerance with the participation of IDPs. In this work, for the first time we experimentally investigated intrinsically disordered nature of tardigrade-unique Dsup protein, its spatial characteristics and described several parameters of Dsup-DNA complex.

First of all we performed sequence-based in silico analysis of Dsup protein using a set of commonly used disorder predictors (PONDR®, CIDER, IUPRED)33,37,53, dealing with protein charge, hydrophobicity, and amino acid residues distributions (Fig. 2). The Dsup protein was predicted to contain 79–100% disordered regions, indicating that Dsup is a highly disordered protein, and the Uversky plot places the Dsup protein in the pool of intrinsically disordered proteins too. Prediction algorithms localize ordered regions in the middle part of the Dsup protein (amino acid residues 142–209), which is relatively hydrophobic, depleted in disorder promoting residues, and lacking potential phosphorylation sites. Oppositely, N- and C-terminal regions of Dsup protein are hydrophilic, positively charged and predicted to be mainly disordered and actively involved in phosphorylation44.

The analysis of obtained experimental SAXS and CD spectroscopy data confirmed that Dsup is an intrinsically disordered protein30,31 with at least 62–66% of disordered regions in PBS and H2O. In addition, CD spectroscopy data indicate the presence of 5–7% of α-helixes and 30–31% of β-sheets in Dsup. However, the question of the content of β-strands in the Dsup protein remains open; based on our experiments, we cannot say whether the algorithms used in this work to interpret the CD data misestimate the presence of β-sheets49 or whether we are dealing with formation of β-sheets in Dsup in solution for instance during phase separation processes or intermolecular interactions54–56. The addition of urea to Dsup protein solutions caused denaturation of the protein and resulted in the formation of an expanded molecule (Fig. 8). In contrast, in the presence of the helix-inducing solvent trifluoroethanol (TFE), additional α-helical regions were formed in the Dsup protein (Fig. S3). It’s interesting that several other tardigrade disodered proteins (LEA, SAHS, CAHS and EtAHS proteins) undergo similar conformational transitions from a predominantly unstructured state to an α-helical state in water-deficient conditions, mimicking the process of anhydrobiosis in tardigrades4,57.

Based on the analysis of SAXS data, we obtained information about the overall size and shape of Dsup protein: i) in PBS, H2O, and with the addition of a denaturing agent (2.3 M urea), the Dsup protein has an elongated rod-shaped form ii) Dsup protein is most likely present in solution as individual molecules iii) the resulting low resolution dummy atom model has linear molecular dimensions of 200 × 80 Å for Dsup in PBS and 270 × 80 Å for Dsup in H2O. The Rg values for Dsup calculated from SAXS data by Fourier transform (Rg ~ 60 Å) and Gaussian coil (Rg ~ 53 Å) (Table 1) are close to results obtained from previous Dsup all-atom molecular dynamic simulation (100 ns) estimated Rg in the range of 55–70 Å20. In addition, intrinsically disordered Dsup in solution undergoes multiple conformations and the protein characteristics can be described as an ensemble of conformations. Ensemble optimization method (EOM)58 allows to select the best models fitting the experimental SAXS curve from a pool of 10,000 random models. The ensemble of seven Dsup conformations obtained using the EOM approach has Rg values in the range of 45–130 Å (Fig. 7) and the high molecular flexibility. Such high flexibility of Dsup molecules may indicate the possibility of weak but frequent intra- and inter-molecular interactions. Interestingly, that Rg distribution generated by EOM for Dsup is bimodal with peaks at ~ 50 and ~ 100 Å. In some cases, such bimodality of the EOM obtained Rg distribution for IDPs reflects conformational heterogeneity of protein arising due to long-range protein self-interactions contribute to the formation of the collapsed and extended conformations56. In other cases, compacted and extended conformers of IDPs were found under macromolecular crowding conditions or at a certain concentration of the IDP itself in solution55,59.

Dsup is DNA and RNA binding protein3,9,17 and it is important to study the characteristics of inner protein’s structure during Dsup-DNA interactions. The results obtained with CD spectroscopy for an experiment using mixture of Dsup (0.2 mg/ml) and plasmid DNA (0.11 mg/ml) (the molar ratio ~ 64:1) showed that in the Dsup-DNA complex there are no transitions from disorder to order and formation of well-defined structures in Dsup protein (Fig. 8b), therefore the Dsup-DNA complex formed at a given ratio of protein and DNA can be classified as a fuzzy complex. Fuzziness in general implies interchangeable interactions sites in interacting molecules60 and formation of fuzzy complexes with conformational heterogeneity is an important feature of many DNA-binding proteins containing intrinsically disordered regions, determining many of their functions including regulation of chromatin compaction, transcription, signaling, reparation etc22–24,43,61–63. The formation of fuzzy complexes between proteins containing intrinsically disordered regions and DNA has been observed in many other studies (Table S3) and the binding affinity in such complexes may be regulated by post-translational modifications of protein and possible subsequent secondary structure transitions22,64,65. We analyzed the distribution of putative phosphorylation-prone sites in Dsup protein and observed that they are densely located within putative intrinsically disordered regions and depleted in the central region containing putative helical structures. It is possible that phosphorylation of the Dsup protein also allows to regulate the binding affinity in Dsup-DNA complex44,65.

The exact functions of the unique Dsup protein in tardigrade cells are still uncertain and its potential protein partners are unknown. The lack of appearance of an additional defined three-dimensional Dsup structure in the Dsup-DNA fuzzy complex makes it possible to assume that Dsup protein does not acquire new functions when interacting with DNA and dye to Dsup conformational heterogeneity it probably retains the ability to interact with other targets. For example, it may interact with several parts of DNA or several DNA molecules simultaneously, bringing some areas of DNA closer together or stabilizing DNA fibers such as linker histone H1, which contains a long C-terminal disordered domain66. In addition, as we showed in in silico analysis, Dsup is highly likely to participate in spontaneous liquid–liquid phase separation and has two regions that promote droplet formation, which may also indicate a possible involvement of Dsup in chromatin organization.

As we mentioned earlier, Dsup can protect DNA from various DNA-damaging agents. Interestingly, some other DNA-binding IDPs (Table S3) have DNA protective properties too. For instance, histone H1 protein has highly disordered structure, binds to DNA without sequence specificity, forms fuzzy complexes with DNA and reduces in dose dependent manner DNA damage under exposure to oxidative stress or irradiation67–70. From this point of view, other representatives of DNA-binding IDPs can also be considered as potential targets for the development of DNA protection agents for biotechnology purposes.

In summary, this work is the first experimental validation of the intrinsically disordered properties of the unique ROS-protective tardigrade Dsup protein, and based on SAXS and CD spectroscopy data, we described a number of its molecular characteristics, including the formation of a fuzzy complex between Dsup and DNA. We believe that further study of the molecular characteristics of the Dsup-DNA complex may reveal detailed mechanism of DNA shielding by Dsup and other DNA-binding IDPs68,71–73.
