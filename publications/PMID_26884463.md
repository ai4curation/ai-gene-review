Abstract

Hsp90 is the ATP-consuming core component of a very abundant molecular chaperone machine that handles a substantial portion of the cytosolic proteome. Rather than one machine, it is in fact an ensemble of molecular machines, since most mammalian cells express two cytosolic isoforms of Hsp90 and a subset of up to 40 to 50 cochaperones and regulate their interactions and functions by a variety of posttranslational modifications. We demonstrate that the Hsp90 ensemble is fundamentally remodeled during muscle differentiation and that this remodeling is not just a consequence of muscle differentiation but possibly one of the drivers to accompany and to match the vast proteomic changes associated with this process. As myoblasts differentiate into myotubes, Hsp90α disappears and only Hsp90β remains, which is the only isoform capable of interacting with the novel muscle-specific Hsp90 cochaperone Aarsd1L. Artificially maintaining Hsp90α or knocking down Aarsd1L expression interferes with the differentiation of C2C12 myotubes. During muscle differentiation, Aarsd1L replaces the more ubiquitous cochaperone p23 and in doing so dampens the activity of the glucocorticoid receptor, one of the Hsp90 clients relevant to muscle functions. This cochaperone switch protects muscle cells against the inhibitory effects of glucocorticoids and may contribute to preventing muscle wasting induced by excess glucocorticoids.

INTRODUCTION

Hsp90 is a highly abundant molecular chaperone, including in nonstressed cells, which is required for the maturation, stability, activity, and/or degradation of a substantial portion of the proteome ( 1 – 5 ) (for a regularly updated list of Hsp90 interactors, see http://www.picard.ch/downloads/Hsp90interactors.pdf ). To its clients, the Hsp90 dimer offers a moving platform whose complex choreography involves ATP hydrolysis, huge conformational changes, and dynamic interactions with a large cohort of cochaperones ( 3 , 6 – 11 ). The cochaperones regulate and are part of the Hsp90 cycle ( 3 , 8 – 11 ), and some may act as client specificity factors ( 12 , 13 ). With 40 to 50 cochaperones, far more have been reported (see http://www.picard.ch/downloads/Hsp90facts.pdf ) than the ones that have been extensively characterized as interacting with Hsp90 as part of the core cycle. Moreover, Hsp90 and cochaperones are extensively regulated by posttranslational modifications (see, for example, references 14 – 17 ), and Hsp90 is present in excess over any individual cochaperone. Hence, the Hsp90 molecular chaperone machine constitutes in fact an ensemble of complexes. These complexes dynamically interchange and are fashioned by the cell specificity of signaling and cochaperone expression patterns.

One of the most extensively characterized components of the Hsp90 core machinery is the cochaperone p23 ( 18 – 20 ), encoded by the PTGES3 gene in mammals. This small acidic protein binds and stabilizes the ATP-bound state of Hsp90 ( 19 – 23 ). p23 binding, which is disrupted by Hsp90 inhibitors competing with ATP and by hyperacetylation of Hsp90, promotes a high-affinity binding state of Hsp90 for client proteins ( 24 , 25 ). p23 binds Hsp90 with its N-terminal CS domain, whose fold, consisting of an antiparallel β-sandwich with 7 strands, it shares with other cysteine and histidine-rich domain ( C HORD)-containing proteins and S gt1 ( 21 , 26 , 27 ).

One of these CS-containing p23 relatives is Aarsd1 ( Fig. 1A ), which is 44% identical to p23 within its CS domain. It was originally described as a 25-kDa protein and named tsp23, for transcript similar to p23; like p23, it was demonstrated to coimmunoprecipitate with the Hsp90 client mineralocorticoid and glucocorticoid receptors (GRs) and to stimulate nuclear receptors in mammalian cells and yeast differentially compared to p23 ( 28 ). Intriguingly, tsp23 was shown to be expressed primarily in heart and skeletal tissues, two tissues where the otherwise ubiquitous p23 protein is found at low levels or is absent. However, more recent genome annotations have revealed that the Aarsd1 gene is far more complex ( Fig. 1 ). The Aarsd1 gene contains a large number of exons and is apparently transcribed into a multitude of alternatively spliced and tissue-specifically expressed mRNAs, of which tsp23 represents only one. Because the 3′ exons encode a protein with homology to the editing domain of tRNA synthetases, the gene gained a new name: alanyl-tRNA synthetase domain-containing 1 ( Aarsd1 ). Nawaz and colleagues confirmed this complex expression pattern for the mouse Aarsd1 gene and discovered that the ubiquitously expressed Ala-tRNA-editing domain (AlaXp) of Aarsd1 actually edits mischarged Ala-tRNAs but that the N-terminal p23-like p23 H domain, present in some splice isoforms, inhibits this editing activity both in cis and in trans ( 29 ). Based on these findings, it seemed possible that this unusual p23 relative, or set of relatives, replaces p23 in muscle and contributes to the fundamental remodeling of the Hsp90 ensemble, conceivably to adapt it to muscle-specific functions. Since Aarsd1 still had not been directly connected to Hsp90 as a client or cochaperone, we decided to investigate this connection and the role of Aarsd1 in muscle differentiation.

DISCUSSION

Compared to other tissues, differentiated striated muscle expresses a considerably different complement of molecular chaperones ( 28 , 34 , 48 – 50 ), presumably to meet its highly specific needs during the final stages of differentiation and for function and tissue homeostasis. For the Hsp90 ensemble, the remodeling is substantial. While this was already apparent from comparing muscle to other tissues, our analysis with the C2C12 cell model provides an insight into the dynamic remodeling of the ensemble during the differentiation process. The changes include the disappearance of one of the two cytosolic Hsp90 isoforms and p23 and the appearance of new Hsp90 cochaperones. Our knockdown and overexpression experiments provide evidence that the switch to Hsp90β and the induction of Aarsd1L are a requirement for myotube differentiation to proceed normally. Thus, the remodeling of the Hsp90 ensemble accompanies and drives muscle differentiation ( Fig. 10 ).

We propose that Aarsd1L is a novel Hsp90 cochaperone. Despite previously reported structural similarities with the cochaperone p23, that is, the presence of a CS domain, and functional effects on steroid receptors ( 28 ), the complexity of Aarsd1 isoforms had not been appreciated in this context, and it was unclear whether Aarsd1 acted through Hsp90. Our results show that Aarsd1L binds Hsp90 through the CS domain, most likely by using a conserved interface, and therefore can compete with p23 for binding. As judged by the effects of Aarsd1L overexpression on the activity of the Hsp90 client GR, Aarsd1L inhibits Hsp90 activity. Instead of simply being a stand-in for p23 in the muscle, at least functionally, Aarsd1L is distinct from p23. Whether Aarsd1L inhibits the Hsp90 ATPase as does p23 ( 19 , 20 ) and how exactly it acts in the Hsp90 cycle remain to be worked out with biochemical analyses. What is clear from our analysis of the Hsp90 client GR is that repression by Aarsd1L requires the large AlaXp domain. Since the tRNA-editing activity itself is dispensable, the AlaXp domain may simply have a steric effect on Hsp90 activities. Whatever the molecular mechanism of repression of GR by Aarsd1L is, we hypothesize that Aarsd1L acts as a break for all or some Hsp90 functions in muscle. For GR, this is apparent not only in reporter gene assays but also by examining the effects on the expression of the GR target gene Klf15 . Therefore, the dampening of GR activity by Aarsd1L translates into a muscle-protective effect considering the known deleterious effects of excessive glucocorticoids on muscle integrity ( 51 , 52 ) and the key role played by Klf15 in promoting the expression of proteins involved in muscle wasting ( 46 ). Switching from p23 to Aarsd1L during muscle differentiation may prevent GR-induced muscle wasting at physiological concentrations of glucocorticoids ( Fig. 10 ).

The remodeling of the Hsp90 ensemble must play important roles beyond controlling GR function. Indeed, the activation of GR only slightly impairs the differentiation of C2C12 cells. In contrast, both knocking down Aarsd1L and artificially maintaining the expression of Hsp90α dramatically interfere with myotube differentiation. While we provide a rationale for why muscle switches from p23 to Aarsd1L, we do not know why muscle dispenses with Hsp90α and retains only Hsp90β or how Hsp90α interferes with muscle differentiation. Too little is known about Hsp90 isoform specificity (for exceptions, see http://www.picard.ch/downloads/Hsp90facts.pdf ) to begin to speculate about this. Hsp90α and Hsp90β are 85% identical and, provided that they are present at equivalent concentrations, are generally thought to fulfill the same functions.

Intriguingly, Aarsd1L is uniquely able to interact with the sole Hsp90 isoform remaining in differentiated muscle. Future work will have to address what the determinants are for the Hsp90β specificity of Aarsd1L. Considering that the CS domain of Aarsd1L is about 50% identical to that of p23, isoform specificity may map either to divergent residues or outside the CS domain within the C-terminal AlaXp domain. Be that as it may, this exquisite Hsp90β specificity is highly reminiscent of that of another cochaperone. Unc45a (also known as GC-UNC45) has been shown to bind Hsp90β preferentially ( 53 , 54 ). Strangely enough, while Unc45a is ubiquitously expressed and largely persists in differentiating C2C12 cells ( 48 ), the muscle-specific isoform Unc45b ( 48 ) appears to bind Hsp90 without isoform specificity ( 13 ). Nevertheless, it is noteworthy that Unc45 was linked to muscle differentiation and myosin assembly in the nematode Caenorhabditis elegans ( 55 , 56 ), an organism that has p23 but no Aarsd1 ortholog. Moreover, in an Unc45 mutant of the same organism, Hsp90 and other cochaperones are required for muscle integrity and motility ( 57 ).

The C2C12 system will provide a powerful system in which to study isoform selectivity and the requirements of particular Hsp90 clients for the muscle-specifically remodeled Hsp90 ensemble. Our proteomic analysis suggests a number of relevant Hsp90 clients other than GR. It will be very interesting to investigate the muscle-specific factors that are affected by Hsp90 inhibition and to determine to what extent they are dependent on the remodeling of the Hsp90 ensemble, i.e., the presence of muscle-specific cochaperones such as Aarsd1L and Unc45, and whether they display a preferential dependence on Hsp90β over Hsp90α. The proteostasis requirements of muscle during both differentiation and physiological functioning may be unique and may have led to the requirement for a very specific Hsp90 ensemble, but one cannot exclude that other tissues or cell types may select yet other sets of Hsp90 molecular chaperone machines.
