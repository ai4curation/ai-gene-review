Amyloid beta-peptide (Abeta) deposition in cerebral vessels contributes to 
cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA) in Alzheimer's disease (AD). Here, we report 
that in AD patients and two mouse models of AD, overexpression of serum response 
factor (SRF) and myocardin (MYOCD) in cerebral vascular smooth muscle cells 
(VSMCs) generates an Abeta non-clearing VSMC phenotype through transactivation 
of sterol regulatory element binding protein-2, which downregulates low density 
lipoprotein receptor-related protein-1, a key Abeta clearance receptor. Hypoxia 
stimulated SRF/MYOCD expression in human cerebral VSMCs and in animal models of 
AD. We suggest that SRF and MYOCD function as a transcriptional switch, 
controlling Abeta cerebrovascular clearance and progression of AD.

## Full Text

Dementia in Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is associated with neurovascular dysfunction1,2 and accumulation of neurotoxic amyloid β-peptide (Aβ) in the wall of cerebral blood vessels and in the brain1–4. Aβ deposition in VSMCs of small cerebral arteries contributes to cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA)1,2,5, the prevalence of which is greater than 80% in AD individuals and 10–40% in the elderly population without AD5,6. CAA is associated with cognitive impairment5. Microbleeds in the brain occur in 80% of CAA cases and in 30% of AD patients with CAA7, whereas lobar cerebral haemorrhage occurs in 7–18% of AD cases5.

Recent work has suggested that Aβ accumulates in the wall of cerebral vessels and in brains of AD individuals because of its impaired clearance from the brain and/or imbalances between its production and clearance2,3,8–10. In mouse models of familial AD expressing the Swedish mutation in the Aβ-precursor protein (APPsw±), or harbouring the Dutch and Iowa vasculotropic APP mutations, the dense plaques may develop initially on blood vessels or as classical CAA11–13 because of inefficient Aβ clearance across the blood-brain barrier (BBB) and/or perivascular Virchow-Robin arterial spaces, respectively1–3. The low-density lipoprotein receptor-related protein 1 (LRP) is a major Aβ clearance receptor at the BBB12,14–17 and in cerebral VSMCs18.

The molecular basis of CAA is unclear. Recently, we reported that serum response factor (SRF)19 and myocardin (MYOCD)20,21, two interacting transcription factors that control VSMC differentiation, are overexpressed in AD and produce a hypercontractile VSMC phenotype in small cerebral arteries through directed expression of SRF–MYOCD-regulated contractile genes22. Here, we show that SRF and MYOCD control Aβ clearance from cerebral VSMCs in AD, as well as the degree of CAA and focal Aβ brain accumulations in animal models of CAA and AD. We demonstrate that SRF and MYOCD overexpression in cerebral VSMCs in AD and in mice in vivo generates an Aβ non-clearing VSMC phenotype through LRP downregulation, which is mediated by the SRF–MYOCD-directed transactivation of the sterol regulatory element binding protein-2 (SREBP2)23, an LRP transcriptional repressor24,25.

RESULTSSRF and MYOCD block Aβ clearance in cerebral VSMCsSRF and MYOCD are overexpressed in cerebral VSMCs in AD patients with CAA22. Western blot analysis of small cerebral leptomeningeal arterial blood vessel homogenates from AD individuals with CAA showed elevated levels of SRF and MYOCD, Aβ accumulation and significantly lower levels of LRP, compared with age-matched, non-demented controls without CAA (Supplementary Information, Fig. S1a–c). As Aβ does not influence SRF and MYOCD expression in VSMCs22, we hypothesized that if the association between Aβ accumulation in the vessel wall and SRF/MYOCD overexpression in cerebral VSMCs in AD was not due to chance, then overexpression of SRF and MYOCD may influence clearance of Aβ from VSMCs. To test this hypothesis, we compared clearance of Cy3-labelled Aβ aggregates by cultured cerebral VSMCs isolated from small intracerebral and leptomeningeal arteries from late-stage AD individuals with CAA and age-matched controls without CAA, using a model similar to that reported for Aβ clearance by cultured astrocytes26. In all studies, VSMCs were early first-passage cells. (see Methods and Supplementary Information, Table S1 for clinical and neuropathological characteristics of AD individuals and controls). Compared with control VSMCs, AD VSMC showed a decrease in Aβ clearance of more than 70% (Fig. 1a, e), and expressed consistently higher levels of SRF and MYOCD (Fig. 1d), as reported previously22, and significantly lower levels of LRP (Fig. 2b). Reduced MYOCD levels are consistent with earlier reports showing that silencing SRF in VSMCs results in downregulation of MYOCD22 and that its expression is sharply reduced in Srf-null mice27–29. Hypoxia increases MYOCD expressionNeither oxidant stress nor Aβ accumulation are upstream of SRF and MYOCD22. Low levels of the mesenchyme homeobox gene-2 (MEOX-2) decrease LRP in brain endothelium of AD individuals31, but MEOX-2 does not regulate SRF/MYOCD in cerebral VSMCs22.
