Interleukin-10 (IL-10), a pivotal anti-inflammatory cytokine, has gotten attention for its involvement in tissue remodeling and organ fibrosis. Pleurisy and subsequent pleural remodeling are recognized as quantifiable indicators of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) activity. However, the role of IL-10 in SLE-associated pleural remodeling remains unknown. In this study, we investigated role of IL-10 in SLE-associated pleural remodeling and the underlying mechanism.

Clinical data and serum specimens were obtained from SLE patients, while pleural mesothelial cells and mouse models served as primary experimental subjects. The protein expression-related technologies, histopathological staining, and other experimental methods were used in the study.

Our investigation got several key findings. Firstly, serum obtained from SLE patients with pleural thickening was found to induce pleural mesothelial cell remodeling. Subsequently, heightened levels of IL-10 were found in serum from SLE patients with pleural thickening compared to that of SLE patients without pleural thickening. Secondly, administration of recombinant IL-10 was confirmed its ability to induce pleural mesothelial cell remodeling, on the contrary, this remodeling was effectively mitigated by IL-10 inhibition. Notably, blockade of IL-10 significantly prevented collagen deposition and prevented thickening in pleura of SLE mouse models. Lastly, the IL-10/JAK2/STAT3/HIF1α/TMEM45A/P4HA1 signaling axis was elucidated to mediate pleural remodeling and thickening.

Our study uncovered that IL-10 mediated pleural remodeling in SLE. We suggested that serum IL-10 level exceeding 6.32 pg/mL was a potential reference threshold for predicting pleural thickening in SLE patients.

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is a chronic autoimmune disorder characterized by a spectrum of clinical manifestations and immunological aberrations [1]. Pleurisy, recognized as a quantifiable indicator of lupus activity [2], afflicts approximately 40–60% of SLE patients [3]. Notably, pleurisy has been integrated into specific classification criteria described by the American College of Rheumatology/European League Against Rheumatism in 2019 [4,5], underscoring its pivotal significance in the context of SLE. Traditionally, diagnosing pleural involvement in SLE has relied on clinical manifestation. Owing to unclear mechanism and less of clinical trial data specific to pleurisy in SLE, therapeutic interventions for pleurisy in SLE were referenced to methods in treatment of other SLE-affected organ damage or experiences in managing pulmonary manifestations for other autoimmune conditions.

As a protective barrier, producing a series of pathophysiological reactions in face of various pathogenic factors, pleura is involved in maintaining homeostasis and has immune functions [6]. Pleural mesothelial cells (PMCs) discern aberrant molecular signal, subsequently trigger a series of responses, such as changes in pleural permeability and consequent pleural effusion. The normal pleural structure damaged in pleurisy which induced wound repair, dys-regulation wound repair led to pleural remodeling which mainly exhibited extracellular matrix (ECM) over-deposition and pleural thickening. Severe pleural thickening resulted in compromised lung function, typified by restrictive ventilation dysfunction. However, the detailed mechanism of pleural remodeling in SLE was still unclear.

Interleukin-10 (IL-10), an indispensable anti-inflammatory cytokine in human immunity, exerts its effects by modulating innate immune responses and antigen-specific immunity [7]. Recent studies illuminated the role of IL-10 in tissue remodeling and organ fibrosis. In this study, heightened levels of IL-10 were found in serum of SLE patients with pleural thickening compared to that of SLE patients without pleural thickening. Consequently, PMCs and mouse models served as primary experimental subjects, and the mechanism of pleural remodeling in patients with SLE was explored focused on IL-10.

Serum obtained from SLE patients was divided into two cohorts based on absence or presence of pleural thickening, namely SLE-control and SLE-pleural thickening (SLE-PT) respectively, was employed as experimental stimuli for PMCs. Changes of remodeling-related protein collagen-I and α-smooth muscle actin (α-SMA) in PMCs were detected by western blotting. As shown in Fig.1A and B, the expressions of collagen-I and α-SMA were up-regulated in primary rat PMCs treated with SLE-PT serum in comparison to those treated with SLE-control serum. Moreover, SLE-PT serum promoted PMC proliferation (Fig.1C and D) and migration (Fig.1E and F). Similar results were also found in human PMCs (Fig.S1). Collectively, these findings revealed that serum from SLE-PT patients induced PMC remodeling, and indicated that serum from SLE patients was needed to further investigate in SLE-associated pleural remodeling.

To understand whether IL-10 mediates pleural thickening, recombinant human and rat IL-10 was respectively used to treat cultured human and rat PMCs. Expressions of remodeling-related protein collagen-I, fibronectin, α-SMA and vimentin were detected. The recombinant human IL-10 dose-dependently increased proteins of collagen-I, fibronectin, α-SMA and vimentin in cultured human PMCs (Fig.3A). Furthermore, 100 ng/mL recombinant human IL-10 increased mRNA levels and protein levels of collagen-I, fibronectin, snail1, α-SMA, vimentin and E-Cadherin (Fig.3B-D). Immunofluorescence staining also revealed that collagen-I and α-SMA proteins increased in IL-10-treated human PMCs (Fig.3E). Recombinant human IL-10 promoted human PMC migration (Fig.3F and G). Same results were also found in rat IL-10-treated primary rat PMCs (Fig.S3A-G). These results indicated that IL-10 which increased in SLE-PT serum should be involved in PMC remodeling and migration.

To further confirm the role of IL-10 in PMC remodeling, small molecule inhibitor (AS101) and neutralizing antibody of IL-10 (anti-IL-10) was used to inhibit serum IL-10. As shown in Fig.3H-K,​ SLE-PT serum induced collagen-I and α-SMA synthesis in primary rat PMCs, but these were prevented by AS101 (Fig.3H and I) and anti-IL-10 (Fig.3J and K). Similar results were also found in immunofluorescence staining (Fig.S3H). Moreover, as shown in Fig.S4, remodeling in human PMCs were also prevented by inhibition of IL-10. These data confirmed that IL-10 mediated PMC remodeling.

The pristane-induced SLE model was a classical model of SLE. Here eight-week-old BALB/c female mice were intra-peritoneally administered pristane to induce SLE model, while intra-peritoneal injection of saline served as the control. In the models, pleural changes were evaluated at first. As shown in Fig.S5, Masson’s trichrome staining revealed significant pleural thickening and collagen deposition in visceral pleura, parietal pleura, and diaphragmatic pleura. Macrophages were considered as the main source of IL-10. In vitro, macrophages were treated by serum from SLE-PT patients. IL-10 secreted from macrophages exactly increased (Fig.S6). Thus, macrophages were detected by immunofluorescence staining of F4/80. As shown in Fig.S7, macrophages obviously aggregated around thickening pleura in pristane-induced models compared to control mice. These data indicated pleural fibrosis in pristane-induced SLE model was established.

Subsequently, IL-10 inhibition experiments were performed. The anti-IL-10 and IL-10 receptor blocker was used as IL-10 inhibition. Serum was collected at days 60 to perform analysis of the representative SLE self-antibodies including ANA, anti-dsDNA and anti-Smith antibody by ELISA. As shown in Fig.4A-C, levels of ANA, anti-dsDNA and anti-Smith antibodies significantly increased in pristane-induced mice. Usage of anti-IL-10 or IL-10 receptor blocker did not change levels of ANA, anti-dsDNA or anti-Smith antibodies in mice. IL-10 levels increased in pristane-induced mice, the anti-IL-10 or IL-10 receptor blocker did not change IL-10 levels (Fig.4D). Survival analysis revealed a 50% survival rate among mice treated solely with pristane, whereas treatment with anti-IL-10 or IL-10 receptor blocker significantly improved the survival rate (Fig.S8). Subsequently, investigation into pleural thickening and fibrosis was undertaken in these models. As presented in Fig.4E-H, and Fig.S9, pristane-induced SLE models exhibited notable thickening and fibrosis of the mouse visceral, parietal, and diaphragmatic pleura. Intra-pleural administration of anti-IL-10 or IL-10 receptor blocker markedly attenuated pleural collagen depositions and thickness. These results revealed that IL-10 mediated PMC remodeling and pleural fibrosis in vivo.

The role and mechanism of IL-10 in pleural remodeling was studied above, which indicated serum IL-10 level should be an index for predicting pleural thickening in SLE patients. Then, clinical data of SLE patients were co-analyzed with serum IL-10 levels. Univariate analysis was performed including age, gender, SLEDAI, activity classification, immune cells (CD 3, CD 4, CD 8, CD 19, and NK), cytokines (IL-2, IL-4, IL-6, IL-10, TNF-α, and IFN-γ), CRP and ESR. Results showed that there was a difference in the age, SLEDAI, CD 19, IL-6, IL-10, and ESR between SLE-control group and SLE-PT group (TableS3). Binary Logistic regression analysis diaplayed that the age, CD 19, IL-10 and ESR were risk factors for pleural thickening in SLE, IL-10 manifesting the strongest correlation (OR = 1.318) (TableS4). Furthermore, to exclude influence of confounding factors the age, CD 19 and ESR, propensity score matching method was used in analysis. A total of 107 pairs of matched SLE-control and SLE-PT patients were identified, the correlation between IL-10 and SLE-PT was analyzed again. The result indicated that IL-10 was a risk factor for SLE-PT (P= 0.000029). Moreover, utilizing receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis, a numerical inflection point (Yoden index = 0.281) was simulated for the diagnosis and identification of SLE-PT based on IL-10 level, yielding a threshold value of 6.315 pg/mL. Considering the serum level of IL-10 tested in patients was accurate to two decimal points, 6.32 pg/mL was selected as the numerical inflection point of IL-10. Collectively, these results implied that serum IL-10 level exceeding 6.32 pg/mL was a potential reference threshold for predicting pleural thickening in SLE patients, with a sensitivity of 34.6% and specificity of 93.5% (Fig.8).

As a pivotal anti-inflammatory cytokine, IL-10 exerts its effects by selectively inhibiting expression of pro-inflammatory genes encoding cytokines and chemokines, thereby acting as a modulator throughout the inflammatory cascade [8,9,10]. Appropriate level of IL-10 has anti-inflammatory function to protect cell and tissue out of damage. However, inappropriate or excessive production of IL-10 has been implicated in impairing host defense, leading to persistent disorder [11]. Despite its recognized role in resolving inflammatory processes, the involvement of IL-10 in pleural remodeling and fibrosis remained poorly understood. In the context of pulmonary fibrosis, conflicting reports existed regarding effect of IL-10. Some studies showed that IL-10 delivery attenuated lung fibrosis in mouse models [12]. Some demonstrated its potential to exacerbate fibrosis. In a mouse model of long-term overexpression of IL-10, researchers observed its obvious induction of lung fibrosis [13]. Barbarin V et al. found increased IL-10 significantly contributed to silica-induced lung fibrosis by exacerbating the Th2 response [14]. Besides, IL-10 was considered to induce macrophage profibrotic function after lung injury [15]. IL-10 was also implicated in promoting collagen synthesis in pulmonary fibroblasts derived from interstitial pneumonia tissues [16]. Building upon these observations, we thought that increasing IL-10 at the inflammatory stage inhibited injury, which was beneficial for tissue repair. However, persistent elevation of IL-10 levels during the tissue repair stage induced fibrosis. In the current study, our experimental data revealed that IL-10 mediated PMC remodeling in vitro and induced pleural thickening and fibrosis in SLE models in vivo. Our findings provided evidences that IL-10 mediated pleural remodeling and fibrosis. IL-10 inhibition did not affect the levels of ANA, anti-dsDNA, and anti-Smith antibodies in mouse SLE models. We thought the reason was that IL-10 increased after inflammation in the later phage of the model. The ANA, anti-dsDNA and anti-Smith antibodies were induced by pristane rather than IL-10.

Occurrence of pleural fibrosis results from pleural ECM over-deposition. Collagen-I is the main component of ECM. The synthesis of collagen-I involved mRNA translation and subsequent post-translational modification (proline hydroxylating into hydroxyproline) via P4HA1 [17,18]. The current study uncovered the involvement of TMEM45A in collagen-I synthesis, with TMEM45A facilitating pleural fibrosis by up-regulating P4HA1 expression. As our knowledge, there was no literature which reported the relationship between IL-10 and TMEM45A/P4HA1. Thus, we explored mechanisms of IL-10 up-regulating TMEM45A in PMCs. We further confirmed that IL-10 activated JAK2/STAT3/HIF1α signaling, resulting in TMEM45A/P4HA1 over-expression, which mediated pleural remodeling, thickening as well as fibrosis.

Lastly, we proposed a threshold value of serum IL-10 (> 6.32 pg/mL) in prediction of pleural thickening in SLE patients, with a specificity of 93.5% and sensitivity of 34.6%. New cell-based therapies have been developed to inhibit IL-10 signaling pathway to reduce fibrosis [19], our findings provided a rationale for IL-10 as potential target in prevention and treatment for pleural remodeling in SLE.

Our study uncovered that IL-10 mediated pleural remodeling in SLE. We suggested that serum IL-10 level exceeding 6.32 pg/mL was a potential reference threshold for predicting pleural thickening in SLE patients.
