Abstract

The spindle apparatus segregates bi-oriented sister chromatids during mitosis but mono-oriented homologous chromosomes during meiosis I. It has remained unclear if similar molecular mechanisms operate to regulate spindle dynamics during mitosis and meiosis I. Here, we employed live-cell microscopy to compare the spindle dynamics of mitosis and meiosis I in fission yeast cells and demonstrated that the conserved kinesin-14 motor Klp2 plays a specific role in maintaining metaphase spindle length during meiosis I but not during mitosis. Moreover, the maintenance of metaphase spindle stability during meiosis I requires the synergism between Klp2 and the conserved microtubule cross-linker Ase1, as the absence of both proteins causes exacerbated defects in metaphase spindle stability. The synergism is not necessary for regulating mitotic spindle dynamics. Hence, our work reveals a new molecular mechanism underlying meiotic spindle dynamics and provides insights into understanding differential regulation of meiotic and mitotic events.

Discussion

Few studies thus far have focused on addressing the difference between mitotic and meiotic spindle dynamics. In this present work, we compared mitotic and meiotic spindle dynamics in a quantitative manner and showed that Klp2 plays a crucial role in regulating spindle and chromosome dynamics during meiosis I but not during mitosis. We further demonstrated that Klp2 works in concert with Ase1 to organize interpolar spindle microtubules during meiosis I and to mediate homologous chromosome segregation ( Fig. 8 ). Hence, our work reveals a new meiotic mechanism underlying the maintenance of metaphase spindle length.

Our comparative analysis elucidated that the spindle dynamics of mitosis and meiosis I exhibits distinct characteristics ( Fig. 1 ). The spindle length of meiotic I phase II is ∼2-fold longer than that of mitosis ( Fig. 1 , B and D ), consistent with the findings reported previously ( 45 , 46 ). In contrast, the preanaphase duration of mitosis and meiosis I appears to be comparable ( Fig. 1 D ). Moreover, the spindle elongation speed during phase I of meiosis I is significantly faster than that of mitosis ( Fig. 1 , B and D ). Klp2 does not appear to contribute to the increased speed of meiotic spindle elongation ( Fig. 2 G ). Instead, Klp2 plays a specific role in maintaining metaphase spindle length during meiosis I but not during mitosis, because ∼50% of the spindles in klp2 Δ cells collapse abruptly during meiosis I ( Fig. 2 , A and C ).

The abrupt collapse of metaphase spindles in klp2 Δ cells is likely due to the defective organization of interpolar microtubules ( Fig. 3 A ). It has been reported that the budding yeast orthologue of Klp2 ( i.e. Kar3) aligns interpolar microtubules to the spindle axis so that Cin8/kinesin-5 can efficiently slide apart the antiparallel microtubules to promote spindle bipolarity during mitosis ( 50 ). Consequently, the absence of Kar3 results in short metaphase spindles during mitosis. In contrast, the absence of Klp2, the Kar3 counterpart in fission yeast, slightly but significantly lengthens the metaphase spindle during mitosis and meiosis I ( Fig. 2 , F and G ). Therefore, it is unlikely that Klp2 regulates meiotic spindle dynamics through enhancing the functionality of kinesin-5. Ase1 functions as a microtubule-cross-linking factor to organize antiparallel interpolar microtubules within the spindle ( 5 , 14 , 37 , 51 , 52 ). In this present work, we showed that the localization of Ase1 to the spindle is impaired in klp2 Δ cells during meiosis I ( Fig. 3 , C , E , and F ). Given that overexpression of Ase1 restores normal meiotic spindle dynamics in klp2 Δ cells ( Fig. 4 ), it is conceivable that Klp2 maintains metaphase spindle stability during meiosis I partly through regulating the proper localization of Ase1 to the spindle (see Table S1 for the summary of the roles of Klp2 and Ase1 in mitotic and meiotic cells). The interplay between Klp2 and Ase1 remains to be investigated.

Klp2 and Ase1 work in concert to organize antiparallel microtubule arrays ( 14 , 15 ). Consistently, our data support the important role of the synergism between Klp2 and Ase1 in maintaining metaphase spindle stability during meiosis I. The absence of both Klp2 and Ase1 exacerbates spindle destabilization during preanaphase of meiosis I ( Fig. 6 , C and D ). Moreover, interpolar microtubules of the meiotic spindles generally splayed apart in klp2 Δ ase1 Δ cells, indicative of defective microtubule organization ( Fig. 6 C ). The synergism may not play an important role in mitotic cells, because the absence of Klp2 and Ase1 does not have an additive effect on spindle dynamics during mitosis ( Fig. 6 , A and B ). Intriguingly, fewer Ase1 molecules localize to the spindle during preanaphase in mitotic cells than in meiotic cells, and the absence of Klp2 does not appear to affect the localization of Ase1 to the spindle in mitotic cells ( Fig. 3 , C and F ). Therefore, the synergism between Klp2 and Ase1 in regulating metaphase spindle stability is likely a meiosis-specific role of Klp2 and Ase1.

Consistent with the previous reports ( 51 , 52 ), our work confirmed that Ase1 is required for promoting spindle elongation during mitotic preanaphase ( Fig. 5 , A and B ). In contrast, the absence of Ase1 does not significantly shorten the maximum metaphase spindle length of meiotic cells ( Fig. 5 D ). Together, these findings demonstrate the different roles of Ase1 in regulating mitotic and meiotic spindle dynamics. We hypothesize that Ase1 functions in a dose-dependent manner within the spindle. When the concentration of Ase1 is elevated within the spindle during meiosis I, presumably in a Klp2-dependent manner ( Fig. 3 , C and F ), Ase1 functions as a limiting factor in restricting spindle elongation and maintaining spindle stability. When the concentration of Ase1 is low within the spindle during mitosis, Ase1 serves as a stabilizing factor to promote spindle elongation ( Fig. 3 , C and F ). This hypothesized model awaits further investigation.

Why are both Ase1 and Klp2, but not Ase1 alone, required to maintain spindle stability during metaphase I in meiotic cells? It has been established that, similar to Ase1, Klp2 family proteins also function as a microtubule cross-linking factor ( 32 , 48 , 49 ). Klp2 and Ase1 have been shown to work in concert to organize antiparallel microtubule arrays ( 14 , 15 ). Therefore, it is conceivable that a more sophisticated mechanism involving multiple types of microtubule cross-linkers ( i.e. Ase1 and Klp2) is required to construct and maintain metaphase spindles of a larger size, e.g. meiotic spindles. Meiotic spindles elongate faster than mitotic spindles during preanaphase I ( Fig. 1 D ). We postulate that the involvement of Ase1 and Klp2 provides robust forces, likely entropic forces, as shown previously ( 32 , 53 ), to antagonize spindle sliding and to maintain the stability of meiotic metaphase spindles.

The fission yeast has two kinesin-14 motors, i.e. Pkl1 and Klp2 ( 38 ). The absence of Pkl1 does not appear to affect spindle dynamics significantly during meiosis I ( Fig. S1 ), suggesting that Pkl1 plays a minor role in regulating spindle dynamics during meiosis I. Nonetheless, Pkl1 plays an important role in maintaining spindle integrity by anchoring microtubule minus ends to the spindle poles in mitotic cells ( 39 , 40 ). It is, therefore, possible that Klp2 has a more specific role in meiotic cells while Pkl1 functions mainly during mitosis.

In conclusion, this present work reveals a meiosis-specific role of Klp2 in maintaining metaphase spindle stability and provides insights into understanding differential regulations of meiotic and mitotic spindle dynamics.
