# The late elongated hypocotyl mutation of Arabidopsis disrupts circadian rhythms and the photoperiodic control of flowering.
**Authors:** Schaffer R, Ramsay N, Samach A, Corden S, Putterill J, Carré IA, Coupland G
**Journal:** Cell (1998)
**DOI:** [10.1016/s0092-8674(00)81465-8](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0092-8674(00)81465-8)

## Abstract

1. Cell. 1998 Jun 26;93(7):1219-29. doi: 10.1016/s0092-8674(00)81465-8.

The late elongated hypocotyl mutation of Arabidopsis disrupts circadian rhythms 
and the photoperiodic control of flowering.

Schaffer R(1), Ramsay N, Samach A, Corden S, Putterill J, Carré IA, Coupland G.

Author information:
(1)John Innes Centre, Norwich, United Kingdom.

The dominant late elongated hypocotyl (lhy) mutation of Arabidopsis disrupted 
circadian clock regulation of gene expression and leaf movements and caused 
flowering to occur independently of photoperiod. LHY was shown to encode a MYB 
DNA-binding protein. In wild-type plants, the LHY mRNA showed a circadian 
pattern of expression with a peak around dawn but in the mutant was expressed 
constantly at high levels. Increased LHY expression from a transgene caused the 
endogenous gene to be expressed at a constant level, suggesting that LHY was 
part of a feedback circuit that regulated its own expression. Thus, constant 
expression of LHY disrupts several distinct circadian rhythms in Arabidopsis, 
and LHY may be closely associated with the central oscillator of the circadian 
clock.
