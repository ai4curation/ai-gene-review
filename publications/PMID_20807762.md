Abstract

The acid sphingomyelinase (aSMase) gene gives rise to two distinct enzymes, lysosomal sphingomyelinase (L-SMase) and secretory sphingomyelinase (S-SMase), via differential trafficking of a common protein precursor. However, the regulation of S-SMase and its role in cytokine-induced ceramide formation remain ill defined. To determine the role of S-SMase in cellular sphingolipid metabolism, MCF7 breast carcinoma cells stably transfected with V5-aSMase WT were treated with inflammatory cytokines. Interleukin-1β and tumor necrosis factor-α induced a time- and dose-dependent increase in S-SMase secretion and activity, coincident with selective elevations in cellular C 16 -ceramide. To establish a role for S-SMase, we utilized a mutant of aSMase (S508A) that is shown to retain L-SMase activity, but is defective in secretion. MCF7 expressing V5-aSMase WT exhibited increased S-SMase and L-SMase activity, as well as elevated cellular levels of specific long-chain and very long-chain ceramide species relative to vector control MCF7. Interestingly, elevated levels of only certain very long-chain ceramides were evident in V5-aSMase S508A MCF7. Secretion of the S508A mutant was also defective in response to IL-1β, as was the regulated generation of C 16 -ceramide. Taken together, these data support a crucial role for Ser 508 in the regulation of S-SMase secretion, and they suggest distinct metabolic roles for S-SMase and L-SMase.

Introduction

The bioactive sphingolipid ceramide (Cer) 2 has been implicated in the regulation of various cellular processes, including apoptosis, infection, and inflammation ( 1 – 4 ). Enzymes of sphingolipid metabolism determine cellular levels of Cer. Serine palmitoyltransferase catalyzes the rate-limiting step in de novo Cer synthesis, the central anabolic Cer pathway. The other prominent mode of Cer generation involves breakdown of complex sphingolipids. One such pathway is the sphingomyelinase (SMase) pathway, which involves generation of Cer in a single step via hydrolysis of sphingomyelin (SM). Although several mammalian SMases have been identified and characterized ( 5 ), neutral sphingomyelinase 2 (nSMase 2, SMPD3 ) and acid sphingomyelinase (aSMase, SMPD1 ) are the most extensively studied enzymes in regulated Cer generation ( 6 ). Recently, glucosylceramidase was also implicated in the regulated formation of ceramide in the salvage pathway ( 7 , 8 ).

Acid sphingomyelinase (aSMase) catalyzes the cleavage of SM to Cer at an optimum pH of 4.5–5.5, suiting its primary localization within the endo-lysosomal compartment ( 9 ). Deficiency of aSMase results in Niemann-Pick disease, a lysosomal storage disorder characterized at the cellular level by accumulation of SM within lysosomes ( 10 ). Interest in aSMase expanded beyond its role in constitutive turnover of SM with the discovery that cells and tissues from aSMase knock-out mice were resistant to cell death in response to multiple stress stimuli ( 11 , 12 ). This resistance to cell death was attributable to lack of stress-induced Cer generation by the aSMase −/− cells, as exogenous Cer was capable of bypassing the metabolic block and inducing cell death ( 13 ). Subsequently, aSMase has been associated with a variety of physiologic and pathophysiologic cellular stress responses ( 14 , 15 ). However, despite continued interest in the roles and regulation of aSMase, some studies have questioned involvement of the aSMase/Cer pathway in cellular stress responses ( 16 , 17 ).

One possible explanation for these conflicting reports is that the aSMase gene gives rise to two distinct enzymes, lysosomal sphingomyelinase (L-SMase), as well as a secretory sphingomyelinase (S-SMase), via differential trafficking of a common protein precursor (pro-aSMase) ( 18 ). Initially characterized in fetal bovine serum ( 19 ), S-SMase is a zinc-dependent enzyme ( 20 ) whose precise biological role is not clear although it has been implicated in the metabolism of lipoprotein-bound SM to Cer and subsequent aggregation of LDL particles ( 21 , 22 ). Early work revealed that S-SMase is preferentially up-regulated in response to certain inflammatory mediators ( 23 ). For example, in human umbilical vein endothelial cells, which are abundant in S-SMase, interferon-γ, interleukin-1β (IL-1β), and tumor necrosis factor (TNF-α) were shown to stimulate S-SMase activity in the culture medium ( 24 ) with concomitant diminution of L-SMase activity. This was also demonstrated in a mouse model of acute systemic inflammation induced by injection of lipopolysaccharide, IL-1β, and TNF-α ( 11 , 25 ). Importantly, S-SMase up-regulation does not appear to be limited to inflammatory cytokines as a recent study demonstrated that the enteric pathogens Salmonella typhi and Escherichia coli specifically and acutely induced up-regulation of S-SMase by macrophages ( 26 ). Elevated S-SMase activity has been reported in the serum of humans in several disease states, including type II diabetes ( 27 ), chronic heart failure (especially cachectic heart failure) ( 28 ), sepsis ( 29 ), hypercytokinemia (in hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis) ( 30 ), and in response to spatially fractionated ionizing radiation therapy in cancer patients ( 31 ). Taken together, these studies suggest physiologic regulation and clinical relevance of enhanced S-SMase activity.

S-SMase is considered a candidate enzyme for hydrolysis of SM at the outer leaflet of the plasma membrane. However, evidence supporting a role for S-SMase in cellular ceramide formation has been lacking. Given the presumed capacity of S-SMase and L-SMase to generate ceramide in distinct regions of the cell, the regulation of S-SMase and L-SMase and the subsequent metabolic impact on cellular ceramide levels were investigated. Here, we describe the regulation of cellular Cer formation by S-SMase in response to inflammatory cytokines. In response to TNF-α and IL-1β, S-SMase is up-regulated in a time- and dose-dependent manner, as is the formation of specific ceramide species. Moreover, we describe a secretion-incompetent mutant of aSMase (S508A) that retains partial L-SMase function. Using the S508A mutant as a tool to probe the S-SMase-specific contribution to cellular ceramide formation, we are able to demonstrate for the first time a role for S-SMase in cellular sphingolipid metabolism.

DISCUSSION

Since the initial description of zinc-dependent aSMase activity by Spence et al. ( 19 ) 20 years ago, only a handful of studies have addressed the role(s) and/or regulation of S-SMase. Up-regulation of S-SMase in the context of inflammation or infection has been reported in cell culture studies ( 24 , 26 ), mouse models ( 25 ), and in human patients ( 27 – 31 ). Despite these connections to inflammatory processes, regulated S-SMase secretion is incompletely understood, and the role of S-SMase in cellular sphingolipid metabolism has largely been unexplored, remaining the subject of great speculation ( 23 , 51 ). The primary aim of this study was to investigate the role of constitutive and regulated S-SMase in cellular sphingolipid metabolism. In this report, we provide evidence that S-SMase is necessary to promote selective formation of specific Cer species.

Several lines of evidence support a role for S-SMase in the generation of cellular Cer. First, in MCF7 stably expressing V5-aSMase WT , inflammatory cytokines ( e.g. IL-1β and TNF-α) induced a time- and dose-dependent increase in V5-S-SMase secretion concomitant with selective elevations in C 16 -Cer ( Figs. 5 and 6 ). Second, IL-1β and TNF-α preferentially up-regulated S-SMase, whereas L-SMase activity remained virtually unchanged ( Fig. 2 ). Last, MCF7 stably expressing a secretion-incompetent mutant (V5-aSMase S508A ) failed to generate C 16 -Cer in response to IL-1β ( Figs. 9 and 10 ). Together, these data provide strong support for the existence of a S-SMase/Cer pathway that promotes formation of specific Cer species from the hydrolysis of cellular SM, presumably in the outer leaflet of the PM.
