Apoptosis-inducing factor (AIF) is a mitochondrial flavoprotein that, beyond its 
apoptotic function, is required for the normal expression of major respiratory 
chain complexes. Here we identified an AIF-interacting protein, CHCHD4, which is 
the central component of a redox-sensitive mitochondrial intermembrane space 
import machinery. Depletion or hypomorphic mutation of AIF caused a 
downregulation of CHCHD4 protein by diminishing its mitochondrial import. CHCHD4 
depletion sufficed to induce a respiratory defect that mimicked that observed in 
AIF-deficient cells. CHCHD4 levels could be restored in AIF-deficient cells by 
enforcing its AIF-independent mitochondrial localization. This modified CHCHD4 
protein reestablished respiratory function in AIF-deficient cells and enabled 
AIF-deficient embryoid bodies to undergo cavitation, a process of programmed 
cell death required for embryonic morphogenesis. These findings explain how AIF 
contributes to the biogenesis of respiratory chain complexes, and they establish 
an unexpected link between the vital function of AIF and the propensity of cells 
to undergo apoptosis.
