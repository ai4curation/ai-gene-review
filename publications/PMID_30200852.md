2017 marks the 60th anniversary of Krebs' seminal paper on the glyoxylate shunt 
(and coincidentally, also the 80th anniversary of his discovery of the citric 
acid cycle). Sixty years on, we have witnessed substantial developments in our 
understanding of how flux is partitioned between the glyoxylate shunt and the 
oxidative decarboxylation steps of the citric acid cycle. The last decade has 
shown us that the beautifully elegant textbook mechanism that regulates carbon 
flux through the shunt in E. coli is an oversimplification of the situation in 
many other bacteria. The aim of this review is to assess how this new knowledge 
is impacting our understanding of flux control at the TCA cycle/glyoxylate shunt 
branch point in a wider range of genera, and to summarize recent findings 
implicating a role for the glyoxylate shunt in cellular functions other than 
metabolism.
