# Antimicrobial Effects of Free Nitrous Acid on Desulfovibrio vulgaris: Implications for Sulfide-Induced Corrosion of Concrete
**Authors:** Shu-Hong Gao, Jun Yuan Ho, Lu Fan, David J. Richardson, Zhiguo Yuan, Philip L. Bond
**Journal:** Applied and Environmental Microbiology (2016)
**DOI:** [10.1128/aem.01655-16](https://doi.org/10.1128/aem.01655-16)

## Content

ABSTRACTHydrogen sulfide produced by sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) in sewers causes odor problems and asset deterioration due to the sulfide-induced concrete corrosion. Free nitrous acid (FNA) was recently demonstrated as a promising antimicrobial agent to alleviate hydrogen sulfide production in sewers. However, details of the antimicrobial mechanisms of FNA are largely unknown. Here, we report the multiple-targeted antimicrobial effects of FNA on the SRBDesulfovibrio vulgarisHildenborough by determining the growth, physiological, and gene expression responses to FNA exposure. The activities of growth, respiration, and ATP generation were inhibited when exposed to FNA. These changes were reflected in the transcript levels detected during exposure. The removal of FNA was evident by nitrite reduction that likely involved nitrite reductase and the poorly characterized hybrid cluster protein, and the genes coding for these proteins were highly expressed. During FNA exposure, lowered ribosome activity and protein production were detected. Additionally, conditions within the cells were more oxidizing, and there was evidence of oxidative stress. Based on an interpretation of the measured responses, we present a model depicting the antimicrobial effects of FNA onD. vulgaris. These findings provide new insight for understanding the responses ofD. vulgaristo FNA and will provide a foundation for optimal application of this antimicrobial agent for improved control of sewer corrosion and odor management.IMPORTANCEHydrogen sulfide produced by SRB in sewers causes odor problems and results in serious deterioration of sewer assets that requires very costly and demanding rehabilitation. Currently, there is successful application of the antimicrobial agent free nitrous acid (FNA), the protonated form of nitrite, for the control of sulfide levels in sewers (G. Jiang et al., Water Res 47:4331–4339, 2013,http://dx.doi.org/10.1016/j.watres.2013.05.024). However, the details of the antimicrobial mechanisms of FNA are largely unknown. In this study, we identified the key responses (decreased anaerobic respiration, reducing FNA, combating oxidative stress, and shutting down protein synthesis) ofDesulfovibrio vulgarisHildenborough, a model sewer corrosion bacterium, to FNA exposure by examining the growth, physiological, and gene expression changes. These findings provide new insight and underpinning knowledge for understanding the responses ofD. vulgaristo FNA exposure, thereby benefiting the practical application of FNA for improved control of sewer corrosion and odor.

Introduction 58 
Sulfate reducing bacteria (SRB) are anaerobic chemoorganotrophic microorganisms 59 
that typically use sulfate as the terminal electron acceptor for respiration and generate 60 
energy with the production of hydrogen sulfide (2). In confined spaces production of 61 
hydrogen sulfide can cause odor and corrosion problems. This is particularly the case 62 
in sewers and inlet structures of wastewater treatment plants where the oxidation of 63 
sulfide produces sulfuric acid which corrodes the concrete surfaces of the sewer. This 64 
results in serious deterioration of sewer assets that requires very costly and demanding 65 
rehabilitation efforts (3, 4).  Consequently there is great interest to efficiently control 66 
SRB and thereby minimising hydrogen sulfide production in sewers.  67 
 68 
Various chemical dosing methods are used to lower hydrogen sulfide production in 69 
sewers and  four strategies currently used include: sulfide  oxidation by injection of 70 
chemical oxidants such as air, oxygen, or nitrate (5, 6) ; sulfide precipitation by 71 
addition of ir on salts (7) ; application of  magnesium hydroxide or lime to raise the 72 
wastewater pH and prevent the release of hydrogen sulfide (8) ; and to inhibit the 73 
activities of SRB to lessen the generation of hydrogen sulfide (9). However, to obtain 74 

4 
the required sulfide control these strategies require continuous chemical consumption 75 
and considerable operational costs (10).  76 
 77 
Free nitrous acid (FNA), the protonated form of nitrite, was recently demonstrated to 78 
be the true metabolic inhibitor behind the usually observed “nitrite inhibition” (11). In 79 
recent treatment of sewer biofilms it was seen that application of FNA for 6-24 hours 80 
at 0.2-0.3 mg N/L decreased the live cell percentage from about 80% to 5 -15% (3). 81 
Since then FNA has been applied in sewe r field trials  in which an 80% reduction of 82 
sulfide production was achieved by intermittent FNA dosing at 0.2 6 mg N/L for 8 -24 83 
hours every four weeks (1). These investigations support that the application of FNA 84 
for control of sulfide levels in sewers is highly feasible.  85 
 86 
Therefore, FNA is emerging as an extremely  promising antimicrobial agent for the 87 
control of SRB, their activities and sulfide production, and there is great interest to 88 
understand how SRB respond to and withstand exposure to FNA. Nitrite is reported to 89 
cause decreased expression of  the genes coding for dissimilatory sulfite reductase 90 
(DsrAB) and thereby disrupting respiration of SRB (12, 13) . Currently, t he 91 
antimicrobial effects of FNA on bacteria in general are believed to be multi -targeted 92 
(14). It is thought that  FNA, and perhaps reactive nitrogen species (RN S) derived 93 
from FNA , causes oxidative stress resulting in damage to cell enzymes, cellular 94 
membranes and walls, and nucleic acids (15). Other hypotheses to explain the 95 
antimicrobial effects include FNA causing disruption of the proton motive force (16), 96 
nitrosylation of metal centres or thiol groups in enzymes (17), and DNA mutation (14). 97 
However, these hypotheses are not well verified and it is not clear whether some of 98 

5 
these effects are  more important than others  in different bacteria. Additionally, 99 
different bacteria will have different levels of tolerance to FNA (18). 100 
 101 
Desulfovibrio species can be  prevalent SRB in sewers (19) and are likely important 102 
for hydrogen sulfide production in sewage.  Desulfovibrio vulgaris Hildenborough is 103 
well studied and is demonstrated to have a periplasm ic cytochrome c nitrite reductase 104 
(NrfA) for the conversion of nitrite to ammonium  (2). It is largely thought that this 105 
nitrite reductase activity is not respiratory or for growth (20), but is a mechanism to 106 
remove the toxic nitrite (13). Although, in a recent twist there is suggestion that the 107 
nitrite reduction activity can conserve energy for growth (21). 108 
 109 
To date, there have been some transcriptional investigations, based on macroarray and 110 
microarray analyses, to examine the effects of nitrite on D. vulgaris (12, 13) . These 111 
studies implicate that nitrite stress could inhibit sulfate reduction and cause possible 112 
oxidative stress, as well as disrupt iron homeostasis. However, all the conclusions and 113 
hypotheses drawn from those  investigations are based only on the transcriptional 114 
responses. A comprehensive and systematic understanding o f the antimicrobial 115 
mechanisms of FNA on D. vulgaris  is still lacking. This could be achieved by 116 
combining the transcriptional response with detection of cell activities  and 117 
physiological changes.  118 
 119 
In this study, substrate transformations and physiological changes were detected in 120 
response to different levels of FNA. In addition, whole genome messenger RNA 121 
sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis was also conducted on D. vuglaris  cultures in the 122 
presence and absence of sub-bactericidal level of FNA  (4.0 g N/L). The global 123 

6 
transcriptome response was combined with  analyses of substrate transformations and 124 
physiological responses  to test the hypotheses mentioned above. From this a more 125 
comprehensive understanding of the effects of FNA was obtained to verify the  key 126 
determinants of FNA stress in this model sewer hydrogen sulfide producing bacterium.  127

Material and Methods 129 
Cultivation of D. vulgaris Hildenborough 130 
D. vulgaris Hildenborough (ATCC 29579) was provide d by Professor Jizhong Zhou 131 
and Dr . Aifen Zhou from Institute for Environmental Genomics, University of 132 
Oklahoma. For all experiments, a defined lactate sulfate medium (LS4D medium)  133 
(22) was used to cultivate the bacterium. The LS4D medium was prepared and added 134 
to serum bottles,  gassed with nitrogen gas for 30 min before capping with butyl 135 
rubber stoppers for autoclaving. 1.5 ml of a glycerol preserved stock of D. vulgaris 136 
was used to inoculate  the serum bottles containing 140 ml of medium. These were 137 
then cultivated at 37°C for 48 h to achieve the early stationary phase of growth ( an 138 
optical density at 600 nm [OD 600] of 0.9 to 1.0 ). The OD600 of the culture  was then 139 
adjusted to 0.5 and multiple 10 ml aliquots of this was used to inoculate serum bottles 140 
containing 140 ml of fresh LS4D medium. The inoculated bottles were then incubated 141 
at 30 °C without shaking  for further use of experiments described below . All 142 
experimental procedures of culture growth, physiological assays  and RNA -seq 143 
analyses were performed on triplicate cultures unless otherwise mentioned.  144

FNA treatment on cultures of D. vulgaris 146 
After 26 h growth at 30 °C, when  the culture s were in early log phase (OD 600 of 147 
around 0.3), nitrite was added to achieve the starting FNA concentrations of 0, 1 .0, 148 

7 
4.0, and 8.0 g N/L. The term “starting concentration” is used to describe the levels of 149 
FNA added in the different experiments, as during the incubations the actual FNA 150 
concentrations change, most likely due to the nitrite reduction activity of D. vulgaris. 151 
For each FNA concentration the culture incubations were performed in triplicate. The 152 
FNA concentration is dependent on the level of nitrite, pH and temperature , and this 153 
was calculated based on the equation already described (23). These concentrations of 154 
FNA were chosen based on preliminary studies (not sh own) where it was observed  155 
that these levels of exposure  covered the spectrum of growth responses to FNA, from 156 
slight inhibition of growth to near complete killing of the organism. Following the 157 
addition of FNA t he cultures were incubated for a further 48 h at 30°C. During 158 
exposure to the different FNA levels  the concentrations of lactate, acetate, sulfate, 159 
sulfite, sulfide, thiosulfate, nitrite and FNA were determined from filtered samples of 160 
the inoculated cultures . Additionally, samples from the triplicate controls (no added 161 
FNA) and FNA-treated cultures ( 4.0 g N/L) were taken for RNA extraction at 1  h 162 
after FNA addition.  These conditions were chosen for study of the transcriptional 163 
response as (i) it has been previously observed in D. vulgaris that the peak of the gene 164 
expression response to nitrite occurs at 1 h after nitrite exposure  (13), and (ii) our 165 
preliminary studies showed that at 4.0 µg N/L of FNA exposure there was significant 166 
effect on the organisms growth, but complete killing did not occur and eventually the 167 
organism could overcome the stress. In preparation for the RNA extraction 5 ml of the 168 
bacterial suspension from each serum bottle was centrifuged at 13,000 x g for 2 169 
minutes, the supernatant was discarded and the pellets were immediately frozen in 170 
liquid nitrogen before storing at -80ºC. Total RNA extraction was performed from the 171 
pellets using the QIAGEN miRNeasy Mini Kit ( catalog number 217004) according to 172 
the manufacturer’s instructions , except for the addition of an extra bead-beating step 173 

8

The raw sequencing data was deposited in NCBI’s Gene Expression Omnibus and are 177

RNA-seq data processing and differentially expressed genes analysis 213 
The raw sequence reads were treated using the NGS QC Toolkit (v2.3.3) (26) to trim 214 
the 3’-end residual adaptor s and primer s as well as removing the ambiguous 215 
characters in the reads. Then the sequence reads consisting of at least 85% bases with 216

were used for downstream analyses. SeqAlto (version 0.5) was used to align the clean 218 
reads of each sample to the reference g enome (NC_00 2937) of D. vulgaris  (27). 219 
Cufflinks (version 2.2.1) was used to calculate the strand -specific coverage for each 220 
gene and to analyse the differential expression on triplicated cultures (28). 221

conduct the statistic analyses and visualization . Gene expression was calculated as 223 

10 
reads per kilobase of a gene per million mapped reads ( RPKM), a normalized value 224 
generated from the frequency of detection and the length of a given gene (28). 225 
Differences in fold -change values were calculated between control and FNA -treated 226

RPKM values of two triplicated experiments. A stringency cut-off of LFC ≥ 1 or ≤ -1 228 
with q value less than 0.0 5 was used to identify the significantly differentially 229 
expressed genes.  230

Results 232 
Concentration-dependent effects of FNA on culture growth and respiratory 233 
activities  234 
In these investigations, D. vulgaris utilises sulfate as the electron acceptor and lactate 235 
as the electron donor with sulfide and acetate as the respective end products  (2). 236 
Additionally, D. vulgaris has NrfA that can reduce nitrite to ammonium, allowing it to 237 
survive in environments in the presence of nitrite  (2). The growth of D. vulgaris  238 
cultures exposed to the low est starting FNA concentration of 1 .0 g N/L was slightly 239 
inhibited during the 48 h incubation (Figure 1). Inhibition of growth increased with 240 
the increasing levels of FNA and growth was almost completely stopped with FNA at 241 
8.0 g N/L (Figure 1).  242 
 243 
In the presence of FNA at 1.0 g N/L  the levels of  lactate oxidation and sulfate 244 
reduction were slightly less than that of the control culture (no FNA addition) (Figure 245 
2C, E) and this coincided  with the observed slight decreased growth (Figure 1) , 246 
indicating FNA was having only a slight inhibitory effect on the organism at this level. 247 
During the incubation 1.0 g N/L  FNA was completely reduced  within 8 h after 248 

11 
addition (Figure 2A, B) . In comparison, much lower lactate oxidation levels were 249 
detected in cultures with starting FNA concentrations of 4.0 and 8.0 g N/L (Figure 250 
C). In these cultures there would be limited electrons available for sulfate reduction, 251 
which was severely diminished (Figure 2E), and this coincided with the reductions in 252 
growth levels detected (Figure 1) . In the batch cultures nitrite reduction occurred 253 
(Figure 2 A, B) after FNA addition. This was evident at all FNA concentrations and 254 
was likely due to nitrite reductase activity of D. vulgaris. 255

The absolute ratios of lactate consumed, sulfate used and acetate produced were 257 
calculated from the concentrations detected during the 48 h incubations of D. 258 
vulgarius at the different FNA levels. The ratios were compared to the theoretical 259 
ratio determine d when acetate is considered as the product of the lactate oxidation 260 
(Table 1). For the control culture not exposed to FNA, these values are reasonably 261 
close to the stoichiometric ratios for the lactate oxidation (Table 1). However, the 262 
ratios of lactate u sed and acetate produced were increased, at the higher level of 263 
applied FNA. This could be explained if there was increased competition for electrons 264 
during FNA exposure, which may likely have resulted from increased nitrite reduction 265 
activity.  266

effect of FNA on cell viability.  At early log phase  prior to FNA addition, the viable 275 
cell numbers in the cultures were around 85%-90% (Figure 3A). When no FNA was 276 
added, the live cell percentage stayed at  this level until 24 h incubation before 277

the bacteriostatic and bactericidal effects of FNA are concentration-determined and 286

Cellular thiol levels of D. vulgaris increased with the addition of FNA (Figure 3B). At 289 
FNA starting concentrations of 1.0 and 4.0 g N/L N/L, a small increase in  cellular 290 
thiol levels was detected  at 12 and 24 h incubation (Figure 3B). However, cellular 291 
thiol levels increased markedly throughout the incubation period when the cells were 292 
exposed to 8.0 g N/L FNA (Figure 3B). It is thought that FNA could nitrosylate thiol 293 
groups, such as those on proteins, which could change the activity or function of those 294 
(14). There is also hypothesis that FNA imposes oxidative stress on bacteria cells (29). 295 
Thus in these batch cultures the cell redox status was determined and it was observed 296 
that with increasing levels of FNA the cells were more oxidised (Figure 3C). Cellular 297 
ATP levels of the D. vulgaris batch cultures (normalised to per live cell)  decreased 298 

13 
with the increase of added FNA concentration s (Figure 3D).  This supports the idea 299 
that FNA acts as a protonophore, to decouple the  proton motive force across cell 300 
membranes and thereby inhibiting ATP synthesis (16).  301

Global transcriptomic analysis of FNA stress 303 
To determine the antimicrobial mechanisms  of FNA  and the possible response 304 
pathways adopted by D. vulgari s, gene expression profiles were examined by RNA 305 
sequencing. To focus on the direct inhibitory mechanisms of FNA on D. vulgaris we 306 
sought to measure changes that report on the primary effect of FNA, not any 307 
secondary affects that would occur over longer timescales more related to changes in 308 
growth rate, substrate, products and batch conditions, rather than the direct response 309 
to FNA. We therefore measured the transcriptional response after only 1 h of the FNA 310 
addition. This was performed  when the cultures were exposed to 4 .0 g N/L at 1 h 311 
after FNA addition and compared to gene expression of control cultures when no 312 
FNA was added. A total of 239 genes, 6.6% of the total 3623 genes, were detected as 313 
significantly differently expressed (see criterion in Material and Methods) . 159 genes 314 
showed increased transcripts and 80 genes showed decreased transcript levels  in 315 
response to FNA stress.  316 
 317 
Evidence of oxidative stress and detoxification of FNA by D. vulgaris 318 
D. vulgaris  possesses NrfA that can catalyse the six -electron reduction of nitrite to 319 
ammonium and hydroxylamine is an  intermediate of the reduction  (30). In these 320 
FNA-added cultures the g ene coding for NrfA (DVU0625) exhibited considerable up 321 
regulation (Table 2 ), implying its detoxifying role by the reduction of nitrite (and 322 
proportional reduction of FNA). This observation agrees with the decreasing nitrite 323 

14 
levels detected in the batch cultures (Fig ure 2A). Additionally, the gene, DVU2543, 324 
which codes for what is known as the hybrid cluster protein (HCP), was the most up-325 
regulated gene detected when exposed to FNA (Table 2 ). The HCP is proposed to 326 
have hydroxylamine reductase  activity (31), and was possibly acting to remove 327 
hydroxylamine as part of the detoxification of nitrite. 328 
 329 
Various genes coding for response to oxidative stress displayed highly increased 330 
transcript levels in the FNA-added cultures (e.g. DVU0772  and ahpC (DVU2247), 331 
Table 2). This implies that FNA caused oxidising  conditions, which is what was 332 
detected by the cellular redox measurement in FNA-added cultures (Figure 3C).  The 333 
genes msrA (DVU1984) and msrB (DVU0576), coding for reductases, that reduce 334 
methionine sulfoxides  as an antioxidant response , were observed to have increased 335 
transcript levels in FNA -added cultures. Additionally, genes of the Fur regulon , 336 
DVU0273, gdp (DVU3176), and DVU2574, showed increased transcript levels in 337 
response to FNA  (Table 2 ), indicating the possibility  that FNA causes a response 338 
relating to iron levels change in the cell .  Alternatively, this could  also a response to 339 
increased oxidative condition , as the genes involved in oxidative stress and iron 340 
homeostasis are of the same superfamily of metalloregulatory proteins (13). 341

FNA inhibited anaerobic respiration and energy generation 343 
In the presence of FNA, various genes coding for enzymes involved in lactate 344 
oxidation and  sulfate reduction proces ses were down regulated (Table 3). This 345 
included the genes DVU084 9-50 and DVU1286-90 that code for the quinone-346 
interacting membrane-bound oxidoreductase complex (Qmo) and the sulfite reductase 347 
complex (DsrAB) respectively (Table 3). It is proposed that Qmo transfers electrons 348 

15 
from lactate oxidation directly to adenosine-5’-phosphosulfate reductase while DsrAB 349 
transfers electrons to the sulfite reductase (2).  350 
 351 
Recently an operon has been described for lactate oxidation genes (luo) in D. vulgaris 352 
(32). This includes the genes DVU3026 for lactate permease, DVU3027-28 and 353 
DVU3032-33 for lactate dehydrogenase subunits, DVU3025 for pyruvate-ferredoxin 354 
oxidoreductase, DVU3030 for acetate kinase, and DVU3029 for phosphate 355 
acetyltransferase. Down-regulation of all these genes in the luo operon , excepting 356 
DVU3026, was observed when exposed to FNA (Table 3). Other genes proposed for 357 
lactate oxidation such as  DVU0600 for lactate dehydrogenase and  DVU1569-70 for 358 
pyruvate-ferredoxin oxidoreductase, were up regulated or had no change respectively 359 
in the presence of FNA . However, DVU0600 and DVU1569 -70 had ver y low 360 
expression values (Table 3), and as discussed later, there is some question regarding 361 
the involvement of the proteins coded by these genes in lactate oxidation. 362 
 363 
Down regulation of these genes, involved in lactate oxidation and transfer of electrons 364 
for sulfate reduction,  correlated with reductions of lactate and sulfate utilisation 365 
(Figure 2C, E) and lowered acetate and sulfide  production (Figure 2D, F) when D. 366 
vulgaris was exposed to FNA.  Additionally, the transcripts  of genes DVU0774-80, 367 
and DVU0918 coding for ATP synthase were a ll markedly down regulated (Table 3). 368 
This coincided with the lowered ATP level detected in the cells. It seems the cells 369 
were shutting down the main energy conserving reactions when exposed to FNA.  370

FNA disrupts DNA replication, transcription and translation 372 

16 
Genes encoding for critical enzymes involved in DNA replication (e.g. chromosomal 373 
replication initiator protein DnaA, DNA polymerase, DNA gyrase, DNA 374 
topoisomerase) and transcription (DNA -directed RNA polymerase) exhibited down 375 
regulation after FNA exposure for 1 h ( Table 4). As well, the genes coding for 30S 376 
and 50S ribosomal structure proteins ( Table 4) and a variety of amino acid transfer 377 
RNA synthetases showed significantly decreased transcripts. It is apparent that FNA 378 
could be causing  decreased cell activit ies of DNA replication, transcription and 379 
protein biosynthesis in D. vulgaris. The phenomenon of decreased DNA replication, 380 
transcription and protein synthesis coincides with the decreased growth and overall 381 
metabolism we detected,  such as the decreased ability to utili se sulfate and lactate, 382 
during FNA exposure  (Figure 1 and 2 ). The gene yfi (DVU1629) coding for the 383 
ribosomal subunit interface protein displayed 14.4 fold up regulation (Table 4). This 384 
factor is demonstrated to stabili se ribosome s and stop  translation in stressful 385 
conditions (33). Possibly D. vulgaris is ceasing translation, and inactivates/conserves 386 
the existing ribosomes in the presence of FNA. Consequently, we measured protein 387 
levels per live cell of the control and FNA exposed  (4.0 µg N/L) cultures after 2 and 388 
8 h incubation periods ( Table 5). Protein levels per cell were less in the FNA - added 389 
culture in comparison to the control , suggesting that protein synthesis was markedly 390 
arrested when exposed to FNA. T his is in agreement with the decreased expression 391 
we detected of many genes involved with ribosome function (Table 4). 392 
 393 
Additionally, the gene s coding for DNA repair protein s MutL and RadC showed 394 
increased transcript levels when FNA was applied (Table 4). This was also the case 395 
for genes coding for chaperone proteins DnaK and DnaJ (Table 4) that are reported to 396 
assist in the re-folding of damaged protein s (34). The up regulation of these genes 397 

17 
indicates that FNA is likely causing damage to DNA and proteins and D. vulgaris was 398 
attempting the repair of these molecules.  399

Discussion 401 
In this study, the antimicrobial effect s of FNA were determined in the model SRB D. 402 
vulgaris. For the first time we use a comprehensive approach by combining substrate 403 
consumption, physiological analyses, and whole genome RNA-seq, in the presence 404 
and absence of FNA , to discover the antimicrobial mechanisms of FNA on D. 405 
vulgaris Hildenborough. Our findings of the transcriptome analysis revealed 406 
significant multiple responses and detoxification  activities of D. vulgaris in response 407 
to FNA stress , and the resulting observations and hypotheses were supported by the 408 
cell activities and physiological changes detected.  409 
 410 
Nitrite consumption and i ncreased transcription of genes coding for the nitrite 411 
reductase NrfA demonstrated that detoxification of FNA by NrfA was evident in D. 412 
vulgaris in the presence of FNA even when electron supply was low since lactate 413 
oxidation was limited (Figure 2A&C, Table 2). This  is a logical response and is in  414 
agreement with previous observations of D. vulgaris  to nitrite exposure (12, 13)  . 415 
Additionally, the most up-regulated gene detected, with greater than 1000 fold change 416 
in the FNA-added cultures  was DVU2543 (Table 2), which encodes a proposed 417 
hydroxylamine reductase (31) . High expression of this gene in D. vulgaris has been 418 
reported previously in response to nitrite exposure (12, 13). The enzyme is thought to 419 
be either for reduction of RNS (the hydroxylamine reductase activity) or for reduction 420 
of reactive oxygen species (35). There is strong possibility here that hydroxylamine or 421 
other RNS accumulate during  FNA exposure, possibly through incomplete reduction 422 

18 
of nitrite by NrfA (30) . Characterisation of the HCP from E. coli shows reduction of 423 
hydroxylamine with production of ammonium (31). While the enzyme has not been 424 
characterised in D. vulgaris, one suggestion is that the HCP is acting similarly or in 425 
conjunction with NrfA to detoxify the high levels of nitrite.  426 
 427 
Genes coding for electron transfer proteins for sulfate reduction, the Qmo and Dsr AB 428 
membrane complexes, were significantly down regulated (Table 3). This down  429 
regulated gene expression is observed in previous studies of nitrite exposure  to D. 430 
vulgaris (12, 13) , and would be an appropriate action in cells that experienced 431 
diminished electron flow from lactate oxidation. Interestingly, thiosulfate, the 432 
intermediary product of the sulfate reduction by the trithionate-reducing pathway (36), 433 
was found to accumulate in FNA treated samples while sulfite did not (Fig 2G and H). 434 
Previous studies demonstrate two possible pathways of sulfite reduction to sulfide in 435 
D. vulgaris, one is by direct reduction without intermediates and the other is via the 436 
trithionate pathway with thiosulfate and sulfite as the intermediates (37). The 437 
observed accumulation of thiosulfate  supports the inhibitory effect of nitrite on 438 
DsrAB and suggests that the trithionate-reducing pathway is the mechanism of sulfite 439 
reduction in D. vulgaris in these conditions . In summary, the FNA exposure caused 440 
the down regulation of these genes which severely inhibited the lactate oxidation and 441 
sulfate reduction processes. 442 
 443 
From early annotations of the D. vulgaris genome the genes associated with lactate 444 
oxidation have included DVU0600 for lactate dehydrogenase , and DVU1569-70 for 445 
pyruvate-ferredoxin oxidoreductase. In a previous microarray study exposing D. 446 
vulgaris to nitrite levels (2.5 mM) similar to that used here (2.3 mM), they reported up 447 

19 
regulation of these genes  (13). We also report up  regulation for the gene  DVU0600 448 
but no change for the genes  DVU1569-70. However , the expression levels (RPKM 449 
values) of these genes in the presence of FNA was extremely low (Table 3), thus, this 450 
questions the involvement of the respected coded proteins in lactate oxidation. 451 
Recently, genes of the luo operon are deemed to be responsible for lactate oxidation 452 
in D. vulgaris rather than DVU0600 and DVU1569-70 (32). In this study we detected 453 
down regulation of most genes in luo operon when exposed to FNA (Table 3) and 454 
importantly these genes had high expression values when lactate and sulfate 455 
utilisation, and when acetate and sulfide production, were active (Table 3, Figure 2C, 456 
D, E, and F). This down  regulation of the luo operon genes during FNA exposure is 457 
logical when the cells respiratory activities were lowered, as we observed. 458 
Additionally, these findings further support the involvement of the recently described 459 
luo operon in lactate oxidation (32).  460 
 461 
In addition to the down regulation of genes involved in respiration, there is suggestion 462 
that FNA directly effected enzymes involved in lactate oxidation. The lack of sulfate 463 
utilisation during high levels of FNA exposure can be readily explained by the 464 
inhibition of the sulfite reductase activity of DsrAB by nitrite /FNA (12). However, 465 
there is the question as to why the lactate oxidation stops during exposure to FNA . 466 
Nitrite reductase activity in the periplasm would act to consum e the protons and 467 
electrons produced by lactate oxidation in the cytoplasm, as described in the model 468 
for respiration by D. vulgaris  (12). Thus, lactate oxidation should continue unless 469 
FNA or nitrite was having an effect on the enzymes involved that proce ss. The 470 
periplasmic hydrogenases are involved in the flow of protons and electrons produced 471 
by lactate oxidation (12). However, gene expression of the highly expressed 472 

20 
periplasmic hydrogenase, detected as the Ni -Fe-Se type (Table 3), was significantly 473 
decreased. Additionally, it has been seen that the activity of these hydrogenases of D. 474 
vulgaris is inhibited by nitrite and other RNS (38). Consequently, this lowered gene 475 
expression, and the enzyme inhibition could lower the flow of electrons and protons 476 
and explain the lowered lactate oxidation activity in the presence of FNA.  477 
 478 
To conserve energy D. vulgaris  performs incomplete lactate oxidation to produce 479 
acetate and uses sulfate as the electron acceptor. In our batch cultures , there is limited 480 
utilisation of lactate and sulfate, and production of acetate and sulfide at 24 h 481 
exposure of 4.0 µg N/L  FNA (Fig ure 2). However, based on 4 electrons being 482 
released per molecule of lactate utilised, and 6 and 8 electrons required for nitrite and 483 
sulfate reductions respectively, we calculated the electron balance when the culture 484 
was exposed to FNA at 4.0 µg N/L  at 48 h incubation . It was deduced that even this 485 
low amount of lactate utilisation, 7.1 2 mmol, would provide 28.5 mmol of electrons, 486 
which is essentially ne arly enough required for the nitrite and sulfate reduction (32.5 487 
mmol) that was detected. 488 
 489 
During FNA exposure the cellular redox state was markedly more oxidised (Figure 490 
3C) and genes coding for response to oxidative stress, and those involved in protein 491 
repair, had increased expression (Table 2). Additionally, as elucidated above, another 492 
possibility for the highly up -regulated gene DVU2543, is that the HCP is acting to 493 
relieve oxidative stress (35). Considering these detected events, oxidative stress is 494 
suggested as an important antimicrobial effect caused by FNA in D. vulgaris. While it 495 
is currently not known, it is possible that oxidative damage to the cell components 496 
was caused by RNS  (14, 23) . Interestingly, when Pseudomonas aeruginosa  was 497 

21 
recently exposed to FNA, a more reduced intracellular condition  was caused  (25). 498 
Therefore, the nature of the oxidative stress caused by FNA is strain-dependent rather 499 
than a general mode of action.  500 
 501 
FNA exposure caused decreased expression of genes involved in protein synthesis in 502 
D. vulgaris . This is consistent with previous stud ies of  the effect of FNA on S. 503 
enterica   and P. aeruginosa (25, 39). Additionally, we detected increased expression  504 
of the gene yfi (DVU1629) (Table 4), the coded protein of which functions to stabilise 505 
ribosomes and stop translation in stressful conditions . Thus, it seems  that FNA stress 506 
caused D. vulgaris  to stop protein synthesis , and to inactivate and stabili se the 507 
existing ribosomes.  This was  supported by the decreased cellular protein  levels we 508 
measured during FNA exposure (Table 5). The nature of these FNA -caused changes 509 
to the  gene regulation detected here  needs further investigation , possibly it was a 510 
response to lowered cell energetics. Irrespective, t he decreased ribosome activity 511 
would have a major impact on the organism’s ability to produce its main components 512 
for successful cellular activity and operation. 513 
 514 
Achieving control of the growth and activity of SRB in sewers is extremely important 515 
to mitigate costly concrete  corrosion. Growth was detected in the D. vulgaris batch 516 
cultures at all the concentrations of FNA applied in this study (Figure 1). Although , 517 
the growth was low at the high FNA starting concentra tion of 8.0 µg N/L , when 518 
approximately 5% of cells remained live (Figure 3A). While only low levels of lactate 519 
and sulfate were consumed in this condition, it appears that the remaining live cells 520 
were able to grow at the high level of FNA. These results suggest that the bactericidal 521 
effects of FNA are concentration-determined and higher concentrations of FNA need 522 

22 
to be applied to kill D. vulgaris . We recently propose d that heterogeneity in 523 
Pseudomonas aeruginosa populations explained differential resistance to FNA (23). It 524 
is also possible here that differential resistance to FNA is detected in SRB. The 525 
occurrence of resistance would be of concern in the application of FNA in the sewers. 526 
Recently, in field trials lasting 6 months the sulfide produc tion from actual sewer 527 
biofilms was reduced by 80% after dosing the sewage with FNA at 0.26 mg N/L (1). 528 
Consequently, good levels of sulfide control w ere achieved and increased tolerance to 529 
the applied FNA was not observed under the dosing regime. Although the laboratory 530 
application of FNA is very different to that in the real sewer situation, through the 531 
improved understanding gained in this study, there is likely opportunity to improve on 532 
real FNA applications. For example, combining FNA with treatments that cause 533 
increased oxidative stress is seen to achieve more effective biofilm control (40).  534 
  535 
In conclusion, several key findings were identified in this study regarding the 536 
responses of D. vulgaris  to FNA stress. A conceptual model was proposed that 537 
summarises the antimicrobial effects of FNA on D. vulgaris  (Figure 4 ). During 538 
exposure to FNA, D. vulgaris switched from a status of prolific growth to a phase of 539 
severely inhibited growth. When exposed to FNA at 4.0 g N/L sulfate reduction and 540 
lactate oxidation coupled with ATP generation were suppressed, leading to energy 541 
starvation in the FNA-added cultures. Expression of genes coding for lactate 542 
oxidation and sulfate reduction were subsequently lowered. In response to energy 543 
starvation, D. vulgaris  stabilised its existing ribosomes and ceased translation of 544 
proteins. In addition, FNA caused more oxidative conditions in D. vulgaris and there 545 
was transcriptional evidence of attempts to alleviate the oxidative stress . The findings 546 
of this study  not only provide insight and  fundamental understanding of the 547 

23 
antimicrobial mechanism of FNA, but also can assist in the application of FNA in real 548 
sewers for control of sulfide production and corrosion, 549
