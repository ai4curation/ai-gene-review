# Haploinsufficiency of the retinoblastoma protein gene reduces diet-induced obesity, insulin resistance, and hepatosteatosis in mice.
**Authors:** Mercader J, Ribot J, Murano I, Feddersen S, Cinti S, Madsen L, Kristiansen K, Bonet ML, Palou A
**Journal:** Am J Physiol Endocrinol Metab (2009)

## Abstract

1. Am J Physiol Endocrinol Metab. 2009 Jul;297(1):E184-93. doi:

Mercader J(1), Ribot J, Murano I, Feddersen S, Cinti S, Madsen L, Kristiansen K, 
Bonet ML, Palou A.

Author information:
(1)Laboratory of Molecular Biology, Nutrition, and Biotechnology, Universitat de

Brown adipose tissue activity dissipates energy as heat, and there is evidence 
that lack of the retinoblastoma protein (pRb) may favor the development of the 
brown adipocyte phenotype in adipose cells. In this work we assessed the impact 
of germ line haploinsufficiency of the pRb gene (Rb) on the response to high-fat 
diet feeding in mice. Rb(+/-) mice had body weight and adiposity 
indistinguishable from that of wild-type (Rb(+/+)) littermates when maintained 
on a standard diet, yet they gained less body weight and body fat after 
long-term high-fat diet feeding coupled with reduced feed efficiency and 
increased rectal temperature. Rb haploinsufficiency ameliorated insulin 
resistance and hepatosteatosis after high-fat diet in male mice, in which these 
disturbances were more marked than in females. Compared with wild-type 
littermates, Rb(+/-) mice fed a high-fat diet displayed higher expression of 
peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor (PPAR)gamma as well as of genes 
involved in mitochondrial function, cAMP sensitivity, brown adipocyte 
determination, and tissue vascularization in white adipose tissue depots. 
Furthermore, Rb(+/-) mice exhibited signs of enhanced activation of brown 
adipose tissue and higher expression levels of PPARalpha in liver and of 
PPARdelta in skeletal muscle, suggestive of an increased capability for fatty 
acid oxidation in these tissues. These findings support a role for pRb in 
modulating whole body energy metabolism and the plasticity of the adipose 
tissues in vivo and constitute first evidence that partial deficiency in the Rb 
gene protects against the development of obesity and associated metabolic 
disturbances.
