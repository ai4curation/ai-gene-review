# Knockout of ERK1 MAP kinase enhances synaptic plasticity in the striatum and facilitates striatal-mediated learning and memory.
**Authors:** Mazzucchelli C, Vantaggiato C, Ciamei A, Fasano S, Pakhotin P, Krezel W, Welzl H, Wolfer DP, Pagès G, Valverde O, Marowsky A, Porrazzo A, Orban PC, Maldonado R, Ehrengruber MU, Cestari V, Lipp HP, Chapman PF, Pouysségur J, Brambilla R

## Abstract

1. Neuron. 2002 May 30;34(5):807-20. doi: 10.1016/s0896-6273(02)00716-x.

Knockout of ERK1 MAP kinase enhances synaptic plasticity in the striatum and
facilitates striatal-mediated learning and memory.

Mazzucchelli C(1), Vantaggiato C, Ciamei A, Fasano S, Pakhotin P, Krezel W,
Welzl H, Wolfer DP, Pagès G, Valverde O, Marowsky A, Porrazzo A, Orban PC,
Maldonado R, Ehrengruber MU, Cestari V, Lipp HP, Chapman PF, Pouysségur J,

Author information:
(1)San Raffaele Research Institute and University, via Olgettina 58, 20132

Extracellular signal-regulated kinases (ERK1 and 2) are synaptic signaling
components necessary for several forms of learning. In mice lacking ERK1, we
observe a dramatic enhancement of striatum-dependent long-term memory, which
correlates with a facilitation of long-term potentiation in the nucleus
accumbens. At the cellular level, we find that ablation of ERK1 results in a
stimulus-dependent increase of ERK2 signaling, likely due to its enhanced
interaction with the upstream kinase MEK. Consistently, such activity change is
responsible for the hypersensitivity of ERK1 mutant mice to the rewarding
properties of morphine. Our results reveal an unexpected complexity of
ERK-dependent signaling in the brain and a critical regulatory role for ERK1 in
the long-term adaptive changes underlying striatum-dependent behavioral
plasticity and drug addiction.
