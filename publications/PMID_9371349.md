# Ribosylative inactivation of rifampin by Mycobacterium smegmatis is a principal contributor to its low susceptibility to this antibiotic.
**Authors:** Quan S, Venter H, Dabbs ER
**Journal:** Antimicrob Agents Chemother (1997)
**DOI:** [10.1128/AAC.41.11.2456](https://doi.org/10.1128/AAC.41.11.2456)
**PMC:** [PMC164144](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC164144/)

## Abstract

1. Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 1997 Nov;41(11):2456-60. doi: 
10.1128/AAC.41.11.2456.

Ribosylative inactivation of rifampin by Mycobacterium smegmatis is a principal 
contributor to its low susceptibility to this antibiotic.

Quan S(1), Venter H, Dabbs ER.

Author information:
(1)Genetics Department, University of the Witwatersrand, Johannesburg, South 
Africa.

Mycobacterium smegmatis inactivates rifampin by ribosylating this antibiotic. 
The gene responsible for this ability was cloned and was shown to confer 
low-level resistance to this antibiotic (MIC increase, about 12-fold) in related 
organisms. A 600-bp subclone responsible for ribosylating activity and 
resistance carried an open reading frame of 429 bp. Targeted disruption of the 
gene in M. smegmatis resulted in mutants with much increased susceptibility to 
rifampin (MICs of 1.5 instead of 20 microg/ml) as well as the loss of 
antibiotic-inactivating ability. Also, disruption of this gene led to a much 
lower frequency of occurrence of spontaneous high-level rifampin-resistant 
mutants.
