During random locomotion, human neutrophils and Dictyostelium discoideum amoebae 
repeatedly extend and retract cytoplasmic processes. During directed cell 
migration--chemotaxis--these pseudopodia form predominantly at the leading edge 
in response to the local accumulation of certain signalling molecules. 
Concurrent changes in actin and myosin enable the cell to move towards the 
stimulus. Recent studies are beginning to identify an intricate network of 
signalling molecules that mediate these processes, and how these molecules 
become localized in the cell is now becoming clear.
