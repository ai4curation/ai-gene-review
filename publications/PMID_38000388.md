The glmS ribozyme riboswitch, located in the 5' untranslated region of the 
Bacillus subtilis glmS messenger RNA (mRNA), regulates cell wall biosynthesis 
through ligand-induced self-cleavage and decay of the glmS mRNA. Although 
self-cleavage of the refolded glmS ribozyme has been studied extensively, it is 
not known how early the ribozyme folds and self-cleaves during transcription. 
Here, we combine single-molecule fluorescence with kinetic modeling to show that 
self-cleavage can occur during transcription before the ribozyme is fully 
synthesized. Moreover, co-transcriptional folding of the RNA at a physiological 
elongation rate allows the ribozyme catalytic core to react without the 
downstream peripheral stability domain. Dimethyl sulfate footprinting further 
revealed how slow sequential folding favors formation of the native core 
structure through fraying of misfolded helices and nucleation of a native 
pseudoknot. Ribozyme self-cleavage at an early stage of transcription may 
benefit glmS regulation in B. subtilis, as it exposes the mRNA to 
exoribonuclease before translation of the open reading frame can begin. Our 
results emphasize the importance of co-transcriptional folding of RNA tertiary 
structure for cis-regulation of mRNA stability.

Introduction

Bacteria utilize structured RNA elements, such as cis-acting riboswitches and thermometers, to regulate gene expression in response to environmental changes. In Bacillus subtilis, the majority of cis-regulatory structures are located in the 5′ untranslated region (UTR) of the target messenger RNA (mRNA) (1). The 5′ to 3′ polarity of RNA synthesis, as well as the ability of nascent transcripts to fold immediately after synthesis, enables regulators in the 5′ UTR to function before transcription of the mRNA is complete.

It is known that transcriptional riboswitches that control termination must fold and bind their ligand before RNA polymerase (RNAP) bypasses the expression platform (2,3). For these riboswitches, regulation efficiency and ligand sensitivity strongly correlate with transcription speed and RNAP pausing (3–6). In contrast, RNA elements that regulate translation initiation or mRNA stability are often assumed to act after transcription is complete (7–9). However, several recent studies have shown that translation and mRNA decay can also be controlled at the co-transcriptional level (10–12), raising the possibility that many more 5′ UTR structures operate during mRNA synthesis than previously realized. Furthermore, there is evidence suggesting that sequential RNA folding and RNAP pausing can help translational riboswitches to reach the ligand-bound conformation more efficiently (13–15).

Here, we adapt single-molecule co-localization and co-transcriptional assembly (12,16) to monitor transcription and self-cleavage of the glmS riboswitch simultaneously. The results show that the riboswitch can respond to ligand before the downstream coding sequence is transcribed, highlighting the ability of RNA stability elements to act at an early stage of mRNA expression.

The glmS ribozyme riboswitch, located in the 5′ UTR of the glmS mRNA, downregulates expression of the downstream gene through mRNA degradation (Figure 1A) (17,18), and therefore serves as a model riboswitch to study co-transcriptional regulation of mRNA stability. The glmS ribozyme senses the level of glucosamine-6-phosphate (GlcN6P) whose synthesis is catalyzed by the glmS gene product, glucosamine synthase (17). The ribozyme pre-folds into the active structure before GlcN6P binding (19), consisting of a catalytic core (red; Figure 1B) and peripheral helices that stabilize the core (gray; Figure 1B) (20). Unlike other riboswitches that change conformation when binding a metabolite, binding of GlcN6P activates the glmS ribozyme by triggering its self-cleavage, which is followed by degradation of the 3′ cleaved mRNA fragment by RNase J1 (18).

It is unknown whether the glmS ribozyme regulates mRNA turnover during transcription, although there is evidence that the ribozyme is more sensitive to ligand during transcription (13). Considering that self-cleavage is fast (80 min−1), once the ribozyme has folded (21), it is reasonable to speculate that the ribozyme can fold and self-cleave before the entire 1803-nt glmS ORF is transcribed, as long as levels of GlcN6P are high. Besides offering a faster response to GlcN6P, co-transcriptional cleavage would allow RNase J1 to begin degrading the cleaved mRNA before ribosomes recognize the Shine–Dalgarno sequence (Figure 1A, top). In contrast, if self-cleavage occurs only after transcription, RNase J1 may be temporarily impeded by initiating ribosomes (Figure 1A, bottom), which have been shown to protect mRNA from degradation by RNase J1 (22–24). Therefore, ribozyme self-cleavage during elongation of the glmS mRNA is expected to provide a faster and more efficient response to GlcN6P.

To determine whether the glmS ribozyme can sense the level of GlcN6P during transcription, we combined biochemical assays and single-molecule fluorescence microscopy to characterize the dynamics of ribozyme self-cleavage during RNA elongation. We found that the ribozyme can fold into its native structure and self-cleave efficiently (>70%) during transcription. A chemical kinetics model that accounts for ribozyme activity at different stages of elongation confirmed that self-cleavage can occur even before the 3′ end of the ribozyme has been synthesized. Our results also suggest that the ribozyme core alone can reach the native state during transcription, but that this active state is short-lived and depends on slow sequential folding of the elongating RNA. Overall, we propose that ligand-dependent turnover of the glmS mRNA can be triggered by ribozyme cleavage during transcription to achieve efficient feedback regulation of cell wall biosynthesis.

ResultsThe glmS ribozyme self-cleaves during transcriptionTo investigate whether the ribozyme self-cleaves during transcription, we performed in vitro single-round transcription assays that simultaneously monitored the time needed for full-length RNA synthesis and ribozyme self-cleavage. As the entire B. subtilis glmS mRNA is >2 kb, we created a shorter DNA template including only the 5′ UTR plus 190 bp of the ORF (Figure 1B). To synchronize transcription, we first prepared stalled TECs (29) using a 29-nt C-less cassette (30). In the absence of CTP, B. subtilis RNAP pauses at the first C in the 5′ leader to form a stable stalled TEC. Next, to measure the kinetics of co-transcriptional cleavage, the TECs were restarted synchronously by adding all four NTPs, together with saturating (10 mM) GlcN6P.The transcription time course showed that the products of ribozyme self-cleavage started to accumulate at 6 s, whereas the full-length transcripts were not detectable until 20 s (Figure 1C). These results indicated that the ribozyme was able to self-cleave during transcription, before the first 190 bp of the ORF was synthesized. When the TECs were restarted without GlcN6P, no cleavage products were observed, confirming that cleavage requires the metabolite co-factor, as expected (17). We also observed several B. subtilis transcription pause sites, with the first major pause located around nt +131 within the P4.1 helix. The P4.1-paused transcripts did not accumulate in the presence of GlcN6P, however, indicating that they are also capable of self-cleavage even though the ribozyme sequence has not yet been completely transcribed.Transcription of the glmS template finished between 12 and 20 s at 37°C, regardless of the co-factor, corresponding to an average speed of 35 nt/s. This in vitro transcription rate is only half of the in vivo rate (73 nt/s) previously measured on the B. subtilis pycA gene (31), potentially due to RNAP pausing or the absence of transcription elongation factors. To investigate whether the ribozyme can self-cleave during transcription at a higher speed, we used T7 RNAP to conduct the bulk cleavage assay on the same glmS sequence (Supplementary Figure S1). By varying the reaction conditions such as temperature, pH and NTP concentrations, we achieved a wide range of transcription rates from 10 to 100 nt/s. Cleaved products accumulated before the end of transcription under all conditions (Supplementary Figure S1A and B). As T7 and B. subtilis polymerases lead to a similar likelihood of self-cleavage during transcription, in the following experiments we used T7 RNAP plus a lower concentration of NTPs to mimic co-transcriptional gene regulation of the glmS ribozyme in B. subtilis.Single-molecule observation of self-cleavage during transcriptionAlthough the bulk cleavage experiments showed that the ribozyme self-cleaves during transcription, they did not reveal how much of the RNA had been transcribed when cleavage occurred. The glmS ribozyme self-cleaves during transcription

To investigate whether the ribozyme self-cleaves during transcription, we performed in vitro single-round transcription assays that simultaneously monitored the time needed for full-length RNA synthesis and ribozyme self-cleavage. As the entire B. subtilis glmS mRNA is >2 kb, we created a shorter DNA template including only the 5′ UTR plus 190 bp of the ORF (Figure 1B). To synchronize transcription, we first prepared stalled TECs (29) using a 29-nt C-less cassette (30). In the absence of CTP, B. subtilis RNAP pauses at the first C in the 5′ leader to form a stable stalled TEC. Next, to measure the kinetics of co-transcriptional cleavage, the TECs were restarted synchronously by adding all four NTPs, together with saturating (10 mM) GlcN6P.

The transcription time course showed that the products of ribozyme self-cleavage started to accumulate at 6 s, whereas the full-length transcripts were not detectable until 20 s (Figure 1C). These results indicated that the ribozyme was able to self-cleave during transcription, before the first 190 bp of the ORF was synthesized. When the TECs were restarted without GlcN6P, no cleavage products were observed, confirming that cleavage requires the metabolite co-factor, as expected (17). We also observed several B. subtilis transcription pause sites, with the first major pause located around nt +131 within the P4.1 helix. The P4.1-paused transcripts did not accumulate in the presence of GlcN6P, however, indicating that they are also capable of self-cleavage even though the ribozyme sequence has not yet been completely transcribed.

Transcription of the glmS template finished between 12 and 20 s at 37°C, regardless of the co-factor, corresponding to an average speed of 35 nt/s. This in vitro transcription rate is only half of the in vivo rate (73 nt/s) previously measured on the B. subtilis pycA gene (31), potentially due to RNAP pausing or the absence of transcription elongation factors. To investigate whether the ribozyme can self-cleave during transcription at a higher speed, we used T7 RNAP to conduct the bulk cleavage assay on the same glmS sequence (Supplementary Figure S1). By varying the reaction conditions such as temperature, pH and NTP concentrations, we achieved a wide range of transcription rates from 10 to 100 nt/s. Cleaved products accumulated before the end of transcription under all conditions (Supplementary Figure S1A and B). As T7 and B. subtilis polymerases lead to a similar likelihood of self-cleavage during transcription, in the following experiments we used T7 RNAP plus a lower concentration of NTPs to mimic co-transcriptional gene regulation of the glmS ribozyme in B. subtilis.

Here, we show that the glmS ribozyme riboswitch can respond to ligand during transcription to regulate mRNA decay at an early stage of mRNA synthesis.

Our results suggest that co-transcriptional cleavage enables immediate downregulation of glmS expression when GlcN6P levels are high (Figure 6). The kinetic model based on our single-molecule results and DMS footprinting results showed that the nascent ribozyme starts to cleave after the P3 helix is transcribed at RT. We speculate that cleavage can occur even earlier at 37°C, and possibly as soon as the ribozyme core is transcribed, based on the larger fast-cleaving population at 37°C (Figure 4B). Early cleavage by the incomplete ribozyme is only efficient when the RNA folds sequentially in the presence of GlcN6P. In contrast, post-transcriptional cleavage enables a response to GlcN6P at any time, but requires the downstream peripheral stability domain, and may encounter delays if mRNA turnover is blocked by ribosomes as discussed below.

Sequential folding of core helices favors metastable structures by avoiding competing interactions with downstream residues. This mechanism is reminiscent of folding in certain catalytic ribozymes and transcriptional riboswitches (38,39). During elongation of the glmS core, sequential folding provides a window for the nascent RNA to form the native-like pseudoknot by fraying the alt P2.1 helix before the downstream alt P2 helix is synthesized (Figure 5). Because the native pseudoknot is only favored over a short range of transcript lengths, extending this time window partitions more transcripts toward native folding pathways. Our results suggest that this metastable native structure lasts for several seconds before converting to the thermodynamically stable but inactive structure (Figure 4D).

Since sequential folding is crucial for co-transcriptional cleavage, this regulatory pathway is intimately connected with the process of transcription (Figure 6). We examined the effects of transcription speed and RNAP pausing, two factors that can commonly improve ligand binding of transcriptional riboswitches. Reducing NTP concentrations to slow down RNAP increased the active population 10–20%. Although we did not observe pausing within the ribozyme core in vitro (Figure 1C), global sequencing of RNAP pause sites in B. subtilis by Larson et al. suggested that there is a short pause at position +74, the second residue from 3′ end of the core (40). Pausing at this site is predicted to improve native folding of the core, because P2.1 and part of P2.2 can form, but not the competing alt P2 helix. Further investigation of B. subtilis RNAP pausing and elongation is required to confirm this possibility.

The temperature dependence of cleavage efficiency suggests that higher temperature may accelerate opening of the alt P2.1 helix to reach the metastable native state. Temperature was similarly observed to accelerate structural rearrangements in the aptamer domain of the translational add riboswitch and the transcriptional 2′-deoxyguanosine sensing riboswitch (41,42).

In addition to these similarities with transcriptional riboswitches, the full-length glmS ribozyme can also respond to GlcN6P long after transcription, like equilibrium-controlled translational riboswitches (43,44). The integrity of the peripheral stability domain is crucial for cleavage during and after transcription of the 5′ UTR. First, the peripheral helices increase direct folding to the native state during transcription, as evidenced by the 80% fast cleavage population of the WT ribozyme (Figure 4B). Second, the stable full-length structure ensures that the ribozyme is competent to self-cleave after transcription whenever a response to GlcN6P is needed.

Although self-cleavage is efficient both during and after transcription, we expect that cleavage during slow elongation is advantageous because it promotes immediate decay of the cleaved glmS mRNA. This may be useful for downshifting cell wall production when NTP pools suddenly drop. First, self-cleavage during transcription exposes the 5′ end of the mRNA to RNase J1 at an early stage. In contrast, exclusively post-transcriptional regulation would delay the turnover of all nascent glmS mRNAs for at least 30 s, or even longer considering RNAP pausing. Second, early RNA degradation may help achieve a complete shutoff of glmS translation. RNase J1 is a processive exonuclease on long RNAs (45), although its velocity is still unknown. If the rate of RNase J1 degradation is similar to other exonucleases (30–80 nt/s) (46,47), co-transcriptional cleavage may allow RNase J1 to degrade the Shine–Dalgarno sequence before ribosomes bind. RNAP pausing within the 5′ UTR downstream of the ribozyme (Figure 1C) does not affect self-cleavage, but such pausing would enlarge the opportunity for co-transcriptional decay (40). Decoupling of transcription and translation in B. subtilis may also provide extra time for RNase J1 to act before translation initiation (31). Although initiating ribosomes can transiently block RNase J1, the glmS Shine–Dalgarno sequence is relatively weak (23), and likely unable to protect the mRNA for a long time. Third, early mRNA cleavage may attenuate transcription. In eukaryotes, the 5′ to 3′ exoribonuclease XRN2/Rat1 has been observed to terminate Pol II transcription through the torpedo effect (48–51). A previous study has shown that RNase J1 is able to dissociate B. subtilis RNAP from stalled TECs (52), although it is not known whether it can torpedo actively elongating TECs. Further work to measure the speed of RNase J1 is needed to determine whether it can terminate transcription following ribozyme self-cleavage.

In summary, our work demonstrates that the glmS ribozyme can self-cleave during transcription, providing an additional layer for metabolite-dependent gene regulation. We hypothesize that co-transcriptional and post-transcriptional regulation pathways coexist when the metabolite level is high (Figure 6). The former enables a quick response to ligand, while the latter ensures that mRNA turnover is controlled at all stages of expression. The combined regulation during and after transcription may be shared by other 5′ UTR RNA regulators. For example, the translational riboswitches thiM and btuB form their ligand-bound structure faster during transcription, compared to rearrangement of the full-length riboswitch from the unbound to bound state (13,14). A recent study on the ROSE thermometer predicted the role of sequential folding in guiding the formation of RNA stem-loops for translation initiation control (53), also implying the possibility of co-transcriptional regulation. Finally, our measurements of glmS cleavage efficiency under different conditions may inform the design of antibiotics targeting this riboswitch or its use in metabolic engineering (54–56).
