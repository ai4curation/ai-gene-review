# Self-assembling protein microarrays.
**Authors:** Ramachandran N, Hainsworth E, Bhullar B, Eisenstein S, Rosen B, Lau AY, Walter JC, LaBaer J
**Journal:** Science (2004)
**DOI:** [10.1126/science.1097639](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1097639)

## Abstract

1. Science. 2004 Jul 2;305(5680):86-90. doi: 10.1126/science.1097639.

Self-assembling protein microarrays.

Ramachandran N(1), Hainsworth E, Bhullar B, Eisenstein S, Rosen B, Lau AY, 
Walter JC, LaBaer J.

Author information:
(1)Harvard Institute of Proteomics, Department of Biological Chemistry and 
Molecular Pharmacology, Harvard Medical School, 320 Charles Street, Cambridge, 
MA 02141, USA.

Protein microarrays provide a powerful tool for the study of protein function. 
However, they are not widely used, in part because of the challenges in 
producing proteins to spot on the arrays. We generated protein microarrays by 
printing complementary DNAs onto glass slides and then translating target 
proteins with mammalian reticulocyte lysate. Epitope tags fused to the proteins 
allowed them to be immobilized in situ. This obviated the need to purify 
proteins, avoided protein stability problems during storage, and captured 
sufficient protein for functional studies. We used the technology to map 
pairwise interactions among 29 human DNA replication initiation proteins, 
recapitulate the regulation of Cdt1 binding to select replication proteins, and 
map its geminin-binding domain.
