Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) matrix (M) protein is indispensable for virion
assembly and release. It is localized to the nucleus during early infection to
perturb host transcription. However, the function of RSV M protein in other
cellular activities remains poorly understood. In this study, several interferon
response-associated host factors, including RACK1, were identified by proteomic
analysis as RSV M interactors. Knockdown of RACK1 attenuates RSV-restricted IFN
signaling leading to enhanced host defense against RSV infection, unraveling a
role of M protein in antagonizing IFN response via association with RACK1. Our
study uncovers a previously unrecognized mechanism of immune evasion by RSV M
protein and identifies RACK1 as a novel host factor recruited by RSV,
highlighting RACK1 as a potential new target for RSV therapeutics development.

ABSTRACT

Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) is a frequent cause of respiratory illness among pediatric and elderly populations. The severity of the respiratory disease is determined, in part, by RSV virulence and the host immune response, especially type I interferon (IFN) production. Using proteomics for the identification of partner proteins associated with RSV-encoded matrix (M) protein in transiently M-expressed and RSV-infected cells, we identified many M-interacting proteins involved in diverse biological processes including cell stress and innate immune response, highlighting M protein as a novel antagonist of IFN-β potentially accounting for limited IFN production in RSV-infected epithelial cells. To clarify the M antagonistic mechanism, we focused on M-interacting receptor of activated C kinase 1 (RACK1), which is an adaptor protein and a negative regulator of IRF3/7. Knockdown of RACK1 with small-interfering RNA attenuated the M-suppressed IFN-β response leading to increased IFN-β production and reduced RSV genome replication, confirming RACK1 as a critical host factor for efficient RSV infection. Our finding unravels an immune-intervening effect of RSV M protein on IFN response and further identifies RACK1 as a promising target for antiviral therapy.

INTRODUCTION

Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), an enveloped RNA virus of the Pneumoviridae family in the order of Mononegavirales ( 1 ), encodes 11 proteins including two nonstructural proteins (NS1 and NS2), three membranous envelope glycoproteins (fusion F, attachment G, and small hydrophobic SH), one viral assembly factor (matrix protein M), four nucleocapsid proteins (nucleoprotein N, phosphoprotein P, large polymerase subunit L, and transcription antitermination factor M2-1), and RNA-regulatory factor M2-2 ( 2 ). RSV is a leading cause of lower respiratory tract disease in young children and the elderly, resulting in more deaths each year than influenza virus ( 3 – 5 ). Severe RSV infection is long-lasting and contributes to high-risk chronic respiratory disease, including childhood asthma and bronchiolitis ( 6 , 7 ). Since the discovery of RSV in 1954, there have been no safe and effective vaccines or therapeutic agents available on the market due to incomplete understanding of the pathogen-host interface ( 8 ). Encouragingly, a vaccine providing potentially safe and effective prevention of RSV infection in adults 60 years of age or older was approved in May 2023 by the US Food and Drug Administration, highlighting the successful targeting of a unique conformation of the F envelope protein prior to cell membrane fusion for RSV vaccine development ( 9 ).

Innate immunity from epithelial cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells is the first line of host defense against virus invasion. It coordinates with adaptive immunity for the efficient elimination of pathogens ( 10 ). Type I interferons (i.e., IFN-α/β) and IFN-induced antiviral host effectors both play a significant role in antiviral innate response ( 11 ). Sensing of viral RNAs by retinoic acid-inducible gene I (RIG-I)-like receptors in the cytoplasm initiates antiviral signaling triggered by RNA virus infection ( 12 ). Mitochondrial antiviral signaling (MAVS) protein is then activated and forms a signaling complex with TANK-binding kinase 1 (TBK1) ( 13 ). Subsequently, IFN-regulatory factors (IRFs) are phosphorylated and translocated to the nucleus to activate type I IFN gene transcription. Type I IFNs induce the expression of various antiviral genes, particularly IFN-stimulated genes, via the JAK1/TYK2/STAT pathway, triggering a powerful antiviral state ( 14 ). In infants and neonatal mice infected by RSV, however, there is no robust type I IFN response to RSV, and thus, administration of IFN-α prior to RSV infection of neonatal mice reduces immunopathology ( 15 – 17 ). It appears that type I IFN production in RSV-infected cells is generally attenuated by RSV NS1, NS2, and the envelope glycoprotein through distinct mechanisms ( 18 ).

RSV M is a structural component of the virion, forming a scaffold bridging the viral ribonucleoprotein complex and the envelope ( 19 ). M, containing a canonical nuclear localization signal and a nuclear export signal ( 20 ), acts as a nucleocytoplasmic trafficking protein regulated by phosphorylation ( 21 ) that is also critical for virion production ( 22 ). An X-ray crystallographic structure of M reveals two compact beta sheets and a positively charged patch potentially necessary for M oligomerization, progeny virus generation, and viral filament interaction with negatively charged membrane surfaces ( 23 , 24 ). Within viral inclusion bodies (IBs) found in the cytoplasm of RSV-infected cells, M interacts with M2-1 and P to inhibit viral gene transcription prior to packaging and associates with viral envelope glycoproteins to facilitate virion filament formation at the lipid rafts of host cell membranes ( 25 , 26 ). M also induces a staged redistribution of mitochondria with compromised respiratory activities and increased reactive oxygen species generation during RSV infection ( 27 ). At present, there are no drug candidates directly targeting RSV assembly. M as well as its cellular interactors are, thus, potential therapeutic targets of interest ( 18 ).

Receptor for activated C kinase 1 (RACK1) is an adaptor protein recruited by specific signaling molecules to regulate diverse cellular processes and viral infection ( 28 – 30 ). The function and selectivity of the 40S ribosomal subunit, for example, is regulated by RACK1 via recruitment of an activated form of protein kinase C ( 31 ) or a poxvirus kinase ( 32 ). The ubiquitin E3 ligase TRAF2 is recruited to modulator of apoptosis-1 by RACK1 to regulate apoptosis ( 33 ). In the case of viral infection, RACK1 is involved in apoptosis regulated by hepatitis B virus core protein ( 34 ) and by infectious bursal disease virus VP5 ( 35 ). It is also implicated in autophagy and virus replication through interaction with ATG5 and hepatitis C virus nonstructural protein 5A ( 36 – 38 ). RACK1 is a negative regulator of inflammatory response via IKK-mediated NF-κB pathway ( 39 ) or NLRP3-regulated inflammasome assembly ( 40 , 41 ). As for type I IFN production, RACK1 inhibits IRF3-mediated IFN-β production by recruiting protein phosphatase 2A following RNA virus infection ( 42 ). RACK1 also regulates MAVS ubiquitination to suppress MAVS-mediated antiviral signaling ( 43 ). RACK1 plays an important role in IFN-STAT1 signaling and regulates STAT1 activation during measles virus and mumps virus infection ( 44 , 45 ).

In the present study, we identified M protein as a novel IFN-β antagonist and RACK1, a negative regulator of IFN, and a new M-interacting protein. Using small-interfering RNA (siRNA) knockdown, we found RACK1 is usurped by RSV to evade host immune surveillance, leading to efficient viral infection. These findings uncover a novel function of M protein and RACK1 in association with RSV immune evasion.

DISCUSSION

RSV is a major human pathogen responsible for severe acute lower respiratory tract infection in infants and young children, particularly among individuals with congenital immunodeficiency. No effective vaccines or treatment regimens are presently available on the market. IFN-triggered antiviral responses are central to host defense against viral infection ( 55 ), and the airway epithelium plays a critical role in pulmonary innate immunity ( 56 ). Nevertheless, IFN is nearly undetectable in nasal washes from RSV-infected patients ( 57 ). The nonstructural proteins of RSV interact with MAVS or RIG-I to inhibit downstream IFN signaling ( 53 , 54 , 58 , 59 ), indicating that RSV has a unique way to evade host surveillance. The strategies utilized by other respiratory RNA viruses include alteration of host ubiquitination pathways, stress granule formation, intracellular trafficking, and protein quality control among others ( 60 – 62 ). In the present study, we provide evidence showing that RSV M protein also plays an inhibitory role in host IFN response via association with RACK1 that is mechanistically distinct from NS1/NS2-inhibited IFN signaling, uncovering a new strategy underlying the ability of RSV to evade host immune surveillance.

RSV M protein is involved in virion assembly/release via association with viral envelope glycoproteins and ribonucleoprotein complexes. RSV M is a nucleocytoplasmic trafficking protein. However, nuclear translocation is dispensable for virus replication but is linked to viral infectivity and pro-inflammatory response, indicating that specific host factors are functionally regulated by M ( 18 , 63 ). Our proteomic analysis identifies multiple cellular activities, including chromatin regulation, cell response and signaling transduction, and protein translation and modification, that are additionally regulated by RSV M protein, highlighting the broader functionality of RSV M protein extending beyond its classical role in virion assembly. M protein has the potential to regulate transcription via its association with cellular chromatin ( 2 , 64 ) and host factors as identified in our proteomics that include proteins involved in the formation of the chromosomal fiber (HIST1H1E, H2AC12, H2AC14, H1-4, H2AC20, H2AJ, H2AC6) and RNA-binding proteins (SERBP1, FUS, HNRPF). Additional M-interacting host factors identified include AZGP1 associated with susceptibility/resistance to SARS-CoV-2 infection ( 65 ), heat shock protein 90 implicated in immune response and viral entry ( 66 ), and protein phosphatases regulating phosphorylation (see Fig. 1F ; Table S7 ).

Our finding of RSV M protein in cellular stress and immune response prompted us to investigate the molecular basis of M-regulated IFN response. We confirmed that the IFN response is strongly enhanced in macrophages (BMM) during early RSV infection, but the increase of IFN-β RNA is delayed in HEp-2 and BEAS-2B airway epithelial cells ( Fig. S2 ), likely attributed to species specificity and cell type difference. Indeed, the levels of IFN-β transcription in respiratory epithelial HEp-2 and BEAS-2B cells were increased 2- to 28-fold by RSV within 48 hpi in our study ( Fig. S2A and B ), consistent with a 4- to 15-fold increase in RSV-infected hSAEC cells within 24 hpi in a previous report ( 67 ). However, a related study did not detect a significant increase in the IFN-β transcript level at the early infection stage in A549 and BEAS-2B cells ( 68 ). These studies, along with our finding that a limited increase of IFN-β mRNA was observed in RSV-infected HEp-2 and BEAS-2B cells, indicate that the innate immune response stimulated by RSV is generally weak at the early infection stage in respiratory epithelial cells. A previous study reporting that RSV-stimulated IFN-β in BMMs increases 140-fold at the early infection stage ( 69 ) is analogous to our study showing a 60-fold increase of IFN-β transcription ( Fig. S2C ). Clearly, different levels of the innate immune response to RSV infection were observed between respiratory epithelial cells and macrophages. A weak IFN response in epithelial cells to early RSV infection could be clarified from different experimental systems with specific cell types used. It is interesting that IFN stimulation by the RIG-I N terminus is suppressed by RSV infection via the previously known IFN antagonist NS2 and the newly identified novel antagonist M protein ( Fig. 3 ). Several matrix proteins encoded by other RNA viruses were also identified as IFN regulators. For example, the matrix protein of Nipah virus targets the E3-ubiquitin ligase TRIM6 to inhibit the IKKε kinase-mediated IFN-α/β antiviral response ( 70 ). The matrix protein of human parainfluenza virus type three induces mitophagy that suppresses the interferon response ( 71 ). The VSV matrix protein inhibits NF-κB and the interferon pathway ( 72 ). The matrix protein of Spring viremia of Carp virus targets the MAVS/TRAF3 axis to inhibit host interferon signaling ( 73 ). The matrix protein of Marburg virus VP40 is an inhibitor of JAK1 to prevent IFN signaling ( 74 ). The matrix protein of Ebolavirus VP24 prevents the IFN-induced nuclear accumulation of tyrosine-phosphorylated STAT1 to inhibit IFN-induced gene expression ( 75 ).

In this study, we identified RACK1 as a new host factor in IFN antagonism. RACK1, being an evolutionarily conserved host factor belonging to a family of tryptophan-aspartate repeat proteins, is involved in viral life cycle spanning entry, replication, translation, and virion release ( 76 , 77 ). Moreover, RACK1 regulates antiviral response via MAVS-IRF3/7 and IFN-STAT1 pathways ( 44 , 78 ). In GO analysis, RACK1 (also known as GNB2L1) is functionally linked to ribonucleoprotein binding and regulation as well as establishment of protein localization (see Table S1 , line in bold). Recent studies also show RACK1 involvement in ribosome function, apoptosis, autophagy, inflammatory response, and IFN stimulation ( 34 , 42 , 76 , 79 , 80 ). In our study, colocalization and interaction between RACK1 and RSV M were found in virus-infected cells and in cells transiently expressing RSV M, indicating that RACK1 could be recruited by RSV M independently of other viral proteins ( Fig. 3 ). Knockdown of RACK1 attenuates the suppressive effect of RSV M on IFN-β production and decreases virus titers, suggesting that RACK1 is recruited by RSV M for immune evasion and efficient RSV infection ( Fig. 4 and 5 ). A similar study found that the matrix protein of influenza A virus interacts with RACK1 in facilitating virus release ( 81 ). Moreover, myeloid RACK1 was shown to promote viral infection in vivo via RACK1-inhibited IFN-I production ( 82 ), and RACK1 binding to IRF3/7 in inhibiting IFN production is regulated by phosphorylation via AMPK ( 78 ). These studies indicate that RACK1 plays a positive role in virus infection and is, thus, a promising candidate for host-targeted antiviral development ( 83 ). Further investigation is needed to define the precise targets of the RACK1-RSV M complex and the critical organelles associated with their intervening effect on IFN-β production.

In summary, our study identifies RSV M protein as a novel antagonist of IFN and provides new insight into the multifunctionality of RSV M. Previous studies focused mostly on the role of viral matrix protein in virion formation, but more and more studies have identified new host factors recruited by the viral matrix protein in regulating diverse cellular processes, such as mitophagy, apoptosis, IFN response, and host gene transcription. RACK1, as an interactor of M protein, is recruited to limit IFN-β production that contributes to efficient virus infection in host cells. These findings extend the molecular mechanisms of immune evasion utilized by RSV. More studies are necessary to define the role of RACK1 in RSV-infected cells, including RIG-I-mediated IFN production and IFN-mediated signaling. Regulation of RACK1 function or competitive binding with RACK1 can be a promising strategy for blocking RSV immune evasion and infectivity.
