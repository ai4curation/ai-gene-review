The burgeoning crisis of antibiotic resistance has directed attention to 
bacteriophages as natural antibacterial agents capable of circumventing 
bacterial defenses. Central to this are the bacterial defense mechanisms, such 
as the BREX system, which utilizes the methyltransferase BrxX to protect against 
phage infection. This study presents the first in vitro characterization of BrxX 
from Escherichia coli, revealing its substrate-specific recognition and 
catalytic activity. We demonstrate that BrxX exhibits nonspecific DNA binding 
but selectively methylates adenine within specific motifs. Kinetic analysis 
indicates a potential regulation of BrxX by the concentration of its 
co-substrate, S-adenosylmethionine, and suggests a role for other BREX 
components in modulating BrxX activity. Furthermore, we elucidate the molecular 
mechanism by which the T7 phage protein Ocr (Overcoming classical restriction) 
inhibits BrxX. Despite low sequence homology between BrxX from different 
bacterial species, Ocr effectively suppresses BrxX's enzymatic activity through 
high-affinity binding. Cryo-electron microscopy and biophysical analyses reveal 
that Ocr, a DNA mimic, forms a stable complex with BrxX, highlighting a 
conserved interaction interface across diverse BrxX variants. Our findings 
provide insights into the strategic counteraction by phages against bacterial 
defense systems and offer a foundational understanding of the complex interplay 
between phages and their bacterial hosts, with implications for the development 
of phage therapy to combat antibiotic resistance.

Introduction

Bacteriophages, viruses capable of infecting bacteria, are ubiquitous in the natural environment and are estimated to be the most abundant biological entities on Earth. In response to the pervasive presence of bacteriophages, bacteria have evolved a suite of defense mechanisms to counteract phage infection. These include abortive infection (1), the restriction–modification (R–M) system (2), clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats–CRISPR-associated proteins system (CRISPR-Cas) (3), cyclic-oligonucleotide-based anti-phage signalling system (CBASS) (4) and prokaryotic Argonautes (5). While the R–M system, CRISPR–Cas and CBASS have been extensively studied, recent systematic analyses of prokaryotic genomes, coupled with experimental validation, have revealed numerous previously uncharacterized anti-phage defense systems (6,7). However, the molecular underpinnings of these novel systems remain largely unexplored.

Among them, the BREX phage exclusion system represents a novel defense mechanism within the bacterial arsenal against phage predation, with six distinct subtypes (I, II, III, IV, V, VI) that are prevalent in ∼10% of bacterial and archaeal genomes (8). The type I BREX system is the most prevalent, constituting 55% of the total BREX systems identified. It is characterized by a gene cluster encompassing six genes: brxA, brxB, brxC, brxL, brxX (pglX) and pglZ. The brxA and brxB genes encode small proteins of currently unknown function, while brxC is recognized as an ATPase. The brxX gene is responsible for encoding a core component methyltransferase, and brxX is associated with N6-methyladenosine methylation, marking the host’s genomic DNA. BrxZ (PglZ) is a putative alkaline phosphatase. The brxL gene encodes a novel AAA+ protein factor with ATPase enzymatic activity, which is implicated in an ATP-dependent assembly (9).

Similar to the R–M system, the BREX system thwarts phage DNA replication. However, it diverges from the R–M system in its mechanism of action. The R–M system consists of modification enzymes, typically methyltransferases, and restriction endonucleases. These modification enzymes are capable of methylating specific palindromic sequences within bacterial DNA, thereby shielding it from cleavage by the restriction endonucleases (10,11). In contrast, restriction endonucleases target and cleave unmethylated foreign DNA, precluding the internalization and infection by exogenous genetic material (12).

The BREX system, composed of six genes, includes the methyltransferase BrxX as a key component. BrxX can recognize and modify single-stranded, asymmetric DNA motifs in the bacterial genome. Notably, the type I BREX system in Bacillus cereus and Escherichia coli has been shown to methylate adenine at position 5 in the 5′-TAGGAG-3′ and 5′-GGTAAG-3′ sites in their own DNA, respectively (8,13). Unlike the R–M system, BREX does not facilitate the cleavage or degradation of phage DNA in cells expressing it (8). Thus, BREX represents a unique phage restriction mechanism that bypasses nucleic acid cleavage enzymes, and its mechanism of action, which prevents phage genome replication, remains to be fully elucidated. Studies have shown that the BREX system can render phage DNA nonreplicative, conferring resistance to phage replication (8,13).

Bacteria deploy diverse defense mechanisms to shield themselves from phage assaults. Conversely, phages have evolved multifaceted strategies to counter host defenses, propelled by their rapid proliferation rates and genomic adaptability (14). Among them, anti-CRISPR (Acr) proteins, encoded by phages, have been a focus of extensive research. These proteins can impede the assembly of CRISPR–Cas complexes, obstruct target binding, and prevent the cleavage and degradation of cyclic oligonucleotide signaling molecules (15). In addition, phages encode anti-restriction proteins that disrupt the R–M system either by directly interacting with restriction enzymes (16) or by concealing restriction sites (17). For example, in the type I R–M system, phage-encoded analog DNA proteins bind to the restriction complex and prevent its binding to unmodified recognition sites in phage DNA (16,18). This interferes with DNA binding and methylation complexes. The typical protein of this type is Ocr (Overcoming classical restriction), which is the product of the lysophage T7 gene 0.3. The 117-amino-acid protein forms a dimer, with the shape and surface charge distribution of each monomer mimicking a helical coil of double-stranded DNA (19). Studies have revealed that Ocr specifically inhibits the BREX system during T7 phage infection, leading to a significant reduction in host genome methylation. T7 phages lacking in Ocr, as well as those with mutations in the Ocr dimer interface, have been observed to lose their resistance to the type I BREX system (20). This suggests that Ocr can specifically inhibit the activity of BrxX methyltransferase, a central component of the BREX system. However, the precise molecular mechanism by which Ocr exerts this inhibitory effect on BrxX methyltransferase activity remains to be elucidated.

This study represents the inaugural in vitro characterization of the core methyltransferase BrxX activity within the BREX system of E. coli. Our findings unveil the S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) concentration-dependent enzymatic activity of BrxX. Furthermore, we establish Ocr as a potent competitive inhibitor of BrxX, forming a stable complex with this methyltransferase. Notably, employing cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) single-particle technology, we delineate the high-resolution structure of the Ocr–BrxX complex. Through rigorous structural and biochemical analyses, we ascertain that Ocr engages with diverse strains of the BREX core methyltransferase BrxX in a conserved binding mode. This binding modality extends across all BREX system core component BrxX methyltransferases, underscoring phages’ adoption of an efficient strategy to counter BREX systems.

The methods used to express the target genes and purify the proteins are consistent with those previously reported (21). For the expression of target protein, the plasmids were transformed into E. coli BL21 strains and cultured in Luria–Bertani (LB) supplemented with 100 μg/ml kanamycin at 37°C. When the OD600 reached 0.4–0.8, 0.5 mM isopropyl β-d-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) was added to induce expression for 20 h at 16°C. Cell pellets were harvested by centrifugation and resuspended in 20 ml lysis buffer [25 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0), 5% (v/v) glycerol, 200 mM NaCl, 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF), 5 mM imidazole]. A low-temperature ultra-high-pressure continuous flow cell disrupter was then used to lyse cell pellets at 4°C and the lysate was clarified by centrifugation for 1 h at 4°C and 20 000 rpm. The clarified supernatant was added 1.5 ml Ni-NTA agarose resin and incubated for 1 h at 4°C. To initially purify the target protein, the resin was then loaded onto a 10-ml column and washed with 15 ml lysis buffer. Then, protein with His tag was eluted by 15 ml elution buffer [25 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0), 5% (v/v) glycerol, 200 mM NaCl, 1 mM PMSF, 250 mM imidazole]. Pooled elution fractions were concentrated for buffer exchange with low-salt buffer [25 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0), 50 mM NaCl, 5% (v/v) glycerol, 2 mM dithiothreitol (DTT)] at 4°C three times, using 15-ml 30-kDa (3 kDa for Ocr) Amicon® Ultra centrifugal filters. An ÄKTA Pure fast protein liquid chromatography system was subsequently used to facilitate the concentrates to pass through a 5-ml HiTrap Q HP anion exchange chromatography column (Cytiva) in low-salt buffer [25 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0), 50 mM NaCl, 5% (v/v) glycerol, 2 mM DTT] at 4°C. A gradient of 0.05–1.0 M NaCl was used to elute the target protein. Fractions with target protein were pooled and concentrated to below 500 μl at 4°C using 15-ml 30-kDa (3 kDa for Ocr) Amicon® Ultra centrifugal filters. The sample was then subjected to further purification using a Superdex 200 Increase 10/300 GL column (GE Healthcare) in buffer [25 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0), 50 mM NaCl, 5% (v/v) glycerol, 2 mM DTT] at 4°C. Based on the analysis by sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS–PAGE), target protein was collected and concentrated to 10 mg/ml using 4-ml 30-kDa (3 kDa for Ocr) Amicon® Ultra centrifugal filters. Masks around one flank region and the rest region were, respectively, generated in Chimera using a filtered consensus map as a template and the volume segger tool (v2.5.3). No signal subtraction of particles was used in local refinement as the overall density distribution of those generated maps was worse as judged by visually inspecting. The two locally refined maps were aligned according to the consensus map and stitched together to generate a composite map in Chimera (28) for the subsequent model building.Model building and refinementThe atomic model of BrxX generated by AlphaFold2 (29) and the crystal structure of Ocr (PDB ID: 1S7Z) with water molecules and other metal ions deleted were used as the templates. All the models were fitted into the composite map as rigid bodies in Chimera and adjusted into the density using Coot (30). Then, the model was real-space refined in Phenix (v1.20.1) (31) using Ramachandran and secondary structure restraints and manually modified in Coot. The process was repeated for several iterations until all parameters were reasonably refined as validated by MolProbity (32). The figures were generated in cryoSPARC or created by Chimera, ChimeraX (33). All data and model statistics are reported in Supplementary Table S3.

Cryo-EM image processingAll the image processing steps were performed by using cryoSPARC v4 (27). After screening, 19 036 images were selected for the subsequent processing. At the very beginning, 1263 particles were manually picked from 32 micrographs and extracted with a box size of 480 × 480 pixels. 2D classification was performed to obtain particles presenting better structural details and different orientations, which were used as templates for the topaz training procedure to perform auto-picking. A total of 1 007 930 particles were then automatically selected. To speed up the data processing, all particles were binned by 2, yielding a pixel size of 1.72 Å with a box size of 240 × 240 pixels. After several rounds of 2D classification to clean the data set, 609 475 particles were retained to perform the ab initio reconstruction into three models and the subsequent heterogeneous refinement. Only one class representing the best overall density distribution was submitted to a homogeneous refinement. A total of 239 863 raw particles were then imported to improve the resolution, yielding a map with a global resolution of 2.98 Å as estimated by gold-standard Fourier shell correlation at a 0.143 threshold. As the two ‘flank’ regions of BrxX seemed somewhat flexible with one side presenting partially missing density and the other side showing very low quality density, local refinement was, respectively, performed to improve the quality of the densities in both the ‘flank’ and center regions. No signal subtraction of particles was used in local refinement as the overall density distribution of those generated maps was worse as judged by visually inspecting. The two locally refined maps were aligned according to the consensus map and stitched together to generate a composite map in Chimera (28) for the subsequent model building.

ResultsCharacterization of BrxX as a core methyltransferase in BREX systemsBrxX, the central element of all BREX systems, is tasked with the methylation of host-specific DNA motifs (Figure 1A). This capability enables the system to discern between self and non-self DNA, consequently preventing the replication of foreign phage DNA. Beyond its role in phage defense (e.g. R–M, pgl), BrxX and other site-specific DNA methyltransferases are also crucial in modulating essential bacterial life processes, including chromosome replication, DNA repair and gene expression (34,35). We conducted a comparative analysis of the functional motifs present in BrxX from E. coli, B. cereus and other class I methyltransferases (MTases), including Dam, an α-group member (36), CcrM, a β-group methyltransferase (37), and CamA, classified under the γ-group (38,39). Besides, a salt bridge forms between His48 (Ocr) and Asp1070 (BrxX) and a π–π interaction can be found between Tyr49 (Ocr) and Tyr1045 (BrxX). Taken together, all these noncovalent interactions assist Ocr in firmly binding within the DNA binding cleft of the two E-BrxX molecules, providing the structural basis for Ocr’s specific interaction with E-BrxX.

DiscussionIn recent years, the issue of bacterial drug resistance has escalated (45). Bacteriophages, as natural antibacterial agents, offer a promising avenue for targeting various bacteria for lysis, thus presenting bacteriophage therapy as a potential solution to address bacterial drug resistance (46,47). However, the widespread adoption of phage therapy faces obstacles due to the presence of multiple antagonistic phage defense systems in bacteria. Interestingly, phages can counter bacterial defense mechanisms by expressing diverse antagonistic factors, including Acr (48), Acb (49) and Gad1 (50). The discovery and investigation of these antagonistic factors enhance our understanding of the intricate antagonistic interplay between phages and bacteria, thereby facilitating the broader application of phage therapy.The BREX system represents a widely distributed defense mechanism among bacteria, employing the core component, the methyltransferase BrxX, to discern self from non-self and thereby impede the replication of untagged phage genomes, thus providing protection against phage infection. The BREX system targets specific DNA motifs in a similar way to R–M systems. However, there are notable differences between the two. First, during phage infection of BREX+ cells, the phage genome was not cleaved or degraded, in contrast to R–M systems (8). Second, in classical type II R–M systems, the palindromic nature of recognition sites ensures that epigenetic protection modifications are heritable (51,52). In contrast, in the BREX system, recognition sites are nonpalindromic, resulting in methylation of one DNA strand during bacterial genome replication. This implies the presence of a unique mechanism in the BREX system to distinguish nonmethylated phage DNA from bacterial progeny DNA, which may require other components within the system to act synergistically.Bacteriophages have evolved a variety of strategies to subvert host defense mechanisms. The T7 bacteriophage gene 0.3 encodes a protein termed Ocr, which is the first protein expressed following T7 infection of E. coli host cells (53). Ocr, existing as a dimeric and acidic entity, has been characterized as a DNA mimic (54). Upon invasion, Ocr is rapidly synthesized and mimetically binds to specific host DNA-binding proteins. It effectively neutralizes the type I R–M enzymes of the host (43), thereby preventing the cleavage of the invading DNA, which in turn facilitates the replication of the remaining bacteriophage DNA. Similar to the R–M system, Ocr can also inhibit the defense function of the BREX system. However, unlike the R–M system, not all DNA mimic proteins (e.g. ArdA, ArdB, etc.) can effectively inhibit the defense function of the BREX system (20,55). This indicates specificity in the interaction between Ocr and the BREX system. Structural analysis of the Ocr–BrxX complex has elucidated particular interactions, including hydrogen bonds, salt bridges and π–π interaction, which being critical for Ocr’s capacity selectively disable the defense functions of the BREX system. Interestingly, the product of the T3 bacteriophage 0.3 gene, SAMase, has been demonstrated to inactivate both the R–M and BREX defense systems by depleting intracellular SAM levels in host cells (42,56,57). This mechanism suggests that bacteriophages employ an efficient and sophisticated strategy that rapidly exploits the host’s synthetic machinery to produce proteins such as Ocr and SAMase. These proteins act as antagonists in the early stages of infection, effectively neutralizing the host’s defense systems soon after invasion. This subversion is critical in facilitating the replication and proliferation of the bacteriophage.BrxX is a pivotal component of the BREX system, responsible for distinguishing between self and non-self DNA. We conducted a sequence alignment comparison of 68 BrxX sequences from Gram-negative bacteria, Gram-positive bacteria and archaea, revealing limited overall similarity across these domains of life (data not shown). Despite this variability, a conserved SAM-binding motif (GSG) and a methyltransferase catalytic motif (NPYY) are consistently observed. The presence of the NPYY motif implies a potential role for BrxX in the specific N6-methyladenine (N6-mA) modification, as suggested by recent studies (40,41,58). Within the same species, BrxX shows remarkable homology at both the N- and C-termini. The N-terminal conservation is associated with regions critical for SAM binding and methyltransferase activity, whereas the function of the highly conserved C-terminus remains elusive. Given the synergistic interactions between BrxX and other components, we speculate that the conservation of the C-terminus may facilitate interactions with other components. Interestingly, we found significant heterogeneity in the TRD regions of these BrxX molecules, corresponding to the ability of BrxX from different strains to specifically recognize differently methylated DNA motifs. This property also allows for the engineering modifications of BrxX molecules for specific recognition and methylation of particular DNA motifs. Collectively, these findings indicate that while BrxX sequences from different strains maintain a high degree of consistency in their catalytic activity, they exhibit diversity in their recognition functions, which is crucial for the discriminatory capacity of the BREX system.In conclusion, our study has delineated the molecular basis of substrate DNA recognition and methylation by E. coli BrxX. Using in vitro biochemical assays and structural biology methods, we have elucidated the mechanism by which Ocr abrogates BrxX methyltransferase activity. Our results highlight the sophisticated countermeasures of the bacteriophage against the bacterial BREX defense system, using Ocr as a DNA mimic. This insight sheds light on the strategies that bacteriophages can use to neutralize complex bacterial defense mechanisms.This discovery provides a fundamental framework for understanding the nuanced ways in which bacteriophages can disrupt and override the diverse anti-phage systems found in bacteria. However, unresolved questions relate to the specific mechanisms of inhibition of foreign genome replication by the BREX system and the potential conformational effects of Ocr binding to BrxX, which may impair the activity of the BREX functional complex. Further research is required to address these issues, including in vivo functional assays, in vitro biochemical studies and structural characterization of the BREX multi-protein complex.

The discovery and investigation of these antagonistic factors enhance our understanding of the intricate antagonistic interplay between phages and bacteria, thereby facilitating the broader application of phage therapy.

The BREX system represents a widely distributed defense mechanism among bacteria, employing the core component, the methyltransferase BrxX, to discern self from non-self and thereby impede the replication of untagged phage genomes, thus providing protection against phage infection. The BREX system targets specific DNA motifs in a similar way to R–M systems. However, there are notable differences between the two. First, during phage infection of BREX+ cells, the phage genome was not cleaved or degraded, in contrast to R–M systems (8). Second, in classical type II R–M systems, the palindromic nature of recognition sites ensures that epigenetic protection modifications are heritable (51,52). In contrast, in the BREX system, recognition sites are nonpalindromic, resulting in methylation of one DNA strand during bacterial genome replication. This implies the presence of a unique mechanism in the BREX system to distinguish nonmethylated phage DNA from bacterial progeny DNA, which may require other components within the system to act synergistically.

Bacteriophages have evolved a variety of strategies to subvert host defense mechanisms. The T7 bacteriophage gene 0.3 encodes a protein termed Ocr, which is the first protein expressed following T7 infection of E. coli host cells (53). Ocr, existing as a dimeric and acidic entity, has been characterized as a DNA mimic (54). Upon invasion, Ocr is rapidly synthesized and mimetically binds to specific host DNA-binding proteins. It effectively neutralizes the type I R–M enzymes of the host (43), thereby preventing the cleavage of the invading DNA, which in turn facilitates the replication of the remaining bacteriophage DNA. Similar to the R–M system, Ocr can also inhibit the defense function of the BREX system. However, unlike the R–M system, not all DNA mimic proteins (e.g. ArdA, ArdB, etc.) can effectively inhibit the defense function of the BREX system (20,55). This indicates specificity in the interaction between Ocr and the BREX system. Structural analysis of the Ocr–BrxX complex has elucidated particular interactions, including hydrogen bonds, salt bridges and π–π interaction, which being critical for Ocr’s capacity selectively disable the defense functions of the BREX system. Interestingly, the product of the T3 bacteriophage 0.3 gene, SAMase, has been demonstrated to inactivate both the R–M and BREX defense systems by depleting intracellular SAM levels in host cells (42,56,57). This mechanism suggests that bacteriophages employ an efficient and sophisticated strategy that rapidly exploits the host’s synthetic machinery to produce proteins such as Ocr and SAMase. These proteins act as antagonists in the early stages of infection, effectively neutralizing the host’s defense systems soon after invasion. This subversion is critical in facilitating the replication and proliferation of the bacteriophage.

BrxX is a pivotal component of the BREX system, responsible for distinguishing between self and non-self DNA. We conducted a sequence alignment comparison of 68 BrxX sequences from Gram-negative bacteria, Gram-positive bacteria and archaea, revealing limited overall similarity across these domains of life (data not shown). Despite this variability, a conserved SAM-binding motif (GSG) and a methyltransferase catalytic motif (NPYY) are consistently observed. The presence of the NPYY motif implies a potential role for BrxX in the specific N6-methyladenine (N6-mA) modification, as suggested by recent studies (40,41,58). Within the same species, BrxX shows remarkable homology at both the N- and C-termini. The N-terminal conservation is associated with regions critical for SAM binding and methyltransferase activity, whereas the function of the highly conserved C-terminus remains elusive. Given the synergistic interactions between BrxX and other components, we speculate that the conservation of the C-terminus may facilitate interactions with other components. Interestingly, we found significant heterogeneity in the TRD regions of these BrxX molecules, corresponding to the ability of BrxX from different strains to specifically recognize differently methylated DNA motifs. This property also allows for the engineering modifications of BrxX molecules for specific recognition and methylation of particular DNA motifs. Collectively, these findings indicate that while BrxX sequences from different strains maintain a high degree of consistency in their catalytic activity, they exhibit diversity in their recognition functions, which is crucial for the discriminatory capacity of the BREX system.

In conclusion, our study has delineated the molecular basis of substrate DNA recognition and methylation by E. coli BrxX. Using in vitro biochemical assays and structural biology methods, we have elucidated the mechanism by which Ocr abrogates BrxX methyltransferase activity. Our results highlight the sophisticated countermeasures of the bacteriophage against the bacterial BREX defense system, using Ocr as a DNA mimic. This insight sheds light on the strategies that bacteriophages can use to neutralize complex bacterial defense mechanisms.

This discovery provides a fundamental framework for understanding the nuanced ways in which bacteriophages can disrupt and override the diverse anti-phage systems found in bacteria. However, unresolved questions relate to the specific mechanisms of inhibition of foreign genome replication by the BREX system and the potential conformational effects of Ocr binding to BrxX, which may impair the activity of the BREX functional complex. Further research is required to address these issues, including in vivo functional assays, in vitro biochemical studies and structural characterization of the BREX multi-protein complex.
