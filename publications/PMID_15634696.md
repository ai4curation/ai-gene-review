# Transcriptional program controlled by the floral homeotic gene AGAMOUS during early organogenesis.
**Authors:** Gómez-Mena C, de Folter S, Costa MM, Angenent GC, Sablowski R
**Journal:** Development (2005)
**DOI:** [10.1242/dev.01600](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.01600)

## Abstract

1. Development. 2005 Feb;132(3):429-38. doi: 10.1242/dev.01600. Epub 2005 Jan 5.

Transcriptional program controlled by the floral homeotic gene AGAMOUS during 
early organogenesis.

Gómez-Mena C(1), de Folter S, Costa MM, Angenent GC, Sablowski R.

Author information:
(1)Department of Cell and Developmental Biology, John Innes Centre, Norwich NR4 
7UH, UK.

Floral organs, whose identity is determined by specific combinations of homeotic 
genes, originate from a group of undifferentiated cells called the floral 
meristem. In Arabidopsis, the homeotic gene AGAMOUS (AG) terminates meristem 
activity and promotes development of stamens and carpels. To understand the 
program of gene expression activated by AG, we followed genome-wide expression 
during early stamen and carpel development. The AG target genes included most 
genes for which mutant screens revealed a function downstream of AG. Novel 
targets were validated by in situ hybridisation and binding to AG in vitro and 
in vivo. Transcription factors formed a large fraction of AG targets, suggesting 
that during early organogenesis, much of the genetic program is concerned with 
elaborating gene expression patterns. The results also suggest that AG and other 
homeotic proteins with which it interacts (SEPALLATA3, APETALA3, PISTILLATA) are 
coordinately regulated in a positive-feedback loop to maintain their own 
expression, and that AG activates biosynthesis of gibberellin, which has been 
proposed to promote the shift from meristem identity to differentiation.
