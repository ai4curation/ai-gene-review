The exocyst complex is an evolutionarily conserved multisubunit protein complex 
implicated in tethering secretory vesicles to the plasma membrane. Originally 
identified two decades ago in budding yeast, investigations using several 
different eukaryotic systems have since made great progress toward determination 
of the overall structure and organization of the eight exocyst subunits. Studies 
point to a critical role for the complex as a spatiotemporal regulator through 
the numerous protein and lipid interactions of its subunits, although a 
molecular understanding of exocyst function has been challenging to elucidate. 
Recent progress demonstrates that the exocyst is also important for additional 
trafficking steps and cellular processes beyond exocytosis, with links to 
development and disease. In this review, we discuss current knowledge of exocyst 
architecture, assembly, regulation and its roles in a variety of cellular 
trafficking pathways.
