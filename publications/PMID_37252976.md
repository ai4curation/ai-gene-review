Defining all sites for a post-translational modification in the cell, and 
identifying their upstream modifying enzymes, is essential for a complete 
understanding of a modification's function. However, the complete mapping of a 
modification in the proteome and definition of its associated enzyme-substrate 
network is rarely achieved. Here, we present the protein methylation network for 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae. Through a formal process of defining and quantifying 
all potential sources of incompleteness, for both the methylation sites in the 
proteome and also protein methyltransferases, we prove that this protein 
methylation network is now near-complete. It contains 33 methylated proteins and 
28 methyltransferases, comprising 44 enzyme-substrate relationships, and a 
predicted further three enzymes. While the precise molecular function of most 
methylation sites is unknown, and it remains possible that other sites and 
enzymes remain undiscovered, the completeness of this protein modification 
network is unprecedented and allows us to holistically explore the role and 
evolution of protein methylation in the eukaryotic cell. We show that while no 
single protein methylation event is essential in yeast, the vast majority of 
methylated proteins are themselves essential, being primarily involved in the 
core cellular processes of transcription, RNA processing, and translation. This 
suggests that protein methylation in lower eukaryotes exists to fine-tune 
proteins whose sequences are evolutionarily constrained, providing an 
improvement in the efficiency of their cognate processes. The approach described 
here, for the construction and evaluation of post-translational modification 
networks and their constituent enzymes and substrates, defines a formal process 
of utility for other post-translational modifications.

## Full Text

The mapping of protein-modifying enzymes to their corresponding substrates is critical. It helps decipher functional roles within the cell, providing information about the regulation, evolution, and mechanisms of post-translational modifications. Such mapping also allows the construction of intracellular enzyme–substrate networks, the best known of which are phosphorylation-driven signaling pathways. While proteomic analyses have discovered thousands of protein modifications, and many modifying enzymes can be predicted from genomes, most protein modification enzyme–substrate networks are largely incomplete. This is both because the vast majority of protein modification sites have no known upstream enzyme, and also because many modifying enzymes have no known protein substrate. In the human phosphorylation network, for example, more than 95% of phosphorylation sites have no known kinase, and over 150 kinases have no known substrates (1). The incompleteness of enzyme–substrate relationships precludes systematic and systems-level insight into the roles of different protein modifications.

Protein methylation is an important modification which regulates numerous cellular processes, including signaling, transcription, RNA splicing, and translation (2–6). It predominantly occurs on lysine and arginine residues, although it also occurs elsewhere, including on histidine and glutamine sidechains and at the N- and C-termini of proteins (7–9). Protein methylation is catalyzed by methyltransferases, all which use the universal methyl donor S-adenosyl-L-methionine (AdoMet) (7, 10–12). There are two main classes of protein methyltransferases based on their core methyltransferase fold: seven-beta-strand (7βS) and Su(var)3-9, Enhancer-of-zeste, Trithorax (SET) domain enzymes. A small number of protein methyltransferases with other domain types, such as the SpoU-TrmD (SPOUT) domain, also exist (13). SET domain enzymes invariably methylate proteins, while 7βS enzymes methylate proteins and also other biomolecules, including RNA, DNA, lipids, and metabolites (10).

Here we formally prove that the protein methylation system in the model yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae is near-complete, with almost all methylation sites and cognate methyltransferases being now known. We show that yeast has few protein methyltransferases which remain putative, that almost all methyltransferases have just one target amino acid in one substrate protein, and that very few high-confidence methylation sites in the yeast proteome remain with no known upstream methyltransferase. In the same way that the first complete genomes were exceptional achievements, the definition of a protein methylation network is an important landmark as few if any enzyme–substrate networks for protein modifications have been completely defined. While the precise molecular function of most methylation sites remains unknown, the network completeness allows new system-level insights to be generated into the roles and evolution of protein methylation in eukaryotes, which are explained below. Finally, we outline potential sources of biases that may affect our definition and understanding of the yeast methylation network.

Why the Yeast Protein Methylation Network Is Near-CompleteA Formal Process to Evaluate Network Completeness and Incompleteness for Protein Post-translational Modifications.In order to formally evaluate the completeness of the yeast protein methylation network, we will first make some observations about post-translational modification enzyme-substrate networks (referred to as “modification networks” from here on) and sources of their incompleteness. Networks are comprised of nodes and edges. In a modification network, nodes are either modifying enzymes or their substrate proteins (which may be modified at one or more residues), whereas the edges represent directional relationships between modifying enzymes and their substrate proteins. Theoretically complete modification networks, by definition, are those wherein every modifying enzyme has at least one substrate, and each substrate has at least one responsible enzyme. That is, each node must have at least one edge connecting it to another node—there can be no “orphan” nodes that are present. Any incompleteness in a modification network can therefore be attributed to unknown edges (i.e., relationships between enzymes and substrates). However, these unknown edges may involve either known or unknown nodes (i.e., known or unknown enzymes or modified proteins). For this reason, there can only be four sources of incompleteness in a modification network (Fig. 1): 1) an unknown relationship between a known enzyme and a known substrate protein; 2) an unknown relationship between an unknown enzyme and a known substrate protein; 3) an unknown relationship between a known enzyme and an unknown substrate protein; and 4) an unknown relationship between an unknown enzyme and an unknown substrate protein. Therefore, the completeness of a modification network can be formally determined by exploring the extent of network incompleteness via these four sources.Fig. 1.Types of incompleteness in post-translational modification enzyme–substrate networks. The most basic element in a modification network is a single protein-modifying enzyme (blue circle) targeting a single substrate (red diamond). There are four sources of incompleteness in an enzyme-substrate network: (1) an unknown relationship between a known enzyme and a known substrate; (2) an unknown relationship between an unknown enzyme and a known substrate; (3) an unknown relationship between a known enzyme and an unknown substrate; and (4) an unknown relationship between an unknown enzyme and an unknown substrate. Unknown elements are indicated by dotted outlines.In the following sections, we will apply this logic to the yeast protein methylation network. We will make observations which, collectively, indicate minimal incompleteness in the yeast methylation network in all four sources of incompleteness. This indicates that the network is near-complete. We then make predictions on unknowns which will likely complete the network.Yeast Protein Methyltransferases Are Nearly Fully Discovered.The first observation we make is that there are few putative protein methyltransferases remaining in yeast. There are approximately 81 methyltransferases in the yeast genome that target proteins, RNA, and small molecules (50). These have been defined through sequence analysis, as all methyltransferases use the same methyl donor, AdoMet, and thus must have a binding site for this co-factor. Equally, all methyltransferases belong to a small number of domain types—mostly SET, SPOUT, and 7βS—which can be discovered through sequence analysis. Remarkably, of these 81 methyltransferases in yeast, only 13 have no assigned substrate (Table 2). The majority of these putative methyltransferases are predicted to methylate RNA, and one enzyme, YKL155C, has been shown to methylate tRNAs in vitro (Table 2). Only five methyltransferases are predicted to target proteins: Set3, Set4, Set6, Mtf1, and YNL092W. Of these, enzymes Set6, Mtf1, and YNL092W are likely to be active methyltransferases. This is because Set3 and Set4 appear to lack methyltransferase activity due to amino acid substitutions at key AdoMet-binding residues (51), similar to their human homologs MLL5 and SETD5 (52, 53). Of these three putative methyltransferases, the most likely protein methyltransferase is Set6, as the SET domain invariably methylates proteins. Mtf1 is a component of the mitochondrial RNA polymerase complex and has structural homology to rRNA methyltransferases. However, as no rRNA methyltransferase activity has been detected for Mtf1 (54), it may in fact be a protein methyltransferase. Lastly, evidence for the substrate specificity of YNL092W remains inconclusive. YNL092W catalyzes methylation of carosine in vitro, and this is in agreement with its mammalian homologs (55). However, yeast does not natively produce carosine, suggesting that this is not its bona fide activity. YNL092W has been shown to methylate histidine in short di- and tripeptides, and together with further structural evidence, it was suggested that YNL092W may in fact be a protein histidine methyltransferase (55, 56). Supporting its potential as a protein methyltransferase, YNL092W has been reported to have automethylation activity in vitro (57). However, no methylation site on YNL092W has been identified, and it remains possible this observed activity was in fact off-target methylation of the hexahistidine tag on the recombinant YNL092W protein. A similar off-target automethylation was observed for hexahistidine-tagged human histidine methyltransferase METTL9 (58). Furthermore, it is unclear what histidine(s) YNL092W would methylate given that knockout of the only known histidine methyltransferase in yeast, Hpm1, appears to abolish all histidine methylation in the cell (59). Overall, there are therefore very few putative protein methyltransferases in yeast—Set6 is most likely to be a protein methyltransferase while YNL092W and Mtf1 may also target proteins. If they are in fact protein methyltransferases, they are most likely to target as-yet undiscovered methylation sites (see below). Given that there are currently 28 known yeast protein methyltransferases, these three putative members make up a very small proportion. Most of the enzymes are highly specific, with more than 75% targeting a single amino acid in a single protein (Table 1) (5, 64). This occurs because many protein methyltransferases appear to recognize their substrates in a structural context, especially those belonging to the 7βS family (Table 1) (11), which limits their capacity to target multiple substrate proteins. Indeed, only three methyltransferases in yeast are known to recognize a linear sequence motif in their substrates: the arginine methyltransferase Hmt1, the N-terminal methyltransferase Ntm1 and the lysine methyltransferase Efm1. Efm1 recognizes a very specific motif to methylate lysine 30 in eEF1A and is therefore unlikely to have additional substrates (65). Ntm1 recognizes an N-terminal A/P/S-P-K motif (33). Two ribosomal proteins which contain this motif, Rps25 and Rpl12, are known to be methylated by Ntm1 (33). Two further proteins, Rpt1 and Hsp31, have been shown to be methylated at their N-termini which contain this motif (34, 35) and are therefore likely Ntm1 substrates. Beyond this, there are a further six proteins whose N-termini match the recognition motif of Ntm1 and who colocalize with Ntm1 in the cytoplasm—Afg2, Art10, Lsg1, New1, Ola1, and Tma46; these may be additional Ntm1 substrates. Lastly, Hmt1 is by far the most non-specific protein methyltransferase and recognizes arginines within Arg-Gly/Arg-Gly-Gly (RG/RGG) motifs (65, 66). Many substrate proteins have been described for Hmt1, and there are a number of putative substrates that are known to be in vivo methylated at RG/RGG motifs, but for which direct evidence of Hmt1 activity is yet to be shown—these are almost certainly Hmt1 substrates (Table 1). Importantly, some proteins with multiple RG/RGG motifs have been shown to have every motif methylated in vivo (67).Because most protein methyltransferases have a single, structurally determined substrate and methyltransferases that recognize motifs have those motifs and thus their substrates well defined, this indicates minimal network incompleteness via sources (1) and (3) (Fig. 1), which require known methyltransferases to have additional known (1) or unknown (3) substrates.It should be noted that there is essentially no redundancy between methyltransferases in the yeast methylation network. In contrast to human, there are no known methylation sites that are targeted by more than one methyltransferase. The only exception is the recently reported methylation of histone H3 K37 by both Set1 and Set2 (43) (Table 1); however, methylation at H3 K37 has not been confirmed by mass spectrometry and heavy methyl Stable Isotope Labeling by Amino acids in Cell culture (SILAC) (68). The lack of redundancy among yeast methyltransferases is supported by the fact that individual deletions of these enzymes result in the complete loss of their cognate methylation site(s). Importantly, this has been shown for all yeast methyltransferases except Efm1, Hsl7, and Set5. In general, the lack of redundancy in the yeast methylation network means that methylation sites with a known, cognate methyltransferase are unlikely to be methylated by any other methyltransferase. This therefore indicates minimal yeast methylation network incompleteness via sources (1) and (2) (Fig. 1), which require methylation sites with known methyltransferases to be catalyzed by additional known (1) or unknown (2) methyltransferase(s). In contrast, yet through the use of similar techniques, the S. cerevisiae methylproteome has been defined as limited. Nine large-scale studies have collectively reported 286 unique methylation sites (Table 3). However, there is poor overlap in the sites reported by these nine studies (Fig. 2). Where studies do agree, it is striking that the methylation sites either have a known methyltransferase or are highly likely to be a substrate of Hmt1, where sites are in an RG/RGG motif (Fig. 2). By contrast, sites described in just a single study, and for which no probable methyltransferase is known, are unlikely to be bone fide methylation sites. In combination with the argument, above, that there are few putative methyltransferases in yeast, we therefore posit that the yeast methylproteome, being the methylated proteins and sites of methylation, is also nearly completely defined. The exceptions to this are likely to be additional Hmt1 substrates, within disordered RG/RGG motifs, and additional Ntm1 substrates, at N-terminal A/P/S-P-K motifs. Any undiscovered methylation sites are thus very likely to be on arginines or protein N-termini, rather than on lysines, and future attempts to discover novel protein methylation sites in yeast should account for this. Only the NOP1 gene for H2A glutamine methyltransferase is essential, where Nop1 is also a substrate of the Hmt1 methyltransferase. It remains to be determined, however, if Nop1 protein methyltransferase activity is essential, or if its essentiality is related to other functions, such as its methylation of rRNA (79). The nonessentiality of protein methyltransferases consequently means that no single methylation site is required for yeast growth under laboratory conditions. For yeast, this includes the methylation of histones. Combinations of methyltransferase knockouts may have more pronounced phenotypes, but it is also notable that no pair of yeast protein methyltransferases are known to be synthetically lethal except for Hsl7 and Set1 (80, 81). Remarkably, a yeast strain with deletions of all five methyltransferases targeting the translation factor eEF1A is viable (82).In contrast to the dispensable nature of yeast methyltransferases, the majority of methylated proteins in yeast are essential (Table 1 and SI Appendix, Table S1). Including synthetic lethality between paralogs, approximately two-thirds of methyltransferase substrates are required for yeast cell viability. This reflects the fact that almost all these proteins are involved in core cellular processes of transcription, RNA processing or translation (see below). The contrast between the essentiality of methylated proteins and the non-essentiality of their methylation or cognate methyltransferases is striking. It suggests that methylation may largely have a fine-tuning function (21, 82, 83), serving to slightly modify the structure and improve the function of essential proteins which are functionally constrained and cannot undergo further or significant sequence variation. While any improvement to a process from methylation may be small, changes in the efficiency or accuracy of processes such as translation will provide a fitness advantage over evolutionary time.What Biological Processes Employ Protein Methylation?The yeast protein methylation system is almost entirely confined to central and highly conserved cellular processes. Nearly every methylated protein is involved in the expression of genes, whether at the level of RNA transcription and processing, or in the translation of mRNA into protein (Fig. 3). It is profound to note that two proteins of exceptional conservation and functional importance, being elongation factor 1A and histone H3, are methylated by five and three enzymes, respectively. This makes them the proteins in the eukaryotic cell that are targeted by the largest number of dedicated and distinct methyltransferases. For elongation factor 1A, its methyltransferases have largely evolved independently (5). It is equally profound to note that the S. cerevisiae methyltransferase with the most protein substrates, arginine methyltransferase Hmt1, exclusively acts on many proteins with related RNA-associated functions. The evolutionary drive for this is less clear but rests with the fact that the RG/RGG motif for this enzyme is frequently found in tandem repeats that are intrinsically disordered and can bind RNA (84). While the biological processes involving methylated proteins are known, it is important to note that the function of individual methylation sites—or combinations thereof—is largely unknown. While enzyme-substrate relationships are nearly fully defined for yeast methylation, detailed structural, mechanistic, and functional studies on core cellular processes will be required to uncover why these relationships evolved.As an interesting note, the “biological bias” of the yeast protein methylation system towards central and highly conserved processes will allow any proposed new methylation sites to be triaged. Putative methylation sites on proteins outside core processes should be appropriately scrutinized. Combining this with knowledge of the essentiality of substrate proteins, but the nonessentiality of methylation, will help the completion of a final, accurate yeast methylation network.How Did Protein Methyltransferases Evolve?The confinement of protein methylation to core processes contrasts with the diversity of methyltransferases that catalyze this modification. Yeast protein methyltransferases belong to many different methyltransferase families containing diverse fold types (Table 1). This indicates that methylation of proteins involved in core processes has been selected for numerous times during evolution (5). That is, yeast methyltransferases did not diverge from a common protein methyltransferase ancestor to methylate a group of similar proteins, but rather methyltransferases targeting proteins involved in similar processes evolved from different methyltransferase scaffolds and lineages. Many yeast protein methyltransferases are conserved in human (Table 1). However, the greater diversity of methyltransferase substrates in human suggests that protein methylation has taken on a more general cellular role in higher eukaryotes, and that the core methylation system, such as that seen in yeast, has been significantly embellished.A broader question is why is the human methylation system so dramatically expanded in comparison to yeast? The large number of lysine methylation events in human appears to be primarily driven by the fact that many histone lysine methyltransferases also target nonhistone proteins (85). Notably, there are at least 23 SET domain histone lysine methyltransferases in human (86), while there are only three in yeast (Table 1). There are also at least nine PRMTs in human, most of which have numerous substrates (4). Six of these PRMTs are type I enzymes which catalyze asymmetric dimethylation. In yeast, however, Hmt1 is the sole type I PRMT and appears responsible for all asymmetric dimethylation (87). However, and in contrast with enzymes of broader activity, there are at least a dozen nonhistone methyltransferases in human known to be highly specific (including METTL21A, VCP-KMT, METTL13, ETFB-KMT, eEF1A-KMT2, eEF1A-KMT3, METTL21C, eEF2-KMT, SETD3, CS-KMT, METTL18, ANT-KMT, and ATPSc-KMT). These enzymes are more analogous to the majority of yeast protein methyltransferases which are also highly specific and target nonhistone proteins. In fact, some of these human enzymes are direct yeast homologs (Table 1).How Prevalent Is Noncanonical Protein Methylation?Protein methylation canonically occurs on lysine or arginine residues. However, a significant proportion of the yeast methylation network is constituted of methylation at residues besides lysine and arginine. Methyltransferases that target histidine, glutamine or the N- or C-termini of proteins comprise more than 25% of yeast methyltransferases (Table 1). This highlights that methylation beyond lysine and arginine is more prevalent than perhaps otherwise considered and should be further investigated in other eukaryotes. In fact, it has recently become apparent that histidine methylation and methyltransferases are prevalent in human (58, 88–91). Furthermore, it should be considered that methyltransferases in non-model organisms may catalyze these noncanonical forms of methylation, rather than just lysine and arginine methylation. For example, the deep-branching eukaryotic parasite, Giardia duodenalis, lacks arginine methylation yet has N-terminal methylation (92).How Many Methyl Groups Are Added by Methyltransferases?Methyltransferases are often able to add multiple methyl groups to the same amino acid sidechain. Across the yeast methylation network 14 enzymes transfer only one methyl group to their substrate(s), six transfer up to two methyl groups and eight enzymes transfer up to three methyl groups (Table 1). Of the enzymes that transfer just a single methyl group, seven cannot add additional methyl groups as they methylate histidine, glutamine, protein C-termini or the delta-nitrogen of arginine. Interestingly, this leaves seven apparent monomethyltransferases, six lysine- and one arginine-specific, that could theoretically generate higher methylation states than are typically observed in vivo. In fact one such enzyme, the eEF1A lysine methyltransferase Efm6, was seen to generate trimethylation upon overexpression (25). Of the enzymes that catalyze dimethylation, three are restricted to this methylation state as they catalyze arginine methylation or N-terminal methylation at prolines. The three remaining apparent dimethyltransferases target lysine and therefore could also be capable of producing trimethylation. It remains unknown why many arginine or lysine methyltransferases do not produce higher methylation states, and whether these distinct methylation states have different functions, as is known for histone methylation (86). However, the diversity of methylation states seen in yeast is notable. For proteins with lysine, arginine or N-terminal methylation sites, methylation states may represent another dimension for the fine-tuning of protein function.Lessons for the Definition of Other Post-translational Modification Networks.Why can the yeast protein methylation network be fully defined while completion of other protein modification networks remains elusive? This may be because of features peculiar to the yeast protein methylation network. Specifically, 1) the yeast methylation network mostly contains highly specific enzymes that target one or a few substrates, but has very few enzymes with broad activity; 2) all protein methylation is enzyme-mediated; and 3) there is minimal “noise” in the yeast protein methylation system as sites are mostly of high stoichiometry. These three features of the methylation network in yeast are compared and contrasted with other modifications below.(1)As discussed above, the vast majority of yeast protein methyltransferases are highly specific—only Hmt1 appears to have a broad range of substrates. Many highly specific protein methyltransferases are also found in human (7, 11), but there are also a significant number of enzymes, mostly in the SET domain family, that have very broad specificity (2, 93). In contrast, most protein kinases recognize highly degenerate motifs and are therefore very nonspecific (94). There are, however, also some highly specific kinases, such as the elongation factor 2 kinase (95). In general, while modification networks usually contain both high- and low-specificity enzymes, the relative proportion of these will affect how “definable” the overall network is. The yeast methylation network is heavily skewed towards high-specificity enzymes, and this has made its full definition possible.(2)When considering all post-translational modifications, whether enzyme-mediated or not, full definition of a protein modification system can become more elusive. This is the case with lysine acetylation, and lysine acylations in general, which are now known to be largely nonenzyme-mediated (96, 97). This creates a disconnect between the modifying enzymes, which are more easily defined through genome sequence analysis, and the modification system in general. Protein methylation, on the other hand, appears to be completely enzyme-mediated. This restrains all systems of protein methylation to the activity of the more definable set of modifying enzyme genes.(3)Different types of post-translational modifications occur with differing stoichiometries (98). For example, protein phosphorylation and acetylation often occur at low stoichiometry (96, 99). Protein methylation, on the other hand, is often of high stoichiometry (100). Indeed, many methylation sites, especially those on the ribosome and translation factors, exist at essentially 100% stoichiometry under standard laboratory conditions (13, 26, 28, 33). This means that protein methylation systems are overall less “noisy” than other modification systems as there is a clearer distinction between proteins that are methylated and those that are not.Sources of Bias That May Affect Our Understanding of the Yeast Methylation NetworkWhile we have argued that the yeast methylation network is near-complete, there are a number of caveats and alternate interpretations of data which must be considered. Broadly speaking, there are three main sources of bias that may affect our understanding of the yeast methylation network. These may impact on our thesis that the network is near-complete. These sources involve research focus bias, experimental bias and technical bias.One research bias is that there has been little targeted follow-up on yeast methylation sites reported in large-scale mass spectrometry studies (Table 3). This means that sites which are likely to be false positives, due to their lack of cognate methyltransferase or lack of heavy methyl SILAC evidence, have not been definitively disproved. A further instance of research bias is that yeast protein methylation has not been studied as extensively as, for example, human protein methylation. It remains possible that the apparent smaller extent of protein methylation in yeast reflects this bias. In terms of experimental bias, we note that most studies on yeast methylation (for methyltransferase substrate and site discovery) have grown yeast in standard conditions. It therefore cannot be discounted that some methyltransferases catalyze additional, condition-specific sites that remain to be identified. Equally, the analysis of mass spectrometry data for methylation has typically searched for this modification on lysine and arginine, even though other types of protein methylation exist. Finally, a number of possible technical biases could also affect the notion of network completeness. The high abundance of most methylated proteins in yeast may suggest a potential bias in substrate discovery, whereby low abundance substrates of methyltransferases remain undetected. While a range of enrichment reagents exist for methylated lysines and arginines, these are imperfect and there are few or no reagents for other types of methylation. Additionally, methylation sites identified by large-scale mass spectrometry studies may reflect a technical bias associated with the use of trypsin for protein digestion (Table 3).Collectively, these sources of bias point to the value in further investigating the yeast methylation system in a range of biological conditions, and for the development and application of new reagents and protocols for protein methylation analysis in yeast. This will allow further critical assessment of our claims.

A Formal Process to Evaluate Network Completeness and Incompleteness for Protein Post-translational Modifications.In order to formally evaluate the completeness of the yeast protein methylation network, we will first make some observations about post-translational modification enzyme-substrate networks (referred to as “modification networks” from here on) and sources of their incompleteness. Networks are comprised of nodes and edges. In a modification network, nodes are either modifying enzymes or their substrate proteins (which may be modified at one or more residues), whereas the edges represent directional relationships between modifying enzymes and their substrate proteins. Theoretically complete modification networks, by definition, are those wherein every modifying enzyme has at least one substrate, and each substrate has at least one responsible enzyme. That is, each node must have at least one edge connecting it to another node—there can be no “orphan” nodes that are present. Any incompleteness in a modification network can therefore be attributed to unknown edges (i.e., relationships between enzymes and substrates). However, these unknown edges may involve either known or unknown nodes (i.e., known or unknown enzymes or modified proteins). For this reason, there can only be four sources of incompleteness in a modification network (Fig. 1): 1) an unknown relationship between a known enzyme and a known substrate protein; 2) an unknown relationship between an unknown enzyme and a known substrate protein; 3) an unknown relationship between a known enzyme and an unknown substrate protein; and 4) an unknown relationship between an unknown enzyme and an unknown substrate protein. Yeast Protein Methyltransferases Are Nearly Fully Discovered.The first observation we make is that there are few putative protein methyltransferases remaining in yeast. There are approximately 81 methyltransferases in the yeast genome that target proteins, RNA, and small molecules (50). These have been defined through sequence analysis, as all methyltransferases use the same methyl donor, AdoMet, and thus must have a binding site for this co-factor. Equally, all methyltransferases belong to a small number of domain types—mostly SET, SPOUT, and 7βS—which can be discovered through sequence analysis. Remarkably, of these 81 methyltransferases in yeast, only 13 have no assigned substrate (Table 2). A similar off-target automethylation was observed for hexahistidine-tagged human histidine methyltransferase METTL9 (58). Yeast Protein Methyltransferases Are Specific and Nonredundant.Further analysis of all known yeast protein methyltransferases reveals additional evidence for minimal incompleteness in the protein methylation network. Most of the enzymes are highly specific, with more than 75% targeting a single amino acid in a single protein (Table 1) (5, 64). Indeed, only three methyltransferases in yeast are known to recognize a linear sequence motif in their substrates: the arginine methyltransferase Hmt1, the N-terminal methyltransferase Ntm1 and the lysine methyltransferase Efm1. The Yeast Methylproteome Is Limited in Extent and Few Bona Fide Methylation Sites Have No Assigned Methyltransferase.Large-scale screens for methylation sites by mass spectrometry, enabled by immunoaffinity enrichment and stable isotope-labeling techniques, have revealed widespread methylation in the human proteome (69–71). In contrast, yet through the use of similar techniques, the S. cerevisiae methylproteome has been defined as limited. Nine large-scale studies have collectively reported 286 unique methylation sites (Table 3). However, there is poor overlap in the sites reported by these nine studies (Fig. 2). Much of this is likely due to the very high false discovery rate in methylation site discovery by mass spectrometry (68), an issue which was unknown in early studies. Where studies do agree, it is striking that the methylation sites either have a known methyltransferase or are highly likely to be a substrate of Hmt1, where sites are in an RG/RGG motif (Fig. 2). By contrast, sites described in just a single study, and for which no probable methyltransferase is known, are unlikely to be bone fide methylation sites. In combination with the argument, above, that there are few putative methyltransferases in yeast, we therefore posit that the yeast methylproteome, being the methylated proteins and sites of methylation, is also nearly completely defined. The Yeast Methylproteome Is Limited in Extent and Few Bona Fide Methylation Sites Have No Assigned Methyltransferase.

Large-scale screens for methylation sites by mass spectrometry, enabled by immunoaffinity enrichment and stable isotope-labeling techniques, have revealed widespread methylation in the human proteome (69–71). Where studies do agree, it is striking that the methylation sites either have a known methyltransferase or are highly likely to be a substrate of Hmt1, where sites are in an RG/RGG motif (Fig. 2). In combination with the argument, above, that there are few putative methyltransferases in yeast, we therefore posit that the yeast methylproteome, being the methylated proteins and sites of methylation, is also nearly completely defined. However, a significant proportion of the yeast methylation network is constituted of methylation at residues besides lysine and arginine. Methyltransferases that target histidine, glutamine or the N- or C-termini of proteins comprise more than 25% of yeast methyltransferases (Table 1). This highlights that methylation beyond lysine and arginine is more prevalent than perhaps otherwise considered and should be further investigated in other eukaryotes. In fact, it has recently become apparent that histidine methylation and methyltransferases are prevalent in human (58, 88–91). Furthermore, it should be considered that methyltransferases in non-model organisms may catalyze these noncanonical forms of methylation, rather than just lysine and arginine methylation. For example, the deep-branching eukaryotic parasite, Giardia duodenalis, lacks arginine methylation yet has N-terminal methylation (92).How Many Methyl Groups Are Added by Methyltransferases?Methyltransferases are often able to add multiple methyl groups to the same amino acid sidechain. Across the yeast methylation network 14 enzymes transfer only one methyl group to their substrate(s), six transfer up to two methyl groups and eight enzymes transfer up to three methyl groups (Table 1). Of the enzymes that transfer just a single methyl group, seven cannot add additional methyl groups as they methylate histidine, glutamine, protein C-termini or the delta-nitrogen of arginine. Interestingly, this leaves seven apparent monomethyltransferases, six lysine- and one arginine-specific, that could theoretically generate higher methylation states than are typically observed in vivo. In fact one such enzyme, the eEF1A lysine methyltransferase Efm6, was seen to generate trimethylation upon overexpression (25). Of the enzymes that catalyze dimethylation, three are restricted to this methylation state as they catalyze arginine methylation or N-terminal methylation at prolines. The three remaining apparent dimethyltransferases target lysine and therefore could also be capable of producing trimethylation. It remains unknown why many arginine or lysine methyltransferases do not produce higher methylation states, and whether these distinct methylation states have different functions, as is known for histone methylation (86). However, the diversity of methylation states seen in yeast is notable. For proteins with lysine, arginine or N-terminal methylation sites, methylation states may represent another dimension for the fine-tuning of protein function.Lessons for the Definition of Other Post-translational Modification Networks.Why can the yeast protein methylation network be fully defined while completion of other protein modification networks remains elusive? This may be because of features peculiar to the yeast protein methylation network. Specifically, 1) the yeast methylation network mostly contains highly specific enzymes that target one or a few substrates, but has very few enzymes with broad activity; 2) all protein methylation is enzyme-mediated; and 3) there is minimal “noise” in the yeast protein methylation system as sites are mostly of high stoichiometry. These three features of the methylation network in yeast are compared and contrasted with other modifications below.(1)As discussed above, the vast majority of yeast protein methyltransferases are highly specific—only Hmt1 appears to have a broad range of substrates. Many highly specific protein methyltransferases are also found in human (7, 11), but there are also a significant number of enzymes, mostly in the SET domain family, that have very broad specificity (2, 93). In contrast, most protein kinases recognize highly degenerate motifs and are therefore very nonspecific (94). There are, however, also some highly specific kinases, such as the elongation factor 2 kinase (95). In general, while modification networks usually contain both high- and low-specificity enzymes, the relative proportion of these will affect how “definable” the overall network is. The yeast methylation network is heavily skewed towards high-specificity enzymes, and this has made its full definition possible.(2)When considering all post-translational modifications, whether enzyme-mediated or not, full definition of a protein modification system can become more elusive. This is the case with lysine acetylation, and lysine acylations in general, which are now known to be largely nonenzyme-mediated (96, 97). This creates a disconnect between the modifying enzymes, which are more easily defined through genome sequence analysis, and the modification system in general. Protein methylation, on the other hand, appears to be completely enzyme-mediated. This restrains all systems of protein methylation to the activity of the more definable set of modifying enzyme genes.(3)Different types of post-translational modifications occur with differing stoichiometries (98). For example, protein phosphorylation and acetylation often occur at low stoichiometry (96, 99). Protein methylation, on the other hand, is often of high stoichiometry (100). Indeed, many methylation sites, especially those on the ribosome and translation factors, exist at essentially 100% stoichiometry under standard laboratory conditions (13, 26, 28, 33). This means that protein methylation systems are overall less “noisy” than other modification systems as there is a clearer distinction between proteins that are methylated and those that are not.

How Prevalent Is Noncanonical Protein Methylation?Protein methylation canonically occurs on lysine or arginine residues. However, a significant proportion of the yeast methylation network is constituted of methylation at residues besides lysine and arginine. Methyltransferases that target histidine, glutamine or the N- or C-termini of proteins comprise more than 25% of yeast methyltransferases (Table 1). This highlights that methylation beyond lysine and arginine is more prevalent than perhaps otherwise considered and should be further investigated in other eukaryotes. In fact, it has recently become apparent that histidine methylation and methyltransferases are prevalent in human (58, 88–91). Furthermore, it should be considered that methyltransferases in non-model organisms may catalyze these noncanonical forms of methylation, rather than just lysine and arginine methylation. For example, the deep-branching eukaryotic parasite, Giardia duodenalis, lacks arginine methylation yet has N-terminal methylation (92).

How Many Methyl Groups Are Added by Methyltransferases?Methyltransferases are often able to add multiple methyl groups to the same amino acid sidechain. Across the yeast methylation network 14 enzymes transfer only one methyl group to their substrate(s), six transfer up to two methyl groups and eight enzymes transfer up to three methyl groups (Table 1). Of the enzymes that transfer just a single methyl group, seven cannot add additional methyl groups as they methylate histidine, glutamine, protein C-termini or the delta-nitrogen of arginine. Interestingly, this leaves seven apparent monomethyltransferases, six lysine- and one arginine-specific, that could theoretically generate higher methylation states than are typically observed in vivo. In fact one such enzyme, the eEF1A lysine methyltransferase Efm6, was seen to generate trimethylation upon overexpression (25). Of the enzymes that catalyze dimethylation, three are restricted to this methylation state as they catalyze arginine methylation or N-terminal methylation at prolines. The three remaining apparent dimethyltransferases target lysine and therefore could also be capable of producing trimethylation. It remains unknown why many arginine or lysine methyltransferases do not produce higher methylation states, and whether these distinct methylation states have different functions, as is known for histone methylation (86). However, the diversity of methylation states seen in yeast is notable. For proteins with lysine, arginine or N-terminal methylation sites, methylation states may represent another dimension for the fine-tuning of protein function.

Lessons for the Definition of Other Post-translational Modification Networks.Why can the yeast protein methylation network be fully defined while completion of other protein modification networks remains elusive? This may be because of features peculiar to the yeast protein methylation network. Specifically, 1) the yeast methylation network mostly contains highly specific enzymes that target one or a few substrates, but has very few enzymes with broad activity; 2) all protein methylation is enzyme-mediated; and 3) there is minimal “noise” in the yeast protein methylation system as sites are mostly of high stoichiometry. These three features of the methylation network in yeast are compared and contrasted with other modifications below.(1)As discussed above, the vast majority of yeast protein methyltransferases are highly specific—only Hmt1 appears to have a broad range of substrates. Many highly specific protein methyltransferases are also found in human (7, 11), but there are also a significant number of enzymes, mostly in the SET domain family, that have very broad specificity (2, 93). In contrast, most protein kinases recognize highly degenerate motifs and are therefore very nonspecific (94). There are, however, also some highly specific kinases, such as the elongation factor 2 kinase (95). In general, while modification networks usually contain both high- and low-specificity enzymes, the relative proportion of these will affect how “definable” the overall network is. The yeast methylation network is heavily skewed towards high-specificity enzymes, and this has made its full definition possible.(2)When considering all post-translational modifications, whether enzyme-mediated or not, full definition of a protein modification system can become more elusive. This is the case with lysine acetylation, and lysine acylations in general, which are now known to be largely nonenzyme-mediated (96, 97). This creates a disconnect between the modifying enzymes, which are more easily defined through genome sequence analysis, and the modification system in general. Protein methylation, on the other hand, appears to be completely enzyme-mediated. This restrains all systems of protein methylation to the activity of the more definable set of modifying enzyme genes.(3)Different types of post-translational modifications occur with differing stoichiometries (98). For example, protein phosphorylation and acetylation often occur at low stoichiometry (96, 99). Protein methylation, on the other hand, is often of high stoichiometry (100). Indeed, many methylation sites, especially those on the ribosome and translation factors, exist at essentially 100% stoichiometry under standard laboratory conditions (13, 26, 28, 33). This means that protein methylation systems are overall less “noisy” than other modification systems as there is a clearer distinction between proteins that are methylated and those that are not.

Sources of Bias That May Affect Our Understanding of the Yeast Methylation NetworkWhile we have argued that the yeast methylation network is near-complete, there are a number of caveats and alternate interpretations of data which must be considered. Broadly speaking, there are three main sources of bias that may affect our understanding of the yeast methylation network. These may impact on our thesis that the network is near-complete. These sources involve research focus bias, experimental bias and technical bias.One research bias is that there has been little targeted follow-up on yeast methylation sites reported in large-scale mass spectrometry studies (Table 3). This means that sites which are likely to be false positives, due to their lack of cognate methyltransferase or lack of heavy methyl SILAC evidence, have not been definitively disproved. A further instance of research bias is that yeast protein methylation has not been studied as extensively as, for example, human protein methylation. It remains possible that the apparent smaller extent of protein methylation in yeast reflects this bias. In terms of experimental bias, we note that most studies on yeast methylation (for methyltransferase substrate and site discovery) have grown yeast in standard conditions. It therefore cannot be discounted that some methyltransferases catalyze additional, condition-specific sites that remain to be identified. Equally, the analysis of mass spectrometry data for methylation has typically searched for this modification on lysine and arginine, even though other types of protein methylation exist. Finally, a number of possible technical biases could also affect the notion of network completeness. The high abundance of most methylated proteins in yeast may suggest a potential bias in substrate discovery, whereby low abundance substrates of methyltransferases remain undetected. While a range of enrichment reagents exist for methylated lysines and arginines, these are imperfect and there are few or no reagents for other types of methylation. Additionally, methylation sites identified by large-scale mass spectrometry studies may reflect a technical bias associated with the use of trypsin for protein digestion (Table 3).Collectively, these sources of bias point to the value in further investigating the yeast methylation system in a range of biological conditions, and for the development and application of new reagents and protocols for protein methylation analysis in yeast. This will allow further critical assessment of our claims.

Conclusions and OutlookWe have proposed here that the yeast protein methylation network is essentially complete. This represents a biological milestone, being the first such network of moderate scale to be near-fully defined. It is now amenable to study as an entire system for a modification; this is especially the case given that its scale is entirely compatible with modern proteomic techniques. For other post-translation modifications, our approach has formalized a means by which the completeness and incompleteness of their networks can be measured. This will assist the definition of knowledge gaps and help focus experiments and technical developments that can address such gaps and thus assist the complete definition of networks for other protein post-translational modifications.
