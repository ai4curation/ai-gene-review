Surface receptors of animal cells, such as integrins, promote mechanosensation 
by forming clusters as signaling hubs that transduce tensile forces. Walled 
cells of plants and fungi also feature surface sensors, with long extracellular 
domains that are embedded in their cell walls (CWs) and are thought to detect 
injuries and promote repair. How these sensors probe surface forces remains 
unknown. By studying the conserved CW sensor Wsc1 in fission yeast, we uncovered 
the formation of micrometer-sized clusters at sites of force application onto 
the CW. Clusters assembled within minutes of CW compression, in dose dependence 
with mechanical stress and disassembled upon relaxation. Our data support that 
Wsc1 accumulates to sites of enhanced mechanical stress through reduced lateral 
diffusivity, mediated by the binding of its extracellular WSC domain to CW 
polysaccharides, independent of canonical polarity, trafficking, and downstream 
CW regulatory pathways. Wsc1 may represent an autonomous module to detect and 
transduce local surface forces onto the CW.
