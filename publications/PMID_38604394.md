Aldehyde dehydrogenase 7A1 (ALDH7A1) catalyzes a step of lysine catabolism. 
Certain missense mutations in the ALDH7A1 gene cause pyridoxine dependent 
epilepsy (PDE), a rare autosomal neurometabolic disorder with recessive 
inheritance that affects almost 1:65,000 live births and is classically 
characterized by recurrent seizures from the neonatal period. We report a 
biochemical, structural, and computational study of two novel ALDH7A1 missense 
mutations that were identified in a child with rare recurrent seizures from the 
third month of life. The mutations affect two residues in the oligomer 
interfaces of ALDH7A1, Arg134 and Arg441 (Arg162 and Arg469 in the HGVS 
nomenclature). The corresponding enzyme variants R134S and R441C (p.Arg162Ser 
and p.Arg469Cys in the HGVS nomenclature) were expressed in Escherichia coli and 
purified. R134S and R441C have 10,000- and 50-fold lower catalytic efficiency 
than wild-type ALDH7A1, respectively. Sedimentation velocity analytical 
ultracentrifugation shows that R134S is defective in tetramerization, remaining 
locked in a dimeric state even in the presence of the tetramer-inducing coenzyme 
NAD+. Because the tetramer is the active form of ALDH7A1, the defect in 
oligomerization explains the very low catalytic activity of R134S. In contrast, 
R441C exhibits wild-type oligomerization behavior, and the 2.0 Å resolution 
crystal structure of R441C complexed with NAD+ revealed no obvious structural 
perturbations when compared to the wild-type enzyme structure. Molecular 
dynamics simulations suggest that the mutation of Arg441 to Cys may increase 
intersubunit ion pairs and alter the dynamics of the active site gate. Our 
biochemical, structural, and computational data on two novel clinical variants 
of ALDH7A1 add to the complexity of the molecular determinants underlying 
pyridoxine dependent epilepsy.

Introduction

Pyridoxine dependent epilepsy (PDE, OMIM 266100) is a rare autosomal neurometabolic disorder with recessive inheritance that affects almost 1:65,000 live births [1]. It is classically characterized by recurrent seizures mainly from the neonatal period [2, 3], or in the atypical form of the disease, seizures appearing months or years after birth [3]. PDE is caused by mutations in ALDH7A1, the gene encoding the enzyme α-aminoadipate semialdehyde (AASAL) dehydrogenase, also known as antiquitin [4]. ALDH7A1 is involved in lysine catabolism and catalyzes the NAD+-dependent oxidation of AASAL to α-aminoadipate (AA) (Fig. 1A). Deficiency in ALDH7A1 enzymatic activity typically results in accumulation of the toxic lysine catabolism intermediates AASAL and Δ1-piperideine-6-carboxylic acid (P6C, cyclized form of AASAL) in blood, urine and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). These metabolites represent the main pathogenetic drivers as well as the diagnostic biomarkers of PDE [5, 6]; however, a remarkably wide range of AASAL in patients has been noted, which might reflect different levels of residual ALDH7A1 activity caused by different ALDH7A1 mutations and/or dietary protein (L-lysine) intake [6, 7]. 6-oxo-pipecolate has also been proposed as a biomarker of PDE [8].

Children with PDE develop seizures resistant to conventional antiepileptic drugs, but seizure activity can be controlled by the administration of therapeutic doses of pyridoxine [9]. Withdrawal of the therapy may precipitate life-threatening status epilepticus, reversed only by pyridoxine [10]. Affected patients may also entail intellectual disability and neurodevelopmental impairments, in some cases associated with structural brain changes [11, 12]. Diagnosis is possible after clinical assessment of the response to pyridoxine, detection of elevated AASAL and P6C in urine, CSF and blood, and ultimately DNA sequence analysis of the ALDH7A1 gene.

Numerous mutations in the 18 exons of ALDH7A1 have been described and include insertions, deletions, nonsense, and missense mutations [1]. Over 70 missense variants of ALDH7A1 have been described in clinical reports. The c.1279G>C genetic mutation, which encodes the protein missense mutation p.Glu399Gln (p.Glu427Gln in the Human Genome Variation Society (HGVS) nomenclature [13]), is the most commonly reported pathogenic variant and is present in 30% of European patients [1, 14, 15]. Currently, 310 missense mutations of ALDH7A1 are listed at ClinVar, an archive of sequence variation in humans [16]. The ClinVar database includes both mutations observed in patients and others of unknown clinical significance.

Genetic testing has become a powerful diagnostic tool in the clinical suspicion of PDE with uncertain targeted testing. However, the presence of variants of uncertain significance or novel variants often complicates the interpretation of genetic tests. Biochemical analysis of individual enzyme variants remains the primary approach to assigning pathogenicity of novel variants. High throughput methods, such as multiplex assessments of variant abundance, can also generate experimental data on variants of unknown pathogenicity [17, 18]. Because the number of known missense variants far exceeds the capacity for experimental testing, many computational tools, known as Variant Effect Predictors, have been developed to assess pathogenicity, although the success of such methods is variable [19-21].

Structural and biochemical studies of human ALDH7A1 have contributed to our understanding of the molecular basis of PDE and the impact of mutations on ALDH7A1 enzyme function. About one-third of the over 70 missense variants of ALDH7A1 associated with PDE have been studied using enzyme activity assays, and in some cases, biophysical and structural methods [22-26]. Collectively, these studies have led to the assignment of “biochemical phenotypes” for missense mutations, including mild, moderate, and severe impact on enzyme activity, as well as misfolding phenotypes [4].

Herein, we report biochemical, structural, and computational characterization of two novel PDE-associated missense variants. We describe a case of a child with rare recurrent seizures from the third month of life. Genetic panel for epilepsy demonstrates novel missense variants of uncertain significance in the ALDH7A1 gene, which encode the protein variants R134S and R441C (p.Arg162Ser and p.Arg469Cys in the HGVS nomenclature). The purified enzyme variants are catalytically compromised, with the catalytic efficiencies of R134S and R441C being 10,000-fold and 50-fold lower than wild-type, respectively. Sedimentation velocity analytical ultracentrifugation suggests that the diminished catalytic activity of R134S is caused by a defect in oligomerization. In contrast, the self-association equilibrium of R441C appears normal, and the 2.0 Å resolution crystal structure of R441C reveals no significant changes in the tertiary structure or NAD+ binding site. Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations of the wild-type enzyme and R441C complexed with NAD+ reveal an intersubunit ion pair involving a hinge residue of the active site gate (Lys500) that is preferentially formed in R441C, which may alter the dynamics of the gate. The biochemical phenotypes of R134S and R441C may be considered as severe and moderate catalytic deficiencies, respectively, suggesting they contribute to disease.

2.Materials and methods2.1.Kinetic characterization of purified R134S and R441CSynthetic genes encoding R134S and R441C were generated using the Agilent QuikChange II site-directed mutagenesis kit. The mutations were verified by Sanger sequencing. Wild-type ALDH7A1 and the mutant variants were expressed in Escherichia coli and purified using protocols described previously [22-24].ALDH7A1 enzymatic activity was measured by monitoring NADH production by absorbance at 340 nm using an Epoch 2 Microplate Reader. The assay buffer contained 100 mM sodium pyrophosphate at pH 8.0 (pH was adjusted with NaOH). Enzyme stock solutions were prepared by diluting to the desired concentration with 100 mM pyrophosphate buffer at pH 8.0 supplemented with 2.5 mM NAD+. AASAL was used as the variable substrate (16 μM – 2000 μM) with NAD+ fixed at 2.5 mM. AASAL was synthesized and quantitated as previously reported [27]. The assays contained a final enzyme concentration of 0.07 μM wild-type or 5 μM of the mutant variants. Data were collected in triplicate. The apparent steady-state kinetic parameters kcat and Km for wild-type ALDH7A1 and R441C were obtained by fitting the initial rate data from all three trials globally to the Michaelis-Menten equation using Origin 2019. Due to the low activity of R134S, it was not possible to determine kcat and Km separately; therefore, the ratio of kcat/Km was estimated from linear regression of the v/Et versus [S] plot.2.2.Sedimentation velocity analysis of R134S and R441CThe oligomeric states of R134S and R441C in solution were determined using sedimentation velocity. These experiments were performed in a Beckman XL-I analytical ultracentrifuge using an An50Ti rotor at 20°C. Prior to sedimentation, the enzymes at 1 mg mL−1 were dialyzed overnight against buffer A (50 mM HEPES at pH 8, 100 mM NaCl, 1 mM dithiothreitol, and 5% (v/v) glycerol) for apo samples. For experiments performed in the presence of NAD+, the enzymes were dialyzed overnight against buffer A supplemented with 1 mM NAD+. A two-sector charcoal-Epon sedimentation velocity centerpiece was loaded with reference buffer and protein samples. After an equilibration period of 2 h, the sample was centrifuged at 35,000 rpm for a total of 300 radial scans spaced at 2-minute intervals. The stock solution of R441C used for crystallization contained 5 mg mL−1 enzyme and 15 mM NAD+ in buffer A. The crystallization reservoir contained 0.2 M MgCl2, 21% (w/v) PEG 3350, and 0.1 M Bis-Tris at pH 6.2. The data were integrated and scaled with XDS [30]. Intensities were merged and converted to amplitudes with AIMLESS [31]. Data processing statistics are listed in Table 1. The space group is C2 with two tetramers in the asymmetric unit. We note this is the same crystal form that we used previously to determine structures of wild-type ALDH7A1 and several mutant variants [23, 24, 32]. Phenix [33] was used for refinement, and COOT [34] was used for model building. The starting model for refinement was prepared from the coordinates of wild-type ALDH7A1 complexed with NAD+ (PDB ID: 4ZUK [32]). The structure was validated using the PDB validation server [35] and MolProbity [36]. Polder omit maps were used to verify the mutation and validate the modeling of NAD+ [37]. Refinement statistics are listed in Table 1. The atomic coordinates and X-ray diffraction structure factor amplitudes have been deposited in the Protein Data Bank (PDB) under the accession code 6V0Z.We note that the amino acid residue numbering convention used here and in the deposited crystal structure differs from the nomenclature of the HGVS [13]. HGVS nomenclature is typically used in articles on the genetics of PDE and patient case studies and refers to isoform 1 of ALDH7A1 (UniProt P49419–1), which includes a mitochondrial transit peptide and contains 539 amino acid residues. For consistency with previous structural and biochemical studies of ALDH7A1, the residues in the crystal structure are numbered according to isoform 2 (UniProt P49419–2), which is generated by alternative splicing and lacks residues 1 – 28 of isoform 1. Residue numbers in the crystal structure may be converted to HGVS nomenclature by adding 28. Thus, Arg134 and Arg441 of the crystal structure correspond Arg162 and Arg469, respectively, in HGVS nomenclature.2.4.Molecular dynamics (MD) simulationsMD simulations were performed for the wild-type enzyme and R441C, both in complex with NAD+. Starting models for MD were prepared from the crystal structures (4ZUK, wild-type; 6V0Z, R441C) as follows. Crystallographic waters and other solvent molecules were removed. Missing side chains were added with the Protein Repair and Analysis Server [38]. N-terminal residues 1-2, which are disordered in the crystal structures, were grafted from an AlphaFold2 model [39], which was generated with Phenix. Because the nicotinamide ribose of NAD+ had not been modeled in the wild-type enzyme (4ZUK), reflecting weak electron density [32], this group was added to the MD starting model based on its conformation in 6V0Z. We note the NAD+ in 6V0Z adopts the canonical hydride transfer conformation observed in certain other variants of ALDH7A1 (e.g., PDB 6O4C) and more widely in the ALDH superfamily.Both wild-type and R441C comprise 511 residues per protomer, with a total residue count of 2,044 for each tetramer. Special creation of NAD+ stream files for each chain of the two systems was performed using the molecular editor Avogadro v1.2.0 [40], where the pH was set to 7.2 to obtain a fully deprotonated pyrophosphate and subsequent −1 formal charge for the dinucleotide. Each system, bound with NAD+, was submitted to the PDB2PQR server for sidechain protonation prediction at pH 7.2 [41]. The CHARMM General Force Field (CGenFF version 4.6 – July 2022) [42, 43] was used for parameter and topology file generation.MD trajectories were generated as follows. The GROMACS 2022.5 package [44] was used for system solvation, equilibration, and simulation runs [45-53]. Each system was solvated using the TIP3P water model [54] and neutralized with Na+/Cl− ions in a rhombic dodecahedral box in which the protein was at least 10 Å away from the box edge. Each solvated system was then subjected to energy-minimization using the steepest descent algorithm until the maximum force was less than 10.0 kJ mol−1 nm−1. Inclusion of van der Waals forces was performed with a second round of energy-minimization using a switching function with inner and outer cutoffs of 1.0 nm and 1.2 nm, respectively. Each fully energy-minimized system then underwent a 100 ps NVT (constant particle number, volume, and temperature) equilibration with non-hydrogen atoms positionally restrained with a time constant of 0.1 ps at 300 K using the v-rescale thermostat [55]. A final 100 ps NPT (constant particle number, pressure, and temperature) equilibration was performed using isotropic C-rescale pressure coupling [55] with the reference pressure equal to 1 bar, isothermal compressibility set to 4.5 × 10−5 bar−1, and time constant set to 2 ps. A 1-μs production simulation was then performed after release of positional restraints on the NPT-equilibrated structure. Electrostatic forces were calculated using the Particle Mesh Ewald method with a real space cutoff equal to 1.2 nm [56, 57], and isotropic Parrinello-Rahman pressure coupling [58-60] was used with the same reference pressure and isothermal compressibility as in NPT equilibration. Periodic boundary conditions were applied in all three dimensions. A 2-fs time step was used for all MD simulations. In addition to the 1-μs production simulation, five parallel, 500 ns simulations for both wild-type and R441C were spawned from the 400 ns timepoint of the original 1-μs production simulation, after re-initialization of their starting atom velocities. Concatenation of the original 1-μs and five 500 ns simulations provided 3.5 μs total simulation time for each system. Coordinates were saved every 100 ps for analysis. Analyses of MD trajectories was performed using MDAnalysis v2.0.0 [61, 62] and MDTraj v1.9.7 [63] packages. Interaction frequency analysis was performed with get_contacts (https://getcontacts.github.io/). Plots were generated using the Matplotlib package v3.5.0 [64]. Details of the simulations are listed in Supplementary Table S1.

We note that the amino acid residue numbering convention used here and in the deposited crystal structure differs from the nomenclature of the HGVS [13]. HGVS nomenclature is typically used in articles on the genetics of PDE and patient case studies and refers to isoform 1 of ALDH7A1 (UniProt P49419–1), which includes a mitochondrial transit peptide and contains 539 amino acid residues. For consistency with previous structural and biochemical studies of ALDH7A1, the residues in the crystal structure are numbered according to isoform 2 (UniProt P49419–2), which is generated by alternative splicing and lacks residues 1 – 28 of isoform 1. Residue numbers in the crystal structure may be converted to HGVS nomenclature by adding 28. The purified recombinant variants R134S and R441C were tested for catalytic activity using steady-state assays that monitor the production of NADH as a function of AASAL concentration. As a control, wild-type ALDH7A1 was also assayed. Wild-type ALDH7A1 displays Michaelis-Menten behavior (Fig. 2A), characterized by kinetic constants for AASAL (at saturating NAD+) of kcat = 0.27 ± 0.02 s−1 and Km of 129 ± 29 μM (Table 2). The catalytic efficiency (kcat/Km) of 2100 ± 480 s−1M−1 is within the range of 700 – 4700 reported previously [23, 24, 27, 66].

In contrast to the wild-type enzyme, R134S displayed very low activity throughout the entire AASAL range tested, even when using 70-fold higher enzyme concentration than the control (Fig. 2A). Due to the very low activity, the data could not be fit to the Michaelis-Menten equation, and thus it was not possible to determine kcat and Km separately. Close inspection of the rate data revealed a barely perceptible linear increase in activity with increasing AASAL concentration (Fig. 2B). Therefore, linear regression of the v/Et versus [S] plot was used to estimate an upper limit for kcat/Km of 0.2 ± 0.1 s−1M−1. This value is 10,000-fold lower than wild-type, indicating a severe catalytic defect (Table 2).

R441C is also catalytically impaired, albeit more active than R134S. Unlike R134S, the R441C variant clearly exhibited Michaelis-Menten behavior (Fig. 2B). Curve fitting yielded kinetic constants of kcat = 0.046 ± 0.002 s−1M−1 and Km of 1100 ± 100 μM (Table 2). The catalytic efficiency of 42 ± 4.2 s−1M−1 is 50-fold lower than wild-type.

This region of the protein appears to be a tetramerization hot spot, i.e., a region harboring residues essential for tetramerization [68, 69]. The inability of these variants to assemble into the active tetramer explains their lack of catalytic activity.

The physical basis for the 50-fold decreased catalytic efficiency of R441C is less obvious. R441C forms the classic ALDH7A1 tetramer in the crystal, and sedimentation velocity confirmed the tetramer in solution. Further, we showed that tetramerization of R441C is encouraged by the binding of NAD+, like the wild-type enzyme. The crystal structure also shows that the tertiary structure and NAD+-binding site appear to be unperturbed.

It is curious that the mutation of Arg441 did not disrupt tetramerization given its proximity to the tetramerization hot spot residues Arg134, Gly138, and Ala149 (Fig. 8). One potentially meaningful difference among these residues is that Arg441 is more solvent-exposed than the others. The GETAREA server [70] classifies Arg441 as an “outside” residue, whereas Arg134, Gly138, and Ala149 are classified as “inside”. For comparison, the accessible surface areas of Arg134 and Arg441 are 27 Å2 and 105 Å2, respectively. Seeking an explanation for the low activity of R441C, we performed MD simulations of the wild-type enzyme and R441C. Thus, formation of the ion pair may influence the dynamics of the active site gate. This may underlie the observed bias of R441C toward open gate conformations (Fig. 5B). Another possibility is that the additional intersubunit ion pairs could decrease the flexibility of the tetramer by inhibiting large-scale (rigid body) motions of chains that may be needed for optimal catalytic efficiency.

Finally, in the patient described here, the two variant proteins could theoretically assemble into heterooligomers. Such heterotetramers might be expected to exhibit even lower activity than the R441C homotetramer due to a phenomenon known as oligomer poisoning whereby the severely compromised R134S variant exerts a dominant negative effect [71]. 5.ConclusionTwo new missense mutations of the ALDH7A1 gene targeting oligomer interface residues were discovered in a patient suspected of having PDE. The enzyme variants R134S and R441C (p.Arg162Ser and p.Arg469Cys in the HGVS nomenclature) were purified and characterized using enzyme kinetic assays, analytical ultracentrifugation, and in the case of R441C, X-ray crystallography and molecular dynamics simulations. Both variants exhibit low catalytic activity, consistent with a causative role in disease. The severe catalytic deficiency of R134S results from a strong defect in tetramerization. In contrast, the diminished activity of R441C has a more subtle origin, possibly in perturbed protein dynamics. We conclude that both variants should be categorized as pathogenic mutations, and the biochemical phenotypes of R134S and R441C may be considered as severe and moderate catalytic deficiencies, respectively.
