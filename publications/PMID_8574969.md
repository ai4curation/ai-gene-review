# Alzheimer-associated presenilins 1 and 2: neuronal expression in brain and localization to intracellular membranes in mammalian cells.
**Authors:** Kovacs DM, Fausett HJ, Page KJ, Kim TW, Moir RD, Merriam DE, Hollister RD, Hallmark OG, Mancini R, Felsenstein KM, Hyman BT, Tanzi RE, Wasco W
**Journal:** Nat Med (1996)
**DOI:** [10.1038/nm0296-224](https://doi.org/10.1038/nm0296-224)

## Abstract

1. Nat Med. 1996 Feb;2(2):224-9. doi: 10.1038/nm0296-224.

Alzheimer-associated presenilins 1 and 2: neuronal expression in brain and 
localization to intracellular membranes in mammalian cells.

Kovacs DM(1), Fausett HJ, Page KJ, Kim TW, Moir RD, Merriam DE, Hollister RD, 
Hallmark OG, Mancini R, Felsenstein KM, Hyman BT, Tanzi RE, Wasco W.

Author information:
(1)Genetics and Aging Unit, Massachusetts General Hospital-East, Harvard Medical 
School, Charlestown 02129, USA.

Mutations in two recently identified genes appear to cause the majority of 
early-onset familial Alzheimer's disease (FAD). These two novel genes, 
presenilin 1 (PS1) and presenilin 2 (PS2) are members of an evolutionarily 
conserved gene family. The normal biological role(s) of the presenilins and the 
mechanism(s) by which the FAD-associated mutations exert their effect remain 
unknown. Employing in situ hybridization, we demonstrate that the expression 
patterns of PS1 and PS2 in the brain are extremely similar to each other and 
that messages for both are primarily detectable in neuronal populations. 
Immunochemical analyses indicate that PS1 and PS2 are similar in size and 
localized to similar intracellular compartments (endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi 
complex). FAD-associated mutations in PS1 and PS2 do not significantly modify 
either their migration patterns on SDS-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis or 
their overall subcellular localization, although subtle differences in 
perinuclear staining were noted for mutant PS1.
