# Syntaphilin: a syntaxin-1 clamp that controls SNARE assembly.
**Authors:** Lao G, Scheuss V, Gerwin CM, Su Q, Mochida S, Rettig J, Sheng ZH
**Journal:** Neuron (2000)
**DOI:** [10.1016/s0896-6273(00)80882-x](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0896-6273(00)80882-x)

## Abstract

1. Neuron. 2000 Jan;25(1):191-201. doi: 10.1016/s0896-6273(00)80882-x.

Syntaphilin: a syntaxin-1 clamp that controls SNARE assembly.

Lao G(1), Scheuss V, Gerwin CM, Su Q, Mochida S, Rettig J, Sheng ZH.

Author information:
(1)Synaptic Function Unit, National Institute of Neurological Disorders and 
Stroke, National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, Maryland 20892-4154, USA.

Syntaxin-1 is a key component of the synaptic vesicle docking/fusion machinery 
that forms the SNARE complex with VAMP/synaptobrevin and SNAP-25. Identifying 
proteins that modulate SNARE complex formation is critical for understanding the 
molecular mechanisms underlying neurotransmitter release and its modulation. We 
have cloned and characterized a protein called syntaphilin that is selectively 
expressed in brain. Syntaphilin competes with SNAP-25 for binding to syntaxin-1 
and inhibits SNARE complex formation by absorbing free syntaxin-1. Transient 
overexpression of syntaphilin in cultured hippocampal neurons significantly 
reduces neurotransmitter release. Furthermore, introduction of syntaphilin into 
presynaptic superior cervical ganglion neurons in culture inhibits synaptic 
transmission. These findings suggest that syntaphilin may function as a 
molecular clamp that controls free syntaxin-1 availability for the assembly of 
the SNARE complex, and thereby regulates synaptic vesicle exocytosis.
