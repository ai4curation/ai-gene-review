# Cyclic di-GMP inhibits nitrate assimilation by impairing the antitermination function of NasT in Pseudomonas putida.
**Authors:** Nie L, Xiao Y, Zhou T, Feng H, He M, Liang Q, Mu K, Nie H, Huang Q, Chen W

Cyclic di-GMP inhibits nitrate assimilation by impairing the antitermination 
function of NasT in Pseudomonas putida.

The ubiquitous bacterial second messenger cyclic diguanylate (c-di-GMP) 
coordinates diverse cellular processes through its downstream receptors. 
However, whether c-di-GMP participates in regulating nitrate assimilation is 
unclear. Here, we found that NasT, an antiterminator involved in nitrate 
assimilation in Pseudomonas putida, specifically bound c-di-GMP. NasT was 
essential for expressing the nirBD operon encoding nitrite reductase during 
nitrate assimilation. High-level c-di-GMP inhibited the binding of NasT to the 
leading RNA of nirBD operon (NalA), thus attenuating the antitermination 
function of NasT, resulting in decreased nirBD expression and nitrite reductase 
activity, which in turn led to increased nitrite accumulation in cells and its 
export. Molecular docking and point mutation assays revealed five residues in 
NasT (R70, Q72, D123, K127 and R140) involved in c-di-GMP-binding, of which R140 
was essential for both c-di-GMP-binding and NalA-binding. Three diguanylate 
cyclases (c-di-GMP synthetases) were found to interact with NasT and inhibited 
nirBD expression, including WspR, PP_2557, and PP_4405. Besides, the 
c-di-GMP-binding ability of NasT was conserved in the other three representative 
Pseudomonas species, including P. aeruginosa, P. fluorescens and P. syringae. 
Our findings provide new insights into nitrate assimilation regulation by 
revealing the mechanism by which c-di-GMP inhibits nitrate assimilation via 
NasT.

32P-labeled c-di-GMP was synthesized using purified E. coli DGC DgcZ and [α-32P]GTP (3000 Ci/mmol, PerkinElmer). High-level c-di-GMP leads to nitrite accumulation and exportNitrate absorbed into cells is reduced to nitrite by nitrate reductase and then reduced to ammonium by nitrite reductase (13). Based on this, we speculated that low nitrite reductase activity in strains with high-level c-di-GMP might lead to nitrite accumulation in cells, which was poisonous to the bacteria, and thus bacteria might export excessive nitrite from cells. To test this hypothesis, we detected intracellular and extracellular nitrite concentrations in strains at different c-di-GMP levels after 3-hour exposure to M9-NO3− medium. The results showed that WT + pdgcZ displayed higher extracellular and intracellular nitrite concentrations, while WT + pdgcZ displayed lower extracellular nitrite concentration and similar intracellular nitrite concentration than WT + pVec (Figure 5B and C). These results are consistent with our hypothesis that high-level c-di-GMP leads to a higher nitrite accumulation and export relative to the low-/normal-level c-di-GMP. To further determine whether the nitrite accumulation and export were caused by decreased nitrite reductase activity, we introduced a plasmid containing nirBD operon into the strains with high-level c-di-GMP (termed as WT + pdgcZ-nirBD) and detected intracellular and extracellular nitrite concentrations. The expression of the nirBD operon was controlled by a constitutive promoter, J23119. After the introduction of nirBD-overexpressing plasmid, extracellular and intracellular nitrite concentration levels in WT + pdgcZ-nirBD were reduced to the levels in WT + pVec (Figure 5B and C), indicating that the nitrite accumulation was relieved by the overexpression of nirBD. These results revealed that high-level c-di-GMP led to nitrite accumulation and export in cells by reducing nirBD expression.

DiscussionAlthough c-di-GMP is related to various cellular processes, there are few reports on the effects of c-di-GMP on nitrate assimilation or on the regulation of c-di-GMP receptor on nitrate assimilation. Herein, by constructing a P. putida transcriptional regulator library and screening c-di-GMP-binding regulators from this library, we identified NasT as a novel c-di-GMP receptor. NasT acted as a transcription antiterminator in the expression of nirBD operon, and it was essential for nitrate assimilation. The binding of c-di-GMP to NasT integrated c-di-GMP into nitrate assimilation. With previous reports on the regulation of nirBD expression, we proposed a potential model to reveal how c-di-GMP regulated nirBD operon expression via NasT (Figure 8). In the absence of c-di-GMP, the antiterminator NasT interacts with the NalA hairpin structure to inhibit the formation of the T-loop, resulting in transcription read-through and the production of nirBD mRNA. In the presence of c-di-GMP, NasT binds c-di-GMP to form the NasT-c-di-GMP complex. NasT consists of an amino-terminal REC domain and a carboxyl-terminal ANTAR domain. Both domains were not classical c-di-GMP-binding domains. Nevertheless, our results showed that both domains were required for c-di-GMP-NasT binding (Figure 4A). The molecular docking prediction showed that c-di-GMP might bind to a predicted pocket between the REC and ANTAR domains (Figure 4B). Five amino acid residues in the predicted pocket (two in the REC domain, two in the linker region, and one in the ANTAR domain) played a key role in c-di-GMP-binding (Figure 4C). These results suggested that a novel c-di-GMP-binding site might form between adjacent domains. Considering this, we speculated that NasT homologs and other proteins consisting of a REC domain and an adjacent ANTAR domain (such as Rv1626 of M. tuberculosis and EutV of E. faecalis) might also have c-di-GMP-binding ability. However, the alignment analysis displayed that except for the R140 residue, the other four key residues involved in c-di-GMP-binding were not conserved in Rv1626, EutV, and NasT of non-Pseudomonas species (Supplementary Figure S11). Recent mutation studies identified several residues in the ANTAR domain of EutV in E. faecalis and NasR in K. oxytoca, and these residues played an important role in RNA-binding, including the R142 of EutV and the R340 of NasR, both of which corresponded to the R140 of NasT in P. putida (27,64). Consistent with these previous results, we also found that the R140 residue of NasT in P. putida was important for RNA-binding (Figure 4D). Interestingly, the R140 residue of NasT also played a crucial role in c-di-GMP-binding (Figure 4C). The interaction between c-di-GMP receptor and DGC shortens their spatial distance and theoretically makes the receptor more sensitive to the changes in local c-di-GMP levels around the DGC. Here we found three DGCs (WspR, PP_2557 and PP_4405) interacted with the c-di-GMP receptor NasT and inhibited nirBD expression (Figure 6A and B). Among the three DGCs, WspR was a conserved DGC in Pseudomonas species that played a key role in maintaining c-di-GMP levels (38), and it showed the strongest inhibitory effect on nirBD expression. Recent studies reported that WspR modulated several processes by changing the c-di-GMP level, such as surface sensing (73), surface colonization (74), ethanol-mediated biofilm promotion (75), oxidative stress resistance (76), and antibacterial activity (77). Here our results revealed another function of WspR as an inhibitor of nirBD expression during nitrate assimilation. Given that these processes are all regulated by WspR, we speculated that bacteria might link these processes through WspR, and WspR might function like a shortcut key on a computer. Thus, to regulate these processes under certain conditions, bacteria only need to change the expression or activity of WspR.As a key regulator in the plankton-to-biofilm lifestyle transition in diverse bacterial species, c-di-GMP promotes biofilm matrix production and inhibits flagellar synthesis and rotation, leading to a biofilm lifestyle (2). Bacterial cells in the biofilm lifestyle have significantly different properties from bacterial cells in the planktonic lifestyle since the dense environment of biofilm can protect cells under harmful conditions and allows cells to cooperate and interact with each other in various ways (78). Previous studies have revealed that high-level c-di-GMP promotes biofilm formation and maintenance in P. putida (39,79). In this study, our results demonstrated that high-level c-di-GMP inhibited nirBD expression and repressed nitrate assimilation. These results indicated that c-di-GMP oppositely regulated biofilm formation and nitrate assimilation. We speculated that this regulation was an energy-saving strategy in P. putida biofilm. The dense biofilm structure may prevent nitrate import, like that in the case of antibiotics and heavy metals. Thus, cells in biofilm may not be able to utilize nitrate as efficiently as planktonic cells, and the c-di-GMP-mediated inhibition of nitrite reductase would avoid excess nitrite reductase synthesis in biofilm cells. In contrast, the contact of planktonic cells with nitrate might be more efficient than biofilm cells, and higher expression of nitrite reductase would be more suitable for planktonic cells.Nitrate is widely used in fertilizers and other industrial fields. With increased agricultural and industrial activities, large amounts of nitrate-containing wastewater are released into the environment, leading to nitrate pollution and environmental problems (80,81). Bioremediation with aerobic denitrifiers that reduce nitrate compounds to harmless dinitrogen gas provides a cost-effective and environmental-friendly method to remove nitrate and other nitrogen compounds from polluted water (82). Several species of the genus Pseudomonas, including P. stutzeri, P. mendocina and P. putida, were culturable and efficient aerobic denitrifying bacteria since they can achieve simultaneous aerobic nitrification and denitrification processes to accelerate nitrogen removal (83–85). Here we revealed that c-di-GMP inhibited nitrate assimilation via NasT in P. putida KT2440 that had no denitrification ability. It is currently unclear whether NasT homologs in Pseudomonas species with denitrification ability could regulate nitrate assimilation in response to c-di-GMP and whether c-di-GMP regulated denitrification in those Pseudomonas species. Besides, nitrate is the substrate for both denitrification and nitrate assimilation. How bacteria coordinated denitrification and nitrate assimilation under certain conditions and whether c-di-GMP was involved in this coordination are interesting questions to be investigated.

In the presence of c-di-GMP, NasT binds c-di-GMP to form the NasT-c-di-GMP complex, which shows a lower affinity to NalA, leading to transcription termination.

Unlike other c-di-GMP receptors with conserved c-di-GMP-binding domains, the c-di-GMP-responsive transcriptional regulator contained different c-di-GMP-binding domains (72). Thus, it is difficult to predict new c-di-GMP-responsive transcriptional regulators via the bioinformatics method, and the molecular inventory, diversity, and function of this type of c-di-GMP receptor remain unclear. NasT consists of an amino-terminal REC domain and a carboxyl-terminal ANTAR domain. Both domains were not classical c-di-GMP-binding domains. Nevertheless, our results showed that both domains were required for c-di-GMP-NasT binding (Figure 4A). The molecular docking prediction showed that c-di-GMP might bind to a predicted pocket between the REC and ANTAR domains (Figure 4B). Five amino acid residues in the predicted pocket (two in the REC domain, two in the linker region, and one in the ANTAR domain) played a key role in c-di-GMP-binding (Figure 4C). These results suggested that a novel c-di-GMP-binding site might form between adjacent domains. Considering this, we speculated that NasT homologs and other proteins consisting of a REC domain and an adjacent ANTAR domain (such as Rv1626 of M. tuberculosis and EutV of E. faecalis) might also have c-di-GMP-binding ability. However, the alignment analysis displayed that except for the R140 residue, the other four key residues involved in c-di-GMP-binding were not conserved in Rv1626, EutV, and NasT of non-Pseudomonas species (Supplementary Figure S11). Moreover, UV cross-linking assay results showed that only NasT homologs in Pseudomonas species bound c-di-GMP (Figure 7A). It seemed that the c-di-GMP-binding ability of NasT was only conserved in Pseudomonas species.

The R140 residue in the ANTAR domain of NasT was highly conserved among NasT homologs, Rv1626, and EutV (Supplementary Figure S11). Recent mutation studies identified several residues in the ANTAR domain of EutV in E. faecalis and NasR in K. oxytoca, and these residues played an important role in RNA-binding, including the R142 of EutV and the R340 of NasR, both of which corresponded to the R140 of NasT in P. putida (27,64). Consistent with these previous results, we also found that the R140 residue of NasT in P. putida was important for RNA-binding (Figure 4D). Interestingly, the R140 residue of NasT also played a crucial role in c-di-GMP-binding (Figure 4C). Together with the results that c-di-GMP inhibited NasT-NalA interaction, we speculated that c-di-GMP competed with NalA for binding the R140 residue of NasT, impeding the interaction between NalA and NalA.

The c-di-GMP receptors are found to colocalize with their particular partner DGCs via protein-protein interactions, establishing a local source and sink of c-di-GMP (67). The interaction between c-di-GMP receptor and DGC shortens their spatial distance and theoretically makes the receptor more sensitive to the changes in local c-di-GMP levels around the DGC. Here we found three DGCs (WspR, PP_2557 and PP_4405) interacted with the c-di-GMP receptor NasT and inhibited nirBD expression (Figure 6A and B). Among the three DGCs, WspR was a conserved DGC in Pseudomonas species that played a key role in maintaining c-di-GMP levels (38), and it showed the strongest inhibitory effect on nirBD expression. Recent studies reported that WspR modulated several processes by changing the c-di-GMP level, such as surface sensing (73), surface colonization (74), ethanol-mediated biofilm promotion (75), oxidative stress resistance (76), and antibacterial activity (77). Here our results revealed another function of WspR as an inhibitor of nirBD expression during nitrate assimilation. Given that these processes are all regulated by WspR, we speculated that bacteria might link these processes through WspR, and WspR might function like a shortcut key on a computer. Thus, to regulate these processes under certain conditions, bacteria only need to change the expression or activity of WspR.

As a key regulator in the plankton-to-biofilm lifestyle transition in diverse bacterial species, c-di-GMP promotes biofilm matrix production and inhibits flagellar synthesis and rotation, leading to a biofilm lifestyle (2). Bacterial cells in the biofilm lifestyle have significantly different properties from bacterial cells in the planktonic lifestyle since the dense environment of biofilm can protect cells under harmful conditions and allows cells to cooperate and interact with each other in various ways (78). Previous studies have revealed that high-level c-di-GMP promotes biofilm formation and maintenance in P. putida (39,79). In this study, our results demonstrated that high-level c-di-GMP inhibited nirBD expression and repressed nitrate assimilation. These results indicated that c-di-GMP oppositely regulated biofilm formation and nitrate assimilation. We speculated that this regulation was an energy-saving strategy in P. putida biofilm. The dense biofilm structure may prevent nitrate import, like that in the case of antibiotics and heavy metals. Thus, cells in biofilm may not be able to utilize nitrate as efficiently as planktonic cells, and the c-di-GMP-mediated inhibition of nitrite reductase would avoid excess nitrite reductase synthesis in biofilm cells. In contrast, the contact of planktonic cells with nitrate might be more efficient than biofilm cells, and higher expression of nitrite reductase would be more suitable for planktonic cells.

Nitrate is widely used in fertilizers and other industrial fields. With increased agricultural and industrial activities, large amounts of nitrate-containing wastewater are released into the environment, leading to nitrate pollution and environmental problems (80,81). Bioremediation with aerobic denitrifiers that reduce nitrate compounds to harmless dinitrogen gas provides a cost-effective and environmental-friendly method to remove nitrate and other nitrogen compounds from polluted water (82). Several species of the genus Pseudomonas, including P. stutzeri, P. mendocina and P. putida, were culturable and efficient aerobic denitrifying bacteria since they can achieve simultaneous aerobic nitrification and denitrification processes to accelerate nitrogen removal (83–85). Here we revealed that c-di-GMP inhibited nitrate assimilation via NasT in P. putida KT2440 that had no denitrification ability. It is currently unclear whether NasT homologs in Pseudomonas species with denitrification ability could regulate nitrate assimilation in response to c-di-GMP and whether c-di-GMP regulated denitrification in those Pseudomonas species. Besides, nitrate is the substrate for both denitrification and nitrate assimilation. How bacteria coordinated denitrification and nitrate assimilation under certain conditions and whether c-di-GMP was involved in this coordination are interesting questions to be investigated.
