# Identification of PTPN23 as a novel regulator of cell invasion in mammary epithelial cells from a loss-of-function screen of the 'PTP-ome'.
**Authors:** Lin G, Aranda V, Muthuswamy SK, Tonks NK
**Journal:** Genes Dev (2011)
**DOI:** [10.1101/gad.2018911](https://doi.org/10.1101/gad.2018911)
**PMC:** [PMC3134084](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3134084/)

## Abstract

1. Genes Dev. 2011 Jul 1;25(13):1412-25. doi: 10.1101/gad.2018911.

Identification of PTPN23 as a novel regulator of cell invasion in mammary 
epithelial cells from a loss-of-function screen of the 'PTP-ome'.

Lin G(1), Aranda V, Muthuswamy SK, Tonks NK.

Author information:
(1)Graduate Program in Molecular and Cellular Biology, Stony Brook University, 
Stony Brook, New York 11794, USA.

We used an RNAi-mediated loss-of-function screen to study systematically the 
role of the protein tyrosine phosphatase (PTP) superfamily of enzymes in mammary 
epithelial cell motility in the absence or presence of the oncoprotein tyrosine 
kinase ERBB2. We report that although shRNAs directed against most of the PTP 
family were without effect, suppression of three PTPs-PRPN23, PTPRG, and 
PTPRR-enhanced cell motility. Furthermore, we found that suppression of PTPN23, 
but not PTPRG or PTPRR, induced cell invasion. Suppression of PTPN23 increased 
E-cadherin internalization, impaired early endosome trafficking of E-cadherin, 
induced the expression of mesenchymal proteins, and caused cell scattering. The 
activity of SRC and β-catenin was elevated when PTPN23 was suppressed. Moreover, 
we identified SRC, E-cadherin, and β-catenin as direct substrates of PTPN23. 
Inhibition of SRC with the small molecular inhibitor SU6656 blocked the effects 
of PTPN23 depletion. These findings suggest that loss of PTPN23 may increase the 
activity of SRC and the phosphorylation status of the E-cadherin/β-catenin 
signaling complex to promote tumor growth and invasive behavior in breast 
cancer. In addition, our studies highlight functional specificity among PTPs and 
reveal new roles for PTPs in mammary epithelial cell biology.
