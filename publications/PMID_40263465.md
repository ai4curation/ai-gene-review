Mitochondrial homeostasis is maintained through complex regulatory mechanisms, 
including the balance of mitochondrial dynamics involving fusion and fission 
processes. A central player in this regulation is the ubiquitin-proteasome 
system (UPS), which controls the degradation of pivotal mitochondrial proteins. 
In this study, we identified cullin-RING E3 ligase 2 (CRL2) and its substrate 
receptor, FEM1B, as critical regulators of mitochondrial dynamics. Through 
proteomic analysis, we demonstrate here that FEM1B controls the turnover of 
PLD6, a key regulator of mitochondrial dynamics. Using structural and 
biochemical approaches, we show that FEM1B physically interacts with PLD6 and 
that this interaction is facilitated by the direct association of FEM1B with the 
mitochondrial import receptor TOM20. Ablation of FEM1B or disruption of the 
FEM1B-TOM20 interaction impairs PLD6 degradation and induces mitochondrial 
defects, phenocopying PLD6 overexpression. These findings underscore the 
importance of FEM1B in maintaining mitochondrial morphology and provide further 
mechanistic insights into how the UPS regulates mitochondrial homeostasis.

INTRODUCTION

Mitochondria are multifunctional organelles in eukaryotic cells, involved in key functions such as energy production, metabolism and cell death1. Cells maintain mitochondrial homeostasis through quality control mechanisms2, with mitochondrial dynamics, involving fusion and fission, playing a key role in regulating homeostasis and enabling cellular adaptation3. Disruptions in these dynamics can alter mitochondrial morphology, leading to either hyperfused or fragmented mitochondria. The rate of these events is balanced by specific regulatory mechanisms4, including the ubiquitin-proteasome system (UPS), which controls mitochondrial fusion by regulating key fusion effectors such as the large dynamin-like GTPases MFN1 and MFN25–15. Additional regulators of mitochondrial fusion operate downstream of MFN1/2, including the outer mitochondrial membrane protein PLD6 (also known as MitoPLD). PLD6 hydrolyzes cardiolipin to produce phosphatidic acid, thus generates a key signaling molecule in mitochondrial dynamics16–18. Dysregulation of PLD6 levels has been associated with aberrant mitochondrial fusion and fission18–21, but the mechanisms regulating PLD6 remain unclear.

The UPS represents the major route for degrading unwanted proteins 22. Ubiquitination, the process of conjugating ubiquitin to substrates, serves as the signal for proteasome targeting and subsequent degradation 23,24. Conventional ubiquitination occurs through a cascade of three enzymes, whereby ubiquitin activated by the E1 enzyme is transferred to an E2 ubiquitin-conjugating enzyme, and then to the target substrate recruited by an E3 ubiquitin ligase (E3) 25. E3s provide specificity to this process 26, and in most cases an E3 directly associates with degradation signals (degrons) in substrates to promote their ubiquitination and degradation 27,28.

The multi-subunit Cullin-RING E3 ligases (CRLs) represent the largest sub-family of E3s, with eight Cullins found in human cells. These Cullins function as scaffolds to recruit target substrates via adaptor and substrate receptor proteins that bind to their N-terminus. Meanwhile, E2 enzymes interact with the C-terminal region of Cullins to facilitate substrate ubiquitination29. In the case of Cullin 2 (CUL2), the heterodimeric adaptor proteins Elongin B (EloB) and Elongin C (EloC) interact with a range of substrate receptors to promote substrate recruitment to the CRL2 complex in a selective manner30,31. Among CUL2 substrate receptors, FEM1 family members—FEM1A, FEM1B and FEM1C— received much attention in recent years. We and others found that while all family members promote substrate degradation by recognizing Arg/C-degron substrates 32–35, FEM1B can also interact with at least two additional classes of degron motifs: Ψ-Pro/C-degron36,37 and internal reduced Cys degron38,39. This selective recognition is enabled by key residues in the ankyrin (ANK) and tetratricopeptide (TPR) tandem repeats of FEM1 proteins 33–35,37,38. Moreover, CRL2FEM1B forms dimers in cells and this dimeric assembly is required for the E3 complex activity toward Arg/ and Ψ-Pro/C-degrons-bearing substrates37.

While FEM1B’s molecular mechanism of substrate selection has been studied in detail, the full scope of its substrates and cellular functions remains unclear. Recent data suggested that CRL2FEM1B regulates myoblast differentiation by controlling the Cys degron bearing substrate FNIP138,39. Additionally, it was demonstrated that FEM1B acts as a mitochondrial gatekeeper. However, some mitochondrial defects in FEM1B-depleted cells could not be solely explained by the accumulation of FNIP139, suggesting the involvement of additional FEM1B substrates in mitochondrial regulation. Furthermore, how FEM1B, which lacks a mitochondrial targeting signal, regulates mitochondria functions was not addressed.

To shed light on the physiological roles of FEM1B, we performed proteomic analysis of FEM1B-deficient cells and revealed that PLD6, a crucial regulator of mitochondrial fusion, is the primary mitochondrial substrate whose turnover is controlled by FEM1B. Utilizing cryo-EM and in vitro pulldown assays, we demonstrated a direct interaction between FEM1B and PLD6, mapping their minimal binding site. Notably, cells lacking FEM1B displayed mitochondrial aggregation in perinuclear regions, a defect that could be rescued by simultaneously depleting PLD6, indicating that FEM1B regulates mitochondrial homeostasis through the modulation of PLD6 levels. Further investigation into the recruitment of FEM1B to mitochondria revealed that FEM1B directly associates with TOM20, a mitochondrial import receptor. Structural analyses pinpointed the N-terminal ANK repeat of FEM1B as the specific region responsible for interaction with TOM20, distinct from its substrate-binding domain. Disrupting the interaction between FEM1B and TOM20 impaired PLD6 degradation and recapitulated mitochondrial defects observed in FEM1B-deficient cells. Together, these findings characterize a TOM20-dependent molecular mechanism by which FEM1B regulates mitochondrial morphology.

RESULTSPLD6 is a CRL2FEM1B substrate as revealed by proteomicPrevious findings indicated that the mitochondrial defects observed in FEM1B-deficient cells could not be fully attributed to FNIP1 accumulation39. This suggests that FEM1B may regulate additional, yet unidentified substrates to maintain mitochondrial functionality. Since FNIP1 is not a mitochondrial protein, identifying direct mitochondrial substrates of FEM1B may provide a more definitive link to its role in mitochondrial processes.To systematically identify mitochondrial protein substrates of FEM1B, we generated two independent lines of FEM1B knockout (KO) cells using CRISPR/Cas9. These two FEM1B KO lines were generated using two distinct sgRNAs to reduce the risk of CRISPR/Cas9 off-target effects and to minimize clonal adaptation impacting the proteomes. Comparative quantitative analysis using tandem mass tags (TMTs) proteomics (Extended Data Fig. 1a) revealed distinct proteomic profiles between FEM1B KO and parental cells (Extended Data Fig. 1b). Of the 10,164 quantified proteins, 13 were significantly altered in FEM1B KO cells, with eight upregulated and five downregulated proteins (Welch’s T-test, S0 = 0.32, 5% FDR) (Fig. 1a, Supplementary Data Set 1). The proteomic experiments successfully identified several known substrates of FEM1B such as BEX1, BEX2 and BEX4 36,40,41 (Fig. 1a and Extended Data Fig. 1c). BEX3 and FNIP1, additional bona fide substrates of FEM1B, showed increased protein levels in both KO lines, but did not reach the statistical significance cutoff (Extended Data Fig. 1c, Supplementary Data Set 1). As expected, the protein levels of FEM1B itself were strongly decreased (Fig. 1b). Importantly, we achieved extensive coverage of the mitochondrial proteome (88.6%), identifying 1003 out of 1132 mitochondrial proteins annotated in MitoCarta3.042 (Supplementary Data Set 1). Notably, PLD6 (n = 5 independent peptides quantified) was the only mitochondrial protein significantly upregulated (~4-fold) in both FEM1B KO cells (Fig. 1b and Extended Data Fig. 1d). As previous studies have linked dysregulation of PLD6 protein levels to abnormal mitochondrial dynamics 18–21, and considering FEM1B as a potential regulator of PLD6, we postulated that FEM1B deficiency may lead to mitochondrial morphological abnormalities due to PLD6 upregulation. To test this hypothesis, we conducted a detailed investigation into the relationship between FEM1B and PLD6.To validate the regulation of PLD6 by CRL2FEM1B suggested by quantitative proteomics, we probed endogenous PLD6 protein levels using Western blot analysis in CUL2 or FEM1B KO cell lines. Our findings confirmed that PLD6 protein levels were significantly elevated in HEK293T and HeLa cells deficient in FEM1B or CUL2 (Fig. 1c). Next, we analyzed the turnover of PLD6. The Cycloheximide (CHX) chase experiments demonstrated that PLD6 turnover is attenuated in FEM1B KO cells, similar to FNIP1, a FEM1B substrate serving as a positive control (Fig. 1d). Importantly, PLD6 mRNA levels were not increased but actually decreased following FEM1B ablation (Fig. 1e), ruling out transcriptional regulation. Given that FEM1B can regulate either the turnover of mitochondrial PLD6 or the levels of pre-imported protein, we assessed PLD6 protein levels in both mitochondrial and cytoplasmic fractions. We observed an increase in PLD6 levels exclusively in the mitochondrial fraction, with no change in the cytoplasmic fraction (Fig. 1f), indicating that FEM1B specifically targets mitochondrial PLD6 for degradation. As expected, PLD6 protein levels increased in cells treated with E1 (MLN7243), CRL (MLN4294), or proteasome (Bortezomib) inhibitors (Fig. 1g), demonstrating that its degradation depends on ubiquitination, CRL activity and the proteasome, respectively, similar to the positive control CRL2 substrate TSPYL1 (Fig. 1g). Most importantly, the regulation of PLD6 by FEM1B is FNIP1-independent as PLD6 levels remain elevated in FEM1B KO cells even after FNIP1 is ablated (Fig. 1h).CRL2FEM1B interacts with PLD6 to mediate its degradationTo further explore the regulation of PLD6 by CRL2FEM1B, we investigated the physical interaction between FEM1B and PLD6 in cells. Due to the lack of effective endogenous antibodies for immunoprecipitation (IP), we created cell lines stably expressing Doxycycline (DOX) inducible HA-FEM1B by lentiviral transduction. IP of HA-FEM1B co-precipitated PLD6 and FNIP1, which was used as a positive control substrate interactor (Fig. 1i), confirming the interaction between FEM1B and PLD6 in cells. To further verify the direct interaction, recombinant FEM1B was expressed and purified in complex with EloB and EloC, which are known to improve its solubility37. PLD6 is a signal-anchored mitochondrial outer membrane protein, with its N-terminus located in the intermembrane space of the mitochondria and C-terminus facing the cytosol18 (Extended Data Fig. 2a). Fusion of the cytoplasmic tail of PLD6 (residues 28–252) to GST followed by GST pulldown experiment demonstrated a direct interaction between PLD628–252 and FEM1B, while GST alone did not bind FEM1B (Fig. 1j).To map the minimal PLD6 degron, a series of truncation mutants were generated. Deletion of the N-terminus (PLD636–252) did not affect FEM1B binding (Extended Data Fig. 2b), while a C-terminal truncation (PLD625–215) abolished it (Extended Data Fig. 2c), thereby mapping the FEM1B interaction site to residues 215–252 of PLD6. Further truncation analysis narrowed the degron to residues 215–239, as PLD636–239 retained binding, while PLD636–231 exhibited markedly reduced binding (Extended Data Fig. 2d). Therefore, although the degron is located in the C-terminal region of the cytoplasmic tail, it does not conform to a canonical C-degron, as it is not situated at the extreme C-terminus. Isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC) measurements revealed a weak binding affinity between the FEM1B-EloB-EloC complex and PLD6 cytoplasmic tail (KD ~20 μM) (Fig. 1k), weaker than FEM1B's interaction with other degron substrates (KD ~4 μM)37. Notably, FEM1B KO HeLa cells exhibited perinuclear clustering of mitochondria (Fig. 3a) and reduction in various morphological parameters, including mitochondrial area (Fig. 3b), perimeter, branch length, and the total number of branches and branch junctions (Extended Data Fig. 4a,b). As the anti-FEM1B antibody was not applicable for immunofluorescence, HeLa and U2OS cells stably expressing HA-FLAG-FEM1B were analyzed. Thus, our results indicate that TOM20-dependent mitochondrial localization is essential for FEM1B to specifically regulate PLD6 degradation.Molecular insights into the TOM20-FEM1B interactionWe next sought to define the molecular mechanism underlying the TOM20-FEM1B interaction. By elucidating this interaction, we can specifically disrupt the TOM20-FEM1B complex, allowing for a precise examination of its role in regulating mitochondrial dynamics, without the confounding effects associated with global TOM20 inhibition, given its critical role in protein import. To explore this in greater detail, we employed structural analysis of the TOM20-CRL2FEM1B complex. To this end, we first added an excess of TOM2025–145 to the purified FEM1B-EloB-EloC complex and prepared the cryo-EM sample by mixing it at a 1:1 ratio with the purified CUL2-RBX1 complex (Fig. 5a, Extended Data Fig. 7). While prior research, including our own, has identified CRL2FEM1B dimerization37,40, our 2D classification unexpectedly revealed that TOM20-CRL2FEM1B particles exist as a mixture of dimers and tetramers (Extended Data Fig. 7). This observation was further supported by multi-angle static light scattering (SLS) experiments, which showed peaks corresponding to molecular weights of 427.4 and 778.1 kDa, indicating the presence of both CRL2FEM1B dimer and tetramer configurations (Extended Data Fig. 8).Next, we solved the cryo-EM structures of TOM20-CRL2FEM1B asymmetric dimer1 (dimera1), asymmetric dimer2 (dimera2), and tetramer at nominal resolutions of 3.56 Å, 3.88 Å, and 4.08 Å, respectively (Supplementary Table 1). We further employed local refinement to obtain improved cryo-EM maps for the TOM20-FEM1B complex, FEM1B dimer, and tetramer at nominal resolutions of 3.64 Å, 4.13 Å, and 4.12 Å, respectively. Consistent with our previous findings, the assembly of two asymmetric dimers in the TOM20-CRL2FEM1B complex is similar to the unneddylated form of CRL2FEM1B 37, with minor orientation changes between subunits. The FEM1B-FEM1B interface in the TOM20-CRL2FEM1B complex is identical to that observed in the unneddylated CRL2FEM1B asymmetric dimer (Fig. 5b–d).
