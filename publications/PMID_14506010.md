# Antibiotic-dependent induction of Pseudomonas putida DOT-T1E TtgABC efflux pump is mediated by the drug binding repressor TtgR.
**Authors:** Terán W, Felipe A, Segura A, Rojas A, Ramos JL, Gallegos MT
**Journal:** Antimicrob Agents Chemother (2003)

## Abstract

1. Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 2003 Oct;47(10):3067-72. doi:

Antibiotic-dependent induction of Pseudomonas putida DOT-T1E TtgABC efflux pump 
is mediated by the drug binding repressor TtgR.

Terán W(1), Felipe A, Segura A, Rojas A, Ramos JL, Gallegos MT.

Author information:
(1)Consejo Superior de Investigaciones Científicas, Estación Experimental del 
Zaidín, Department of Plant Biochemistry, Molecular and Cellular Biology, 
Granada, Spain.

Pseudomonas putida is well known for its metabolic capabilities, but recently, 
it has been shown to exhibit resistance to a wide range of antibiotics. In P. 
putida DOT-T1E, the TtgABC efflux pump, which has a broad substrate specificity, 
extrudes antibiotics such as ampicillin, carbenicillin, tetracycline, nalidixic 
acid, and chloramphenicol. We have analyzed the expression of the ttgABC efflux 
pump operon and its regulatory gene, ttgR, in response to several structurally 
unrelated antibiotics at the transcriptional level and investigated the role of 
the TtgR protein in this process. ttgABC and ttgR are expressed in vivo at a 
moderate basal level, which increases in the presence of hydrophobic antibiotics 
like chloramphenicol and tetracycline. In vitro experiments show that, in the 
absence of inducers, TtgR binds to a palindromic operator site which overlaps 
both ttgABC and ttgR promoters and dissociates from it in the presence of 
chloramphenicol and tetracycline. These results suggest that the TtgR repressor 
is able to bind to structurally different antibiotics, which allows induction of 
TtgABC multidrug efflux pump expression in response to these antimicrobial 
agents. This is the first case in which the expression of a drug transporter of 
the resistance-nodulation-division family has been shown to be regulated 
directly by antibiotics.
