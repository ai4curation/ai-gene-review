Mycobacterium tuberculosis is an extremely successful pathogen, and its success 
is widely attributed to its ability to manipulate the intracellular environment 
of macrophages. A central phenomenon of tuberculosis pathology enabling immune 
evasion is the capacity of virulent M. tuberculosis (H37Rv) to induce macrophage 
necrosis, which facilitates the escape of the mycobacteria from the macrophage 
and spread of infection. In contrast, avirulent M. tuberculosis (H37Ra) induces 
macrophage apoptosis, which permits Ag presentation and activation of adaptive 
immunity. Previously, we found that H37Rv induces plasma membrane 
microdisruptions, leading to necrosis in the absence of plasma membrane repair. 
In contrast, H37Ra permits plasma membrane repair, which changes the host cell 
death modality to apoptosis, suggesting that membrane repair is critical for 
sequestering the pathogen in apoptotic vesicles. However, mechanisms of plasma 
membrane repair induced in response to M. tuberculosis infection remain unknown. 
Plasma membrane repair is known to induce a Ca2+-mediated signaling, which 
recruits lysosomes to the area of damaged plasma membrane sites for its 
resealing. In this study, we found that the small GTPase Arl8b is required for 
plasma membrane repair by controlling the exocytosis of lysosomes in cell lines 
and in human primary macrophages. Importantly, we found that the Arl8b secretion 
pathway is crucial to control the type of cell death of the M. 
tuberculosis-infected macrophages. Indeed, Arl8b-depleted macrophages infected 
with avirulent H37Ra undergo necrotic instead of apoptotic cell death. These 
findings suggest that membrane repair mediated by Arl8b may be an important 
mechanism distinguishing avirulent from virulent M. tuberculosis-induced 
necrotic cell death.

Introduction

With nearly 9 million new cases and 1.5 million deaths annually, tuberculosis (TB) remains a global challenge (1–3). Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb) is transmitted through the air and primarily infects alveolar macrophages in the lungs. To evade innate and adaptive immunity, Mtb has developed multiple strategies to subvert mechanisms normally employed by macrophages to clear bacterial infection, including phagosome-lysosome fusion (4–6), antigen presentation (7) and cell death pathways (8–11). With regard to cell death pathways, Mtb inhibits macrophage apoptosis and instead promotes necrosis of infected host cells. This appears to be an important mechanism of immune evasion as apoptosis supports presentation of intracellular antigens, thereby facilitating Mtb elimination (8, 12–15). In contrast, necrosis allows Mtb to exit the macrophages and infect surrounding cells (12–13, 16–17).

Plasma membrane repair is an essential mechanism that allows eukaryotic cells to maintain cellular integrity. Much has been learned about plasma membrane repair through the study of muscle cells and fibroblasts, which are prone to plasma membrane disruptions from intense mechanical forces and cell migration, respectively (18–21). Perturbation of the plasma membrane triggers a calcium (Ca2+) flux that is rapidly followed by exocytosis of lysosomes (22–23). Subsequently, the damaged portion of the membrane is endocytosed to recover the membrane integrity (24). The Ca2+ sensor synaptogamin VII (Syt VII) that localizes to the lysosomal membrane has been shown to play a central role in sensing local Ca2+ concentration elevation and triggering lysosomal fusion with the plasma membrane (22, 25–27).

Notably, membrane repair plays an important role in apoptosis - a pathway of cell death avoided by Mtb. In macrophages infected with the virulent Mtb strain (H37Rv), micro-disruptions in the plasma membrane are not repaired, resulting in part from impaired lysosomal exocytosis and resulting in necrosis (10, 28). On the other hand, infection with the avirulent Mtb strain (H37Ra) induces lysosomal exocytosis and subsequent apoptosis. The mechanisms by which Mtb subverts membrane repair, putting macrophages on the path to necrosis, is unknown.

ADP-ribosylation factor-like 8b (Arl8b) is a small GTPase that localizes specifically to the lysosomal membrane. We and others showed that Arl8b controls delivery of cargoes to the lysosome by binding to its effectors HOPS complex and PLEKHM1, permitting fusion of endosomes and autophagosomes to lysosomes (29–34). Thus, Arl8b has been shown to play a central role in regulating lipid- and peptide-antigen presentation through CD1d and MHC class II, respectively (31, 35). Arl8b also interacts with the kinesin motor Kif5b via a direct interaction with SKIP (SifA and kinesin-interacting protein) that links lysosomes to the microtubule network (36–37). In this case Arl8b promotes the outward movement of lysosomes and lysosome-related organelles from the microtubule organizing center (MTOC) to the cell periphery. Cells depleted of Arl8b are unable to move their lysosomes away from the MTOC (37) and Arl8b-depleted NK cells display a defect in lytic granules secretion (36). Additional studies demonstrate that this interaction with the microtubule network is required for lysosome tubulation in LPS-treated macrophages and Salmonella- infected cells (38–40).

Here, we report that Arl8b is required for the process of plasma membrane repair in controlling the Ca2+-dependent exocytosis of lysosomes in cell lines and primary monocyte-derived macrophages (MDMs). Further, we demonstrate that the Arl8b secretion pathway is crucial to control the outcome of Mtb infection in human primary MDMs. While infection of MDMs with the aviruent Mtb strain H37Ra results in apoptosis, MDMs that are depleted of Arl8b and infected with H37Ra die by necrosis. Thus, in blocking plasma membrane repair mediated by Arl8b, an avirulent Mtb displays virulent microbial pathology resulting in necrotic cell death of infected cells. Together, our results provide a mechanistic insight into how Mtb evades membrane repair to escape from their host cells and infect new cells.

Moreover, control siRNA treated MDMs incubated with 4 μM of ionomycin showed a 3.7 fold increase of LAMP-1 surface expression compare to untreated cells. However, MDMs silenced for Arl8b expression using Arl8b siRNA#1 and Arl8b siRNA#2 displayed only a 2 and 1.9 fold increase, respectively (Figs 3C and 3D). We also confirmed that Arl8b silencing did not affect the total levels of LAMP-1 (Fig 3E). The efficiency of Arl8b gene silencing in MDMs was assessed by Western blot and both Arl8b siRNA sequences were equally effective in strongly reducing the Arl8b expression (Fig 3F). Taken together, these findings show that Arl8b is required for optimal plasma membrane repair in human primary MDM cultures as was the case in cell lines.

Arl8b silencing impairs plasma membrane repair in macrophages infected with avirulent Mtb H37RaWe previously reported that infection of MDMs with the virulent Mtb strain H37Rv or avirulent Mtb strain H37Ra results in an increase of the plasma membrane permeability due to the formation of membrane micro-disruptions. For these experiments, we first silenced the expression of Arl8b (using two different siRNA oligonucleotides targeting Arl8b) in human primary MDMs and then performed the infection with H37Ra at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) 10:1 for 3, 6 and 12 hours. At the end of each time point of infection, MDMs were collected and stained for cell surface of LAMP-1 and analyzed by flow cytometry. As previously reported (28), control siRNA transfected MDMs infected with H37Ra displayed a significant increase of LAMP-1 cell surface staining when analyzed at different time points post-infection (Fig 4A). This result indicates that infected MDMs secreted lysosomes to reseal the plasma membrane upon H37Ra infection. However, Arl8b-depleted MDMs did not display the expected increase in LAMP-1 cell surface expression when analyzed at different times post-infection, indicating that Arl8b depletion severely impacts lysosome exocytosis in MDMs in response to the Mtb H37Ra infection. To confirm these results, we analyzed in parallel the secretion of lysosomal enzymes cathepsin-D and lysozyme in the primary MDM culture supernatants (Fig 4B). At each time point post Mtb H37Ra infection, the culture supernatants of control siRNA- and Arl8b siRNA-transfected MDMs were collected, and the amounts of secreted mature cathepsin-D and lysozyme were analyzed by Western blot. Accumulation of mature cathepsin-D and lysozyme over time (compare lanes 1, 4, 7 and 10) in culture supernatants of H37Ra infected control MDMs was observed (Fig 4B). In contrast, Arl8b depleted MDMs infected with H37Ra showed reduced accumulation of these enzymes in culture supernatants (compare lanes 2, 5, 8 and 11 for the Arl8b siRNA#1 and compare lanes 3, 6, 9, 12 for the Arl8b siRNA#2). The gene silencing efficiency for Arl8b in this assay was confirmed by Western blotting of cell lysates with an anti-Arl8 antibody (Fig 4B). Taken together, these results indicate that the plasma membrane repair process in response to Mtb infection depends on Arl8b.

Apoptosis sequesters the pathogens within the cell, which facilitates efficient pathogen killing and antigen presentation and enhances microbicidal activity by macrophages and dendritic cells that engulf apoptotic corpses. In contrast, necrosis, another form of macrophage death (observed during H37Rv infection), by inflicting plasma membrane lesions allows the bacteria to escape from their host macrophages and infect new cells. Also, apoptosis of Mtb-infected macrophages (as seen upon H37Ra infection) is only ensured, if membrane repair by lysosomal-derived vesicles occurs, which prevents damage to the macrophages and prevents release of intracellular Mtb by resealing large pores in cellular membranes (10, 15, 28, 47). Since we found that lysosomal exocytosis is dependent on the function of Arl8b; we hypothesized that its inhibition would drive MDMs infected with H37Ra into necrosis instead of apoptosis. To assess the apoptosis rate of MDMs infected with H37Ra, we analysed the amount of cleaved caspase-3 by Western blotting. As noted previously, compared to uninfected cells, MDMs infected with H37Ra at MOI 10 and 20 for 6 hours displayed a 17 kDa band corresponding to cleaved caspase-3 indicating that in these H37Ra infected MDMs apoptosis-induction is initiated (Fig 5A). On the other hand, MDMs infected with the virulent strain of Mtb H37Rv undergoing necrosis at the same MOIs, failed to show cleaved caspase-3 protein. We then analyzed the amount of cleaved caspase-3 in Arl8b-silenced MDMs upon infection with H37Ra. Uninfected Arl8b-silenced MDMs displayed a weak cleaved caspase-3 band suggesting that Arl8b is essential for the survival of some cells. Interestingly, upon performing H37Ra infection in control siRNA- and Arl8b siRNA-transfected MDMs, the cleaved caspase-3 band was observed in both the conditions (Fig 5B). This assay allows the discrimination between live and apoptotic or necrotic cells based on the reaction of a fluorescent reactive dye with cellular amines. The reactive dye can enter the compromised membranes of necrotic cells and react with free amines both in the interior and on the cell surface, resulting in intense fluorescent staining. In contrast, only the cell-surface amines of viable cells are available to react with the dye, resulting in relatively dim staining. As previously reported (28), infection of MDMs with the virulent strain H37Rv for 72 hours leads to a higher necrosis rate than the infection with the avirulent H37Ra (Fig 5C). Interestingly, Arl8b-depleted MDM cultures infected with H37Ra for 72 hours showed almost twice as many necrotic cells than control siRNA transfected MDM cultures, while uninfected MDMs did not show any significant necrosis (Fig 5D). Additionally, we performed a MPT assay to measure the percentage of viable cells following infection with H37Ra. Compared to control siRNA treated MDMs, infection of Arl8b-depleted MDMs with H37Ra for 72 hours leads to a loss of viable cells indicating that necrosis is significantly increased (3 fold higher) (Fig 5E). Uninfected control siRNA- and Arl8b siRNA-transfected MDMs showed no difference in their viability. We also stained H37Ra infected MDMs with crystal violet to determine cell death (Fig S3A). In agreement with the results above, Arl8b-depleted MDM cultures showed large number of dead cells (~2 fold) than control siRNA-treated MDMs upon H37Ra infection.Finally, we examined Arl8b-depleted MDMs infected with H37Ra by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). In summary, Arl8b is instrumental in the plasma membrane repair pathway, and the inhibition of this pathway through silencing of Arl8b expression modifies the death modality of H37Ra infected MDMs.

DiscussionWe investigated the role of the small GTPase Arl8b in controlling lysosomal exocytosis and plasma membrane repair. In conditions mimicking plasma membrane injury, Arl8b silencing impaired lysosome exocytosis in HeLa cells as well as in human primary MDMs. In the context of avirulent Mtb infection, human primary MDMs depleted of Arl8b were unable to secrete lysosomes, resulting in necrotic cell death. Thus, depletion of Arl8b enabled an avirulent Mtb to display virulent microbial-induced macrophage responses in causing necrotic cell death in infected cells. This highlights the crucial role of the plasma membrane repair pathway regulated by Arl8b in controlling virulent microbial-induced macrophage responses against Mtb. Since necrotic cell death is well known to reduce effective immune responses and enhance microbial spread, Arl8b likely plays an important role in the outcome of host defense.The small GTPase Arl8b has been previously reported to control several aspects of lysosome trafficking. Based on which effector Arl8b binds (HOPS complex, PLEKHM1 or SKIP/Kif5b), it can control the delivery of endocytosed material to lysosomes (29, 31–32, 34–35) or control lysosome and lysosome-related organelle motility toward the cell periphery, respectively (36–37). Rosa-Ferreira et al. identified SKIP and kinesin-1 as Arl8b binding-partners in directing conventional lysosomes to the cell periphery (37). In addition, we demonstrated that polarization of the MTOC and lytic granules toward the immunological synapse in NK cells depends on Arl8b recruitment of SKIP and the kinesin-1 motor to bind to the microtubule network (36). Now, we extend these findings to define a critical role of Arl8b in membrane repair that determines the difference between apoptotic and necrotic cell dealth in Mtb infection. Our data reinforces the crucial role of plasma membrane repair to dampen Mtb virulence (47). Although plasma membrane repair is inhibited during infection with the virulent Mtb, the mechanism by which Mtb hijacks this pathway is unclear (10, 28). Infection of Arl8b-depleted MDMs with H37Ra results in the cleavage of caspase-3. However, despite caspase-3 cleavage, Arl8b-depleted MDMs infected with avirulent H37Ra undergo necrosis, as occurs during virulent H37Rv Mtb infection (13, 28). This necrosis phenotype resulting from the nonvirulent organism can be explained by assuming two steps. First, the infection of MDMs with avirulent H37Ra does not inhibit apoptosis. The apoptotic machinery is functional explaining the cleavage of the caspaes-3. However, the Arl8b-depleted MDMs are incapable of repairing their plasma membrane, whose integrity is a prerequisite to apoptosis. In contrast, necrotic cell death occurs when cells lose their plasma membrane integrity leading to loss of the cytoplasm from the cell with subsequent swelling of organelles (10, 48). Thus, despite activation of the apoptotic program, the loss of plasma membrane integrity redirects the death modality of the infected cells towards necrosis. Interestingly, it has been reported that T-cells infected with vaccinia virus have altered host-apoptosis resulting in cell death by necrosis (49–50). Another example of an apoptosis/necrosis switch occurs in the setting of energy depletion where ATP depletion blocks completion of the apoptotic process, and results in cell necrosis (50–52). Deciphering the mechanisms by which Mtb hijacks lysosomal exocytosis might reveal new ways to prevent the host macrophages from undergoing Mtb-dependent necrosis and spread of the infection. Rather than targeting Mtb directly, recent studies aim to modulate the host response to the pathogen (53–54). These studies provide evidence that IL-1-induced production of PGE2 by host macrophages limits bacterial infection. This innovative strategy is referred to as host-directed tuberculosis therapy (55–56). The results of our current study lead us to hypothesize that plasma membrane repair controlled by Arl8b is hijacked by Mtb to promote its virulence. An obvious in vivo consequence of Arl8b depletion or Arl8b function, e.g., in Arl8b−/− mice, might be susceptibility to Mtb infection. Modulation of this pathway may therefore be of interest to control the cell death of the host, and consequently inhibit the virulence of the Mtb infection.
