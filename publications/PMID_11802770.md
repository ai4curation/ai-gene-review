Carnitine is indispensable for energy metabolism, since it enables activated 
fatty acids to enter the mitochondria, where they are broken down via 
beta-oxidation. Carnitine is probably present in all animal species, and in 
numerous micro-organisms and plants. In mammals, carnitine homoeostasis is 
maintained by endogenous synthesis, absorption from dietary sources and 
efficient tubular reabsorption by the kidney. This review aims to cover the 
current knowledge of the enzymological, molecular, metabolic and regulatory 
aspects of mammalian carnitine biosynthesis, with an emphasis on the human and 
rat.
