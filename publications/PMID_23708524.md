Abstract

Hormone-stimulated lipolysis is a rapid way to mobilize fat from its storage depot for use in peripheral tissues. By convention, activation of cytosolic lipases via the β-adrenergic receptor (ADRB2)-cAMP signaling pathway is the only molecular mechanism considered to liberate fatty acids from triglycerides stored in lipid droplets (LDs) of cells. Herein, we provide evidence that, aside from the activation of cytosolic lipases, autophagy contributes to this hormone-stimulated lipolysis. The ADRB2-stimulated lipolysis was reduced after inhibition of early or late autophagy using either pharmacological inhibitors or shRNA-mediated autophagic gene knockdown. ADRB2 stimulation has caused a marked increase in the autophagy-targeted LDs for lysosomal degradation, which is dependent on the LD-associated RAB7 as evidenced by the use of both shRNA-mediated RAB7 knockdown and a dominant-negative RAB7 mutant. In addition, RAB7 is involved in unstimulated (basal) lipolysis, and mediates the enhanced basal lipolysis in PLIN1/perilipin 1 knockdown fat cells. In conclusion, our results showed a contribution of lipophagy to both basal and hormone-stimulated lipolysis and that RAB7 plays a pivotal role in the regulation of this autolysosome-mediated lipid degradation in fat cells.

Introduction

When nutrients are plentiful, excess calories are stored in fat cells as triglycerides (TGs) into cellular compartments termed lipid droplets (LDs). Accordingly, during metabolic stress when nutrient supply is limited, cells undertake numerous strategies to adapt and switch to alternate substrate sources such as stored TGs in LDs. During such metabolic stress, breakdown of LDs and hydrolysis of stored TGs occur through the orchestrated activation of cytosolic lipases mediated by hormones, such as epinephrine and glucagon. In brief, hormone binding to an adrenergic receptor activates a coordinated signaling cascade starting with a rise in intracellular 3′-5 cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) levels, which promotes the activation of cAMP-dependent protein kinase A (PRKACA/PKA). PRKACA hyperphosphorylates a major LD structural protein, perilipin 1 (PLIN1) 1 and the subsequent release of Comparative Gene Identification-58 (CGI-58) from its binding with PLIN1, which then activates patatin-like phospholipase domain containing 2/adipose-specific triglyceride lipase (PNPLA2/ATGL). 2 PNPLA2 has recently been established to catalyze the initial, rate-limiting step of hydrolyzing TGs to diglycerides. 3 PRKACA also phosphorylates and activates hormone-sensitive lipase (LIPE/HSL) that was previously perceived as a versatile lipase mediating the complete hydrolysis of TG to glycerol and fatty acids, 4 but this former concept has been reassessed and the enzyme is considered to have a strong preference for diglycerides as substrate and to be critical for the hydrolysis of diglycerides to monoglycerides. 5 , 6 LIPE, to be able to reach the TG core for hydrolysis, is recruited onto the LD surface and interacts with PLIN1 in a PRKACA-dependent manner. 7 Ultimately, MG lipase (MGLL) hydrolyzes monoglycerides into glycerol and free fatty acids. 5 , 8

Another cellular adaptation during metabolic stress conditions is known as macroautophagy (herein referred to as autophagy). Autophagy is an evolutionarily conserved process of sequestering cytosolic components in the double-membrane structure termed autophagosome and delivering them to the lysosome for degradation to recycle and provide alternate substrates in times of metabolic need. 9 LD degradation was previously regarded to be solely dependent on the enzymatic activity of cytosolic lipases. However, a recent finding proves that LDs, or a portion of them, can also be degraded via autophagy. Accordingly, a new term, “lipophagy,” has been introduced to specify this selective autophagy-mediated lysosomal lipid degradation. 10 It is thought that an increased lipophagy facilitates basal lipolysis to counteract the adverse effects of lipid overload. 11 The finding further suggests that LDs might be recognized for autophagy through an LD-associated protein, though the actual mechanism behind LD recognition remains unclear. 10

LD, instead of being just a passive lipid storage compartment, is now considered to be a highly dynamic organelle. Proteomic studies on LDs from different model systems ranging from worms, flies, yeasts and mammals, have uncovered a variety of proteins on its surface, of which many are involved in membrane trafficking processes. 12 - 16 Among the trafficking proteins identified, about 20 are RAB GTPases, with which RAB5A, RAB7, RAB14 and RAB18 are the most consistent and also have been found concomitantly in 3T3-L1 adipocytes after lipolytic stimulation. 12 , 17 RAB GTPase family is comprised of a large number of small GTPases that organize accurate delivery of cargoes to their destination by coordinating membrane identity and vesicle trafficking. 18 The identification of RABs in LD surface suggests that LDs are actively communicating with other intracellular organelles in response to the cellular metabolic state. This is exemplified by RAB18, which has been found mainly in the LD surface where it regulates interaction and possible lipid exchange between LDs and the ER in response to insulin and ADRB2 stimulation. 19 , 20 Similarly, RAB5A has also been shown to regulate the interaction of early endosomes with LDs, possibly to transfer lipid between membrane compartments. 21 Among these LD-associated RABs, RAB5A and RAB7 are most abundant in mammalian LD proteomes. 22 - 25 However, RAB7 has not been investigated for its role in LD function. Nevertheless, RAB7, being primarily associated with late endosomes, is a central factor in endosomal trafficking and is, perhaps, the only RAB found in lysosomes to date. 26

Similar to the late endocytic fusion, the late autophagic process involves the fusion of the autophagosome to the lysosome to form an autolysosome, which also requires RAB7 activity. 24 , 25 Considering the critical role of RAB7 in lysosome/autolysosome biogeny and the recent findings in lipid catabolism involving autophagy of LDs, it is possible that the LD-associated RAB7 is the factor that mediates LD-recognition during lipophagy and thus involved in lipid metabolism. In this study, we reveal that the activation of ADRB2 signaling pathway markedly increases the autophagic activity that targets LDs for lysosomal degradation and contributes in part to the ADRB2-cAMP-stimulated lipolysis in fat cells. We further demonstrate that RAB7 is essential for the recruitment of autophagic components onto the LD surface and hence establish a role for RAB7 in LD degradation in both basal (unstimulated) and hormone-stimulated lipolyses.

Discussion

It is well established that cytosolic lipases mediate the ADRB2-stimulated lipolysis wherein both PNPLA2 and LIPE account for the majority of TG hydrolysis. 35 Herein, we provide evidence that, during ADRB2 stimulation, a subset of LDs are packaged into autophagosomes and delivered to the lysosomes for degradation, and that this autolysosome-mediated degradation partially contributes to the ADRB2-stimulated lipolysis. Furthermore, the active, LD-associated RAB7 likely mediated this cytosolic lipase-independent lipid degradation by facilitating the recruitment of autophagic components and the subsequent fusion with lysosomes.

In recent years, the metabolic significance of autophagy has attracted considerable attention. 11 In addition to its well-characterized function in protein catabolism and removal of cellular components, the role of autophagy in regulating hepatocyte lipid metabolism and adipocyte differentiation has also been explored. 10 , 36 , 37 Recent studies have linked defects in autophagic activity to the dysregulation of lipid homeostasis and development of metabolic disorders such as insulin resistance in obesity. 37 Our findings that, in fat cells, autophagy contributed to both basal and hormone-stimulated lipolysis further heighten the functional significance of autophagy in energy homeostasis. Conceivably, autolysosomal lipid degradation, if defective in fat cells, would result in the energetic compromise in organisms in a situation like “adrenaline rush” when a sudden energy demand from peripheral tissues is triggered during emergency or stressed conditions that stimulates hormone release to rapidly mobilize fat store for energy supply.

Among the 20 LD-associated RAB GTPases, 17 RAB5A and RAB18 have been further studied in relation to LD functions. RAB5A is involved in regulating the interaction of early endosomes with LDs and may mediate the transfer of lipids between LDs and other membrane compartments, 21 while RAB18 is localized to a subset of LDs and may have a role in bringing LDs closer to the ER for catabolism during lipolysis. 19 , 20 On the other hand, despite being the most identified in mammalian LD proteomes, RAB7 is yet to be explored for its role in LD function. In this study, however, we have shown direct evidence that RAB7 is localized on a subset of LDs, and that this localization can be enhanced by the ADRB2 activation and by a decrease in PLIN1 expression level. We can also conclude that RAB7 plays a role in trafficking LDs to the lysosome for degradation by facilitating the recruitment of autophagic components, which is supported by functional studies with Rab7 knockdown and a dominant-negative RAB7 mutant. Taken together, our evidence demonstrated that RAB7 has a direct role in LD function and is associated with both the basal and ADRB2-regulated LD catabolism via the lipophagy machinery.

It has been suggested that lipophagy is a constitutive process in many cell types. 10 Although the mechanism underlying LD recognition and sequestration during lipophagy remains unclear, it has been proposed that specific LD-associated factors may be required to initiate interaction between LDs and the autophagic components. 11 Considering the well-established function of RAB7 in coordinating membrane trafficking from the late endosome and/or autophagosome to the lysosome and RAB7 being LD-associated, it is logical to consider RAB7 as the factor to mediate recognition of LDs by the autophagic machinery during normal and particularly under stressed conditions. This is supported by evidence that Rab7 knockdown reduces the levels of both basal and stimulated lipolysis, while the dominant-negative form of RAB7 abolishes the autophagosome/autolysosome-targeted LDs in both conditions.

There have been reports that protein constituents of the LD surface are heterogenous and are made up of various combinations of members of the PLIN family, 38 , 39 indicating that LDs possess unique components, making them respond differently to the cellular metabolic state. As a result, LDs vary in their suitability to be targeted for lipophagy. Among the PLINs, only PLIN1 has been well studied in its response to ADRB2-stimulation. PLIN1 coats the LD surface and prevents lipases from accessing the TG core. Upon ADRB2 activation, the PRKACA-phosphorylated PLIN1 not only exposes the TG core of LDs to lipases but also serves as a dock site for LIPE to enhance the breakdown of TG or DG core. 33 , 40 Although it remains to be studied on how RAB7 is selectively recruited onto the LD surface, PLIN1 appears to be disposable in this process under both basal and stimulated conditions. Instead, our results support that during the unstimulated state, PLIN1 also wards off RAB7, as it does LIPE and other lipases, from targeting LDs for autolysosomal degradation, since absence of PLIN1 makes the LDs more accessible to RAB7 and the subsequent degradation in autolysosomes.
