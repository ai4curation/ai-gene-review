Abstract

Cystatin B (CSTB), an inhibitor of the cysteine proteases, belongs to the cathepsin family and it is known to interact with a number of proteins involved in cytoskeletal organization. CSTB has an intrinsic tendency to form aggregates depending on the redox environment. The gene encoding for CSTB is frequently mutated in association with the rare neurodegenerative condition progressive myoclonus epilepsy. Increased levels of CSTB have been observed in the spinal cord of transgenic mice modeling SOD1-linked familial amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, a fatal neurodegenerative disease affecting motoneurons. In the present study, we have investigated the relationship occurring between the expression of SOD1 and CSTB either wild-type or double-cysteine substitution mutant (Cys 3 and Cys 64). Whether or not there is a physical interaction between the two proteins was also investigated in overexpression experiments using a human neuroblastoma cell line and mouse-immortalized motoneurons. Here we report evidences for a reciprocal influence of CSTB and SOD1 at the gene expression level and for a direct interaction of the two proteins.

Introduction

Cystatin B (CSTB) belongs to a large superfamily of protease inhibitors (Turk and Bode 1991 ; Turk et al. 1997 ) and forms complexes with papain and cathepsins B, L, and S. CSTB is a monomeric protein, made of 98 amino acid, lacking intramolecular disulfide bonds (Ritonja et al. 1985 ) and organized into a five-stranded β-sheet wrapped around a five turn α-helix, with an additional carboxyl terminal strand running on the convex side of the sheet (Stubbs et al. 1990 ). In vitro, cystatins form highly stable domain-swapped dimers at physiological protein concentrations (Staniforth et al. 2001 ); in the dimerization process, loop1 breaks its non-covalent bonds with the remainder of the molecule and interacts with high affinity aminoacid residues in the same position of a second molecule, giving origin to a dimeric form lacking antiprotease activity. CSTB polymerization and aggregates formation depend on the redox environment, being sensitive to alkaline pH and reducing agents (Cipollini et al. 2008 ). CSTB polymerization occurs in vivo and might be related to CSTB alternative functions other than the known protease inhibition (Cipollini et al. 2008 ).

CSTB has been extensively studied in relation to progressive myoclonus epilepsy (EPM1), an autosomal recessive disease, in which mutations in the CSTB gene have been reported (Pennacchio et al. 1996 ). CSTB mutations related to EPM1 result in lower expression of the mutant proteins, which appear to be cell-specific as it affects only Purkinje cell population (Riccio et al. 2005 ; Lalioti et al. 1998 ; Pennacchio et al. 1996 ). CSTB role in EPM1 is further supported by the observation that CSTB knock-out (KO) mice display neurological alterations similar to those described for EPM1 patients.

The main cytological alteration in CSTB KO mice, as also observed in EPM1, is the loss of cerebellar granule cells, which frequently show apoptotic bodies, chromatin condensation, and other changes typical of the programmed cell death (Pennacchio et al. 1998 ), in spite of the ubiquitous expression (not restricted to cerebellum) and cellular localization of CSTB (Riccio et al. 2001 ). However, how the low levels of CSTB may contribute to EPM1 pathogenesis has not been, so far, clarified. Using a two-hybrid system, CSTB has been shown to interact with proteins related to cytoskeletal structures and the physiological significance of its ability to form protein complexes has been discussed (Di Giaimo et al. 2002 ). CSTB aggregates have also been reported in association with senile plaques in Alzheimer and Parkinson diseases as well as in patients suffering from senile dementia, suggesting that CSTB aggregates could be considered as amyloid constituents (Li et al. 1993 ). It is pertinent to mention that association to Alzheimer senile plaques has been also reported for Cystatin C and a possible neuroprotective role for Cystatin C has been proposed (Kaur and Levy 2012 ).

Interestingly, altered CSTB levels have been observed in transgenic mice overexpressing human mutant G93A-SOD1 form (Wootz et al. 2006 ), one of the SOD1 mutants described in patients with familial amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (fALS), a late-onset neurodegenerative disease characterized by the death of the upper and lower motoneurons, leading to muscle atrophy, paralysis, and death (Rosen et al. 1993 ). fALS-linked SOD1 proteins are very heterogeneous, since point mutations can affect the active site or influence folding or stability (Shaw and Valentine 2007 ), with many mutants maintaining a wild-type-like enzymatic activity.

SOD1 has the main function of preventing accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS). CSTB aggregation has been reported to be related to the cell redox state (Cipollini et al. 2008 ; Zerovnik et al. 2002 , 2006 ); in addition, studies in KO mice for CSTB suggest that this protein may play a role in protecting neurons from oxidative stress, as indicated by experiments on primary cultures of rat cerebellar granules, where silencing CSTB increases cell death caused by exposure to H 2 O 2 (Lehtinen et al. 2009 ). These observations suggest a possible relation between functional alterations of CSTB and SOD1. Both proteins can in fact undergo conformational modifications, leading to the formation of aggregates and CSTB tendency to polymerization is dependent on the cell redox state, which is at least in part dependent on SOD1.

These considerations suggested investigating a possible relation between the two proteins in different cellular model systems.

Discussion

CSTB has been reported to protect neurons from oxidative stress (Lehtinen et al. 2009 ) and SOD1 is involved in controlling the redox state of the cells. Thus their ability to interact may contribute to make neurons less able to maintain a physiological redox state and thus more prone to respond to oxidative stress entering pathways of cell death. Our results show a direct interaction between CSTB and SOD1 proteins in two different cellular systems and suggest the existence of a common mechanism for the transcriptional regulation of two proteins.

CSTB and SOD1 have been largely studied for the alterations described in their expression and/or function in association to neurodegenerative diseases such as EPM1 and fALS.

CSTB polymerization and aggregate formation has been related to the redox environment (Cipollini et al. 2008 ) and on the binding of copper ions. In fact CSTB binds two copper atoms with high affinity and their presence impairs CSTB aggregation (Zerovnik et al. 2006 ). Similarly, mutant SOD1 lacking metal ion binding are prone to formation of insoluble SOD1 aggregates, which can be relevant in the ALS pathogenesis (Johnston et al. 2000 ). Alterations in redox homeostasis resulting from oxidative stress are commonly found in EPM1 (Hurd et al. 1996 ; Edwards et al. 2002 ) and ALS (Shaw and Valentine 2007 ; Shibata et al. 2001 ). An increase in lipid peroxidation, a marker of oxidative damage, together with a decrease of SOD1 and GSH activity have been described in the cerebellum of CSTB KO mice, an animal model for EPM1 (Lehtinen et al. 2009 ), indicating a possible correlation between SOD1 and CSTB expression levels. Oxidative stress has been reported to stimulate CSTB expression, driven by the transcription factor Sp1 (Lehtinen et al. 2009 ). Moreover, Sp1 has also been suggested as a coactivator of the SOD1 gene promoter (Seo et al. 1996 ).

Since both Sp1 and AP1 are regulatory factors for CSTB and SOD1 gene promoters, the existence of a common regulatory pathway may be hypothesized (Alakurtti et al. 2000 ; Seo et al. 1996 ).

As far as the functional consequence of the SOD1/CSTB interaction, it is pertinent to mention that EPM1 patients show an increase in cathepsin B activity (Rinne et al. 2002 ), which has also been reported in primary cerebellar granule cultures of CSTB KO mice (Lehtinen et al. 2009 ), most likely due to the loss of CSTB antiprotease activity (generally associated with EPM1). On the other hand, high levels of cathepsins B, L, and D together with increased CSTB mRNA levels are a common finding in the spinal cord of transgenic mice overexpressing the G93A mutant SOD1 (Wootz et al. 2006 ). Although they share some characteristics and may influence one another’s function in a yet unknown way, no direct relation between the two proteins has been reported so far.

In this context, our data, shown in Fig. 3 , indicate a direct interaction between CSTB and SOD1 proteins, although SOD1 overexpression is necessary in order to technically detect it. Co-IP of CSTB and SOD1 was revealed in two different cellular systems (the human SH-SY5Y line and the mouse hybrid NSC-34 line) and under different expression conditions (transient or stable), using different promoters governing SOD1 expression. In addition, the data reported in Fig. 5 show that the Δcys-CSTB mutant, unable to form aggregate, has an unchanged ability to interact with SOD1, as compared to WT-CSTB, thus giving support to a specific interaction between the two proteins. On the other hand, the lack of G93A-SOD1 interaction with CSTB may be related to the mutant SOD1 tendency to form insoluble aggregates when overexpressed (Cozzolino et al. 2008 ).

In conclusion, our results support the hypothesis of a direct interaction between the two proteins that may be relevant to render cells more responsive to oxidative stress conditions.
