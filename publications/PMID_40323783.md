## Full Text

Abstract

The molecular mechanisms underlying wound-induced tissue and organ regeneration in plants are unclear. Here, we identified a signaling pathway that governs the wound-induced regeneration of Arabidopsis ( Arabidopsis thaliana ) roots, highlighting a key role for the peptide RAPID ALKALINIZATION FACTOR33 (RALF33) and its receptor FERONIA (FER). Wounding triggers RALF33 accumulation, which promotes root regeneration, and fer mutants exhibit an enhanced regeneration capacity. The accumulated RALF33 hinders FER-mediated phosphorylation of the transcriptional co-repressor TOPLESS-RELATED4 (TPR4), impairing TPR4 nuclear localization and inhibiting its interaction with the transcription factor ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR115 (ERF115). ERF115 has greater transcriptional activity when it is not associated with TPR4. Disrupting the FER-targeted phosphorylation sites in TPR4 via alanine substitutions (TPR4 A ) resulted in TPR4 mislocalization and impaired binding of TPR4 A to ERF115. Our findings reveal that RALF33–FER–TPR4–ERF115 compose a key signaling cascade that orchestrates wound-induced regeneration, providing valuable insights into the regulation of plant regenerative responses.

Introduction

Plants possess a remarkable capacity to heal injured tissues and organs through a program known as regeneration. This ability relies on the presence of pluripotent cells in the wounded plant organs, which act as stem cells and provide the foundation for regeneration ( Ikeuchi et al. 2019 ). Upon wounding, plants generate a series of second messengers, including reactive oxygen species and calcium ions, along with elevated levels of wound-related phytohormones, such as jasmonic acid (JA) and ethylene ( Farmer et al. 2014 ; Yan et al. 2018 ; Zhang et al. 2019 ). These wounding signals trigger remodeling of the transcriptome in cells surrounding the wound site, leading to their dedifferentiation and subsequent redifferentiation ( Efroni et al. 2016 ; Ikeuchi et al. 2019 ).

Many factors that regulate regeneration have been identified ( Liu et al. 2014 , 2022 ; Kareem et al. 2015 ; Iwase et al. 2017 ; Zhang et al. 2017 , 2019 ; Zhou et al. 2019 ). For instance, WUSCHEL ( WUS ), the first identified member of the WUSCHEL-RELATED HOMEOBOX ( WOX ) family, is crucial for shoot regeneration ( Zhang et al. 2017 ). The wus mutant in Arabidopsis ( Arabidopsis thaliana ) is unable to regenerate shoots, and in callus, the expression zone of WUS delineates the region where buds are generated ( Zhang et al. 2017 ). Similarly, the plt3 plt5 plt7 triple mutant, harboring defects in members of the PLETHORA ( PLT ) family, had drastically impaired shoot regeneration ( Kareem et al. 2015 ).

Recently, MYC2, a crucial transcription factor in the JA signaling pathway, was identified as being essential for wound-induced regeneration. MYC2 directly binds to the promoters of ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR109 ( ERF109 ) and ERF115 , promoting root-tip regeneration ( Zhou et al. 2019 ). Recent studies have described the REGENERATION FACTOR1 (REF1)–PEPR1/2 ORTHOLOG RECEPTOR-LIKE KINASE1 (PORK1) signaling pathway, which functions upstream of the transcription factor gene WOUND-INDUCED DEDIFFERENTIATION 1 ( WIND1 ) to regulate wound healing and regeneration ( Yang et al. 2024 ). However, the molecular mechanisms linking wound signals to regeneration factors remain largely unknown.

Receptor-like kinases (RLKs) function in transmembrane signal transduction ( Chen et al. 2016 ; Liu et al. 2024 ; Yang et al. 2024 ). FERONIA (FER) is a well-studied RLK that responds to various external stresses, including salinity, mechanical stress, and pathogens ( Masachis et al. 2016 ; Stegmann et al. 2017 ; Feng et al. 2018 ; Zhao et al. 2018 ; Zhang et al. 2020 ; Malivert et al. 2021 ; Chen et al. 2024b ). FER senses cell-wall integrity (CWI) during salinity stress in cooperation with its ligands, the small peptides RAPID ALKALINIZATION FACTOR22 (RALF22) and RALF23 ( Feng et al. 2018 ; Zhao et al. 2018 ). TOPLESS (TPL) and TOPLESS-RELATED (TPR) proteins are corepressors (constituting the TPL/TPR family, TPL, and 4 TPRs) that are extensively involved in plant responses to biotic and abiotic stresses ( Pauwels et al. 2010 ; Jiang et al. 2013 ; Darino et al. 2021 ; Navarrete et al. 2022 ; Griebel et al. 2023 ). As mediators of environmental cues, TPL/TPRs suppress signaling pathways related to ethylene, auxin, JA, and strigolactone in plants. These proteins are also critical regulators of gene expression and interact with multiple transcription factors to exert their repressive function ( Causier et al. 2012 ; Saini and Nandi 2022 ).

In this study, we report the involvement of the RALF33–FER signaling module in initiating root-tip regeneration in Arabidopsis in response to wounding. The RALF33 peptide, which accumulates and inhibits FER phosphorylation after wounding, promotes regeneration, whereas FER inhibits regeneration. FER interacts with and phosphorylates TPR4, facilitating its localization in the nucleus and interaction with ERF115, thereby preventing ERF115 from activating regeneration. We propose a model of the signaling cascade linking wound signaling to root-tip regeneration.

Discussion

Based on the results of this study, we propose a previously undescribed signaling pathway for the regeneration of wounded roots, providing a deeper understanding of root-tip regeneration. Specifically, a RALF33–FER complex perceives wound signals, preventing FER from phosphorylating TPR4. The loss of TPR4 phosphorylation leads to its impaired nuclear localization, while also diminishing the binding affinity between TPR4 and ERF115, thereby blocking the inhibitory effect of TPR4 and permitting transcription by ERF115, triggering regeneration ( Fig. 6 ).

Currently, most studies on wound-induced root-tip regeneration have focused primarily on downstream steps, such as identifying transcription factors like ERF115 and ERF109 which control regeneration ( Zhang et al. 2019 ; Zhou et al. 2019 ; Canher et al. 2022 ). However, how these factors are regulated by wound signals remains poorly understood. Previous research has shown that the transcription factor MYC2, which is involved in the JA pathway, binds to the promoters of ERF109 and ERF115 to initiate regeneration ( Zhou et al. 2019 ). Although the genetic role of MYC2 in root-tip regeneration remains unclear, the regeneration efficiency of ERF115-SRDX (expressing ERF115 fused to a strong repressor domain) is lower than that of the WT ( Zhou et al. 2019 ). This finding suggests that ERF115 is likely regulated by additional signaling pathways beyond MYC2. Recent studies have demonstrated that ERF115 expression is strongly regulated by FER ( Canher et al. 2022 ). The CWI pathway also regulates ERF115 ( Canher et al. 2022 ). Thus, transmembrane signaling might be an upstream event for ERF115 activation. In parallel, extracellular peptide signals, such as REGENERATION FACTOR1 (REF1), have also been shown to have a strong regulatory effect on regeneration ( Yang et al. 2024 ). Our work confirms that the transmembrane signaling module RALF33–FER regulates the activity of the regeneration factor ERF115. More importantly, we made considerable progress toward elucidating the components and regulatory mechanisms involved in this signaling pathway ( Fig. 6 ). Our study reveals some of the upstream molecular components in wound-induced root-tip regeneration and helps fill the gap in the molecular mechanisms linking wounding to regeneration.

Interestingly, single mutants in members of the TPL/TPR family exhibit contrasting genetic phenotypes ( Fig. 3 ; Supplementary Fig. S6 ). Specifically, tpr4 showed significantly enhanced regeneration capacity, whereas tpr1 exhibited impaired regeneration. This result suggests that although the TPL/TPRs family members participate in regeneration in general, they likely act through distinct pathways. Although we detected an interaction between TPR1 and FER ( Supplementary Fig. S7 ), TPR1 did not bind to ERF115 ( Supplementary Fig. S16 ). Therefore, the regulatory effects of TPR1 and TPR4 on root-tip regeneration are likely mediated through different pathways. Given that TPR1 positively regulates regeneration, and TPL/TPRs function primarily as negative transcriptional regulators, it is plausible that they act by repressing negative regulatory factors rather than by activating positive ones like ERF115. We noticed that TPR1 and TPR4 share a conserved Groove 3 in their N-terminal regions, formed by α-helices 5, 7, and 8 ( Supplementary Fig. S11 ; marked by 3 magenta areas); this region mediates interaction with EAR motifs ( Martin-Arevalillo et al. 2017 ). Although there are no notable differences within α5 between TPR1 and TPR4, these 2 TPR members do differ by 1 and 2 amino acid residues in α7 and α8, respectively. We hypothesize that these subtle differences explain why TPR4 can bind to ERF115, whereas TPR1 cannot. This differential molecular recognition may further underpin the distinct biological roles of these 2 TPL/TPR family members, shedding light on their evolutionary specialization. Further structural and biochemical investigations will be essential to substantiate this hypothesis.

Currently, numerous studies have investigated the cross-talk between RALF–FER and auxin signaling pathways in the regulation of root growth. The RALF1–FER signaling pathway initiates auxin signaling by promoting auxin biosynthesis, contributing to the sustained growth inhibition induced by RALF1 ( Li et al. 2022 ). RALF22 functions as a transcriptionally regulated signaling molecule that simultaneously engages in auxin signaling to mediate root hair growth ( Morcillo et al. 2024 ). RALF34 regulates formative cell divisions during lateral root initiation ( Murphy et al. 2016 ; Kiryushkin et al. 2023 ). FER serves as an upstream regulator of the RHO GTPases (RAC/ROP) signaling pathway to influence root hair development ( Duan et al. 2010 ). Additionally, the FER–NADPH signaling pathway controls root hair development by regulating the nuclear/cytoplasmic localization of TRANSPORT INHIBITOR RESPONSE 1 (TIR1)/AUXIN SIGNALING F-BOX (AFB) ( Lu et al. 2024 ). FER is also essential for root nutation, as it regulates asymmetric root growth through polar auxin transport ( Li et al. 2020b ). It will be crucial to investigate whether and how auxin is involved in RALF–FER–TPR4 signaling to regulate wound-induced regeneration.

The connection between RALF peptides and CWI has been extensively studied. For instance, RALF4 and RALF19 interact with LEUCINE-RICH REPEAT EXTENSIN (LRX) proteins to monitor cell-wall changes, thereby regulating pollen tube integrity ( Mecchia et al. 2017 ); and RALF22 and RALF23 act as CWI sensors to mediate response to salinity stress ( Zhao et al. 2018 ). However, none of these studies have reported that the accumulation of RALFs is induced by cell-wall damage. Our findings indicate that RALF33 indeed accumulates upon cellular damage ( Fig. 1D ). However, the underlying molecular mechanisms remain unclear. One plausible explanation involves upstream signals, such as damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). For example, REF1 is known to activate WIND1, a critical transcription factor in regeneration, through a positive feedback loop that amplifies the signal ( Yang et al. 2024 ). This strong connection between DAMPs and regeneration-associated gene expression suggests that DAMPs regulate RALF33 expression. Alternatively, RALF33 might act independently of DAMPs, potentially through a feedback regulatory mechanism involving the RALF–FER pathway. Further studies are required to dissect whether DAMP signaling or a direct RALF–FER feedback loop is responsible for the increase in RALF33 abundance.
