Abstract

Bone Morphogenetic Proteins are key signaling molecules in vertebrate development. Little is known about Bmp gene regulation in any organ. In Drosophila, the Bmp gene, dpp is regulated by Dorsal, the invertebrate homologue of Rel-NFkB. In this study we examined whether TNF-alpha, which activates NF-kB, can regulate Bmp4 gene expression. TNF-alpha reduced Bmp4 mRNA in lung adenocarcinoma A549 cells and repressed transcriptional activity of the human Bmp4 promoter in a dose-dependent manner. Similar repression was observed when the Bmp4 promoter was co-transfected with a p65 (RelA) expression vector in the absence of TNF-alpha treatment, suggesting that RelA mediates the effect of TNF-alpha. In support of this finding, the repressor effect of TNF-alpha on Bmp4 was abrogated by a co-transfected dominant negative mutant of IkB (S32A/S36A). The human Bmp4 promoter contains 3 putative consensus binding sites for NF-kB. Surprisingly, only one of the latter binding sites was capable of binding NFkB. Repressor effect of NFkB was not dependent on any of the three binding sites, but localized to a 122 bp fragment which bound both RelA and SP1. SP1stimulated transcription, whereas increasing doses of RelA opposed this effect. In vivo, TNF-alpha inhibited branching morphogenesis and LacZ gene expression in Bmp4-lacz transgenic lungs. These data support a model in which TNF-alpha-induced RelA interacts with SP1 to bring about transcriptional repression of Bmp4 gene. These findings provide a mechanistic paradigm for interactions between mediators of inflammation and morphogenesis with relevant implications for normal lung development and pathogenesis of disease.

1. INTRODUCTION

BMP4 is a member of the Bone Morphogenetic Protein family of signaling molecules that constitute a subclass of the transforming growth factor-beta superfamily ( Wozney et al., 1988 ; Hogan, 1996 ). In general, BMPs play key roles in a variety of processes in embryonic development, including bone formation and repair, organogenesis, pattern formation in the early embryo, and epithelial-mesenchymal interactions ( Jones et al., 1991 ; Vainio et al., 1993 ; Fainsod et al., 1994 ; De Robertis and Sasai, 1996 ). On the basis of sequence similarities, members of the BMP subfamily can be further subdivided into three subgroups (BMP2 and BMP4 in group 1; BMP3/osteogenin in group 2; and BMP5, BMP6, BMP7 and BMP8 in group 3) ( Hahn et al., 1992 ). Bmp5 and Bmp7 are expressed ubiquitously in the lung mesenchyme and the epithelium, respectively ( King et al., 1994 ; Bellusci et al., 1996 ). Bmp4 expression during lung development occurs with precise spatial and temporal specificity, being highly localized to select epithelial cells forming the branching tip of the airways. The operational connection between lung development and Bmp4 was elegantly demonstrated by ectopic expression of X-noggin , an inhibitor of BMP4 or a dominant negative BMP4 receptor, Alk-6, both of which inhibited branching morphogenesis ( Weaver et al., 1999 ).

Little functional data are currently available regarding the regulation of Bmp4 gene expression in any organ. The genomes of both humans and mice contain a single Bmp4 gene that generates multiple transcripts with 5′-end heterogeneity. Accordingly, two regions of promoter activity on the Bmp4 gene were subsequently identified ( Van den Wijngaard et al., 1999 ). In the mouse, only one of the two promoters has been functionally characterized ( Kurihara et al., 1993 ; Feng et al., 1995 ). The 5′ regulatory DNA of the human Bmp4 gene has been cloned and sequenced ( van den Wijngaard et al., 1996 ). As in the mouse, the two regions of the gene designated hBmp4.1 (distal) and hBmp4.2 (proximal) exhibit activity in cultured cells and generate distinct mRNAs ( van den Wijngaard et al., 1996 ). Within each of the two human promoters, DNA sequence analysis has identified putative binding sites for a number of transcription factors. Amongst these, GATA-1 and Msx have been implicated in the regulation of the hBmp4.1 promoter in osteosarcoma cell lines ( Van den Wijngaard et al., 1999 ). Of interest to the current study, the DNA sequence of the hBmp4.1 promoter includes putative consensus binding sites for members of the transcription factor family NF-kB. The latter suggested that Bmp4 may be a target of regulation by pro-inflammatory cytokines such as Tumor necrosis factor, TNF-alpha.

TNF-alpha is a non-glycosylated protein of 17 kDa with 157 amino acids that acts as a pleiotropic pro-inflammatory cytokine. TNF-alpha is produced primarily by activated macrophages, but also by a variety of other structural cell types including fibroblasts, epithelial and smooth muscle cells. Biological responses of TNF-alpha are mediated by specific binding either via a Type I or a Type II receptor ( Tartaglia et al., 1991 ) expressed on the surface of many cell types. Binding of TNF-alpha to its receptors results in activation of the inflammatory response classically mediated by a wide variety of pro-inflammatory cytokines including interleukins, Interferon-gamma and Transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-beta). In addition, intracellular signal transduction generated by TNF-alpha elicits a wide spectrum of other cellular responses, including modulation of differentiation and proliferation of a variety of cell types, and induction of apoptosis ( Aggarwal, 2003 ). Lung-specific ectopic overexpression of Tnf-alpha .( Miyazaki et al., 1995 ) and other inflammatory cytokines inhibits branching morphogenesis ( DiCosmo et al., 1994 ; Ray et al., 1997 ).

Signal transduction in the TNF-alpha pathway occurs partly through the activity of the NF-kB family of transcriptional factors. In unstimulated cells, NF-kB exists in the cytoplasm as a homodimer or heterodimer of a family of structurally related proteins with a conserved Relhomology domain ( Baldwin, 1996 ). To date, five proteins, RelA (p65), c-Rel, Rel-B, NF-kB1 (p50/p105) and NF-kB2 (p52/p100) have been identified ( Baldwin, 1996 ). The classical NF-kB dimer contains RelA and NF-kB1 ( May and Ghosh, 1998 ). NF-kB is held in the cytoplasm by binding to inhibitory molecules known as IkB ( Baldwin, 1996 ). Cytokines such as TNF-alpha and IL-1beta are potent inducers of NF-kB as are lipopolysaccharides. These cytokines initiate a cascade of events that leads to degradation of IkB by a ubiquitin-proteasome pathway that is triggered by phosphorylation. Degradation of IkB leads to unmasking of the NF-kB nuclear localization signal and its transport to the nucleus as an active transcription factor. It has been well documented that many signal transduction pathways in inflammation, cell proliferation and apoptosis can converge to activate NF-kB ( Mercurio and Manning, 1999 ).

In Drosophila, decapentaplegic, dpp is the invertebrate homologue of Bmp4 . Decapentaplegic is repressed by Dorsal, which is the Drosophila homologue of Rel-NF-kB family involved in the establishment of dorso-ventral axis during development ( Nusslein-Volhard and Roth, 1989 ). Dorsal also activates twist , which is required for transcription of genes encoding FGF receptors. In the vertebrate limb, inhibition of Rel-NF-kB activity represses twist , but stimulates Bmp4 gene expression and results in arrest of outgrowth, suggesting negative control of Bmp4 by NF-kB in limb development ( Bushdid et al., 1998 ; Kanegae et al., 1998 ). In the lung, studies by Muraoka et al (2000) showed that experimentally induced perturbations in Nf-kB gene expression results in abnormal lung morphogenesis. As in the limb, inhibition of NF-kB activity in the lung results in greatly increased Bmp4 mRNA. Based on the Drosophila regulation of dpp by Dorsal , we examined the hypothesis that the human Bmp4 may be a target of negative regulation by members of the Rel-NF-kB family of transcription factors. In the current study, we show that TNF-alpha treatment of lung cells represses Bmp4 gene transcription through the interaction of RelA and SP1 within a specific domain located around the transcriptional start site on the human Bmp4 gene. Thus, the findings establish a functional relationship between mediators of inflammation (e.g. TNF-alpha) and key morphoregulatory molecules, such as Bmp4, that may have significant implications for both normal development as well as pathogenesis of disease.

4. DISCUSSION

Although Bmp4 is a critical regulator of morphogenesis in many organs, regulation of its gene expression has not been adequately studied. Based on temporal and spatial specificity of its expression pattern during embryonic development it is likely that Bmp4 regulation is complex and precisely controlled by various transcription factors and inter- and intra-cellular signaling pathways. In the present study we tested the ability of the pro-inflammatory cytokine, TNF-alpha to regulate the hBmp4.1 promoter activity in lung epithelial carcinoma cell lines. The rationale for the studies was based on a number of observations. First, during establishment of dorso-ventral axis in Drosophila development, Dorsal the homologue of Rel-NF-kB represses dpp , the invertebrate homologue of Bmp ( Nusslein-Volhard and Roth, 1989 ). Second, both in the vertebrate limb and in the lungs, experimentally-induced inhibition of NF-kB leads to increased Bmp4 mRNA levels ( Muraoka et al., 2000 ). Thirdly, the 5' region of the human Bmp4 gene (i.e. hBmp4.1 promoter) contains consensus binding sites for the TNF-alpha activated transcription factor NF-kB.

TNF-alpha treatment of H441 and A549 cells decreased the steady-state level of mRNA from the endogenous resident Bmp4 gene only in the latter cell line ( Figure 1 ). The reason for the lack of a measurable response in H441 cell is unknown, but may reflect differences, such as mRNA stability in Bmp4 gene regulation. TNF-alpha also reduced the activity of a construct containing the hBmp4.1 promoter cloned upstream of luciferase reporter ( Figure 2 ), suggesting that the effect of TNF-alpha was at the transcriptional level. To assess whether the latter observation was mediated through NF-kB, we conducted transient co-transfection studies in which the activity of NF-kB was inhibited through over-expression of a dominant negative IkB, which is not phosphorylated and hence blocks the entry of NF-kB into the nucleus. The results of these studies confirmed that the repressor effect of TNF-alpha on the hBmp4.1 promoter is mediated through the activity of NF-kB ( Figure 3 ). Additional transient co-transfection experiments revealed that the NF-kB protein p50 is an inducer of hBmp4.1 activity whereas RelA or p65 acts as a repressor ( Figure 4 ).

Three NF-kB consensus binding sites were identified by DNA sequence analysis at positions −1085, −577 and −514 within the 5' region of the hBmp4.1 promoter. Direct examination of these sites by EMSA revealed that only one site, located at −514 was capable of binding NF-kB protein ( Figure 5 ). This is not surprising since identification of the NF-kB binding sites was merely based on presence or absence of consensus DNA sequences. Further functional examination, using deletional analysis of the hBmp4.1 promoter revealed no relationship between the three consensus putative binding sites and the repressor activity of RelA, nor stimulatory activity of p50 on the hBmp4.1 promoter. Thus, we have concluded that the binding revealed by EMSA to the site located at −514 has little or no functional consequences on the regulatory role of NF-kB on the hBmp4.1 promoter.

The sites of cis-active DNA elements responsive to both stimulatory activity of NF-kB p50 and repressor activity of RelA were localized by deletional analysis to a fragment of 122 base pairs. This fragment was further divided into three regions and the sequences located within −17 and +20 were shown to bind nucleoprotein complexes that contained both NF-kB RelA and SP1 proteins by EMSA ( Figure 8B ). The ubiquitous transcription factor Sp1 is composed of four transactivation domains and three zinc-finger DNA binding domains that form complexes on GC-rich DNA motifs ( Courey and Tjian, 1988 ). We found a GC-rich motif that fits the sequence requirements as a consensus binding site for Sp1 within the DNA sequences of fragment between −17 to +20 of hBmp4.1 promoter. Furthermore, when cloned upstream of luciferase, an oligonucleotide spanning the region of −42 and +80 (nucleotides) conferred not only basal promoter activity, but also responsiveness to both SP1 and NF-kB proteins. In transient co-transfection studies the promoter activity of the construct containing oligo −42 to +80 increased in response to Sp1 ( Figure 7 ). Importantly, RelA also interacted with the same fragment, but repressed its transcriptional activity. Stimulatory effect of Sp1 was overcome in a dose dependent manner by increasing amounts of the RelA p65 expression plasmid, suggesting that RelA can inhibit the positive impact of Sp1 on pSLA 4.1/−42/+80 ( Figure 7 ). Thus, it appears that at least some of the responsive cis elements are located around the site of transcriptional start site. Presence of Sp1 sites around the transcriptional start site and in coding sequences has been reported for other genes. For example CCCTC-binding factor binds to the region that includes the first two exons of the hTERT gene which encodes the catalytic subunit of Telomerase, and represses its transcription ( Renaud et al., 2005 ). Antagonistic and synergistic interactions between NFkB and other promoter- bound transcription factors have been previously described. Functional interference of Sp1 and NF-kB through the same DNA binding site has been reported ( Hirano et al., 1998 ). In addition, direct protein∼protein interactions between Sp1 and NF-kB transcription factors have also been observed ( Perkins et al., 1993 ; Hirano et al., 1998 ). The data presented in the current report are consistent with the latter model and may explain the molecular basis of NFkB-induced repression of the hBmp4.1 promoter.

To demonstrate the in vivo relevance of the in vitro transient co-transfection experiments, we examined the response of explanted genetically modified embryonic lungs to TNF-alpha. The impact of TNF-alpha on lung branching morphogenesis has not been studied in detail. Early reports suggested that TNF-alpha enhanced embryonic lung branching morphogenesis ( Jaskoll et al., 1994 ). However, numerous studies in both human and animal models have shown a correlation between TNF-alpha and inhibition of lung growth and development. For example, endotoxin which is a potent inducer of TNF-alpha administered to fetal sheep in utero inhibits lung development and causes respiratory distress in postnatal life ( Kallapur et al., 2004 ). Also, the effect of TNF-alpha on lung gene expression, such as that of the pulmonary surfactant protein B has been documented to involve repression ( Berhane et al., 2000 ). In our study we used genetically modified mice that carry a knock-in LacZ gene in the Bmp4 locus. Morphogenesis of E11.5 Bmp4-lacz lungs in culture was inhibited by 10 ng/ml of TNF-alpha ( Figure 10 ). Inhibition was accompanied by repression of Bmp4 gene expression as evidenced by reduced or absent LacZ staining in the branching epithelium ( Figure 10 ). In previously published studies, repression of Bmp4 either through over-expression of Noggin, an inhibitor, or dominant negative receptor in transgenic mice resulted in inhibition of lung morphogenesis ( Weaver et al., 1999 ). Thus, it is likely that TNF-alpha-induced inhibition of branching observed in studies described in Figure 10 may be caused by its repressor effect on Bmp4 gene expression.

The physiological implications of the findings reported here can be important both in the context of normal development as well as in injury and pathogenesis of lung disease. As stated above, Bmp4 is a crucial component of the program that directs embryonic lung morphogenesis and may also be critical for maintenance of pulmonary homeostasis in adult organisms. Muraoka et al. (2000) showed that NF-kB is expressed both in the epithelial and mesenchymal compartments of the lung and experimentally-induced changes in its level lead to changes in Bmp4 mRNA and perturbation of embryonic lung morphogenesis. Also, even though Sp1 is regarded as a ubiquitous transcription factor, its expression is highly regulated during embryonic development and cellular differentiation ( Saffer et al., 1991 ). Thus, it is possible that the activity of NF-kb and its interactions with Sp1, may be a part of a program that controls precise expression pattern of Bmp4 in the lung and other tissue. Furthermore, pro-inflammatory cytokines and TNF-alpha in particular are well recognized as mediators of injury in a variety of disease settings including neonatal chronic lung disease known as bronchopulmonary dysplasia. It is currently thought that mediators of inflammation may interfere with the morphogenetic mediators and derail the normal processes of repair and development, leading to a pathology described as alveolar hypoplasia. The linkage between inflammation and morphogenesis, however, has been elusive. Repressor activity of TNF-alpha-induced NF-kB on Bmp4 gene expression provides an intriguing paradigm for the mechanisms by which inflammation may derail normal morphogenetic processes.
