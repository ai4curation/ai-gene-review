Copper (Cu) is a trace metal that readily gains and donates electrons, a 
property that renders it desirable as an enzyme cofactor but dangerous as a 
source of free radicals. To regulate cellular Cu metabolism, an elaborate system 
of chaperones and transporters has evolved, although no human Cu chaperone 
mutations have been described to date. We describe a child from a consanguineous 
family who inherited homozygous mutations in the SLC33A1, encoding an acetyl CoA 
transporter, and in CCS, encoding the Cu chaperone for superoxide dismutase. The 
CCS mutation, p.Arg163Trp, predicts substitution of a highly conserved arginine 
residue at position 163, with tryptophan in domain II of CCS, which interacts 
directly with superoxide dismutase 1 (SOD1). Biochemical analyses of the 
patient's fibroblasts, mammalian cell transfections, immunoprecipitation assays, 
and Lys7Δ (CCS homolog) yeast complementation support the pathogenicity of the 
mutation. Expression of CCS was reduced and binding of CCS to SOD1 impaired. As 
a result, this mutation causes reduced SOD1 activity and may impair other 
mechanisms important for normal Cu homeostasis. CCS-Arg163Trp represents the 
primary example of a human mutation in a gene coding for a Cu chaperone.

Introduction

Copper is an essential metal that facilitates electron transfer reactions (Festa and Thiele, 2011) and is required by enzymes involved in mitochondrial respiration, antioxidant defense, connective tissue formation, neurotransmitter and melanin biosynthesis and peptide amidation (Kaler, 2011). Excess amounts of copper, however, are toxic due to the production of reactive oxygen species (Rae et al., 1999). Following absorption in the duodenum, copper is taken up into blood and circulated to tissues, including the kidney and liver, the central organ in copper homeostasis. In the liver, copper is incorporated into ceruloplasmin (EC 1.16.3.1), a secreted glycoprotein which functions as an iron oxidase and accounts for approximately 95% of copper measureable in serum (de Bie et al., 2007). Alternatively, hepatic copper is incorporated by other cuproenzymes and excess copper is excreted into the bile.

Several other copper transport proteins are specifically involved in human copper metabolism (Lutsenko, 2010). Copper is taken up into cells via copper transporter 1 (CTR1). Intracellular copper chaperones (Robinson and Winge, 2010) distribute copper to various organelles and protein targets: ATOX1 delivers copper to ATP7A and ATP7B at the trans-Golgi network; COX17, SCO1 and SCO2 ferry copper within mitochondria to the copper-dependent enzyme, cytochrome C oxidase (EC 1.9.3.1); the copper chaperone for superoxide dismutase (CCS) delivers copper to Cu/Zn superoxide dismutase 1 (SOD1, EC 1.15.1.1).

Human disorders affecting copper metabolism with a known molecular pathogenesis include Menkes disease (MIM# 309400), occipital horn syndrome (MIM# 304150), ATP7A-related distal motor neuropathy (MIM# 300489), Wilson disease (MIM# 277900), and aceruloplasminemia (MIM# 604290). These conditions are caused by mutations in ATP7A, ATP7B, or ceruloplasmin (CP) (Das et al., 1994; Ferenci, 2004; Harris et al., 1995; Kaler et al., 1994; Kennerson et al., 2010; Moller et al., 2009) Mutations in human copper uptake or copper chaperone genes have not yet been reported.

CCS is a ubiquitously expressed cytosolic protein responsible for delivery of copper to SOD1 (Culotta et al., 1997; Rothstein et al., 1999). The delivery requires a direct protein-protein interaction involving a CCS-SOD1 heterodimeric intermediate in which SOD1 binds to a highly homologous (SOD1-like) domain of CCS (domain II) (Casareno et al., 1998; Culotta et al., 1997; Schmidt et al., 1999). Studies in liver and erythrocytes of rodents fed copper-deficient diets have shown high concentrations of CCS; thus, CCS levels provide a useful biomarker for copper deficiency (Bertinato et al., 2003; Lassi and Prohaska, 2011; Prohaska et al., 2003a). Conversely, CCS concentrations are reduced in conditions of cellular copper excess, presumably due to increased proteasomal degradation of CCS (Bertinato and L’Abbe, 2003). In a Drosophila melanogaster model, CCS enhanced CTR1-mediated Cu uptake (Hua et al., 2010) supporting the concept that CCS may normally interact with CTR1 in the process of copper uptake.

CCS may also act as a chaperone to another copper-binding protein, XIAP (X-linked inhibitor of apoptosis) (Brady et al., 2010; Mufti et al., 2006). Metallation of XIAP with copper provokes a conformational change or covalent modification which may mediate XIAP degradation, providing a possible link between copper metabolism and apoptosis. XIAP also appears to be implicated in copper homeostasis by influencing hepatic copper excretion through its regulation of MURR1 (also named COMMD1), the protein defective in a dog model of hepatic copper toxicosis (Burstein et al., 2004; van De Sluis et al., 2002). Investigation of a mouse knockout of CCS disclosed no apparent effects on copper uptake and distribution or incorporation into cuproenzymes other than SOD1 (Wong et al., 2000).

Here, we describe an infant, born to consanguineous parents, in whom we documented a homozygous mutation in domain II of the copper chaperone to SOD1. Although this individual’s predominant clinical phenotype (cataracts, deafness, severe developmental delay) was presumed due mutation of SLC33A1 (MIN# 614482) (Huppke et al., 2012), we characterized the biochemical and molecular effects of his CCS mutation, given additional symptoms not evident in other infants with SLC33A1 mutations.

Comparing the clinical details of all patients we became aware that the patient with the concurrent CCS mutation had additional symptoms not evident in the other patients. As a neonate, he showed severe muscular hypotonia, hypoglycemia and a pericardial effusion. At age 18 month he showed rapid developmental regression and epilepsy following a bout of pneumonia. Cranial MRI performed after this crisis showed persistent bilateral thalamic lesions that were not present in the other patients (Fig. 1). Given these finding and since no human disorder has been linked to mutations in CCS, we investigated the biochemical and molecular effects of the p.Arg163Trp CCS mutation.

The pathogenicity of the CCS mutation, c.478C->T, detected in our patient was supported by yeast complementation assays using the S. cerevisiae deletion strain Lys7Δ, the CCS homolog, which showed reduced complementation by p.Arg163Trp compared to wild-type human CCS, indicating impaired Cu delivery to yeast SOD. CCS has three functional domains. Domain I receives incoming copper from Ctr1 and moves it to domain III, which mediates insertion of copper into apo-SOD1. Domain II, which contains residue 163 that is mutated in the patient, is highly homologous to SOD1, and has no catalytic activity but facilitates the recognition and binding of CCS to SOD1 (Schmidt et al., 1999). Other CCS mutations created in domain II have been shown to disrupt binding to SOD1 (Schmidt et al., 2000). Immunoprecipitation experiments showed clearly that the p.Arg163Trp CCS mutation disrupted in vitro binding to SOD1 (Fig. 5C).

The structural importance of Arg 163 was also evident from the crystal structure of CCS domain II (Lamb et al., 2000). Domain II of CCS has over 50% amino acid identity to Cu/Zn SOD1 (Endo et al., 2000). As seen in Fig. 3, Arg163 forms a salt bridge with Asp185 that spans the beta sheets of CCS domain II. This salt bridge is one of several electrostatic interactions among neighboring residues that stabilize the protein fold in this region, including those in the zinc-binding site, the bridge between nearby Asp160 and Arg209, and the bridges between nearby Arg165 and Asp151 and Asp192. Arg163 and Asp185 are conserved in human SOD1 and yeast SOD1, as are Asp 160 and the four residues that bind the zinc ion: His147, His155, His164, and Asp167. In addition, both human and yeast SOD1 have a lysine in the position corresponding to Arg209, retaining the positive charge at this site. The p.Arg163Trp mutation deletes the salt bridge with Asp185 and presumably destabilizes other conserved interactions in this region.

In addition, we documented low CCS protein levels despite normal SOD levels (Fig. 5A) and normal copper egress mechanisms. In CCS−/− mouse fibroblasts transfected with CCS mutated in domain II, turnover of the mutant protein was increased, leading to decreased CCS protein levels (Caruano-Yzermans et al., 2006). The abundance of CCS is normally in inverse proportion to dietary intake of copper, a regulation that is post-translational. In a low copper state, copper is exchanged rapidly from CCS to SOD1 and CCS concentrations become high. In an excess copper state, CCS with copper bound is degraded, resulting in lowering of CCS levels (Bertinato and L’Abbe, 2003). However, in the context of the p.Arg163Trp CCS mutation, these normal physiologic responses would likely be perturbed.

SOD1, representing 1–2% of total cellular protein, is a ubiquitous cytoplasmic enzyme involved in antioxidant defense by converting superoxide to hydrogen peroxide. In mice with targeted disruption of the CCS alleles, SOD1 protein levels were reported to be normal in the original study (Wong et al. 2000) while a subsequent analysis (Prohaska et al 2003) found that SOD1 protein levels in CCS−/− liver were reduced 79% compared to CCS+/+. Both studies showed that SOD1 enzymatic activity was markedly reduced in nearly all tissues (Prohaska et al., 2003b; Wong et al., 2000).

Analyses of hepatic copper metabolism in the CCS−/− knockout mice showed normal copper distribution and incorporation into ceruloplasmin, leading the authors to conclude that CCS was not crucial for proper copper homeostasis (Wong et al., 2000). However, since liver from the CCS−/− mouse showed significantly higher SOD1 activity than all other tissues (about 30% compared to normal), the presence of some functional CCS in this tissue seemed possible. Liver tissue from wildtype, CCS+/− and CCS−/− mouse showed immunoreactive bands on Western blots probed with an anti-CCS antibody (Wong et al., 2000), which suggested presence of a truncated version of CCS. Since the CCS knockout mouse was generated by deletion of CCS exons 1 and 2 (Wong et al., 2000), an in-frame CCS transcript beginning in exon 3, perhaps related to a liver-specific CCS promoter, could explain the additional band visible. The Reference Sequence (RefSeq) collection annotates a representative transcript for Mus musculus CCS and cDNA sequences, co-aligned on the genome and clustered in a minimal non-redundant way by the manually supervised AceView program, support at least seven splice variants (Thierry-Mieg and Thierry-Mieg, 2006). One splice variant in fact lacks CCS exons 1 and 2. Thus, the clinical and biochemical phenotypes in that mouse model may not reflect complete hepatic knockout of CCS.

In the patient’s fibroblasts, we consistently found reduced SOD1 activity despite normal SOD1 protein levels indicating that the p.Arg163Trp CCS mutation disrupts normal SOD1 metallation in this tissue. Our combined data indicate that the mutant CCS cannot deliver copper to SOD1, which could enhance oxidative stress. Since CCS is a cytosolic, rather than endoplasmic reticulum–associated protein, the misfolded protein response we documented may represent a consequence of general cellular oxidative stress.

The p.Arg163Trp CCS mutation may have more global effects on intracellular copper homeostasis through potential interactions with two other proteins, XIAP and COMMD1 (Brady et al., 2010; Burstein et al., 2004; Mufti et al., 2006). CCS is felt to normally deliver copper to XIAP as well as to SOD1. Our results indicate that the p.Arg163Trp mutation disrupts the binding of CCS to SOD1, but does not seem to impair interaction with XIAP (Fig. 4C). It has been proposed that copper delivery to XIAP via CCS leads to autodegradation of XIAP with concomitant upregulation of COMMD1 (Mufti et al., 2006) and enhanced hepatic copper excretion. Alternatively, impaired metallation of XIAP due to CCS mutation could lead to unbridled degradation of COMMD1 and hepatic copper overload. These potential effects could be formally evaluated through in vitro experiments by over-expressing mutant CCS in HepG2 cells, or through study of a mutant CCS-R163W mouse model.

It is unclear whether the signs and symptoms present in this patient but not in the other patients with SLC33A1 mutations namely neonatal hypotonia and hypoglycemia, pericardial effusion, dramatic neurodevelopmental regression after infection, thalamic signal hyperintensity on T2-weighted brain MRI, were related to the CCS mutation with concomitant reduced SOD1 activity. Mutations in SOD1, causing misfolding of the protein and a gain in toxicity, have been found in about 25% of patients with familial amyotrophic lateral sclerosis emphasizing that CCS mutations could act as modifying factors in various neurological diseases (Boillee et al., 2006). To date no human disease directly caused by SOD1 deficiency has been reported. Mice deficient in SOD1 show slow progressive motor loss and enhanced vulnerability to motor neuron loss after axonal injury under stressful conditions (Reaume et al., 1996; Shefner et al., 1999).

Our studies on the biochemical effects of CCS-Arg163Trp, the first human mutation reported in CCS, highlight the importance of this intracellular copper chaperone. Identification of other individuals with homozygous CCS mutations would help clarify whether such defects have pathogenic health consequences in humans.
