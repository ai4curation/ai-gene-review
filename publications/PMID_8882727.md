# Purification and identification of a novel and four known serine proteinase inhibitors secreted by human glioblastoma cells.
**Authors:** Koshikawa N, Nakamura T, Tsuchiya N, Isaji M, Yasumitsu H, Umeda M, Miyazaki K
**Journal:** J Biochem (1996)
**DOI:** [10.1093/oxfordjournals.jbchem.a021244](https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.jbchem.a021244)

## Abstract

1. J Biochem. 1996 Feb;119(2):334-9. doi: 10.1093/oxfordjournals.jbchem.a021244.

Purification and identification of a novel and four known serine proteinase 
inhibitors secreted by human glioblastoma cells.

Koshikawa N(1), Nakamura T, Tsuchiya N, Isaji M, Yasumitsu H, Umeda M, Miyazaki 
K.

Author information:
(1)Division of Cell Biology, Yokohama City University.

Our previous studies have shown that some human cancer cell lines produce 
pancreatic trypsinogen, plasminogen, and tissue-type kallikrein. To understand 
the regulatory mechanism of these proteinases, serine proteinase inhibitors 
secreted by human glioblastoma cell line T98G were analyzed by gelatin reverse 
zymography with trypsin. The serum-free conditioned medium of T98G cells showed 
more than ten trypsin inhibitor bands ranging from 16 to 150 kDa in the reverse 
zymography. Major trypsin inhibitors were purified by trypsin-affinity 
chromatography. Analysis of their N-terminal amino acid sequences demonstrated 
that the purified inhibitors were identical to the secreted forms of amyloid 
protein precursors (APPs), tissue factor pathway inhibitor (TFPI), placental 
protein 5 (PP5)/TFPI-2, and secretory leukocyte proteinase inhibitor (SLPI). In 
addition, a novel 25-kDa trypsin-binding protein, tentatively named p25TI, was 
identified. p25TI showed weak inhibitory activity against trypsin in reverse 
zymography as compared with the other inhibitors. The secretion of multiple 
forms of serine proteinase inhibitors by human cancer cells raises the 
possibility that they might be involved in the abnormal growth of cancer cells.
