Cancer cells often experience nutrient-limiting conditions because of their 
robust proliferation and inadequate tumour vasculature, which results in 
metabolic adaptation to sustain proliferation. Most cancer cells rapidly consume 
glucose, which is severely reduced in the nutrient-scarce tumour 
microenvironment. In CRISPR-based genetic screens to identify metabolic pathways 
influenced by glucose restriction, we find that tumour-relevant glucose 
concentrations (low glucose) protect cancer cells from inhibition of de novo 
pyrimidine biosynthesis, a pathway that is frequently targeted by chemotherapy. 
We identify two mechanisms to explain this result, which is observed broadly 
across cancer types. First, low glucose limits uridine-5-diphosphate-glucose 
synthesis, preserving pyrimidine nucleotide availability and thereby prolonging 
the time to replication fork stalling. Second, low glucose directly modulates 
apoptosis downstream of replication fork stalling by suppressing BAK activation 
and subsequent cytochrome c release, key events that activate 
caspase-9-dependent mitochondrial apoptosis. These results indicate that the low 
glucose levels frequently observed in tumours may limit the efficacy of specific 
chemotherapeutic agents, highlighting the importance of considering the effects 
of the tumour nutrient environment on cancer therapy.

Introduction

The rapid growth of cancer cells can outpace angiogenesis and nutrient diffusion, which creates a hypoxic and nutrient-starved microenvironment1. Cancer cells employ multiple strategies to acquire nutrients to sustain their rapid proliferation and survive this challenging environment1-3. Moreover, nutrient availability in tumor interstitial fluids is different from that in circulation, suggesting distinct nutrient utilization by tumors4. For solid and blood tumors, tumor nutrient availability can be impacted by the interplay between cancer cells, infiltrated immune cells, and non-transformed stromal cells5-9. As metabolic adaptations that uniquely support cancer cell proliferation and survival may provide an opportunity to selectively target cancer cells, advancing our understanding of the impact of the tumor nutrient environment on cancer cell metabolic and signaling pathways is needed to improve cancer treatment.

Glucose is a central carbon source in proliferating non-transformed and transformed cells. Not only a source for cellular energy, glucose is also fundamental for synthesizing essential biomolecules including the sugar backbone of DNA and RNA, phospholipids, and redox-active cofactors such as NADPH. It is well-established that glucose is severely depleted in the tumor microenvironment10, which implies that a metabolic evolutionary stress is imposed on cancer cells. Using a continuous-flow culture vessel (Nutrostat), we previously performed RNAi- and CRISPR/Cas9-based genetic screens to identify metabolic pathways essential for surviving tumor-relevant glucose-depleted conditions (low glucose) and identified specific mechanisms by which oxidative phosphorylation and serine catabolism drive cancer cell proliferation under those conditions11,12.

While this prior work identified metabolic liabilities that can be induced by the tumor microenvironment, there has been limited focus on how the nutrient levels present in tumors could also limit therapy effectiveness. Here, by further analyzing our CRISPR/Cas9 screening dataset, we report that glucose limitation is selectively advantageous for proliferation and survival of cancer cells with loss of the enzymes for de novo pyrimidine synthesis, but not other biosynthetic pathways. Glucose restriction mitigates the consequences of pyrimidine synthesis inhibition by 1) limiting UTP to UDP-glucose shunting and thereby preserving pyrimidine availability and 2) blocking MOMP and consequent activation of the intrinsic apoptotic pathway. Together, our findings demonstrate that the low glucose availability found in the tumor microenvironment selectively confers resistance to chemotherapy targeting de novo pyrimidine synthesis.

ResultsPyrimidine synthesis inhibition effect is glucose dependentTo identify metabolic pathway(s) influenced by glucose restriction in cancer cells, we performed a CRISPR screen targeting 3000 metabolic genes encoding a comprehensive set of metabolic enzymes and transporters, in high (10mM) and tumor-relevant low glucose conditions (0.75-1 mM, low glucose hereafter, Fig.1a). We chose Jurkat cells as an experimental model because their growth in suspension allowed for culture in continuous-flow cell culture vessels (termed a Nutrostat) and because they are only slightly sensitive to low glucose concentrations in these conditions11. Prior work using this dataset focused on genes selectively required in low glucose, nearly all of which support mitochondrial function12. Here, we focus on genes where the effect of inhibition is mitigated by low glucose compared to standard conditions, ranking genes by their differential gene scores (Fig. 1b). The top four hits – CAD, DHODH, UMPS and TYMS – encode enzymes that fall into a single metabolic pathway controlling de novo pyrimidine synthesis (Fig. 1b). To understand whether this result is due to a general effect of glucose limitation on sensitivity to metabolic gene suppression, we compared the effect of metabolic gene inhibition under standard culture conditions to the differential effect under low glucose conditions, and detected no general correlation (Fig. 1b). Thus, the effect of glucose limitation on the requirement for pyrimidine synthesis is highly specific. As Jurkat cells proliferate at a nearly equal rate under high and low glucose conditions in Nutrostats11, and nucleotide expansion via de novo pathways is crucial in proliferating cells13, we found these results unexpected. We validated the results using inhibitors of pyrimidine biosynthetic enzymes (Fig. 1b). N-phosphonacetyl-L-aspartate (PALA) inhibits the aspartate transcarbamoylase activity of CAD, a multifunctional enzyme which catalyzes the first three steps of de novo pyrimidine synthesis14, and brequinar inhibits DHODH, the second enzyme in the pathway15. As DNA replication forks stall upon nucleotide depletion, leading to prolonged S-phase or cell cycle arrest, we assessed the relative sensitivity of pathway inhibition in low glucose by assaying cell proliferation and EdU-based cell cycle profiling. Consistent with the screen results, the IC50 of brequinar and PALA increase 4-fold and 2.6-fold, respectively, in low glucose (Fig. 1c and Extended Data Fig. 1a). We obtained similar results in Raji cells, a B-lymphocyte cell line derived from a patient with Burkitt’s lymphoma whose proliferation is not affected by low glucose culture (Fig. 1d and Extended Data Fig. 1b). Since the pyrimidine salvage pathway operates downstream of CAD and DHODH (Fig. 1b), uridine can bypass their inhibition by feeding the pyrimidine biosynthesis pathway through uridine-cytidine kinases16. Uridine rescued the inhibitory effects of brequinar and PALA (Extended Data Fig. 1c), demonstrating that the impact of these small molecules on proliferation is due to on-target inhibition of pyrimidine synthesis.We then asked whether glucose limitation preserved overall pyrimidine pools despite inhibition of pyrimidine synthesis. However, we were unable to detect significant protection (Extended Data Fig. 2a-b). We reasoned that under conditions of de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition, the consumption of nucleotides exceeds demand, and therefore any produced pyrimidines are almost immediately used. Thus, detection of pyrimidine pools becomes extremely difficult, and pyrimidine availability may only be apparent by the continued production of DNA macromolecules. To test this hypothesis, we used glutamine tracing to query the turnover rate of pyrimidines under glucose limitation and DHODH inhibition. We observed that pyrimidines including UMP, UTP, and CTP were labeled substantially more rapidly in low glucose (Fig. 1e). In contrast, purine pools including IMP (a precursor to ATP/GTP), ATP, and GTP were labeled similarly or only slightly more in low glucose (Extended Data Fig. 2c). We then monitored DNA synthesis in these conditions at the single cell and single replication fork level. EdU incorporation revealed that cells arrest in S phase within 48h of brequinar treatment, which is delayed in low glucose (Fig. 1f,g). Quantitation of EdU incorporation also showed a higher incorporation rate in brequinar-treated cells under low glucose, implying that glucose restriction supports DNA synthesis despite DHODH inhibition (Fig. 1h). To better understand the effects of pyrimidine depletion on replication fork dynamics, we performed a DNA fiber assay, which measures the rate of nucleotide incorporation at individual replication forks. As expected, DHODH inhibition decreased fork speed and increased fork stalling (Fig. 1i,j). Importantly, low glucose culture significantly mitigated the effects of DHODH inhibition, while low glucose culture alone did not impacting replication fork dynamics and cell cycle profile distribution (Fig. 1i,j and Extended Data Fig. 2d). Similar results were observed in Raji cells (Extended Data Fig. 2e,f). These data demonstrate that upon pyrimidine depletion glucose restriction permits continued nucleotide incorporation at the single replication fork level.We then asked whether the proliferative advantage of culture in low glucose extended to inhibition of purine synthesis. We addressed this question using two different IMPDH inhibitors, mizoribine and mycophenolic acid (MPA), to block GMP production17,18. Unlike brequinar and PALA, low glucose only marginally affected the IC50 of these IMPDH inhibitors (Extended Data Fig. 1d-g). In addition, the IC50 of aminopterin, a DHFR inhibitor that blocks de novo synthesis of both pyrimidines and purines19, is unchanged (Extended Data Fig. 1d-g), indicating a selective effect of glucose limitation on pyrimidine synthesis inhibition, consistent with our screening results. This difference might be due to the impact of purine restriction on many cell-essential functions requiring ATP and GTP hydrolysis, which are expected to limit cell viability independent of replication or transcription inhibition20,21. These results further demonstrate that the effects of glucose limitation are not simply related to changes in nucleotide usage related to differences in proliferation. Therefore, we conclude that glucose limitation specifically supports cancer cell proliferation upon inhibition of de novo pyrimidine synthesis.Glucose level impacts replication and apoptosis signalingReplication fork stalling is a hallmark of replication stress22. At the stalled fork, the ATR-Chk1 pathway is activated to coordinate several downstream processes that stabilize replication forks and repair damaged DNA22. These processes comprise the Intra-S cell cycle checkpoint, mediated in part by regulation of CDK2 activity. Unless resolved, stalled forks tend to collapse, leading to severe DNA damage that can trigger apoptotic cell death23,24. We tested whether pyrimidine nucleotide depletion elicited by brequinar or PALA treatment induces replication stress and apoptosis, and if so, whether low glucose is protective. Upon inhibition of CAD or DHODH, phospho-Chk1 (S345), a marker of replication fork stalling, is increased (Fig. 2a). Furthermore, DNA double-strand breaks as assessed by phospho-H2A.X (S139), ssDNA as assessed by phospho-RPA32 (S4/S8), and apoptosis as assessed by caspase-3 and PARP cleavage, are induced in brequinar/PALA-treated cells (Fig. 2a,b). Remarkably, low glucose suppresses all of these responses (Fig. 2a,b), which are rescued by uridine supplementation (Extended Data Fig. 3a). A pan-caspase inhibitor, Z-VAD-FMK (Z-VAD) reversed brequinar or PALA-induced PARP cleavage without affecting phospho-Chk1, demonstrating that apoptosis is activated downstream of replication fork stalling (Fig. 2b). Phospho-RPA32 and phospho-H2A.X were largely rescued by Z-VAD (Fig. 2b), indicating that these consequences of DNA damage are mostly induced upon execution of apoptosis when de novo pyrimidine synthesis is blocked, as previously described in cells undergoing apoptosis induced by genotoxins and FAS signaling25-28. We quantitatively assessed apoptosis in single cells using annexin V and PI staining. At 72h post-treatment with brequinar, we observed a significant reduction in apoptotic cells (annexin V-positive/PI-negative) in low glucose (7% vs. 23%, Fig. 2c). We then determined the viability of these cells by washing out the drug and assaying them for up to 48h. Proliferation of brequinar-exposed cells in high glucose ceased, whereas cells cultured in low glucose continued to proliferate following drug removal (Fig. 2d). Accordingly, PARP cleavage persisted in high glucose, entering late stages of apoptosis within 48h after washout as evidenced by dramatic decreases in both intact and cleaved PARP, while apoptotic markers were not observed in low glucose (Fig. 2d). These data indicate that engagement of apoptosis upon pyrimidine synthesis inhibition can be substantially repressed by low glucose.Brequinar and PALA target the initial, upstream steps of de novo pyrimidine synthesis, blocking production of all species of pyrimidine nucleotides, and both ribonucleotides and deoxyribonucleotides. Therefore, we tested whether inhibitors of downstream enzymes that are responsible for generation of either thymidine or cytidine nucleotides recapitulate the effect of brequinar and PALA toward inducing replication stress and apoptosis, and whether low glucose is protective. Raltitrexed (RTX) and 3-deazauridine (3-DAU) inhibit TYMS and CTPS1, respectively29,30 (Fig. 1b). Hydroxyurea (HU) inhibits ribonucleotide reductase (RNR), which catalyzes conversion of ribonucleotides to deoxyribonucleotides, inhibiting DNA replication31. Like brequinar and PALA, these three downstream enzyme inhibitors (RTX, 3-DAU, HU) activate replication stress and apoptosis in cells under high glucose, as assessed by phospho-Chk1 and cleaved PARP (Fig. 2e,f and Extended Data Fig. 3b). Low glucose can suppress apoptosis induced by these downstream enzyme inhibitors (Fig, 2e,f and Extended Data Fig. 3b). For RTX and 3-DAU, these effects can be rescued by their respective downstream products (thymidine or cytidine) demonstrating on-target effects of the inhibitors at these concentrations (Fig. 2e,f). Prior work has demonstrated that pyrimidine imbalance can contribute to replication fork stalling,32,33 and inhibiting these late-step enzymes that targeting a single pyrimidine species are expected to cause pyrimidine nucleotide imbalance. However, we did not observe substantial mitigation of the effects of RTX on replication stress and apoptosis by co-treatment with 3-DAU, indicating that pyrimidine imbalance is not a primary cause of replication stress (Extended Data Fig. 3c). In a washout experiment, proliferation of RTX-treated cells was halted in drug-free media regardless of glucose concentration, likely due to the prolonged retention of RTX upon entry into cells30,34 (Extended Data Fig. 3d). Therefore, we accompanied washout of RTX with thymidine supplementation, and observed that cells treated with RTX in low glucose retained the ability to proliferate, a result that was not observed in high glucose (Extended Data Fig. 3d). Moreover, apoptosis markers were only cleared in cells treated with RTX under low glucose (Extended Data Fig. 3d). The protective effects of low glucose were also evident in media and oxygen concentrations designed to mimic physiologic conditions 35,36(Fig 2g and Extended Data Fig. 3e). In contrast, and consistent with its IC50, purine synthesis inhibitor MPA equally induced replication stress and apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner in high and low glucose (Extended Data Fig. 3f). Inhibition of pyrimidine synthesis can also limit ribonucleotides necessary for rRNA and mRNA synthesis, triggering apoptosis. However, low glucose had no effect on markers of replication stress and apoptosis induced by treatment with actinomycin D, an inhibitor of mRNA and rRNA synthesis, demonstrating that altered RNA synthesis is not a proximal glucose-regulated mechanism by which pyrimidine synthesis inhibition triggers apoptosis (Extended Data Fig. 3g).To generalize our findings to adherent cancer cell lines derived from solid tumors, we examined the responses of A549 (mutant KRAS), HCT116 (mutant KRAS) and MDA-MB-468 (PTEN-null) to pyrimidine synthesis inhibition. We chose these cell lines based on findings that mutant KRAS-driven cancer cells or PTEN-mutant/null cancer cells are vulnerable to DHODH inhibition37,38. Apoptosis induced upon pyrimidine synthesis inhibition is suppressed under low glucose in all three cell lines based on a reduction in PARP cleavage and phospho-H2A.X (Fig. 2h). Clonogenic assay also supports increased survival of RTX-treated HCT116 cells cultured under low glucose (Extended Data Fig. 3h). In p53-WT A549 and HCT116 cells, brequinar treatment resulted in Chk1 degradation and minimal Chk1 phosphorylation, precluding assessment of suppression of replication stress by low glucose. MDA-MB-468 cells, which express mutant/dysfunctional p53 like Jurkat cells, also suppressed Chk1 phosphorylation upon brequinar treatment in low glucose (Fig. 2h).We then asked whether apoptosis induced by other DNA-targeting agents with different mechanisms of action could be suppressed by low glucose. While the effects of agents targeting topoisomerases (etoposide and doxorubicin) or damaging DNA (cisplatin) on Chk1 phosphorylation and apoptosis were unaltered or mildly suppressed in low glucose, apoptosis induced by aphidicolin, an inhibitor of replicative DNA polymerases, was significantly blunted in low glucose (Extended Data Fig. 3i,j). These data indicate that glucose suppresses apoptosis when it is primarily induced by replication inhibition, while other genotoxic stresses predominantly trigger apoptosis by a distinct, glucose-agnostic, mechanism.Thus, low glucose limits apoptosis induced by inhibition of multiple steps in de novo pyrimidine synthesis, inhibition of dNTP production, or blockade of replication. Remarkably, however, the impact of low glucose on replication fork stalling (Chk1-phosphorylation) differs depending on whether early (CAD, DHODH) or late steps (TYMS, CTPS1, RNR) of pyrimidine deoxyribonucleotide synthesis are inhibited (Fig. 2i). These data indicate that low glucose impacts the response to de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition by two independent mechanisms: (1) low glucose limits replication fork stalling upon inhibition of early steps in pyrimidine synthesis and (2) low glucose inhibits apoptotic pathways triggered by replication stress downstream of nucleotide depletion.Impact of glucose on pyrimidines is independent of salvageTo understand how inhibiting early and late steps in de novo pyrimidine biosynthesis might differ in their impact on replication fork stalling, we considered that perturbing these pathways differ in how they are affected by existing ribonucleotide pools. We first hypothesized that glucose limitation might promote pyrimidine salvage via uridine to circumvent inhibition of the early steps and mitigate consequent replication stress. At least two possible sources could contribute to intracellular availability of uridine, autophagic degradation of ribosomes (ribophagy), a major reservoir of RNA, and serum uridine, typically present at 1-10 μM39-41. We first monitored ribophagy upon de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition using the RPS3-Keima system42. RPS3 is a subunit of 40S ribosome and Keima is a fluorescent protein with a pH-sensitive dual excitation property (neutral vs. acidic)43. Keima is also resistant to lysosomal proteases, which enables immunoblotting assessment of lysosomal processing of RPS3-Keima in cells undergoing ribophagy42,43. Using a previously described CRISPR knock-in approach42, we established Jurkat cells expressing an RPS3-Keima-3xFLAG fusion protein in place of both endogenous RPS3 alleles (Extended Data Fig. 4a). We confirmed reporter function by treating cells with torin1, which induces ribophagy via mTOR inhibition, and observed processing of RPS3-Keima (Extended Data Fig. 4b). Upon breqiunar/RTX treatment, however, there was no evidence of Keima processing in low glucose (Fig. 3a and Extended Data Fig. 4b). Pan-caspase inhibition revealed that the minimal processing observed is likely downstream of apoptosis (Fig. 3a). We further tested whether autophagic recycling of nucleosides and nucleotides underlies the suppression of Chk1 phosphorylation in low glucose by deleting the gene encoding ATG7, an essential autophagic factor. Although ATG7 loss prevents autophagic flux as evidenced by disappearance of LC3B-II, a marker of mature autophagosomes, we observed no significant effect of autophagy inhibition on replication stress and apoptosis in brequinar- or RTX-treated cells (Fig. 3b), suggesting that the protective effects of low glucose are autophagy-independent.We next examined whether serum could serve as a meaningful uridine source by culturing cells in media containing regular serum (FBS) or dialyzed FBS (dFBS). Upon brequinar treatment, culture in media containing dFBS accelerated Chk1 phosphorylation and apoptosis compared to FBS-containing media (Fig. 3c), implying that serum uridine significantly contributes to pyrimidine nucleotide pools. In contrast, these differential effects of serum were not observed in cells treated with RTX (Fig. 3c), which inhibits de novo pyrimidine synthesis downstream of uridine entry.We therefore tested whether salvage of uridine is responsible for the effects of glucose on pyrimidine synthesis inhibition by ablating uridine-cytidine kinases (UCKs). UCK1 and 2 are key enzymes that phosphorylate uridine or cytidine44, whereas UCK-like 1 (UCKL1) is a homologous enzyme with predicted UCK activity. In an initial attempt to delete UCK2, we were unable to secure UCK2-deficient clones, leading us to consider that UCK2 might have an essential role independent of its UCK activity, as suggested by others.45 Indeed, we succeeded in deleting UCK2 in cells with ectopic expression of a kinase-dead UCK2 mutant (D62A)46,47, allowing us to subsequently delete UCK1. These double KO cells retained a considerable amount of UCK activity as evidenced by partial rescue of pyrimidine synthesis inhibition by uridine (Extended Data Fig. 4c). We therefore generated a cell line also lacking the predicted UCK, UCKL1 (UCK tKO), and observed that uridine was unable to rescue the effects of de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition on proliferation and Chk1 phosphorylation, confirming the absence of UCK activity (Fig. 3c and Extended Data Fig. 4d). The protective effects of FBS on replication stress and apoptosis were not observed in UCK tKO cells (Fig. 3c), further demonstrating that serum uridine can suppress the effects of de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition.With these validated UCK tKO cells in hand, we could now ask whether glucose could impact pyrimidine availability via uridine salvage. At 24h after brequinar treatment, Chk1 phosphorylation was robustly induced in UCK tKO cells, an effect which was still suppressed by culture in low glucose (Fig. 3d). Moreover, low glucose suppressed apoptosis in UCK tKO cells as effectively as parental cells (Fig. 3d). These data demonstrate that, while uridine salvage from serum contributes meaningfully to pyrimidine availability, modulating uridine salvage is not necessary for low glucose to protect cells from replication fork stalling and apoptosis upon inhibition of pyrimidine synthesis.Glucose diverts UTP into UDP-glucose synthesisAnother possible mechanism by which low glucose maintains pyrimidine availability upon inhibition of upstream pyrimidine synthesis enzymes is to limit the use of pyrimidine nucleotides by branching pathways. Pyrimidine nucleotide sugars are a major fate of UTP, which is combined with glucose-1-phosphate via UDP-glucose pyrophosphorylase 2 (UGP2), forming UDP-glucose (Fig. 4a). The intracellular level of UDP-Glucose (200uM) rivals that of uridine nucleotides (150uM for UMP and UDP and 300-500uM for UTP)48-50. Since UGP2 activity is largely unregulated and inorganic pyrophosphate (PPi) is rapidly metabolized by cellular inorganic pyrophosphatases, the forward reaction is dominant. In pyrimidine-limited conditions, UGP2 and CTPS1 may compete over limited UTP pools, and the relatively high Km of UTP for human CTPS1 (590uM) versus UGP2 (76uM) indicates that UGP2-mediated production of UDP-Glucose should dominate over CTPS1-mediated production of CTP51,52. Indeed, we observe a better preservation of UDP-Glucose levels under brequinar treatment compared to CTP levels (19% UDP-Glucose remaining versus a nearly undetectable level of CTP) (Extended Data Fig. 2a,b). To test whether a meaningful amount of UTP can be shunted into UDP-glucose, we first asked whether glucose availability influences the synthesis of UDP-glucose. [U-13C]-glucose tracing revealed that the levels of both preexisting (M+0) and newly synthesized (M+6) UDP-glucose were reduced in low glucose compared to high glucose (Fig. 4b). While brequinar treatment reduced UDP-glucose levels as early as 8h, cells in high glucose retained significantly more UDP-glucose (Fig. 4b).We therefore considered whether UGP2 ablation would protect cells in high glucose from the effects of DHODH inhibition, similar to low glucose culture. UGP2-deficient Jurkat cells proliferated slower than parental cells, which was rescued by introducing a resistant UGP2 cDNA (Extended Data Fig. 5). Remarkably, UGP2-deficient cells retained the ability to incorporate EdU in high glucose in response to brequinar treatment to a similar extent as parental cells in low glucose (Fig. 4c,d). In contrast, UGP2 deletion did not affect EdU incorporation when cells were treated with the downstream inhibitor, RTX (Fig. 4c,d). Accordingly, brequinar-induced Chk1 phosphorylation was suppressed by UGP2 loss to the same extent as low glucose culture, whereas RTX-induced Chk1 phosphorylation was minimally altered by UGP2 deficiency (Fig. 4e). Reintroduction of UGP2 rescued the protective effects of its deficiency on replication stress (Fig. 4e). Overall, these data demonstrate that low glucose prevents shunting of UTP into the UDP-glucose synthesis pathway, preserving sufficient pyrimidine availability to prolong DNA incorporation upon inhibition of upstream enzymes, DHODH and CAD. In contrast, inhibition of late steps in de novo pyrimidine synthesis—those downstream of the UTP–UDP-Glucose shunt—still limit pyrimidine deoxyribonucleotide pools and cause replication fork stalling irrespective of glucose level.Low glucose blunts apoptosis upon replication inhibitionAs described above, low glucose limits apoptosis triggered by inhibition of late steps in de novo pyrimidine synthesis or direct inhibition of replication, despite not affecting replication fork stalling. These data indicate that glucose impacts a second process downstream of replication fork stalling. Therefore, we first investigated which caspase cascades are activated by pyrimidine synthesis inhibitors. Genotoxic stress is known to predominantly activate initiator caspases including caspases-8 and −9, which then activate caspase-3, an executioner caspase53,54. Consistently, pyrimidine synthesis inhibitors promoted cleavage of caspases-8 and −9, and the caspase-8 target BID, indicative of their activation (Fig. 5a). Uridine and thymidine reversed the cleavage of caspases-8 and −9 upon treatment with brequinar/PALA and RTX, respectively (Extended Data Fig. 6a), confirming that caspase cascades act downstream of replication stress induced by pyrimidine nucleotide depletion. This activation of initiator caspases was blocked in low glucose (Fig. 5a). In contrast, low glucose failed to suppress FAS death receptor-mediated (caspase-8-dependent) and staurosporine-induced (caspase-9-dependent) apoptosis, demonstrating that low glucose is not a general suppressor of these caspase cascades (Extended Data Fig. 6b,c). Certain genotoxic stressors (etoposide, doxorubicin and UV) can induce extrinsic FASL-activated FAS death receptor signaling by inducing secretion of FASL55,56; however conditioned media from cells cultured in the presence of brequinar/RTX failed to induce apoptosis in unexposed cells (Extended Data Fig. 6d).Under certain circumstances, caspase-8 (as well as caspase-3 and −10) can mediate truncation of BID, a pro-apoptotic protein that can activate MOMP and thereby trigger caspase-9 activation. Therefore, to determine the relative contribution of caspases-8 and 9 to apoptosis induction in this system, we singly deleted them. Notably, deletion of caspase-9 completely blocked BID truncation and apoptosis induction upon pyrimidine synthesis inhibition, while caspase-8 was dispensable (Fig. 5b). We also observed that ectopic expression of cFLIP, which can inhibit caspase-8 and can completely block FAS-mediated apoptosis57 (Extended Data Fig. 6e), had no effect on brequinar/RTX-induced apoptosis despite normal interaction of cFLIP with procaspase-8 and FADD (Extended Data Fig. 6f,g). These data suggest that caspase-3 and/or caspase-8 cleavage downstream of caspase-9 initiates a positive feedback loop mediated by BID, consistent with prior work58,59. Given that caspase-3 also mediates cleavage of caspases-8 and −1060,61, we concluded that BID truncation downstream of caspase-9 and caspase-3 is achieved via this amplification loop.We tested the requirement for this amplification loop in triggering apoptosis mediated by pyrimidine synthesis inhibition by abrogating FADD, a death domain (DD)-containing adaptor protein that forms a complex with caspases-8 and −10 and is required for their activation. Apoptosis was partially suppressed in FADD-deficient cells upon brequinar or RTX treatment, indicating that FADD complexes contribute to apoptosis downstream of caspase-9 (Fig. 5c). Besides the FADD-dependent pathway, a PIDD-driven pathway can be activated downstream of replication stress62. PIDD serves as a scaffold to recruit and activate procaspase-2, which promotes BID truncation63. PIDD deficiency partially rescued apoptosis to a similar extent as FADD loss upon brequinar or RTX treatment, whereas dual deficiency of FADD and PIDD suppressed apoptosis substantially, indicating that FADD and PIDD jointly contribute to the amplification loop (Fig. 5c). However, low glucose culture of FADD/PIDD double-deficient cells further suppressed apoptosis (Fig. 5d). Taken together, these data demonstrate that low glucose modulates apoptosis signaling upstream of caspase-9, which precipitates downstream caspase cascades, including caspase-3, influencing FADD/PIDD-dependent feedback amplification loops involving caspase-8 and BID.As caspase-9 activation is triggered by cytochrome c release via mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP), we considered whether glucose levels impact these upstream steps. Indeed, prior work indicates that mitochondrial state can influence apoptosis execution64,65. We measured cytochrome c release by an established flow cytometry-based assay66 and observed increased cytochrome c release upon RTX treatment, an effect which was suppressed in low glucose (Fig. 5e). Impact of glucose on pyrimidines is independent of salvageTo understand how inhibiting early and late steps in de novo pyrimidine biosynthesis might differ in their impact on replication fork stalling, we considered that perturbing these pathways differ in how they are affected by existing ribonucleotide pools. We first hypothesized that glucose limitation might promote pyrimidine salvage via uridine to circumvent inhibition of the early steps and mitigate consequent replication stress. At least two possible sources could contribute to intracellular availability of uridine, autophagic degradation of ribosomes (ribophagy), a major reservoir of RNA, and serum uridine, typically present at 1-10 μM39-41. We first monitored ribophagy upon de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition using the RPS3-Keima system42. RPS3 is a subunit of 40S ribosome and Keima is a fluorescent protein with a pH-sensitive dual excitation property (neutral vs. acidic)43. Keima is also resistant to lysosomal proteases, which enables immunoblotting assessment of lysosomal processing of RPS3-Keima in cells undergoing ribophagy42,43. Using a previously described CRISPR knock-in approach42, we established Jurkat cells expressing an RPS3-Keima-3xFLAG fusion protein in place of both endogenous RPS3 alleles (Extended Data Fig. 4a). We confirmed reporter function by treating cells with torin1, which induces ribophagy via mTOR inhibition, and observed processing of RPS3-Keima (Extended Data Fig. 4b). Upon breqiunar/RTX treatment, however, there was no evidence of Keima processing in low glucose (Fig. 3a and Extended Data Fig. 4b). Pan-caspase inhibition revealed that the minimal processing observed is likely downstream of apoptosis (Fig. 3a). We further tested whether autophagic recycling of nucleosides and nucleotides underlies the suppression of Chk1 phosphorylation in low glucose by deleting the gene encoding ATG7, an essential autophagic factor. Although ATG7 loss prevents autophagic flux as evidenced by disappearance of LC3B-II, a marker of mature autophagosomes, we observed no significant effect of autophagy inhibition on replication stress and apoptosis in brequinar- or RTX-treated cells (Fig. 3b), suggesting that the protective effects of low glucose are autophagy-independent.We next examined whether serum could serve as a meaningful uridine source by culturing cells in media containing regular serum (FBS) or dialyzed FBS (dFBS). Upon brequinar treatment, culture in media containing dFBS accelerated Chk1 phosphorylation and apoptosis compared to FBS-containing media (Fig. 3c), implying that serum uridine significantly contributes to pyrimidine nucleotide pools. In contrast, these differential effects of serum were not observed in cells treated with RTX (Fig. 3c), which inhibits de novo pyrimidine synthesis downstream of uridine entry.We therefore tested whether salvage of uridine is responsible for the effects of glucose on pyrimidine synthesis inhibition by ablating uridine-cytidine kinases (UCKs). UCK1 and 2 are key enzymes that phosphorylate uridine or cytidine44, whereas UCK-like 1 (UCKL1) is a homologous enzyme with predicted UCK activity. In an initial attempt to delete UCK2, we were unable to secure UCK2-deficient clones, leading us to consider that UCK2 might have an essential role independent of its UCK activity, as suggested by others.45 Indeed, we succeeded in deleting UCK2 in cells with ectopic expression of a kinase-dead UCK2 mutant (D62A)46,47, allowing us to subsequently delete UCK1. These double KO cells retained a considerable amount of UCK activity as evidenced by partial rescue of pyrimidine synthesis inhibition by uridine (Extended Data Fig. 4c). We therefore generated a cell line also lacking the predicted UCK, UCKL1 (UCK tKO), and observed that uridine was unable to rescue the effects of de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition on proliferation and Chk1 phosphorylation, confirming the absence of UCK activity (Fig. 3c and Extended Data Fig. 4d). The protective effects of FBS on replication stress and apoptosis were not observed in UCK tKO cells (Fig. 3c), further demonstrating that serum uridine can suppress the effects of de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition.With these validated UCK tKO cells in hand, we could now ask whether glucose could impact pyrimidine availability via uridine salvage. At 24h after brequinar treatment, Chk1 phosphorylation was robustly induced in UCK tKO cells, an effect which was still suppressed by culture in low glucose (Fig. 3d). Moreover, low glucose suppressed apoptosis in UCK tKO cells as effectively as parental cells (Fig. 3d). These data demonstrate that, while uridine salvage from serum contributes meaningfully to pyrimidine availability, modulating uridine salvage is not necessary for low glucose to protect cells from replication fork stalling and apoptosis upon inhibition of pyrimidine synthesis.

Impact of glucose on pyrimidines is independent of salvage

To understand how inhibiting early and late steps in de novo pyrimidine biosynthesis might differ in their impact on replication fork stalling, we considered that perturbing these pathways differ in how they are affected by existing ribonucleotide pools. We first hypothesized that glucose limitation might promote pyrimidine salvage via uridine to circumvent inhibition of the early steps and mitigate consequent replication stress. At least two possible sources could contribute to intracellular availability of uridine, autophagic degradation of ribosomes (ribophagy), a major reservoir of RNA, and serum uridine, typically present at 1-10 μM39-41. We first monitored ribophagy upon de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition using the RPS3-Keima system42. RPS3 is a subunit of 40S ribosome and Keima is a fluorescent protein with a pH-sensitive dual excitation property (neutral vs. acidic)43. Keima is also resistant to lysosomal proteases, which enables immunoblotting assessment of lysosomal processing of RPS3-Keima in cells undergoing ribophagy42,43. Using a previously described CRISPR knock-in approach42, we established Jurkat cells expressing an RPS3-Keima-3xFLAG fusion protein in place of both endogenous RPS3 alleles (Extended Data Fig. 4a). We confirmed reporter function by treating cells with torin1, which induces ribophagy via mTOR inhibition, and observed processing of RPS3-Keima (Extended Data Fig. 4b). Upon breqiunar/RTX treatment, however, there was no evidence of Keima processing in low glucose (Fig. 3a and Extended Data Fig. 4b). Pan-caspase inhibition revealed that the minimal processing observed is likely downstream of apoptosis (Fig. 3a). We further tested whether autophagic recycling of nucleosides and nucleotides underlies the suppression of Chk1 phosphorylation in low glucose by deleting the gene encoding ATG7, an essential autophagic factor. Although ATG7 loss prevents autophagic flux as evidenced by disappearance of LC3B-II, a marker of mature autophagosomes, we observed no significant effect of autophagy inhibition on replication stress and apoptosis in brequinar- or RTX-treated cells (Fig. 3b), suggesting that the protective effects of low glucose are autophagy-independent.

We next examined whether serum could serve as a meaningful uridine source by culturing cells in media containing regular serum (FBS) or dialyzed FBS (dFBS). Upon brequinar treatment, culture in media containing dFBS accelerated Chk1 phosphorylation and apoptosis compared to FBS-containing media (Fig. 3c), implying that serum uridine significantly contributes to pyrimidine nucleotide pools. In contrast, these differential effects of serum were not observed in cells treated with RTX (Fig. 3c), which inhibits de novo pyrimidine synthesis downstream of uridine entry.

We therefore tested whether salvage of uridine is responsible for the effects of glucose on pyrimidine synthesis inhibition by ablating uridine-cytidine kinases (UCKs). UCK1 and 2 are key enzymes that phosphorylate uridine or cytidine44, whereas UCK-like 1 (UCKL1) is a homologous enzyme with predicted UCK activity. In an initial attempt to delete UCK2, we were unable to secure UCK2-deficient clones, leading us to consider that UCK2 might have an essential role independent of its UCK activity, as suggested by others.45 Indeed, we succeeded in deleting UCK2 in cells with ectopic expression of a kinase-dead UCK2 mutant (D62A)46,47, allowing us to subsequently delete UCK1. These double KO cells retained a considerable amount of UCK activity as evidenced by partial rescue of pyrimidine synthesis inhibition by uridine (Extended Data Fig. 4c). We therefore generated a cell line also lacking the predicted UCK, UCKL1 (UCK tKO), and observed that uridine was unable to rescue the effects of de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition on proliferation and Chk1 phosphorylation, confirming the absence of UCK activity (Fig. 3c and Extended Data Fig. 4d). The protective effects of FBS on replication stress and apoptosis were not observed in UCK tKO cells (Fig. 3c), further demonstrating that serum uridine can suppress the effects of de novo pyrimidine synthesis inhibition.

With these validated UCK tKO cells in hand, we could now ask whether glucose could impact pyrimidine availability via uridine salvage. At 24h after brequinar treatment, Chk1 phosphorylation was robustly induced in UCK tKO cells, an effect which was still suppressed by culture in low glucose (Fig. 3d). Moreover, low glucose suppressed apoptosis in UCK tKO cells as effectively as parental cells (Fig. 3d). These data demonstrate that, while uridine salvage from serum contributes meaningfully to pyrimidine availability, modulating uridine salvage is not necessary for low glucose to protect cells from replication fork stalling and apoptosis upon inhibition of pyrimidine synthesis.

Glucose diverts UTP into UDP-glucose synthesisAnother possible mechanism by which low glucose maintains pyrimidine availability upon inhibition of upstream pyrimidine synthesis enzymes is to limit the use of pyrimidine nucleotides by branching pathways. Pyrimidine nucleotide sugars are a major fate of UTP, which is combined with glucose-1-phosphate via UDP-glucose pyrophosphorylase 2 (UGP2), forming UDP-glucose (Fig. 4a). The intracellular level of UDP-Glucose (200uM) rivals that of uridine nucleotides (150uM for UMP and UDP and 300-500uM for UTP)48-50. Since UGP2 activity is largely unregulated and inorganic pyrophosphate (PPi) is rapidly metabolized by cellular inorganic pyrophosphatases, the forward reaction is dominant. In pyrimidine-limited conditions, UGP2 and CTPS1 may compete over limited UTP pools, and the relatively high Km of UTP for human CTPS1 (590uM) versus UGP2 (76uM) indicates that UGP2-mediated production of UDP-Glucose should dominate over CTPS1-mediated production of CTP51,52. Indeed, we observe a better preservation of UDP-Glucose levels under brequinar treatment compared to CTP levels (19% UDP-Glucose remaining versus a nearly undetectable level of CTP) (Extended Data Fig. 2a,b). To test whether a meaningful amount of UTP can be shunted into UDP-glucose, we first asked whether glucose availability influences the synthesis of UDP-glucose. [U-13C]-glucose tracing revealed that the levels of both preexisting (M+0) and newly synthesized (M+6) UDP-glucose were reduced in low glucose compared to high glucose (Fig. 4b). While brequinar treatment reduced UDP-glucose levels as early as 8h, cells in high glucose retained significantly more UDP-glucose (Fig. 4b).We therefore considered whether UGP2 ablation would protect cells in high glucose from the effects of DHODH inhibition, similar to low glucose culture. UGP2-deficient Jurkat cells proliferated slower than parental cells, which was rescued by introducing a resistant UGP2 cDNA (Extended Data Fig. 5). Remarkably, UGP2-deficient cells retained the ability to incorporate EdU in high glucose in response to brequinar treatment to a similar extent as parental cells in low glucose (Fig. 4c,d). In contrast, UGP2 deletion did not affect EdU incorporation when cells were treated with the downstream inhibitor, RTX (Fig. 4c,d). Accordingly, brequinar-induced Chk1 phosphorylation was suppressed by UGP2 loss to the same extent as low glucose culture, whereas RTX-induced Chk1 phosphorylation was minimally altered by UGP2 deficiency (Fig. 4e). Reintroduction of UGP2 rescued the protective effects of its deficiency on replication stress (Fig. 4e). Overall, these data demonstrate that low glucose prevents shunting of UTP into the UDP-glucose synthesis pathway, preserving sufficient pyrimidine availability to prolong DNA incorporation upon inhibition of upstream enzymes, DHODH and CAD. In contrast, inhibition of late steps in de novo pyrimidine synthesis—those downstream of the UTP–UDP-Glucose shunt—still limit pyrimidine deoxyribonucleotide pools and cause replication fork stalling irrespective of glucose level.

This activation of initiator caspases was blocked in low glucose (Fig. 5a). Notably, deletion of caspase-9 completely blocked BID truncation and apoptosis induction upon pyrimidine synthesis inhibition, while caspase-8 was dispensable (Fig. 5b). We also observed that ectopic expression of cFLIP, which can inhibit caspase-8 and can completely block FAS-mediated apoptosis57 (Extended Data Fig. 6e), had no effect on brequinar/RTX-induced apoptosis despite normal interaction of cFLIP with procaspase-8 and FADD (Extended Data Fig. 6f,g). PIDD serves as a scaffold to recruit and activate procaspase-2, which promotes BID truncation63. PIDD deficiency partially rescued apoptosis to a similar extent as FADD loss upon brequinar or RTX treatment, whereas dual deficiency of FADD and PIDD suppressed apoptosis substantially, indicating that FADD and PIDD jointly contribute to the amplification loop (Fig. 5c). BAX/BAK oligomerization and MOMP are triggered by BH3-only proteins and BH3-mimetics by inducing a BAX/BAK conformational change. Immunoprecipitation assays revealed that conformational change-driven activation of BAK was also blocked by low glucose culture upon RTX treatment (Fig. 5f). Moreover, BH3-mimetic ABT-263 and DOX-inducible expression of tBID, which directly activate BAX/BAK, equally induce caspase-9 activation/apoptosis in high and low glucose (Fig. 5g and Extended Data Fig. 6j). Caspase-8, BID, and associated pathways (PIDD/FADD) can contribute to the degree of apoptosis activation, but are entirely downstream of MOMP and caspase-9 cleavage and not strictly required for caspase-3 activation and PARP cleavage. While the ATR-Chk1 pathway is clearly activated upon inhibition of nucleotide synthesis, activation of this pathway is adaptive and occurs via the CDK2-regulated Intra-S checkpoint. As such, ATR or Chk1 inhibition synergizes with inhibition of de novo pyrimidine synthesis in both high and low glucose conditions to trigger apoptosis (Extended Data Fig. 6l,m).

DiscussionIn the present study, we analyzed a metabolism-focused CRISPR/Cas9 knockout screen we performed previously to identify pathways influenced by a tumor-relevant level of glucose restriction. We found that glucose limitation is protective against the consequences of inhibiting de novo pyrimidine synthesis in cancer cells by two distinct mechanisms: (1) limiting UTP shunting into UDP-glucose synthesis and (2) by preventing BAK activation and thereby MOMP and apoptosis downstream of replication stress (Fig. 6). We propose that the limited glucose available in many solid tumors is a novel metabolic factor that could contribute to resistance to common chemotherapeutics that induce replication stress, especially those targeting de novo pyrimidine synthesis. This resistance could potentially be overcome by combined targeting of the ATR-Chk1 pathway. Salvaged nucleotides provide a primary bypass mechanism against certain nucleotide synthesis inhibitors. Nucleotides can be recovered from circulating intermediates, autophagy, and non-malignant stromal cells such as macrophages and fibroblasts8,9,69,70. We show that serum uridine significantly slows the onset of action for inhibitors targeting the early steps of the de novo synthesis. Indeed, circulating uridine is present at high concentrations compared to other nucleosides71. While insufficient to maintain proliferation upon pathway inhibition, these uridine levels can influence apoptosis induction. Therefore, we posit that prolonged target engagement is crucial in preclinical research and human trials that deplete pyrimidine ribonucleotides.Our work delineates how glucose availability can influence the activity of the UTP-UDP-glucose shunt, enabling resistance to pyrimidine ribonucleotide depletion in tumor-relevant glucose concentrations. UDP-glucose, a key precursor for glycogen synthesis and N-linked protein glycosylation, is the only nucleotide sugar whose synthesis is directly activated by glucose72. UGP2 synthesizes UDP-glucose from UTP and glucose-1-phosphate. It has been shown that UGP2 is a transcriptional target of YAP, and UGP2 knockdown causes reduced levels of glycogen synthesis and protein N-glycosylation, resulting in defective proliferation of PDAC cells73. In proliferating cells, partitioning of UTP into nucleotide production and UDP-glucose synthesis may be balanced to meet demand. When UTP supply is limited, however, shunting into UDP-glucose exacerbates pyrimidine shortage, enhancing the effects of inhibitors blocking pyrimidine ribonucleotide synthesis. DHODH inhibition has been shown to deplete another nucleotide sugar, UDP-GlcNAc, and globally reduce protein O-linked N-acetylglycosylation, impacting differentiation74. Since low glucose further decreases UDP-glucose level and possibly UDP-GlcNAc, conditions which rescue apoptosis in our system, our work does not support the idea that defects in protein glycosylation trigger apoptosis upon DHODH inhibition in proliferating cells.We also delineate how glucose restriction directly promotes cancer cell survival by suppressing apoptosis downstream of replication stress. Because the response to BCL-2 inhibitors is not impacted by glucose level, our data exclude the possibility that glucose restriction directly influences MOMP activation and subsequent cristae remodeling, which is known to promote sensitivity to BCL-2 inhibitors in leukemia64. Instead, upon replication stress, low glucose blocks a process that promotes BAK conformational change, a signal that initiates MOMP. Under standard conditions, BAK associates with MCL-1 and BCL-xL, anti-apoptotic BCL-2 family members75, and numerous redundant BH3-only proteins can mediate the displacement of BAK from MCL-1 and BCL-xL. We hypothesize that replication stress following pyrimidine depletion triggers an apoptotic signal selectively regulating one of these proteins, and a systemic approach will be necessary to identify those that play a major role in the initial activation of MOMP and test whether signaling from the nucleus to those BH3-only proteins is modulated by glucose level. Alternatively, low glucose may regulate a DNA damage response mechanism mediated by the ATR-Chk1 pathway, including fork stabilization and asymmetric segregation of damaged DNA76,77. Indeed, we observe that low glucose culture decreases cellular EdU incorporation despite not affecting replication fork speed, suggesting that glucose restriction may limit origin firing and thereby reduce fork stalling during replication stress.In addition to inhibiting replication, drugs targeting de novo nucleotide synthesis also limit ribonucleotide pools required for mRNA transcription. Purine and pyrimidine synthesis inhibiton and direct inhibition of RNA polymerase have each been shown to trigger p53 stabilization via a ribosome biogenesis cell cycle checkpoint that involves specific ribosomal proteins forming a complex with MDM278,79. Cells with loss of p53 bypass this checkpoint and undergo apoptosis more rapidly. We observe that inhibition of RNA polymerases with Actinomycin D triggers apoptosis through a mechanism that is not suppressed by glucose limitation. Moreover, we find that compounds that cause DNA damage without directly targeting replication trigger apoptosis that are also not suppressed by glucose restriction. In contrast, we observe that inhibition of replication through methods that do not directly reduce ribonucleotide pools triggers apoptosis that can be suppressed in low glucose. Together, these data indicate that there is a specific mechanism to trigger intrinsic apoptosis downstream of replication fork stalling that is impacted by glucose availability, distinct from other mechanisms that trigger apoptosis downstream of other genotoxic stresses, including inhibition of RNA synthesis.In the present study, we model intratumoral glucose concentrations by culturing cells in 1mM glucose. Prior work has documented resistance to chemotherapy or death receptor-induced apoptosis by culturing cells in glucose free conditions or treating with glycolysis inhibitor 2-deoxyglucose80-83. In these studies, chemotherapy resistance is attributed to glucose stress resulting in accumulation of cells in G0/G1, or through limiting ATP levels, while death receptor sensitivity is attributed to glucose limitation modulating cFLIP. However, in the low glucose conditions used here, we do not observe a reduction in the S-phase population, a decrease in replication fork speed, or a change in cFLIP, and ATP levels are minimally affected. As a result, we observe specific protection from perturbations that selectively inhibit DNA replication in low glucose conditions, while not observing resistance to other chemotherapeutic agents that are likely selectively targeting S phase cells, or death receptor agonists that may be affected by cFLIP expression. These results further underscore the substantial differences in metabolism and the response chemotherapy elicited by low glucose levels versus complete glucose withdrawal or glycolysis inhibition.Prior work suggested that DHODH inhibition by brequinar causes a type of cell death triggered by iron-catalyzed oxidative damage, termed ferroptosis84, while others have argued against that hypothesis, demonstrating an off-target effect of brequinar on ferroptosis-suppressing protein 1 at very high concentrations85. The work presented here clearly demonstrates that inhibition of de novo pyrimidine biosynthesis induces apoptotic cell death, and uses concentrations of brequinar whose effects are readily rescued by uridine supplementation. Of note, uridine supplementation itself inhibits de novo pyrimidine biosynthesis, due to feedback inhibition of CAD16. As such, conditions in which uridine is supplemented, used as controls throughout our study, effectively block DHODH activity with no effect on cell viability.In solid tumors, hypoxia and an acidic microenvironment are known to cause chemoresistance1. As hypoxia often coincides with a nutrient-starved microenvironment, glucose restriction may further promote chemoresistance, highlighting the need to consider these factors to improve chemotherapy efficacy. Glucose limitation may also occur in hematologic malignancies. In the deeper region of bone marrow, oxygen pressure is lower compared to blood vessels entering the tissue, which is associated with large clusters of proliferating cells86. This local hypoxic region is likely also hypoglycemic due to elevated metabolic activity, creating a metabolic niche akin to solid tumors. Indeed, glucose levels in bone marrow fluid are significantly lower than peripheral blood87. These data suggest that normal hematopoiesis and perhaps expansion of malignant blood cells can create a hypoxic/nutrient-limited bone marrow niche that might influence the outcome of chemotherapy. Our data indicate that cell lines derived from both solid tumors and hematologic malignancies exhibit reduced sensitivity to pyrimidine synthesis inhibitors upon glucose restriction, and this effect is more pronounced in T and B cell lines. These differences may be related to the heightened apoptosis sensitivity of hematologic malignancies.Recent studies suggest that pyrimidine synthesis enzymes could be promising targets to treat leukemia in the bone marrow. Bone marrow stromal cells can sustain proliferation of leukemic cells by providing substrates for pyrimidine synthesis,6 and targeting this connection using brequinar augments the efficacy of combined regimen of cytarabine and doxorubicin6. Furthermore, DHODH inhibition releases pyrimidine-dependent blockade of leukemic cell differentiation in bone marrow, leading to loss of leukemic stem cell activity74. We anticipate that resolution of the potentially glucose-starved bone marrow niche will further improve the efficacy of pyrimidine synthesis inhibition in these models.

Because the response to BCL-2 inhibitors is not impacted by glucose level, our data exclude the possibility that glucose restriction directly influences MOMP activation and subsequent cristae remodeling, which is known to promote sensitivity to BCL-2 inhibitors in leukemia64. Instead, upon replication stress, low glucose blocks a process that promotes BAK conformational change, a signal that initiates MOMP. Under standard conditions, BAK associates with MCL-1 and BCL-xL, anti-apoptotic BCL-2 family members75, and numerous redundant BH3-only proteins can mediate the displacement of BAK from MCL-1 and BCL-xL. We hypothesize that replication stress following pyrimidine depletion triggers an apoptotic signal selectively regulating one of these proteins, and a systemic approach will be necessary to identify those that play a major role in the initial activation of MOMP and test whether signaling from the nucleus to those BH3-only proteins is modulated by glucose level. Alternatively, low glucose may regulate a DNA damage response mechanism mediated by the ATR-Chk1 pathway, including fork stabilization and asymmetric segregation of damaged DNA76,77. Indeed, we observe that low glucose culture decreases cellular EdU incorporation despite not affecting replication fork speed, suggesting that glucose restriction may limit origin firing and thereby reduce fork stalling during replication stress.

In addition to inhibiting replication, drugs targeting de novo nucleotide synthesis also limit ribonucleotide pools required for mRNA transcription. Purine and pyrimidine synthesis inhibiton and direct inhibition of RNA polymerase have each been shown to trigger p53 stabilization via a ribosome biogenesis cell cycle checkpoint that involves specific ribosomal proteins forming a complex with MDM278,79. Cells with loss of p53 bypass this checkpoint and undergo apoptosis more rapidly. We observe that inhibition of RNA polymerases with Actinomycin D triggers apoptosis through a mechanism that is not suppressed by glucose limitation. Moreover, we find that compounds that cause DNA damage without directly targeting replication trigger apoptosis that are also not suppressed by glucose restriction. In contrast, we observe that inhibition of replication through methods that do not directly reduce ribonucleotide pools triggers apoptosis that can be suppressed in low glucose. Together, these data indicate that there is a specific mechanism to trigger intrinsic apoptosis downstream of replication fork stalling that is impacted by glucose availability, distinct from other mechanisms that trigger apoptosis downstream of other genotoxic stresses, including inhibition of RNA synthesis.

In the present study, we model intratumoral glucose concentrations by culturing cells in 1mM glucose. Prior work has documented resistance to chemotherapy or death receptor-induced apoptosis by culturing cells in glucose free conditions or treating with glycolysis inhibitor 2-deoxyglucose80-83. In these studies, chemotherapy resistance is attributed to glucose stress resulting in accumulation of cells in G0/G1, or through limiting ATP levels, while death receptor sensitivity is attributed to glucose limitation modulating cFLIP. However, in the low glucose conditions used here, we do not observe a reduction in the S-phase population, a decrease in replication fork speed, or a change in cFLIP, and ATP levels are minimally affected. As a result, we observe specific protection from perturbations that selectively inhibit DNA replication in low glucose conditions, while not observing resistance to other chemotherapeutic agents that are likely selectively targeting S phase cells, or death receptor agonists that may be affected by cFLIP expression. These results further underscore the substantial differences in metabolism and the response chemotherapy elicited by low glucose levels versus complete glucose withdrawal or glycolysis inhibition.

Prior work suggested that DHODH inhibition by brequinar causes a type of cell death triggered by iron-catalyzed oxidative damage, termed ferroptosis84, while others have argued against that hypothesis, demonstrating an off-target effect of brequinar on ferroptosis-suppressing protein 1 at very high concentrations85. The work presented here clearly demonstrates that inhibition of de novo pyrimidine biosynthesis induces apoptotic cell death, and uses concentrations of brequinar whose effects are readily rescued by uridine supplementation. Of note, uridine supplementation itself inhibits de novo pyrimidine biosynthesis, due to feedback inhibition of CAD16. As such, conditions in which uridine is supplemented, used as controls throughout our study, effectively block DHODH activity with no effect on cell viability.

In solid tumors, hypoxia and an acidic microenvironment are known to cause chemoresistance1. As hypoxia often coincides with a nutrient-starved microenvironment, glucose restriction may further promote chemoresistance, highlighting the need to consider these factors to improve chemotherapy efficacy. Glucose limitation may also occur in hematologic malignancies. In the deeper region of bone marrow, oxygen pressure is lower compared to blood vessels entering the tissue, which is associated with large clusters of proliferating cells86. This local hypoxic region is likely also hypoglycemic due to elevated metabolic activity, creating a metabolic niche akin to solid tumors. Indeed, glucose levels in bone marrow fluid are significantly lower than peripheral blood87. These data suggest that normal hematopoiesis and perhaps expansion of malignant blood cells can create a hypoxic/nutrient-limited bone marrow niche that might influence the outcome of chemotherapy. Our data indicate that cell lines derived from both solid tumors and hematologic malignancies exhibit reduced sensitivity to pyrimidine synthesis inhibitors upon glucose restriction, and this effect is more pronounced in T and B cell lines. These differences may be related to the heightened apoptosis sensitivity of hematologic malignancies.

Recent studies suggest that pyrimidine synthesis enzymes could be promising targets to treat leukemia in the bone marrow. Bone marrow stromal cells can sustain proliferation of leukemic cells by providing substrates for pyrimidine synthesis,6 and targeting this connection using brequinar augments the efficacy of combined regimen of cytarabine and doxorubicin6. Furthermore, DHODH inhibition releases pyrimidine-dependent blockade of leukemic cell differentiation in bone marrow, leading to loss of leukemic stem cell activity74. We anticipate that resolution of the potentially glucose-starved bone marrow niche will further improve the efficacy of pyrimidine synthesis inhibition in these models.

Slides were incubated in 2.5 N HCl solution for 30 minutes to denature the DNA and then washed with 1X PBS. The DNA fibers were incubated in a room temperature blocking buffer (5% BSA in PBS with 0.1% Triton X-100) for 1 hour and then probed with primary antibodies (mouse anti-IdU [B44] (BD Biosciences 347580, 1:150 dilution) and rat anti-CldU [BU1/75 (ICR1)] (Abcam ab6326, 1:200 dilution)) diluted in blocking buffer. Slides were washed in 1X PBS before being probed with fluorescent-dye conjugated secondary antibodies (goat anti-mouse IgG (H+L) Alexa Fluor 488 (Thermo Fisher A11001, 1:350 dilution) and goat anti-rat IgG (H+L) Alexa Fluor 594 (Thermo Fisher A11007, 1:350 dilution)) diluted in blocking buffer. The stained DNA fibers were imaged by a Keyence BZ-X710 microscope. For each experiment assessing DNA replication fork speed (CldU track length/speed), at least 200 DNA fibers were measured from at least two independent biological replicates, and p values were calculated using the Mann–Whitney rank-sum t-test (ns = no significance). The analysis shows the mean of the biological replicates per condition. Tract lengths were determined in ImageJ using the scale of 1 μm = 2.59 kb91. For each independent biological experiment measuring the percentage of stalled forks (IdU-only tracks) or new origins (CldU-only tracks), at least 150 DNA fibers were analyzed. The analysis shows the mean of at least two independent biological experiments per condition, and p-values were calculated using t-test with Welch’s correction (ns = no significance).
