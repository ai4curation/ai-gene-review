A fundamental process occurring during early development is the zygotic genome 
activation, i.e., the initiation of transcription from the embryonic genome. 
Before that step, cellular processes in the developing embryo are dictated by 
transcripts produced by the maternal genome and accumulated in the egg during 
oogenesis. The maternal-to-zygotic transition (MZT) involves both the clearance 
of maternal RNAs and the initiation of transcription of the embryonic genome and 
is a tightly regulated process. In some species, decay of maternal transcripts 
may be facilitated by the activity of microRNAs. These small RNAs can act 
pleiotropically, blocking translation and inducing destabilization of hundreds 
of different maternal targets. In this review, we will discuss the role of 
microRNAs during MZT, focusing on Drosophila melanogaster and vertebrate models, 
Xenopus laevis, Zebrafish and mouse, in which such a mechanism has been more 
extensively studied.
