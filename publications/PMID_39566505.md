In yeast, multiprotein bridging factor 1 (Mbf1) has been proposed to function in 
the integrated stress response (ISR) as a transcriptional coactivator by 
mediating a direct interaction between general transcription machinery and the 
process's key effector, Gcn4. However, mounting evidence has demonstrated that 
Mbf1 (and its human homolog EDF1) is recruited to collided ribosomes, a known 
activator of the ISR. In this study, we connect these otherwise seemingly 
disparate functions of Mbf1. Our biochemical and structural analyses reveal that 
Mbf1 functions as a core ISR factor by interacting with collided ribosomes to 
mediate Gcn2 activation. We further show that Mbf1 serves no role as a 
transcriptional coactivator of Gcn4. Instead, Mbf1 is required for optimal 
stress-induced eukaryotic initiation factor 2α (eIF2α) phosphorylation and 
downstream de-repression of GCN4 translation. Collectively, our data establish 
that Mbf1 functions in ISR signaling by acting as a direct sensor of 
stress-induced ribosome collisions.

Introduction

During their lifetime, organisms face a multitude of stresses that can be detrimental for cellular homeostasis and that have driven the evolution of distinct stress-response pathways that allow organisms to survive and adapt through regulation of gene expression. One key pathway is the Integrated Stress Response (ISR), a highly conserved eukaryotic mechanism for integrating multiple signals to reprogram gene expression.1 These signals are sensed by protein kinases that phosphorylate the α subunit of initiation factor 2 (eIF2). Mammals have four known eIF2α kinases: GCN2, PERK, HRI, and PKR – which are activated in response to amino-acid starvation, ER stress, cytoplasmic protein misfolding and viral infection, respectively. Yeast has only one such kinase, Gcn2, and hence has been effectively used as a model organism for studying a streamlined ISR.2–6 Phosphorylation of eIF2α by Gcn2 represses global translation,7,8 and results in the selective translation of pro-survival genes including, in yeast, the key ISR effector GCN4, a transcription factor with hundreds of target genes.4,9 In humans, selective translation of ATF4 serves a similar purpose.10–12 Both GCN4 and ATF4 are translated via an unusual translational de-repression mechanism when eIF2α is phosphorylated.4 The transcripts of both genes harbor upstream open reading frames (uORFs) that inhibit translation of the main ORF under normal conditions12,13 but not stress conditions. In the latter case, a portion of eIF2α is phosphorylated, reducing formation of initiator tRNA ternary complex such that the ribosomal small subunit bypasses the inhibitory uORFs during scanning and initiates translation on the main ORF of GCN4/ATF4.4,12,14–16

Over the past several decades, much effort has focused on understanding the mechanism of Gcn2 activation in response to amino-acid starvation and more recently, to general stress conditions.17–19
In vivo, Gcn2 activity is fully dependent on the presence of the coactivator Gcn1 and less so on another coactivator Gcn20.20–23 Initial models suggested that Gcn2, with the help of Gcn1 and Gcn20, is activated by deacylated tRNA binding to Gcn2’s histidyl-tRNA synthetase-like domain.24–27 However, this model could not explain how various cellular stresses that do not necessarily result in accumulation of deacylated tRNAs can lead to robust Gcn2 activation.17,28–30 Consequently, recent evidence has pointed to a ribosome-centric mode of activation. Support for such a model began with a study by Ishimura and Nagy et al. which reported elevated ribosome stalling and GCN2-mediated eIF2α phosphorylation in neurons of mice deficient in tRNAArgUCU (but not presenting with deacylated tRNAs) and the ribosome recycling factor GTPBP2.29 Further support came from two independent groups who showed ribosomes, and in particular the ribosomal P-stalk, activate GCN2 far more robustly than deacylated tRNAs.31,32 More recent work from our groups revealed that Gcn2 is activated by collided ribosomes.18,19 Consistent with these ideas, structural studies show that Gcn1 binds collided ribosomes.33,34

The transcription factor Gcn4 is the key effector of the ISR while the highly conserved multiprotein bridging factor 1 (Mbf1; EDF1 in humans) was identified as a transcriptional coactivator of Gcn4. This co-activation was thought to occur through Mbf1 binding both Gcn4 and TATA binding protein (Spt15 in yeast), thus recruiting the general transcriptional machinery to Gcn4-bound promoters.35,36 Other studies extended Mbf1’s coactivation function to other transcription factors in different species, such as JUN in humans.36 However, these early studies on Mbf1 function were primarily based on in vitro experiments with recombinant proteins, providing little direct evidence for the proposed coactivation mechanism.

In contrast to its proposed transcriptional coactivation role in the nucleus, Mbf1 has been shown to bind ribosome collisions in the cytoplasm.37,38 Moreover, in agreement with a ribosome-centric function, Mbf1 prevents +1 frameshifting during ribosome stalling.39,40 At first glance, it is difficult to integrate this newly described function of Mbf1 on ribosomes with its previously proposed role in transcription co-activation of Gcn4 during ISR induction. However, recent observations that ribosome collisions lead to de-repression of GCN4 translation 18,19 provide a potential link between these two functions.

Here we examine the function of Mbf1 during ISR induction, and the role of ribosome binding in modulating this activity. Transcriptomic analysis of mbf1Δ cells following activation of the ISR shows impaired induction of the Gcn4 regulon, similar to that seen in gcn4Δ and gcn2Δ cells.41 These observations are consistent with a role for Mbf1 as a coactivator of Gcn4. However, contrary to initial studies on Mbf1, immunoblot analyses reveal that loss of Mbf1 leads to decreased phosphorylation of eIF2α by Gcn2 and reduced accumulation of Gcn4. We provide compelling data that show Mbf1 is required for robust Gcn2 activation and that Mbf1 has no role in the nucleus as a transcriptional coactivator. Indeed, in cells that constitutively produce Gcn4 in a Gcn2-independent manner, Mbf1 is no longer required for induction of Gcn4 target genes. We further show that Mbf1 recruitment to collided ribosomes is important for Gcn2 activation. Finally, we present a cryo-EM structure of an Mbf1-bound collided disome where the N terminus of the factor is resolved bound to the stalled ribosome; moreover, this region is shown to be important for Gcn2 activation. Collectively, our data suggest that Mbf1 functions to optimize ribosome collision-mediated Gcn2 activation and its downstream Gcn4-mediated transcriptional program.

ResultsMbf1 is necessary for robust induction of Gcn4 target genes in the presence of MMSWe first confirmed Mbf1’s connection to the ISR by demonstrating the sensitivity of our mbf1∆ strain to 3-aminotriazole (3-AT), an inducer of histidine starvation, as previously reported (Figure 1A).36 We next opted to use the alkylating agent methyl methane sulfonate (MMS) to activate the ISR since we have previously shown that MMS causes RNA damage, ribosome stalling42 and robust Gcn2-mediated eIF2α phosphorylation.17,18 Quantitative reverse-transcription PCR (qRT-PCR) analysis verified that transcription of the ISR-induced gene ARG1 was activated in the wild-type cells but impaired in the gcn4Δ, gcn2Δ and mbf1Δ strains (Figure 1B), recapitulating previously reported findings with 3-AT-mediated induction of HIS3, another known ISR target.36To more widely probe the role of Mbf1 in induction of Gcn4 targets, we subjected WT, gcn4Δ, gcn2Δ and mbf1Δ cells to RNA-seq in the absence and presence of MMS. After confirming that samples segregated as expected by principal component analysis (Figure 1C), we conducted differential gene expression analysis, paying particular attention to genes previously annotated as part of the Gcn4 regulon (red dots in Figure 1D).41 As predicted, 155 and 170 of the 223 genes annotated in the Gcn4 regulon exhibited reduced MMS-induced expression (Log2 fold change < 0) in the gcn4Δ and gcn2Δ strains relative to the wild type strain, respectively. Similar impairment was observed in the mbf1Δ strain, at 154 genes out of 223, suggesting a general requirement for Mbf1 for induction of the Gcn4 regulon in the presence of MMS. To provide further support for these observations, we expanded the analysis to encompass all MMS-responsive genes – defined as genes induced by MMS treatment with at least two-fold induction and adjusted p-value ≤ 0.05 in the wild-type strain. 1124 genes fit this category, in agreement with previous findings on MMS-induced changes to mRNA levels,43 confirming that our RNA-seq analysis was effective in capturing the entirety of the MMS-induced response (Figure 1E). A comparison of genes that exhibited any impairment in expression showed near complete overlap in impaired genes shared between the three genotypes (Figure 1E).Gcn2 activation is severely compromised in the absence of Mbf1Our analyses so far are in agreement with the previously proposed transcriptional co-activation function of Mbf1.36 However, an alternative ribosome-centric model is that Mbf1 binding to the ribosome during stress38 plays a direct role in inducing de-repression of GCN4 translation downstream of Gcn2 activation. While previous immunoblot analyses showed no difference in Gcn4 protein levels in between mbf1Δ and WT cells under 3-AT induction,36 the anti-Gcn4 sera used in this previous study produced nonspecific bands on our blots that prevented interpretation of Gcn4 protein levels.To circumvent this issue, we tagged the native GCN4 locus with a 3 × FLAG tag in the wild type and mbf1Δ backgrounds. As expected, immunoblot analysis of wild type cell extracts showed robust induction of Gcn4 and eIF2α phosphorylation upon MMS addition (Figure 2A). Importantly, in contrast with previous reports,36 deletion of MBF1 resulted in dramatic reduction of Gcn4 levels, with a correlated decrease in eIF2α phosphorylation (Figure 2A). Reintroduction of MBF1 with its regulatory regions, into the ADE2 locus fully rescued Gcn4 levels and eIF2α phosphorylation (Figure 2A).It is possible that the effect of MBF1 deletion on Gcn2-mediated eIF2α phosphorylation is somehow specific to alkylative stress induced by MMS. To assess this possibility, we tested the importance of Mbf1 on induction of the ISR when cells were treated with other Gcn2-activating stress inducers. In particular, we used 4-NQO and 3-AT which activate Gcn2 through RNA oxidation and amino-acid starvation, respectively18,42,44; under both conditions, Gcn4 levels and eIF2α phosphorylation were significantly reduced in the absence of Mbf1 (Figure 2B). These observations suggest that Mbf1 is required for robust Gcn2 activation regardless of the initiating stress signal.To directly assess this potential role of Mbf1 in de-repression of GCN4 translation, we conducted time-course experiments to follow Gcn4 induction and degradation following MMS addition (Figure 2C). In WT cells, Gcn4 rapidly accumulated and reached its maximum induction at 20 minutes, after which its levels slowly decrease. In contrast, in mbf1Δ cells, Gcn4 accumulated more slowly and to lower levels than observed in the wild type, even at earlier time points (Figure 2C). Since we observed that Gcn4 levels are exquisitely sensitive to the presence of Mbf1, along with previous reports that the two proteins interact with each other,36,45 we considered the possibility that Mbf1 may impact the stability of Gcn4. To address this, we directly measured the turnover rate of Gcn4 using cycloheximide chase experiments (Figure 2D). Our data indicate that the presence of MBF1 does not alter the degradation rate of Gcn4.To add further support for our model that Mbf1 plays a critical role during de-repression of GCN4 translation, we introduced a LacZ translation-fusion reporter in the native GCN4 locus (Figure 2E). As expected, in a wild-type background, addition of 3-AT and MMS resulted in ten- and three-fold induction of the reporter, respectively. By contrast, in an mbf1Δ background, we observed decreased induction of LacZ in the presence of either 3-AT or MMS. Altogether, our data show that contrary to previous literature, Mbf1 plays an important role during Gcn2-mediated eIF2α phosphorylation and downstream de-repression of GCN4 translation.Although our data revealed that Mbf1 is required for Gcn2 activation regardless of stress type (Figure 2B), it is unclear if all these conditions cause ribosomes to collide and recruit Mbf1 to them. To address this, untreated and RNase I treated lysates of cells challenged with MMS, 4-NQO, and 3-AT were fractionated using sucrose-gradient ultracentrifugation. Polysomes containing collisions collapse into both monosomes (non-collided ribosomes) and higher-order structures such as disomes and trisomes when treated with RNase I.19 As expected, RNase I treatment of lysates from untreated cells or cells treated with high concentration of cycloheximide resulted in few to no disome peaks on the polysome profile, while treatment with MMS, 4-NQO, 3-AT, or intermediate doses of cycloheximide resulted in significant accumulation of collided disome and trisome structures. Importantly, immunoblot analysis revealed that Mbf1 is specifically recruited to these higher-order structures (Figures 2F and G). These findings add to the growing body of evidence highlighting the importance of collided ribosomes during ISR activation, and the role of Mbf1 in sensing these collisions.Mbf1 is dispensable for induction of the ISR when Gcn4 is constitutively synthesized in a Gcn2-independent manner.Our data suggest that Mbf1 is required for robust de-repression of GCN4 translation and induction of its regulon. Our observations, however, do not address whether Mbf1’s proposed coactivation function additionally contributes to transcription of Gcn4 targets. To disentangle the proposed coactivation function of Mbf1 (a nuclear role) from its role in Gcn2 activation (a cytoplasmic role), we removed all four uORFs from the endogenous 5’-UTR of GCN4, generating a construct which we call “short-UTR” (Figure 3A) that should be constitutively expressed. Indeed, in the short-UTR strain, both Gcn4 and Arg4 accumulate to high levels even under uninduced conditions, with the addition of MMS having no noticeable effect on their levels (Figures 3A and 3B). We also generated a long-UTR control GCN4 strain, by increasing the distance between uORF1 and uORF4, which is known to dramatically reduce de-repression of GCN4 translation.46 As anticipated, MMS addition to a growing culture of this strain induced much lower levels of Gcn4 and its target Arg4 relative to wild-type cells (Figures 3A and 3B). The effects on Gcn4 target induction were corroborated by qRT-PCR analysis (Figure 3C). In particular, we observe no increase in ARG1 transcript levels upon MMS addition in the short-UTR strain (Figure 3C), while the long-UTR strain exhibited impaired induction of ARG1 transcript in response to MMS (Figure 3C). These differences were not due to a differential response to MMS, as analysis of the RNR3 transcript – a readout for the DNA damage response (DDR) –confirmed that the cells remained sensitive to MMS (Figure 3C).Having established that Gcn4 is constitutively produced in an eIF2α-phosphorylation independent manner in the short-UTR strain, we next asked whether Mbf1 is still important for transcription of Gcn4 targets. Interestingly, in this strain, deletion of MBF1 had no effect on Gcn4 protein or transcript levels (Figures 3D, 3E, and S1). Moreover, qRT-PCR analysis of two Gcn4 targets, ARG1 and ARG4, revealed dramatic increase in their levels in the short-UTR strain (Figure 3F) and deletion of MBF1 resulted in no significant changes in their levels (Figure 3F). These findings strongly argue that Mbf1 plays no direct role in transcriptional activation with Gcn4.The helix-turn-helix domain of Mbf1, which binds collided ribosomes, is required for optimal induction of the ISROur data suggest that Mbf1 plays an important role in Gcn2 activation, and that in its absence, eIF2α phosphorylation is significantly reduced. As noted earlier, Mbf1 (and its homolog EDF1) has been shown to bind collided ribosomes,33,38 which have also been implicated in Gcn2 activation (Figure 4A).18,19,33 Based on these reports and our observations, we speculated that Mbf1 binding to collided ribosomes is important for Gcn2 activation. To test this idea, we took advantage of a previous screen that identified residues in Mbf1 and in ribosomal proteins that are important for maintaining reading frame, and are predicted to be important for Mbf1 binding to the collided ribosome (Figure 4A).33,38,39We initially introduced an R89G mutation in the conserved helix-turn-helix (HTH) domain of the protein, which recent structures revealed to be important for binding helix 33 of the collided ribosome (Figure 4A).33,38 Consistent with earlier reports, the R89G mutation promoted +1 frameshifting on a dual luciferase stalling reporter harboring the inhibitory CGA codons between Renilla and Firefly luciferase genes (Figure S2).39,47 Additionally, immunoblot analysis revealed that unlike wildtype Mbf1, the R89G mutant does not migrate with the polysome fractions in cells treated with collision-inducing concentrations of cycloheximide, suggesting that the mutation inhibits its recruitment to collided ribosomes (Figure 4B). Interestingly, the R89G variant did not rescue the mbf1Δ strain for Gcn4 induction (Figures 4C and 4D), nor the accumulation of myc-tagged Arg4 in response to MMS (Figure 4C), nor the MMS-induced expression of ARG1 (Figures 4E and S3).It is worth noting that R89 of Mbf1 resides in an alpha helix of the protein and, as a result, mutating the residue to a glycine residue in its place might disrupt the structure of the helix. Therefore, we introduced either an alanine or lysine at this site instead, as both are non-disruptive to helical structures and the lysine substitution conserves the positive charge of arginine.33,39 Both mutations significantly inhibited recruitment of Mbf1 to collided ribosomes (Figure S4A) and increased +1 frameshifting on a stalling reporter, though with the more conservative lysine change displaying slightly less frameshifting relative to the alanine mutant (Figure S4B). More crucially, however, both mutations reduced the accumulation of Gcn4 and its target Arg4 upon MMS treatment (Figures S4C and S4D). These observations suggest that ribosome binding by Mbf1 through its α3 helix is important for robust de-repression of GCN4 translation.Mutations in ribosomal protein uS3 inhibit Mbf1 binding to ribosomes and phenocopy its loss.Our data presented above are consistent with a model wherein Mbf1 binding to stress-induced collided ribosomes is important for Gcn2 activation. In an alternative model, the HTH domain of Mbf1 could be important to its function independent of a role in ribosome binding. To distinguish between these two models, we introduced mutations in a region of ribosomal protein uS3 which was previously shown to be important for reading-frame maintenance on stalling reporters.39 Importantly, cryo-EM structures of Mbf1-bound collided-ribosomes revealed that this conserved region of uS3 interacts with α2 helix of Mbf1 (Figure 4F). Based on this analysis, we introduced three sets of mutations to this region of uS3 – single: K108E; double: K108E, L113A; and triple: K108E, L113A, G121A. We first assessed the effect of these mutations on the recruitment of Mbf1 to collided ribosomes (Figure 4G) and found that the single mutation only moderately inhibited recruitment of Mbf1 to collided ribosomes (compare Figure 4B to Figure 4G), while the double and triple mutations severely inhibited this recruitment (Figure 4G). Notably, all mutants exhibited increased +1 frameshifting on the dual luciferase stalling – (CGA)4 – reporter (Figure S5). Consistent with the idea that the uS3 mutants promote frameshifting by decreasing recruitment of Mbf1, deletion of Mbf1 in these uS3 mutant backgrounds did not result in further increases in frameshifting (Figure S5).We next asked whether these same uS3 mutations impact Gcn2 activation. We found that the double and triple mutations resulted in dramatic reductions in eIF2α phosphorylation and downstream Gcn4 levels upon MMS addition, whereas the single mutation resulted in noticeable but less dramatic outcomes (Figures 4H and 4I). qRT-PCR analysis of ARG1 expression in these strains in response to MMS revealed modest changes for the single uS3 mutation, and, again, more substantial effects for the triple uS3 mutation (Figure 4J). Reassuringly, the mutations had no effect on induction of the DDR gene RNR3 (Figure S6). Collectively, these observations suggest that inhibition of Mbf1 recruitment to collided ribosomes compromises activation of Gcn2 and downstream de-repression of GCN4 translation.The N-terminal domain of Mbf1 interacts with the stalling ribosome and is important for induction of the ISRIn previous cryo-EM structures, the N-terminus of Mbf1, which is predicted to be disordered, was not observed.33 More importantly, even though the structures revealed how Mbf1 interacts with the colliding ribosome in the disome unit, it was unclear how the factor specifically recognized ribosomal collisions.33 To gain more molecular details about the recruitment of Mbf1 to collided ribosomes, we performed cryo-EM analysis using the trisome fraction from crosslinked in vitro translation reactions containing a catalytically dead C64,67A Hel2 mutant and a stalling SDD1 mRNA.48 We were able to resolve both the stalled and the collided ribosome, allowing us to reconstruct a combined composite map representative of the disome collision unit (Figures 5A, S7, and S8A–S8B). In agreement with previous publications,49 the SDD1 stalling ribosome is in a POST state containing a P/P tRNA and the collided ribosome is in a hybrid state with A/P P/E tRNAs (Figures 5B and S8A–S8B).38,48 As reported before, we observed the C-terminal globular domain of Mbf1 on the collided ribosome at the mRNA entry site tugged in by h16 (Figure 5C).33,38 Moreover, a part of the N-terminus that protrudes from the collided ribosome underneath the mRNA was observed to engage in a specific interaction with eS26 and eS1 of the stalled ribosome (Figures 5C and 5D). More specifically, residues 8–13 of Mbf1 formed backbone to backbone H-bond interactions with residues 59–64 of eS26 in an antiparallel beta sheet-like conformation (Figures S8C–S8D). In addition to these backbone interactions, the side chain of I10 of Mbf1 binds in a hydrophobic pocket of eS26 composed of V45, V50, L53, Y62, and L64 (Figures S8E–S8G). eS1 also appears to interact with Mbf1, whereby the side chain of R115 interacts with W4 of Mbf1 through cation-pi interactions (Figure 5D). This structure uncovers a previously unappreciated role for the N-terminus of Mbf1 in connecting the two collided ribosomes, thereby recognizing and potentially stabilizing the distinct architecture of the collided disome.To dissect the role of Mbf1’s N-terminus in mediating Gcn2’s activity, we deleted the first 26 amino acids of Mbf1 (previously annotated as the disordered domain of the N-terminus) and integrated the truncated construct into the ADE2 locus in the mbf1Δ background. Sucrose gradient fractionation of the ΔN26 strain showed that unlike wildtype Mbf1, the N-terminally truncated Mbf1 remained entirely in the light fraction even under collision-inducing conditions (Figure 5E). Consistent with a critical role for the N-terminus of Mbf1 in binding colliding ribosomes, the ΔN26 mutant was found to promote +1 frameshifting on (CGA)4 stalling reporters (Figure S9). 47,50We next asked how the N-terminal truncation of Mbf1 impacts its role in Gcn2 activation. First, we established that the mbf1Δ strain complemented by a C-terminally tagged wildtype Mbf1 protein restored MMS-induced eIF2α phosphorylation, accumulation of Gcn4 and its target Arg4 (Figures 5F and 5G). By contrast, the ΔN26 variant only partially rescued these same signature events of the ISR (Figure 5F). Cryogenic electron microscopy data collection and processing3.5 µL of crosslinked trisome sample was applied to R3/3 holey carbon support copper grids pre-coated with 3 nm carbon (Quantifoil). Grids were blotted for 3 s with 45 s wait time using a Vitrobot (FEI) before plunge freezing in liquid ethane. Two datasets were collected using a Titan Krios (FEI) equipped with a K2 Summit direct electron detector (Gatan) with a nominal pixel size of 1.045 Å, an electron dose of 1.08 and 1.09 e-/Å2/frame for 40 frames and defocus ranges of 0.5–3.5 and 0.4–3.5 µm, respectively (Table 1). Motion correction was done with MotionCor2 65 and contrast transfer function (CTF) estimation with CTFFIND4.66 Resulting micrographs were manually curated yielding 6,696 and 13,761 images (20,457 total) for datasets 1 and 2, respectively. Initially, particles were picked for each dataset independently using Gautomatch v.0.56 (http://www.mrc-lmb.cam.ac.uk/kzhang/), subjected to 2D classification in CryoSPARC v.4.4.0 67 and then combined for the initial 3D refinement (Figure S7). Particles were further 3D sorted in Relion 3.1.1 68 using no mask. Classes containing clear density for a colliding ribosome but none for a leading one were combined (Classes 1 and 2) as representative for the SDD1 stalling ribosome. Using a mask on the mRNA entry remaining particles with a leading ribosome were eliminated. Another round of unmasked 3D classification was performed to sort out ribosomes not containing a collided neighboring ribosome. Then, using a mask on the mRNA exit, 48,521 particles (3.3%) with density for the Mbf1 N-terminus were selected, giving a final reconstruction for the P/P stalled ribosome with a hybrid-state colliding neighbor ribosome at a resolution of 2.9 Å after per particle defocus and global CTF refinement in CryoSPARC. To obtain a reconstruction for the colliding ribosome, a hybrid class from the initial sorting (Class 4) without density for another colliding ribosome was further classified, removing different tRNA states. Additionally, A/P P/E hybrid classes (Classes 5 and 6) containing both colliding and leading ribosome densities were further sub-sorted, removing particles without leading ribosome density. Then, all sorted hybrid-state classes containing a leading neighbor were combined and a mask around the mRNA entry was used to select 141,390 particles (9.5%) with a strong connection between leading and colliding ribosome for both mRNA and Mbf1. After refinement in CryoSPARC a reconstruction for the collided hybrid-state ribosome containing the globular domain (C-terminus) of Mbf1 was obtained at a resolution of 2.7 Å. After local resolution filtering, densities for the stalled and collided ribosome were used to generate a composite map representative for the SDD1 ribosome collision unit containing Mbf1.

Chromatin Immunoprecipitation – quantitative PCRCells were grown in 100 ml of YAPD medium to OD600 nm 0.6–0.8. Culture was split in two, half was crosslinked immediately, and the other half was treated with 0.1% MMS for 30 minutes before crosslinking by adding formaldehyde to a final concentration of 1% with occasional shaking at room temperature for 15 minutes. The reaction was quenched by adding glycine to a final concentration of ~350 mM and allowing it to react with excess formaldehyde for an additional 5 minutes. Cells were pelleted by centrifugation (5000 × g for 5 min at 4°C), washed twice with cold TBS (20 mM Tris, pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl), transferred to 1.5 ml Eppendorf tubes and flash frozen on dry ice. Pellets were resuspended in 400 µl lysis buffer (50 mM HEPES–KOH, pH 7.5, 140 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100, 0.1% sodium deoxycholate, 1 mM PMSF, and 1mM protease inhibitor cocktail). ~0.5 g of acid-washed glass beads was then added to each sample. Lysis was completed by using 4 × 1 min cycles on a FastPrep-24 machine (MP-Biomedical). To shear the genomic DNA, the cell lysates were sonicated 6 cycles on a Fisher brand Model 505 Sonic Dismembrator with a 35% output and a 5 second incubation period. Cell lysate was chilled on dry ice briefly and then on wet ice in between each sonication cycle. The volume of the sonicated samples was adjusted to ~500 µl using lysis buffer, and centrifuged (14,000 × g for 5 min) to remove cell debris. The cleared lysate was used for immunoprecipitation (IP) and an aliquot of the same was saved as input. To assess the efficiency of the sonication step, the crosslinks were reverted on a small aliquot by incubating it at 65°C overnight. gDNA was then purified using phenol-chloroform extraction followed by ethanol precipitation. The size of the sheared DNA fragments was assessed using Agarose gel electrophoresis.IP was carried out by incubating the samples with washed and equilibrated magnetic beads (Anti-FLAG, Anti-Myc, Anti-HA) at 4°C overnight with continuous rotation. Beads were then washed with 1.5 ml of lysis buffer (rotating at room temperature for 5 min), then with IP wash buffer 1 (same as the lysis buffer except 500 mM NaCl), followed by IP wash buffer 2 (10 mM Tris–HCl pH 8.0, 250 mM LiCl, 0.5% NP 40, 0.5% sodium deoxycholate, and 1 mM EDTA), and finally with TE buffer (10 mM Tris pH 8.0, 1 mM EDTA). Magnetic beads were then resuspended in 100 μL of TE pH 8.0, containing 20 µg of DNAse-free RNAse A and incubated at 37°C for 30 minutes. Similarly input samples were also treated with RNAse A. After RNase A treatment, beads were washed with 1 ml TE and DNA-protein complexes were eluted by resuspending the beads in 500 μL of IP elution buffer (1% SDS, 0.1 M NaHCO3 and 200 mM NaCl), incubating overnight at 65°C with shaking (1000 rpm) to revert the crosslinking. Similarly, 200 mM NaCl was added to input samples and incubating overnight at 65°C with shaking to revert the crosslinking. After crosslink reversal, Proteinase K (NEB) was added to both input and output samples to a final concentration of 1% and incubated at 50°C for 30 min. Samples were then purified using the PureLink Quick PCR purification kit (ThermoFisher Scientific), following manufacturer’s instructions. Purified DNA was used for qPCR following the protocol described above. Enrichment in expression was calculated by normalizing expression in the IP samples to that of their corresponding input using the percent input calculation method described in Solomon et al.88

Pellets were resuspended in 400 µl lysis buffer (50 mM HEPES–KOH, pH 7.5, 140 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100, 0.1% sodium deoxycholate, 1 mM PMSF, and 1mM protease inhibitor cocktail). ~0.5 g of acid-washed glass beads was then added to each sample. Lysis was completed by using 4 × 1 min cycles on a FastPrep-24 machine (MP-Biomedical). To shear the genomic DNA, the cell lysates were sonicated 6 cycles on a Fisher brand Model 505 Sonic Dismembrator with a 35% output and a 5 second incubation period. Cell lysate was chilled on dry ice briefly and then on wet ice in between each sonication cycle. The volume of the sonicated samples was adjusted to ~500 µl using lysis buffer, and centrifuged (14,000 × g for 5 min) to remove cell debris. The cleared lysate was used for immunoprecipitation (IP) and an aliquot of the same was saved as input. To assess the efficiency of the sonication step, the crosslinks were reverted on a small aliquot by incubating it at 65°C overnight. gDNA was then purified using phenol-chloroform extraction followed by ethanol precipitation. The size of the sheared DNA fragments was assessed using Agarose gel electrophoresis.

IP was carried out by incubating the samples with washed and equilibrated magnetic beads (Anti-FLAG, Anti-Myc, Anti-HA) at 4°C overnight with continuous rotation. Beads were then washed with 1.5 ml of lysis buffer (rotating at room temperature for 5 min), then with IP wash buffer 1 (same as the lysis buffer except 500 mM NaCl), followed by IP wash buffer 2 (10 mM Tris–HCl pH 8.0, 250 mM LiCl, 0.5% NP 40, 0.5% sodium deoxycholate, and 1 mM EDTA), and finally with TE buffer (10 mM Tris pH 8.0, 1 mM EDTA). Magnetic beads were then resuspended in 100 μL of TE pH 8.0, containing 20 µg of DNAse-free RNAse A and incubated at 37°C for 30 minutes. Similarly input samples were also treated with RNAse A. After RNase A treatment, beads were washed with 1 ml TE and DNA-protein complexes were eluted by resuspending the beads in 500 μL of IP elution buffer (1% SDS, 0.1 M NaHCO3 and 200 mM NaCl), incubating overnight at 65°C with shaking (1000 rpm) to revert the crosslinking. Similarly, 200 mM NaCl was added to input samples and incubating overnight at 65°C with shaking to revert the crosslinking. After crosslink reversal, Proteinase K (NEB) was added to both input and output samples to a final concentration of 1% and incubated at 50°C for 30 min. Samples were then purified using the PureLink Quick PCR purification kit (ThermoFisher Scientific), following manufacturer’s instructions. Purified DNA was used for qPCR following the protocol described above. Enrichment in expression was calculated by normalizing expression in the IP samples to that of their corresponding input using the percent input calculation method described in Solomon et al.88
