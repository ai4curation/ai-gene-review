Abstract

Centromeric chromatin plays a crucial role in kinetochore assembly and chromosome segregation. Centromeres are specified through the loading of the histone H3 variant CENP-A by the conserved chaperone Scm3/HJURP. The N-terminus of Scm3/HJURP interacts with CENP-A, while the C-terminus facilitates centromere localization by interacting with the Mis18 holocomplex via a small domain, called the Mis16-binding domain (Mis16-BD) in fission yeast. Fungal Scm3 proteins contain an additional conserved cysteine-rich domain (CYS) of unknown function. Here, we find that CYS binds zinc in vitro and is essential for the localization and function of fission yeast Scm3. Disrupting CYS by deletion or introduction of point mutations within its zinc-binding motif prevents Scm3 centromere localization and compromises kinetochore integrity. Interestingly, CYS alone can localize to the centromere, albeit weakly, but its targeting is greatly enhanced when combined with Mis16-BD. Expressing a truncated protein containing both Mis16-BD and CYS, but lacking the CENP-A binding domain, causes toxicity and is accompanied by considerable chromosome missegregation and kinetochore loss. These effects can be mitigated by mutating the CYS zinc-binding motif. Collectively, our findings establish the essential role of the cysteine-rich domain in fungal Scm3 proteins and provide valuable insights into the mechanism of Scm3 centromere targeting.

Introduction

Centromeres are the regions of chromosomes that direct and ensure proper segregation of the genome during cell division. Defective centromere function can lead to aneuploidy, defined by the loss or gain of chromosomes. Aneuploidy is the most common form of chromosome abnormality in humans linked to cancer and birth defects. Point centromeres, found in budding yeasts, consist of specific ∼125-bp centromeric DNA. Regional centromeres are found in organisms ranging from the fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe to humans and are composed of several kilobases to megabases of repetitive arrays ( 1 , 2 ). Paradoxically, rather than strictly relying on DNA sequence, the identity of regional centromeres is epigenetically defined by a unique histone H3 variant CENP-A (also known as CenH3). Their epigenetic nature allows centromeres to be activated in noncentromeric regions (i.e. neocentromeres), inactivated on dicentric chromosomes (e.g. when two chromosomes are fused) or repositioned to other locations on the chromosome ( 3–5 ).

Schizosaccharomyces pombe is a genetically amenable model system widely used to study regional centromeres and chromosome segregation ( 6 , 7 ). Fission yeast centromeres consist of two distinct domains: (i) a central core domain where CENP-A replaces the canonical histone H3, enabling kinetochore assembly, and (ii) heterochromatic outer repeats containing hypoacetylated nucleosomes methylated on lysine 9 of histone H3 ( 8 ). Similar to canonical histones, the deposition of CENP-A into chromatin requires a dedicated chaperone. In vertebrates, this function is performed by HJURP (Holliday junction recognition protein) ( 9 ), which associates with CENP-A in a prenucleosomal form via a conserved domain at the N-terminal region ( 10–12 ). Similarly, the HJURP orthologs in Saccharomyces cerevisiae and S. pombe , named Scm3, also play an essential role in centromere loading of CENP-A via this conserved N-terminal domain ( 13–18 ). In budding yeast, a unique hexameric nucleosome Scm3/CENP-A Cse4 /H4, which lacks H2A/H2B, has been proposed to exist ( 14 ). In fission yeast, Scm3 mediates the assembly of CENP-A chromatin without being a nucleosome component, although it dissociates from centromeres only during a small fraction of the cell cycle (from mitotic onset until late anaphase) ( 16 , 18 ). In any case, loss of Scm3 is deleterious as it disrupts CENP-A chromatin, leading to defective kinetochore assembly, aberrant chromosome segregation and ultimately loss of cell viability.

In both vertebrates and fission yeast, the centromere targeting of Scm3/HJURP depends on the Mis18 holocomplex, but not vice versa ( 16 , 18–20 ). The recruitment of HJURP to human centromeres requires a direct interaction between the Mis18α and Mis18β subunits of the Mis18 holocomplex and the HJURP centromere targeting domain ( 21–23 ). The S. pombe Mis18 holocomplex consists of Mis18, which shares high sequence similarity with human Mis18α/β, as well as the subunits Mis16—a homolog of the WD40-repeat proteins RbAp46/RBBP7 and RbAp48/RBBP4—and Mis19/Eic1 ( 24–26 ). In this case, targeting Scm3 to centromeres requires an interaction between Mis16 and the C-terminus of Scm3 ( 18 , 27 , 28 ). Specifically, residues ∼270–305 of Scm3 were identified as the minimum region required for Mis16 binding in vitro ( 28 ). The importance of this Mis16-binding domain (hereafter referred to as Mis16-BD) is consistent with the finding that the S281L mutation ( sim1-15 ) impairs Scm3 function ( 16 ).

Although conserved, fungal Scm3 proteins vary greatly in size, ranging from ∼200 aa in S. cerevisiae to ∼1300 aa in Neurospora crassa . The conserved N-terminal region of fungal Scm3 orthologs contains a single globular domain that is responsible for the specific interaction with CENP-A ( 14 , 29 ). The C-terminal region of Scm3 proteins harbors various types of motifs, including DNA-binding MYB and C2H2 zinc-finger domains, AT-hooks and cysteine-rich regions predicted to have metal-chelating properties with the sequence signature [CH]x4–6Cx4Cx2C. Remarkably, these motifs are not found in vertebrate HJURP proteins and are specific to certain lineages. For instance, they are absent from the smaller Scm3 proteins found in yeasts with point centromeres, which possess the distinctive centromere-specific DNA-binding protein Ndc10 ( 29 ). It is possible that the motifs present in larger Scm3 proteins have coevolved in response to the diversity of centromeric DNA and may therefore be required for centromere targeting. However, the role of these domains in Scm3 localization and function has not yet been explored.

In this study, we investigated the role of a small cysteine-rich domain, referred to as CYS, located in the C-terminus of the Scm3 protein in S. pombe . Through in silico , in vitro , genetic and genomic approaches, we identified a requirement for CYS in Scm3 centromere targeting, alongside the previously described Mis16-BD. We also find that CYS is capable of binding zinc. Specifically, we show that deleting CYS or mutating the zinc-binding motif prevents Scm3 from localizing to the centromere and compromises kinetochore integrity. Overall, our findings highlight the essential nature of the cysteine-rich domain in fungal Scm3 proteins and contribute to our understanding of the molecular mechanisms involved in the localization of Scm3/HJURP to centromeres.

Fission yeast strains and methods

Standard procedures were used for fission yeast cell culture and genetic manipulations ( 30 ). Schizosaccharomyces pombe strains used in this study are listed in Supplementary Table S1 . The following mutant alleles and reporters were previously described: tetO at cen2 and tetR-tdTomato adjacent to zfs1 ( 31 ), scm3-19 ( 18 ), scm3-139 ( 16 ), mis16-53 ( 32 ), cnp3-tdTomato and sim4-tdTomato ( 33 ), mEOS2-cnp1 ( 34 ) and minichromosome pNBg ( 35 ). To create an scm3Δ strain, a standard auxotrophic strain SP44 was transformed with a ura4 + - marked plasmid pREP82- scm3 + resulting in SPDF1978. Subsequently, SPDF1978 was transformed with a NAT cassette replacing the entire scm3 ORF to generate ura + NAT R strains (e.g. SPDF2014) that were employed in plasmid-shuffling assays.

Expression of truncated Scm3 is toxic and results in kinetochore loss

When expressed at high levels, truncated proteins lacking the N-terminal CENP-A binding domain might compete with the endogenous Scm3 protein and have detrimental effects on centromere assembly and cell fitness. Indeed, we found that overexpression of GFP-Scm3-CT significantly reduced cell viability (Figure 6B ). Notably, the toxicity was correlated with expression levels, as cells expressing GFP-Scm3-CT at endogenous levels (see +Thiamine, Figure 6B ) grew similarly to the wild-type control. We confirmed that the toxicity was not caused by the presence of cen2-tetO-tdTomato in the strains used for analysis. Expression of GFP-Scm3-CT resulted in a growth defect even in cells where cen2 remained unaltered (Figure 6B ).

We then asked whether toxicity is associated with disruption of the kinetochore. Indeed, we observed extensive missegregation of chromosome II in cells expressing Scm3-CT, indicating that the toxicity is likely caused by centromere/kinetochore malfunction (Figure 6C and D). Remarkably, CENP-A, CENP-C and CENP-K foci were barely detectable in cells overexpressing GFP-Scm3-CT, indicating a severe kinetochore deficiency in those cells (Figure 7A – D ). Unlike Scm3-CT, overexpression of Scm3-CYS did not result in severe phenotypes, suggesting that it is not strong enough to displace endogenous Scm3 and disrupt the kinetochore (Figure 6B and D). Based on these results, it is likely that truncated Scm3 proteins harboring both Mis16-BD and CYS outcompete endogenous Scm3 resulting in loss of kinetochore integrity.

Discussion

In this study, we investigated the role of a novel cysteine-rich domain in the fission yeast CENP-A chaperone Scm3. We discovered that this domain is essential for centromere targeting and function. In both vertebrates and fission yeast, the centromere targeting of Scm3/HJURP relies on the Mis18 holocomplex, which consists of Mis16, Mis18 and Mis19 subunits in S. pombe . While fission yeast Scm3 has a specific region that interacts with the Mis18 holocomplex called Mis16-BD ( 28 ), we identified CYS as an additional requirement for targeting Scm3 to centromeres. Importantly, we found that CYS can bind zinc, and disruption of the zinc-binding motif prevents centromere targeting.

Our results argue that the role of CYS is independent of Mis16-BD in centromere targeting. Although we cannot completely rule out the possibility that CYS and Mis16-BD work together to facilitate the interaction of Scm3 with Mis16 in S. pombe , the finding that the last 32 aa of the Scm3 protein, which contain CYS, are not required for interaction with Mis16 argues against this scenario ( 28 ). Additionally, multiple sequence alignments of proteins from different organisms show that the presence of Mis16-BD and CYS together is not required ( Supplementary Figure S10A ). Furthermore, CYS domains are present in both phyla of the fungal subkingdom Dikarya, such as Neurospora and Ustilago from Ascomycota and Basidiomycota, respectively ( 29 ). However, we could not find Mis16-BD outside of Schizosaccharomycetes , except in the budding yeast Komagataella phaffii (also known as Pichia pastoris ), where the Scm3 protein has lost the CYS domain ( Supplementary Figure S10B ).

If CYS does not interact with Mis16, it raises the question of what the target for CYS might be. The presence of various predicted DNA-binding motifs in the C-terminus of other fungal Scm3 proteins ( 29 ) suggests that DNA could be the target. Supporting this idea, we provide evidence that CYS binds centromeric DNA and can itself localize to the centromere (Figure 5 and Supplementary Figure S8 ), which may confer additional ‘anchoring’ of Scm3 to centromeres. Notably, S. cerevisiae Scm3, as well as CENP-C from both budding yeast and human, also displayed in vitro binding preference for AT-rich DNA ( 55 , 56 ) that is a feature of centromeres and neocentromeres ( 57 ). Another possibility, which is not mutually exclusive, is that CYS may interact with centromeric noncoding RNA (ncRNA). In this regard, ncRNAs from CENP-A chromatin regions have been detected in S. pombe ( 58 ). Moreover, a long centromeric ncRNA has been shown to interact with HJURP/CENP-A complex in humans ( 59 ), while an optimal level of transcription is required for a functional point centromere in budding yeast ( 60 ). Alternatively, CYS might potentially recognize specific chromatin features, such as unmodified or hypoacetylated histones. Given that Mis16 depletion leads to increased levels of histone acetylation at centromeres ( 32 ), this scenario would explain the observed requirement for Mis16 in Scm3 localization.

Finally, Scm3 overexpression is toxic in various centromere/kinetochore temperature-sensitive mutants, including cnp1 CENP-A , mis16 and mis18 . However, the specific molecular mechanisms behind such toxicity remain unclear ( 18 ). In our study, we show that overexpression of a truncated version of Scm3, harboring CYS but lacking the CENP-A interacting domain, is extremely toxic. This leads to loss of kinetochore integrity, as evidenced by the absence of CENP-A/C/K centromere foci (Figure 7 ). This finding may have relevance to the reported overexpression and mutation of HJURP in various cancers ( 61 , 62 ). Indeed, HJURP is a significant biomarker, as its overexpression has been observed in many cancer types, including breast, gliomas and pancreatic cancer, and it is associated with poor prognosis ( 62–64 ). The penetrance of the deleterious phenotype may be influenced by the nature of HJURP mutations. For example, nonfunctional HJURP proteins lacking CENP-A could localize to centromeres and displace endogenous counterparts, as observed in our study. This would result in loss of kinetochore integrity and chromosomal instability, a hallmark of cancer progression.
