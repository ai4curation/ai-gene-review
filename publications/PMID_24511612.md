# Loss of cAMP-specific phosphodiesterase rescues spore development in G protein mutant in dictyostelium.
**Authors:** Schwebs DJ, Nguyen HN, Miller JA, Hadwiger JA
**Journal:** Cell Signal (2014)
**DOI:** [10.1016/j.cellsig.2013.10.003](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cellsig.2013.10.003)
**PMC:** [PMC3934825](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3934825/)

## Abstract

1. Cell Signal. 2014 Feb;26(2):409-18. doi: 10.1016/j.cellsig.2013.10.003.

Loss of cAMP-specific phosphodiesterase rescues spore development in G protein 
mutant in dictyostelium.

Schwebs DJ, Nguyen HN, Miller JA, Hadwiger JA.

Cyclic AMP (cAMP) is an important intracellular signaling molecule for many G 
protein-mediated signaling pathways but the specificity of cAMP signaling in 
cells with multiple signaling pathways is not well-understood. In Dictyostelium, 
at least two different G protein signaling pathways, mediated by the GőĪ2 and GőĪ4 
subunits, are involved with cAMP accumulation, spore production, and chemotaxis 
and the stimulation of these pathways results in the activation of ERK2, a 
mitogen-activated protein kinase that can down regulate the cAMP-specific 
phosphodiesterase RegA. The regA gene was disrupted in gőĪ2(‚ąí) and gőĪ4(‚ąí) cells 
to determine if the absence of this phosphodiesterase rescues the development of 
these G protein mutants as it does for erk2(‚ąí) mutants. There gA(‚ąí) mutation had 
no major effects on developmental morphology but enriched the distribution of 
the GőĪ mutant cells to the prespore/prestalk border in chimeric aggregates. The 
loss of RegA function had no effect on GőĪ4- mediated folate chemotaxis. However, 
the regA gene disruption in gőĪ4(‚ąí) cells, but not in gőĪ2(‚ąí) cells, resulted in a 
substantial rescue and acceleration of spore production. This rescue in 
sporulation required cell autonomous signaling because the precocious 
sporulation could not be induced through intercellular signaling in chimeric 
aggregates. However, intercellular signals from regA(‚ąí) strains increased the 
expression of the prestalk gene ecmB and accelerated the vacuolization of stalk 
cells. Intercellular signaling from the gőĪ4(‚ąí)regA(‚ąí) strain did not induce ecmA 
gene expression indicating cell-type specificity in the promotion of prestalk 
cell development. regA gene disruption in a GőĪ4(HC) (GőĪ4 overexpression) strain 
did not result in precocious sporulation or stalk cell development indicating 
that elevated GőĪ4 subunit expression can mask regA(‚ąí) associated phenotypes even 
when provided with wild-type intercellular signaling. These findings indicate 
that the GőĪ2 and GőĪ4-mediated pathways provide different contributions to the 
development of spores and stalk cells and that the absence of RegA function can 
bypass some but not all defects in G protein regulated spore development.

ResultsLoss of RegA has a subtle affect on developmental morphologyDictyostelium G protein-mediated signaling pathways can regulate cAMP levels in response to vegetative and developmental signals and in some cases this regulation might involve the down regulation of the RegA phosphodiesterase. To determine if the loss of RegA function can suppress developmental defects associated with G protein mutations a regA gene disruption created in strains that lacked either the GőĪ2 or GőĪ4 subunit gene (Supplementary Fig. 1). When starved, most gőĪ2‚ąíregA‚ąí cells failed to aggregate but some cells were capable of forming very small mounds that became raised from the substratum after 48 hours (Fig. 1). These small aggregates were also observed for gőĪ2‚ąíregA‚ąí cells grown on bacterial lawns (data not shown). As expected from previous studies, the starvation of gőĪ2‚ąí cells did not result in multicellular aggregates. Disruption of the regA gene did not substantially rescue the morphological development of gőĪ4‚ąí cells that typically terminate development as multicellular aggregates with extended tip structures. The gőĪ4‚ąíregA‚ąí aggregates terminated development as slug-like structures but without the slug migration associated with wild-type aggregates. As previously described by others, the disruption of the regA gene in wild-type cells resulted in accelerated development to form fruiting bodies with small spore heads and stalks with a thick base [26]. These developmental phenotypes indicated that the loss of RegA function does not substantially change the morphological development of wild-type or these G protein mutant strains.Localization of regA‚ąí strains in developing chimerasBoth GőĪ2 and GőĪ4 subunits can mediate chemotactic responses and so gőĪ2‚ąíregA‚ąí and gőĪ4‚ąíregA‚ąí cells were compared to gőĪ2‚ąí and gőĪ4‚ąí cells, respectively, to determine if the loss of RegA function alters the localization of cells within a developing aggregate. The distribution of mutant cells was analyzed in chimeras consisting primarily of wild-type cells to ensure proper intercellular signaling within the aggregate and the mutant cells were distinguished from the wild-type cells using a GFP expression vector (Fig 2). Very few gőĪ2‚ąíregA‚ąí and gőĪ2‚ąí cells were associated with the chimeric aggregates supporting the idea these cells are defective with respect to cAMP chemotaxis during aggregation. Most of the gőĪ2‚ąí and gőĪ2‚ąíregA‚ąí cells retained in the chimeric slugs were detected near the posterior region but some gőĪ2‚ąíregA‚ąí cells were localized to a region near the anterior but not at the anterior tip. Both gőĪ4‚ąíregA‚ąí and gőĪ4‚ąí cells co-aggregated efficiently with wild-type cells and both mutant strains were distributed throughout the entire length of the chimeric slugs. However, the gőĪ4‚ąíregA‚ąí cells were enriched in a region near the anterior, similar to that observed for gőĪ2‚ąíregA‚ąí and regA‚ąí cells. In contrast, gőĪ4‚ąí cells were most abundant near the posterior of chimeric slugs. These observations suggest that the loss of RegA function can promote the localization of cells near the anterior of developing aggregates regardless of whether the GőĪ2 or GőĪ4 subunit is present but the contributions of cell migration and cell-cell adhesion to the distribution of cells in this region remain to be determined.Folate chemotaxis is not affected by the loss of RegAThe altered localization of regA‚ąí mutants in chimeras supports a possible role for RegA in the regulation of cell movement and previous studies have shown that the loss of RegA function partially impairs cAMP chemotaxis due to the reduced ability to suppress lateral pseudopod formation [37]. To examine the possible impact of the regA gene disruption on folate chemotaxis, wild-type, gőĪ4‚ąí and gőĪ2‚ąí cells with or without regA gene disruption were assayed for folate chemotaxis in an above agar assay (Fig. 3). The loss of RegA function had very little affect on the ability of wild-type or gőĪ2‚ąí mutants to chemotax to folate indicating RegA function is not required for folate chemotaxis. The importance of RegA function in cAMP but not folate chemotaxis suggests the role of RegA function can be variable with respect to chemotactic responses. Perhaps cAMP chemotaxis is more sensitive to the loss of RegA because RegA directly regulates the level of endogenously produced chemoattractant. DiscussionThe treatment of submerged Dictyostelium monolayers with the membrane-permeable cAMP analog 8-bromo-cAMP (8-Br-cAMP) results in sporulation and the loss of RegA or PKA-R can also promote precocious sporulation [24, 38]. These earlier studies have firmly established the importance of intracellular cAMP and PKA in sporulation but do not provide insight into how external signals work down stream of G protein-mediated signaling pathways to regulate sporulation. Our findings suggest that signaling through multiple G protein signaling pathways is necessary for the regulation of this process and that these pathways are not redundant in their contribution to sporulation. The rescue of sporulation when the regA gene is disrupted in gőĪ4‚ąí cells suggests the GőĪ4 subunit mediates a signal transduction pathway that down regulates RegA activity as part of a developmental mechanism involved with spore development. This down regulation of RegA function is in part cell autonomous and likely to occur in cells destined to become spores because intercellular signaling from gőĪ4‚ąíregA‚ąí cells is not sufficient to stimulate sporulation of gőĪ4‚ąí cells in developing chimeras. GőĪ4-mediated signaling is thought to function early in spore development because cells expressing spore coat proteins do not display strong GőĪ4 expression [15, 18]. The ability of gőĪ4‚ąíregA‚ąí cells to readily form spores without fruiting body formation indicates that sporulation does not require GőĪ4-mediated cell movement or morphology.We propose that the GőĪ4-mediated activation of ERK2 results in the phosphorylation and down regulation of RegA activity, allowing a rise in cAMP and the activation of PKA (Fig. 8). PKA is the primary effector of cAMP signaling in many eukaryotic pathways and others have already demonstrated that PKA plays an important role in Dictyostelium sporulation [39, 40]. Sporulation of regA‚ąí cells can be significantly reduced by the expression of a mutant PKA-R subunit that constitutively inhibits the release of active PKA-C, implying that regA‚ąí sporulation requires PKA activity [24]. Earlier studies have indicated that prespore-specific overexpression of the PKA-C subunit does not rescue sporulation in gőĪ4‚ąí aggregates even though the expression of some late prespore genes (e.g., pspA) can be enhanced [41]. However, a possible requirement for GőĪ4-mediated PKA activation prior to prespore-specific gene expression might explain the inability of the prespore-specific PKA-C to suppress gőĪ4‚ąí sporulation defects. Alternatively, the rescue of sporulation in gőĪ4‚ąíregA‚ąí aggregates might be accomplished through cAMP effectors other than PKA (e.g., cyclic nucleotide regulated ion channels or EPACs) but thus far no other effectors have been identified that regulate Dictyostelium sporulation. The external signal for GőĪ4-mediated sporulation remains to be determined but the signal is not likely to be folate because responsiveness to folate decreases during cellular aggregation [42]. Others have proposed that the GőĪ4 subunit couples with the GrlA receptor and mediates the release of GABA (ő≥-aminobutyric acid) in response to spore differentiation factor 3, SDF-3 [43]. However, the involvement of SDF-3 in the down regulation of RegA function has not been examined. The inhibited sporulation in the GőĪ4HC and GőĪ4HCregA‚ąí strains implies that GőĪ4 subunit overexpression blocks a developmental mechanism, such as prestalk cell differentiation, that cannot be rescued through the loss of RegA function. It is possible that the absence of RegA only accelerates the maturation of prestalk cells once this cell type has been established. One such defect might be the loss of chemotactic movement to cAMP, an essential mechanism for proper aggregation and cell movement within a multicellular aggregate. This idea is supported by low representation of gőĪ2‚ąíregA‚ąí cells in the prespore region in chimeric aggregates where intercellular signaling is likely to be optimal for spore development. However, multicellular development is not essential for the sporulation of wild-type cell monolayers induced by the 8-Br-cAMP but this induction does not exclude the importance of extracellular cAMP or other intercellular signals in sporulation. GőĪ2 function is necessary for the transient rise in cAMP in response to extracellular cAMP and so perhaps the GőĪ2 function is biased for adenylyl cyclase activation rather than regulating cAMP levels through RegA inactivation (Fig. 8). Unlike the mammalian GőĪs subunit, the Dictyostelium GőĪ2 subunit is not a direct activator of adenylyl cyclase and other studies have indicated the importance of other signaling proteins in the activation of adenylyl cyclase [44]. The enrichment of regA‚ąí mutants cells near the prespore/prestalk O border of chimeric aggregates is consistent with the development of prestalk cells that express ecmB in the prestalk B region. EcmB expression can also be elevated in other areas of the slugs as indicated by the distribution of ecmB expressing gőĪ4‚ąíregA‚ąí cells in chimeras. The increased ecmB expression and precocious stalk cell vacuolization can be mediated through intercellular signaling provided by cells that lack RegA function. However, the overexpression of the GőĪ4 subunit blocks the production and reception of the signal that promotes precocious stalk development in a cell autonomous manner. The inability of this strain to make prestalk cells probably accounts for the lack of signal reception but how GőĪ4 subunit overexpression blocks signal production in the absence of RegA is not clear. The enhancement of this prestalk subtype specific signal in regA‚ąí strains suggests that the intercellular signal could be cAMP or perhaps the production of other morphogens that are regulated by cAMP levels (Fig. 8). Given the ability of cAMP to diffuse throughout a cell, pathway specific cAMP signaling could be achieved by separating the pathways to different cell types or stages of development.
