SUMMARYHeat shock protein 90 (Hsp90) participates in proteostasis by 
facilitating protein folding, activation, disaggregation, prevention of 
aggregation, degradation, and protection against degradation of various cellular 
proteins. It is highly conserved from bacteria to humans. In bacteria, protein 
remodeling by Hsp90 involves collaboration with the Hsp70 molecular chaperone 
and Hsp70 cochaperones. In eukaryotes, protein folding by Hsp90 is more complex 
and involves collaboration with many Hsp90 cochaperones as well as Hsp70 and 
Hsp70 cochaperones. This review focuses primarily on bacterial Hsp90 and 
highlights similarities and differences between bacterial and eukaryotic Hsp90. 
Seminal research findings that elucidate the structure and the mechanisms of 
protein folding, disaggregation, and reactivation promoted by Hsp90 are 
discussed. Understanding the mechanisms of bacterial Hsp90 will provide 
fundamental insight into the more complex eukaryotic chaperone systems.

INTRODUCTION

Protein quality control (proteostasis) is the process by which cells manage and regulate the proteome. Maintaining the proteome requires a delicate balance between protein synthesis, folding, aggregation, and degradation. Protein misfolding and aggregation is the major underlying cause of disease conditions such as neurodegenerative diseases, cancer, diabetes, and cystic fibrosis ( 1 – 6 ). Moreover, impairment of proteostasis is one of the hallmarks of aging and results in accumulation of misfolded proteins and protein aggregates ( 7 – 9 ).

Molecular chaperones are important proteins that collaborate in protein quality control ( 10 ). The major cellular chaperones that maintain protein quality control include heat shock protein 90 (Hsp90) ( 11 – 13 ), Hsp70 ( 14 , 15 ), chaperonin (GroEL/ES in bacteria) ( 16 – 18 ), and TriC/CCT in eukaryotes ( 19 , 20 ), Clp/Hsp100 ( 21 – 24 ), and ribosome-associated chaperones [Trigger factor in bacteria ( 25 , 26 ) and nascent polypeptide-associated protein, NAC, and ribosome-associated complex, RAC, in eukaryotes ( 25 , 27 , 28 )]. Many chaperones are highly expressed under normal growth conditions and are further induced by heat and other environmental stressors. Chaperones participate in all aspects of protein quality control ( 10 , 29 – 31 ) ( Fig. 1 ). During protein biosynthesis, some proteins can fold spontaneously ( Fig. 1 , step 1), while others require assistance in cotranslational folding by ribosome-associated chaperones like Trigger factor, NAC, RAC, and Hsp70 as they emerge from the ribosome ( Fig. 1 , step 2). Other polypeptides are released from ribosomes in unfolded or misfolded forms ( Fig. 1 , step 3) and their folding is facilitated by a variety of chaperones, including Hsp70s, chaperonins, and Hsp90s ( Fig. 1 , step 4). Stressors, such as heat, also contribute to the pool of misfolded proteins by causing protein unfolding ( Fig. 1 , step 5). However, unfolded and non-native proteins that are not rapidly refolded by chaperones tend to form aggregates ( Fig. 1 , step 6). While some chaperones protect non-native proteins from aggregation in a process referred to as protein holding ( Fig. 1 , step 7), other chaperones, including Clp/Hsp100 in collaboration with Hsp70, as well as Hsp90 in collaboration with Hsp70, disassemble protein aggregates, releasing folded proteins or unfolded polypeptides that may spontaneously refold or be refolded with the assistance of other chaperones ( Fig. 1 , step 8). Some misfolded proteins that are not refolded are targeted for degradation by chaperones associated with proteases, including Clp two-component proteases and Lon protease in bacteria ( 23 , 32 , 33 ) and the proteasome in eukaryotes ( 34 , 35 ) ( Fig. 1 , step 9), although some chaperones can protect unfolded polypeptides from degradation ( Fig. 1 , step 10). For all these chaperone activities, cochaperones, which are chaperone accessory proteins, act in concert with molecular chaperones to assist in protein remodeling.

This review focuses on recent advances that elucidate the fundamental mechanisms of protein remodeling involving the Hsp90 molecular chaperone, with an emphasis on bacterial Hsp90 and a comparison with eukaryotic Hsp90. The role of Hsp90 in protein folding, holding, degradation, and virulence is discussed. We also describe the structures and conformational dynamics of Hsp90 and Hsp70, and the collaboration between Hsp90, Hsp70, and J-domain cochaperones in protein remodeling.

CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVES

Living cells are the product of a well-orchestrated balance between protein synthesis, reactivation, and degradation. Molecular chaperones are team players that integrate into the protein quality control network, during which decisions are constantly made that help determine the fate of a myriad of client proteins. They play key roles in proteostasis, but it is evident that they do not act in isolation; rather, they collaborate with one another and with cochaperones and proteases, directly and indirectly to regulate the proteome.

A remarkable amount of research has already uncovered many aspects of the cellular function, structure, and mechanism of action of Hsp90. Work with the bacterial Hsp90 has provided insight to the molecular mechanism of Hsp90 without the added complexity introduced by Hsp90 cochaperones. Many of the fundamental findings for the bacterial systems were also shown to be conserved in eukaryotic systems and vice versa. However, there are still many questions that need further exploration. For instance, understanding how Hsp90 activity is modulated by Hsp90 cochaperones, client proteins, Hsp70, and Hsp70 cochaperones will provide a better understanding of the molecular mechanism of action of Hsp90. Additionally, the interplay of Hsp90 and other chaperones and proteases in modulating proteostasis is an important area for future research. Research aimed to better understand the role of Hsp90 in protein folding diseases and in microbial and parasitic infections is important for the development of drugs designed to treat cancer, neurodegenerative diseases, and pathogenic infections. Together, a comprehensive understanding of Hsp90 chaperone machinery will provide further insight into the mechanistic workings of the molecular chaperone in protein quality control and the role it plays in protein folding disorders.
