# Trinucleotide expansion within the MJD1 gene presents clinically as spinocerebellar ataxia and occurs most frequently in German SCA patients.
**Authors:** Schöls L, Vieira-Saecker AM, Schöls S, Przuntek H, Epplen JT, Riess O
**Journal:** Hum Mol Genet (1995)
**DOI:** [10.1093/hmg/4.6.1001](https://doi.org/10.1093/hmg/4.6.1001)

## Abstract

1. Hum Mol Genet. 1995 Jun;4(6):1001-5. doi: 10.1093/hmg/4.6.1001.

Trinucleotide expansion within the MJD1 gene presents clinically as 
spinocerebellar ataxia and occurs most frequently in German SCA patients.

Schöls L(1), Vieira-Saecker AM, Schöls S, Przuntek H, Epplen JT, Riess O.

Author information:
(1)Department of Neurology, St. Josef Hospital, Bochum, Germany.

Autosomal dominant spinocerebellar ataxia (SCA) is a clinically and genetically 
heterogeneous neurodegenerative disorder which leads to progressive cerebellar 
ataxia. A gene responsible for SCA type 3 has been mapped to human chromosome 
14q, close to the Machado-Joseph disease (MJD) locus. The MJD1 gene has recently 
been cloned and the disease causing mutation has been identified as an unstable 
and expanded (CAG)n trinucleotide repeat. As some clinical features of MJD 
overlap with those of SCA we investigated the MJD mutation in 38 German families 
with dominantly inherited SCA. The MJD1 (CAG)n expansion was identified in 19 
families. In contrast, the trinucleotide expansion was not observed in 21 ataxia 
patients without family history of the disease. Analysis of the (CAG)n repeat 
length in 30 patients revealed an inverse correlation with the age of onset. The 
(CAG)n stretch of the affected allele varied between 67 and 78 trinucleotide 
units, the normal alleles carried between 12 and 28 simple repeats. These 
results demonstrate that the MJD mutation causes the disease phenotype of most 
SCA patients in Germany.
