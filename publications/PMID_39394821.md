There are three isoforms of human collagen prolyl 4-hydroxylases (C-P4Hs), each
of which has been reported to play an important role in regulating the
progression of a variety of human cancers. By analyzing TGCA datasets on human
head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSC), we find that a higher expression
of all three C-P4HAs (the α subunit of C-P4Hs) is a superior prognostic
indicator than a higher expression of two or a single C-P4HA. Unexpectedly, some
patients with higher levels of three C-P4HAs survive longer than patients whose
tumors have lower expression of C-P4HAs. Therefore, there may be molecule(s)
that can negate the deleterious effects of overexpressing C-P4HAs during cancer
progression. By constructing a functional protein interaction network of C-P4HAs
and analyzing molecules whose expressions are correlated significantly with that
of C-P4HAs, we identify scribble cell polarity complex component 2 (LLGL2) as a
factor that antagonizes the effects of overexpressed C-P4HAs on HNSC. Silencing
of LLGL2 in the human oral squamous cell line Cal-27 upregulates the expression
of occludin and increases cancer cell invasion and migration. In contrast,
knocking down C-P4HA alone inhibits cell migration and invasion. Furthermore,
simultaneously downregulating three C-P4HAs has more pronounced effects on
inhibiting cell migration and invasion. Accordingly, high LLGL2 expression is
also a marker indicating improved prognosis in patients with HNSC. These results
suggest that the interplay between LLGL2 and C-P4HAs may be targeted to mitigate
HNSC tumorigenesis and progression.

Abstract
There are three isoforms of human collagen prolyl 4-hydroxylases (C-P4Hs), each of which has been reported to
play an important role in regulating the progression of a variety of human cancers. By analyzing TGCA datasets on
human head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSC), we find that a higher expression of all three C-P4HAs (the
α subunit of C-P4Hs) is a superior prognostic indicator than a higher expression of two or a single C-P4HA. Un-
expectedly, some patients with higher levels of three C-P4HAs survive longer than patients whose tumors have
lower expression of C-P4HAs. Therefore, there may be molecule(s) that can negate the deleterious effects of
overexpressing C-P4HAs during cancer progression. By constructing a functional protein interaction network of
C-P4HAs and analyzing molecules whose expressions are correlated significantly with that of C-P4HAs, we identify
scribble cell polarity complex component 2 (LLGL2) as a factor that antagonizes the effects of overexpressed
C-P4HAs on HNSC. Silencing of LLGL2 in the human oral squamous cell line Cal-27 upregulates the expression of
occludin and increases cancer cell invasion and migration. In contrast, knocking down C-P4HA alone inhibits cell
migration and invasion. Furthermore, simultaneously downregulating three C-P4HAs has more pronounced effects
on inhibiting cell migration and invasion. Accordingly, high LLGL2 expression is also a marker indicating improved
prognosis in patients with HNSC. These results suggest that the interplay between LLGL2 and C-P4HAs may be
targeted to mitigate HNSC tumorigenesis and progression.

Introduction
Head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSC) accounted for
approximately 870,000 new cases and 440,000 deaths worldwide in
2020 [1]. It includes cancers of the oral cavity (40%), pharynx
(25%), and larynx (15%) [2]. The prognosis of patients with HNSC
is generally poor, especially for those with recurrent or metastatic
disease, and treatment options for these patients are limited.
Therefore, a better understanding of the biology of HNSC may

reveal novel targets for therapeutic interventions.
Histologically, HNSC is encased by a dense extracellular matrix,
which is mainly composed of collagens in which the 4-hydroxypro-
line residues are essential for the stability of the collagen triple helix.
In mammalian cells, three collagen prolyl 4-hydroxylases (C-P4Hs)
catalyze the hydroxylation of proline to 4-hydroxyproline at the Y
position in the glycine-X-Y motif of the α chain. C-P4Hs are
tetramers composed of two identical α chains and two identical β
chains. The enzymatic activity resides in the α chain, and the
chaperone activity is in the β chain. Although the catalytic
properties of the isoenzymes are very similar, each isoform appears
to have distinct peptide substrate binding properties and sensitivity
to inhibitors. Additionally, the glycine-X-Y motif is also present in
noncollagenous proteins; these proteins may be substrates of C-
P4Hs [3,4].
Accumulating evidence shows that α subunits of C-P4Hs (C-
P4HAs) are overexpressed in a variety of human cancers and
contribute to cancer progression [5–7]. In addition to playing a
role in collagen maturation and secretion [5], C-P4HAs have been
reported to play other roles in cancer biology. For example, they
have been reported to stimulate glycolysis in some tumor cells,
which is important in tumor cell biology [8]. C-P4HA is also
known to adjust the stability of hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF)
and fine-tune the demethylase activity of Jumonji (JMJ), a
histone demethylase, and ten-eleven translocation (TET), which
modifies DNA by oxidizing 5-methylcytosine (5mC) in breast
cancer. In addition, C-P4HA has been reported to alter the
hydroxylation of proline on Carabin, an endogenous calcineurin
inhibitor, in B-cell lymphoma [9–11]. Therefore, suppressing the
activity of C-P4HAs has been considered a strategy to block
cancer progression [9,12–14].
In HNSC tissues, the levels of P4HA1 and P4HA3 are significantly
greater than those in normal tissues. High P4HA1 expression in
HNSC tissue is significantly correlated with tumor grade, lymphatic
metastasis, pathological stage, and poor prognosis [12,15,16]. In
addition, the expression of P4HA3 alone has been shown to induce
epithelial- mesenchymal transition (EMT) and increase tumor cell
proliferation and metastasis [16]. However, whether the simulta-
neous overexpression of all three C-P4HAs indicates poorer
outcomes for patients than the over-expression of single or two C-
P4HAs is unknown.
According to our preliminary bioinformatics analysis, HNSC
patients who had higher expression of three C-P4HAs had shorter
survival than patients who had higher expression of one or two C-
P4HAs. Surprisingly, at the later stage of cancer progression, some
patients with higher expression of all three C-P4HAs survived
longer than patients with lower expression of C-P4HAs. Therefore,
there might be molecule(s) that negate the deleterious effect of
overexpressing three C-P4HAs at a later stage of cancer progression.
We postulate that these molecules have the following properties: (1)
their expression levels are significantly positively or negatively
correlated with those of C-P4HAs ; (2) they can functionally interact
with C-P4HAs; and (3) they can counter the function of C-P4HAs,
thus being markers indicating improved outcomes for HNSC
patients.
Based on the analysis of the TCGA datasets and using the STRING
platform, we identified LLGL2 as a potential molecule that
functionally antagonizes the effects of overexpressing three C-
P4HAs. Using an in vitro cell model, we showed that knocking
down C-P4HA inhibited the migration and invasion of the human
oral squamous cell line Cal-27. Furthermore, simultaneous down-
regulation of the three C-P4HAs had more pronounced effects on
inhibiting cell migration and invasion. In addition, the expression of
LLGL2 rescued the effects of overexpressing C-P4HAs on cancer cell
invasion by modulating the level of occludin, a molecule that plays
a vital role in tight junction formation and cell-matrix adhesion and
is pivotal for cell migration and invasion. Thus, LLGL2 may be a
potential target for therapeutic intervention in HNSC.

Bioinformatics analysis
We ran the program at UALCAN (http://ualcan.path.uab.edu/) to
identify the types of cancers listed in the TCGA database expressing
C-P4HAs. We then compared the relative expression of C-P4HAs
between HNSC and normal samples and identified types of cancers
that overexpressed two or three members of the C-P4HAs. Finally,
we analyzed the expression levels of individual C-P4HAs according
to cancer stage.
Survival analysis
The prognostic value of the expression of C-P4HA family genes was
analyzed using the Kaplan-Meier plotter database (http://kmplot.
com/). The samples were divided into high and low-expression
groups with a 50% expression level as the cutoff value. The
correlations between gene expression and overall survival (OS)
were calculated against the survival period (months).
The expression of C-P4HAs and corresponding clinical data of
patients with HNSC were downloaded from the TCGA database.
Kaplan-Meier survival analysis was performed for patients with
high expression of individual or multi-C-P4HAs against the survival
period with the R programming package “survival”. The samples
were divided into high- and low-expression groups with a 50%
expression level as the cutoff value.

water. For each chamber, three fields were randomly imaged at 10×
with an inverted microscope. Invaded cells on the bottom of the
filter were then quantified with ImageJ (NIH) software. To test the
effects of a drug, the drug or the corresponding vehicle was added to
the culture medium during cell invasion. Relative invasiveness is
defined as follows: invasive cell counts in reagent-treated cells/
invasive cell counts in control-treated cells, which is defined
arbitrarily as 1.0. The assay was repeated at least three times with
comparable results.

Results
Combining the levels of three C-P4HAs is a better
predictor of poorer prognosis in patients with HNSC
C-P4HAs are known to modulate the progression of some human
tumors [5]. We searched the TCGA dataset (UALCAN) to identify
tumor types with higher or lower levels of all three C-P4HAs.
P4HA1 , P4HA2 , and P4HA3 were detected in 24 types of cancers,
although in some types of cancers, such as kidney chromophobe
renal cell carcinoma (KICH, green), lung squamous cell carcinoma
(LUSC, green), and prostate adenocarcinoma (PARD, green), the
levels of P4HA1 , P4HA2 , and P4HA3 were significantly lower than
those in normal tissues (Figure 1A). We further assessed the
coexpression pattern of C-P4HAs in those cancers and found that
the levels of P4HA1 and P4HA2 were significantly upregulated in
seven types of cancers, the levels of P4HA2 and P4HA3 were
significantly upregulated in eight types of cancers, and the levels of
P4HA1 and P4HA3 expression were significantly increased in ten
types of malignant tumors (Figure 1B). However, a significant
increase in the levels of all three P4HA1 / 2 / 3 genes was detected in
all six types of cancer (Figure 1B). These were kidney renal cell
carcinoma (KIRC), stomach adenocarcinoma (STAD), lung adeno-
carcinoma (LUAD), esophageal carcinoma (ESCA), head and neck
squamous cell carcinoma (HNSC), and breast invasive carcinoma
(BRCA).
We then performed Kaplan-Meier analysis for patients with these
cancers to assess the prognostic value of individual C-P4HAs.
Interestingly, a higher level of any individual C-P4HA was
associated with poorer outcomes only in HNSC but not in the other
five types of cancer (Figure 1C and Supplementary Figure S1A). We
thus focused on HNSC to investigate the biology and prognostic
value of C-P4HAs.

Downregulation of C-P4HAs inhibits the migration and
invasion of cancer cells
Since HNSC patients with higher levels of three C-P4HAs had poorer
prognoses, we investigated the potential mechanism underlying this
observation. We first investigated the effects of silencing individual
C-P4HAs on Cal-27 cells with siRNA. Two different targets were
chosen for P4HA1, P4HA2, and P4HA3 based on the efficacy of
silencing, as determined by quantitative PCR (Supplementary
Figure S2A) and western blot analysis (Figure 2A). We then
performed wound healing and transwell invasion assays. The
results showed that downregulation of P4HA1, P4HA2, or P4HA3
significantly suppressed the migratory and invasive ability of Cal-27
cells (Figures 2B,C).
We then investigated the effects of cosilencing C-P4HAs on
cancer cells. The efficiency of C-P4HA silencing was verified by
quantitative PCR (Supplementary Figure S2B) and western blot
analysis (Figure 2D). Wound healing and in vitro invasion assays
showed that downregulating the expressions of all three C-P4HAs at
the same time greatly impacted the motility and invasion of Cal-27
cells (Figures 2E,F). More importantly, downregulating the expres-
sions of all three C-P4HAs simultaneously significantly mitigated
the migratory and invasive capability of Cal-27 cells compared to
downregulating the expression of individual C-P4HAs (Figure 2G
and Supplementary Figure S2C). Hence, there are additive or
synergistic effects of the overexpression of three C-P4HAs on the
migration and invasion of Cal-27 cells.
Inhibiting prolyl hydroxylase function reduces the
mobility and invasion of cancer cells
Although C-P4HAs have also been shown to modulate metabolism,
which subsequently alters epigenetics and EMT [9,11], a major
function of C-P4HAs is to hydroxylate proline on collagens
[5,18,21,22]. However, collagen is normally produced by cancer-
associated fibroblasts but not by cancer cells. To assess why C-
P4HA downregulation affects the migration and invasion of Cal-27
cells, we first determined whether Cal-27 cells produces type I
collagen. Cal-27 cells indeed produced type I collagen (Supplemen-
tary Figure S2D). We then treated Cal-27 cells with EDHB or/and
ascorbate (Vc). EDHB is a well-known inhibitor of C-P4HAs, while
Vc is required to maintain their prolyl-catalyzing activity. EDHB
treatment did not increase C-P4HA protein levels (Figure 3A and
Supplementary Figure S2E). Compared with Vc treatment, treat-
ment with EDHB or EDHB plus Vc significantly increased the mRNA
levels of type I collagen, P4HA1, P4HA2, and P4HA3 (Supplemen-
tary Figure S2F). No mature type I collagen was detected when
EDHB was used to treat the cancer cells even in the presence of Vc
(Supplementary Figure S2D). These results confirm that EDHB
inhibits the prolyl hydroxylase activity of C-P4HAs, thus reducing
the production of mature type I collagen. Subsequently, we treated
cells with pythiDC, another inhibitor of C-P4HAs, in the presence or
absence of Vc. Treatment with pythiDC did not increase the protein
levels of C-P4HAs (Figure 3A and Supplementary Figure S2E).
However, compared with treatment with Vc alone, treatment with
pythiDC or pythiDC plus Vc also significantly increased the mRNA
levels of C-P4HAs (Supplementary Figure S2F). We then detected
cancer cell migration and colony formation before and after EDHB
or pythiDC treatment. Treatment of Cal-27 cells with these
inhibitors significantly decreased cancer cell migration, invasion,
and colony formation (Figure 3B–D). Collectively, these results
suggest that C-P4HAs may exert their effects on HNSC migration
and invasion via prolyl hydroxylase activity.

Discussion
Bioinformatics analysis revealed that the median survival time of
HNSC patients with higher levels of all three C-P4HAs was much
shorter than that of patients with higher levels of a single or two C-
P4HAs or significantly shorter than that of patients with lower levels
of three C-P4HAs (Figure 1D and Table 3). We further showed that
upregulated C-P4HAs increased cancer cell migration and invasion in
vitro (Figure 2B,C). Compared with the knockdown of an individual
member of the C-P4HA s, knockdown of all three C-P4HA s also had a
more significant impact on tumor cell migration and invasion (Figure
2G and Supplementary Figure S2C). Currently, we are unable to
discern whether this is an additive or a synergistic effect.
We also showed that the median survival time of HNSC patients
with higher levels of three C-P4HA s plus higher levels of ITGA5 or
lower levels of LLGL2 , ERBB3 , or FUT6 was significantly shorter
than that of patients with lower levels of three C-P4HAs plus lower
levels of ITGA5 or higher levels of LLGL2 , ERBB3 , or FUT6 (Figure
5B and Table 4), suggesting that the expression of ITGA5 may
functionally counteract, whereas the expression of LLGL2 , ERBB3 ,
or FUT6 may functionally synergize, the effects of three C-P4HAs.
Although the mRNA levels of LOXL2 , ITGA5 , LLGL2 , ERBB3 ,
GPR110 , RBM47 , SAMD10 , and FUT6 did not significantly change
as cancer progressed (Supplementary Figure S3C), the protein levels
of these molecules may be significantly increased or decreased,
since protein but not mRNA fulfills physiological functions.
Furthermore, the transcript level is known to have a limited
correlation with the protein level. It is also possible that
posttranslational modifications of these molecules may have
occurred, thus altering their functions as cancer progresses.
Although the expressions of LOXL2 , GPR110 , SAMD10 , and
RBM47 did not have prognostic value for HNSC patients (Supple-
mentary Figure S3D), the median survival time of patients with high
expressions of three C-P4HA s plus high expression of LOXL2 or low
expression of GPR110 or of SAMD10 was significantly shorter than
that of patients with low expressions of three C-P4HA s plus low
expression of LOXL2 or high expression of GPR110 or SAMD10
(Supplementary Figure S3E and Table S1). The underlying
mechanisms remain to be investigated.
The ability of LLGL2 overexpression to mitigate the function of
overexpressed C-P4HA s is intriguing, especially because the role of
LLGL2 in tumor biology has been reported to be cancer cell-specific
[23,24]. LLGL2 is a membrane-associated protein that plays
important roles in establishing apical‒basal polarity in human
epithelial cells and participates in the formation of hemidesmo-
somes and cell-matrix adhesion [25–27]. Moreover, LLGL2 pro-
motes tumorigenesis in some cases, while it delays tumorigenesis in
others [23,24,28–35]. In epidermal tumors formed in zebrafish,
LLGL2 acts as a tumor suppressor. Loss of lgl2 induces tumor
formation, while mutation of pen / lgl2 results in the EMT of basal
epidermal cells as an outcome of ErbB2 overactivation [34].
However, in ER
+
breast cancer, overexpressed LLGL2 forms a
complex with SLC7A5 and YKT6 to promote cancer cell prolifera-
tion under nutrient starvation conditions [23]. Similarly, LLGL2 has
been reported to stimulate liver cancer cell proliferation, migration
and invasion by promoting the PI3K-Akt cascade [28]. Our
functional analysis revealed that LLGL2 can negate the effects of
C-P4HA upregulation on HNSC invasion (Figure 6B) by increasing
the level of occludin. This observation was further supported by the
observation that silencing of three C-P4HA s decreased the level of
occludin (Figure 6D). However, at this time, we still cannot
conclude that the overexpression of LLGL2 at a later stage of HNSC
progression can mitigate the effects of overexpressed C-P4HAs. The
development of an HNSC mouse model in which the expression of
Llgl2 can be induced at a later stage of tumor development is
required to answer this question.
The finding that ERBB3 negatively correlates with the expression
of C-P4HAs is also intriguing (Figure 4A and Supplementary Figure
S3A). ErbB3 has been shown to modulate HNSC survival and
growth in a 3D microenvironment [36]. Additionally, in the pen / lgl2
mutant zebrafish epidermis, ErbB2 but not ErbB3 signaling is
responsible for the EMT phenotype [34]. Hence, LLGL2 and ERBB3
may coordinate signaling to counteract the effects of C-P4HAs.
Currently, whether SRC proto-oncogene (SRC) connects LLGL2 to
ERBB3 in HNSC remains to be investigated (Figure 6C). Interest-
ingly, ErbB2 increases EMT but not basal epidermal cell prolifera-
tion in pen / lgl2 mutant zebrafish, while in our system, silencing of
LLGL2 increased the level of occludin, a protein that potentially
plays a role in regulating EMT. However, since the levels of other
EMT proteins were not altered in LLGL2- or C-P4HA -silenced cells,
it is unlikely that EMT was significantly impacted in Cal-27 cells.
Collagen, one of the most abundant components within the
cancer immune environment, plays a critical role in cancer cell
migration. In vivo , dense collagen can sequester cytotoxic T cells,
thus hampering their ability to kill tumor cells, and may also form a
barrier inhibiting effective adoptive T-cell or NK-cell therapy [37].
In vitro , collagen may be useful for cancer cell migration. Although
the migration of Cal-27 cells seems to be influenced by the silencing
of C-P4HA s (Figure 2B,C,E–G) and essentially by their prolyl
hydroxylation ability (Figure 3B–D), we cannot exclude the
possibility that C-P4HA-mediated HNSC migration occurred
through collagen production. The prolyl hydroxylation of HIF-1α
by P4HA1 has been shown to be important for its stability and
function [9], and the observation that when C-P4HA s are silenced,
the level of occludin decreases, suggesting that the stability of
occludin may be regulated by prolyl hydroxylation. Since reduced
stability of occludin can be observed in prolyl hydroxylase domain
protein 3-deficient mice in a hydroxylase-independent manner [38],
how LLGL2 expression regulates the level of occludin remains to be
investigated.
In summary, our results suggest that overexpression of C-P4HA
family genes is associated with a poorer prognosis in patients with
HNSC and that overexpression of three C-P4HAs indicates a poorer
prognosis for patients with HNSC. In addition, we show that the
expression of LLGL2 counteracts the effect of upregulated C-P4HAs
