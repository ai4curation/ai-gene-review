# Metastasis-promoting anterior gradient 2 protein has a dimeric thioredoxin fold structure and a role in cell adhesion.
**Authors:** Patel P, Clarke C, Barraclough DL, Jowitt TA, Rudland PS, Barraclough R, Lian LY
**Journal:** J Mol Biol (2013)
**DOI:** [10.1016/j.jmb.2012.12.009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jmb.2012.12.009)

## Abstract

1. J Mol Biol. 2013 Mar 11;425(5):929-43. doi: 10.1016/j.jmb.2012.12.009. Epub
2012  Dec 26.

Metastasis-promoting anterior gradient 2 protein has a dimeric thioredoxin fold 
structure and a role in cell adhesion.

Patel P(1), Clarke C, Barraclough DL, Jowitt TA, Rudland PS, Barraclough R, Lian 
LY.

Author information:
(1)Institute of Integrative Biology, University of Liverpool, Crown Street, 
Liverpool L69 7ZB, UK.

Anterior gradient 2 (AGR2) is a normal endoplasmic reticulum protein that has 
two important abnormal functions, amphibian limb regeneration and human cancer 
metastasis promotion. These normal intracellular and abnormal extracellular 
roles can be attributed to the multidomain structure of AGR2. The NMR structure 
shows that AGR2 consists of an unstructured N-terminal region followed by a 
thioredoxin fold. The protein exists in monomer-dimer equilibrium with a K(d) of 
8.83μM, and intermolecular salt bridges involving E60 and K64 within the folded 
domain serve to stabilize the dimer. The unstructured region is primarily 
responsible for the ability of AGR2 to promote cell adhesion, while dimerization 
is less important for this activity. The structural data of AGR2 show a 
separation between potential catalytic redox activity and adhesion function 
within the context of metastasis and development.
