## Full Text

Abstract

This review highlights current insights into the regulation of the mitochondrial respiratory chain (electron transport chain, ETC) activity. The regulation of ETC properties optimizes ATP synthesis and controls the generation of the superoxide anion radical (O 2 •− ) which can be converted into other reactive oxygen species (ROS) playing a dual role by initiating signaling cascades or contributing to oxidative stress. We examine how ETC activity is influenced by the structure and conformation of its complexes, their allosteric or post-translational modifications, and their interactions with membrane lipids. The formation and function of supercomplexes, as well as their cell-type-specific characteristics, are also discussed, alongside with the role of intracellular Ca 2+ concentration in the modulation of ETC activity. Furthermore, we discuss mechanisms and sites of O 2 •− generation within ETC complexes, O 2 •− fate in the mitochondrial matrix, and the impact of cytochrome c (Cyt c) conformation and allosteric modifications on ETC function. Finally, we discuss various abnormalities in ETC complexes, emphasizing their relevance to mitochondrial dysfunction and disease.

Introduction

It is widely accepted that production of ATP is the main function of mitochondria which oxidative phosphorylation activity should tightly follow the current cellular metabolic demand. As by-products of the electron transport in ETC, mitochondria can produce reactive oxygen species, as was firstly demonstrated in 1966 (Jensen 1966 ). A year later, Hinkle et al. showed that H 2 O 2 production could be observed by ATP-dependent oxidation of cytochrome b in submitochondrial particles from beef heart in the presence of succinate, malonate, sulfide, and antimycin A (Hinkle et al. 1967 ). In the 1970s, Chance and his colleagues isolated mitochondria to show that they produce H 2 O 2 (Loschen et al. 1971 ; Boveris et al. 1972 ; Boveris and Chance 1973 ; Chance et al. 1979 ).

These findings initiate multiple studies of oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) showing that ETC-related ROS production had high importance for cells’ function under normal and pathological conditions. Complexes I, II, and III are considered the primary sites for the production of reactive oxygen species (Hinkle et al. 1967 ; Takeshige and Minakami 1979 ; Hirst et al. 2008 ; Cadenas et al. 2022 ). It was previously thought that superoxide anion was the first to form in mitochondria and then turn into hydrogen peroxide, but both can actually be formed in mitochondria (Loschen et al. 1974 ; Forman and Kennedy 1974 ; Cadenas et al. 2022 ). Recent studies have shown that besides complexes I, II, and III, other mitochondrial enzymes can also produce reactive oxygen species under physiological or pathological conditions (Tretter et al. 2007 ; Hail et al. 2010 ; Samhan-Arias and Gutierrez-Merino 2014 ; Chouchani et al. 2014 ; Andreyev et al. 2015 ; Wong et al. 2017 ; Oldford et al. 2019 ; Koufos and Mailloux 2023 ; Di Sante et al. 2023 ). For example, in mammalian mitochondria, at least 11 sites of ROS production associated with substrate catabolism, electron transport, and oxidative phosphorylation were identified (Brand 2016 ). In this review, we discuss complexes I, II, and III in more detail, as they are the main sources of ROS production. We will also briefly describe other potential sites of ROS formation in mitochondria and superoxide “fate” in the mitochondrial matrix. We also describe regulation of cytochrome c and complex IV activity since the electron transport on the level complex III-cytochrome c-complex IV has high importance for the operation of the downstream complexes and ROS generation. At present, there is a growing number of techniques for the investigation of intracellular ROS (Kostyuk et al. 2024 ). It is clear that understanding of the relations between electron transport, ROS generation, and ATP synthesis is crucial for the development of novel diagnostic tools for the detection of cellular metabolic abnormalities and oxidative stress. It is also important for the creation of therapeutic approach for the prevention of cell death under hypoxia/reperfusion injury or mitochondria-related pathologies. The review is focused on the summarizing of current data on the regulation of the activity of ETC complexes I–IV, their interaction with membrane lipids and with one another and formation of supercomplexes, leading to the optimization of ATP synthesis in ATP-synthase. Here we do not cover ATP-synthase also known as complex V since we aim to review current data about the electron-transporting ETC components and we aim to describe known mechanisms of ROS generation in ETC. We also do not discuss methods and tools to study ROS production and ETC redox state, as such description requires separate review and can be found elsewhere (Kostyuk et al. 2024 ). In the review, we focus on the description of the cell-specific complicated interrelated network of processes and interactions that are necessary for the maintenance of the optimal electron transport rate without pathological ROS formation and we also discuss some ETC-related pathologies.

Conclusion

The importance of the precise regulation of the respiratory chain activity cannot be overestimated, especially for excitable cells with rapidly changing metabolic demands. Thus, in neurons and cardiomyocytes ATP is necessary for the maintenance, local decrease or increase of Na + , K + , and Ca 2+ intracellular concentrations, thus providing conditions for the action potential generation, firing activity, synaptic transmission (in neurons), or contraction/dilation (in cardiomyocytes). In neurons, ATP is important for vesicular and organelles transport, accumulation of neurotransmitters in synaptic vesicles, etc. In spite the high cellular need in ATP, the uncontrolled increase OXPHOS activity is as dangerous, as low level of ATP synthesis due to the generation of superoxide and hydrogen peroxide in ETC resulting in the development of oxidative and nitrosative stress. Therefore, the precise regulation of ETC activity is necessary for the optimal production of ATP with the generation of ROS in non-toxic concentrations. So, mitochondria have diverse systems of ETC regulation, sensitive to the cell metabolic demands. Besides, to avoid accumulation of electrons in certain ETC complexes, the activation or inactivation of ETC has to be done almost simultaneously on the level of each complex or in the level of complexes I and II that provide electron entry to ETC. Inhibition of the electron transfer in complex III or complex IV or in between them will definitely results in the accumulation of reduced ubiquinol, reversed electron transport and superoxide generation in Q-cycle of complex III or/and in complexes I and II. Therefore, there are multiple mechanisms of allosteric and post-translational modifications of ETC complexes, imbedding of isoforms possessing different sensitivity to O 2 and pH, modulation of ETC activity due to the supercomplexes’ formation, complexes’ interaction with lipids and small proteins. Importantly, some cell types as astrocytes, use ETC as an important source of superoxide, keeping ETC in a form of free non-interacting complexes without respirasome formation with high amount of inactive complex I and low amount of complex III. Such ETC structure provides conditions of electrons’ accumulation in ETC and generation of superoxide anion-radical under physiological pO 2 and hypoxia and nitric oxide under hypoxia. Afterwards, superoxide is used for the formation of H 2 O 2 – signaling molecule between astrocytes and neurons and microglia, whereas nitric oxide acts as a vasodilator in the adjacent blood vessels. At the same time, prolonged mitochondria and cell exposure to ROS leads to the oxidative stress and cell death. Understanding of mechanisms of ETC regulation and reasons of its disruption are important for the illucidation mitochondria-related cell pathologies. Therefore, development and application of novel methodological approach allowing simultaneous monitoring of various ROS and RNS and estimation of ETC redox state are highly important for the unrevealing of the complex net of processes and interactions in mitochondria ETC.
