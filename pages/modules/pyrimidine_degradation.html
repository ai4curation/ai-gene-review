Pyrimidine degradation (reductive catabolism of uracil/thymine); DPD deficiency / 5-FU toxicity

Pyrimidine (uracil and thymine) catabolism is the three-step reductive pathway that degrades the pyrimidine bases to beta-amino acids, disposing of nucleic-acid/nucleotide turnover products and generating beta-alanine (from uracil) and beta-aminoisobutyrate (from thymine). Dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase (DPYD) catalyses the first, rate-limiting step, using NADPH to reduce uracil to 5,6-dihydrouracil and thymine to 5,6-dihydrothymine; it is a large flavoprotein (FAD, FMN, four [4Fe-4S] clusters). Dihydropyrimidinase (DPYS) then hydrolytically opens the ring (Zn2+-dependent), converting dihydrouracil to N-carbamoyl-beta-alanine (and dihydrothymine to N-carbamoyl-beta-aminoisobutyrate). Finally beta-ureidopropionase (UPB1) hydrolyses N-carbamoyl-beta-alanine to beta-alanine + CO2 + ammonia (and the isobutyrate analogue to beta-aminoisobutyrate). Because DPYD is also the main catabolic enzyme for the fluoropyrimidine chemotherapeutics, this pathway is clinically pivotal: DPD deficiency (DPYD) causes severe/fatal 5-fluorouracil toxicity and DPYD genotyping guides fluoropyrimidine dosing; DPYS and UPB1 deficiencies (dihydropyrimidinuria and N-carbamyl-beta-amino aciduria) carry a similar theoretical fluoropyrimidine-toxicity risk and variable neurodevelopmental phenotypes.

Three-step reductive pyrimidine (uracil/thymine) catabolism grounded to the human enzymes DPYD (UniProtKB:Q12882, GO:0017113, EC 1.3.1.2), DPYS (Q14117, GO:0004157, EC 3.5.2.2) and UPB1 (Q9UBR1, GO:0003837, EC 3.5.1.6). GO molecular-function terms were taken from the human GOA records; Reactome reaction ids and titles were verified against the local reactome cache. Each step uses a PANTHER family selector (generic over orthologs) with the human enzyme as representative. The module is drawn for the uracil branch (-> beta-alanine); the parallel thymine branch (-> beta-aminoisobutyrate) uses the same three enzymes (Reactome R-HSA-73616 / R-HSA-73618 for the DPYD/DPYS thymine reactions). Upstream, the free bases come from nucleotide/nucleic-acid turnover (and dietary pyrimidines); downstream, beta-alanine feeds carnosine and pantothenate/CoA metabolism. Clinical significance: DPYD is the principal fluoropyrimidine (5-FU/capecitabine) catabolic enzyme, so DPD deficiency causes severe/fatal chemotherapy toxicity and DPYD is a major pharmacogenetic gene; DPYS and UPB1 deficiencies (dihydropyrimidinase deficiency, beta-ureidopropionase deficiency) present with the corresponding metabolite aciduria, variable neurodevelopmental features and a theoretical fluoropyrimidine-toxicity risk.
