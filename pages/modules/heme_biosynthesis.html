Heme B biosynthesis through coherent protoporphyrin IX routes

A reusable cross-taxon model of protoporphyrin-dependent heme B biosynthesis. Two complete, incompatible route realizations are separated at the top level. The C5 glutamyl-tRNA realization, found in bacteria, archaea, and plastid-bearing eukaryotes, uses HemA and HemL to form 5-aminolevulinate (ALA), then a shared tetrapyrrole trunk and compatible HemF/HemN and HemJ/HemG/HemY late-enzyme alternatives. The alternative C4/Shemin realization instead uses 5-aminolevulinate synthase followed by an oxygen-dependent CPOX/PPOX late route. Both realizations converge at protoporphyrin IX, which a species-neutral ferrochelatase reaction converts to heme B.

Boundary: this module begins with route-specific ALA formation and ends with heme B. HemA/HemL and the trunk through uroporphyrinogen III can supply sibling tetrapyrrole pathways; this does not bring siroheme-, corrin-, or cobalamin-specific enzymes into the module. GltX supplies a translation substrate used by HemA and remains upstream. Coproheme routes, heme uptake/storage/degradation, and conversion of heme B to heme O or heme A are outside the boundary. Exact KT2440 and E. coli accessions are exemplars of conserved C5-route roles, not route-level taxon constraints. The late variant catalog is evidence-based but non-exhaustive. KT2440 hemB and hemBB are both plausible ALAD-family enzymes, so neither paralog is declared uniquely dominant.
