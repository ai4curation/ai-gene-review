Molybdenum cofactor (MoCo) biosynthesis and maturation (MOCS1/MOCS2/MOCS3/GPHN/MOCOS + SUOX)

The molybdenum cofactor (MoCo) is the essential prosthetic group of the human molybdoenzymes — sulfite oxidase (SUOX), xanthine dehydrogenase/oxidase (XDH) and aldehyde oxidase (AOX1) — and is synthesised de novo from GTP through a conserved four-step pathway. First, the bifunctional MOCS1 (MOCS1A + MOCS1B) converts GTP to cyclic pyranopterin monophosphate (cPMP): its MOCS1A domain is a radical-SAM enzyme carrying two [4Fe-4S] clusters that makes the cyclic pterin intermediate, and MOCS1B completes cPMP. Second, molybdopterin synthase — the (MOCS2A)2(MOCS2B)2 heterotetramer of the bicistronic MOCS2 gene — inserts two sulfurs into cPMP to form molybdopterin (MPT), with the sulfur delivered by the thiocarboxylated C-terminus of the ubiquitin-like MOCS2A, which is regenerated by the adenylyltransferase/sulfurtransferase MOCS3 (which also activates URM1 for tRNA thiolation). Third, the bifunctional scaffold protein gephyrin (GPHN) completes MoCo: its G domain adenylylates MPT and its E domain inserts molybdenum to give the mature cofactor (GPHN also moonlights as the master scaffold clustering glycine and GABA-A receptors at inhibitory synapses). Finally, the PLP-dependent molybdenum cofactor sulfurase MOCOS converts MoCo to its terminal-sulfido form required specifically by XDH and AOX1, whereas SUOX uses the mature dioxo-MoCo (with a b5-type heme) directly to oxidise sulfite to sulfate. Loss of the biosynthetic enzymes causes molybdenum cofactor deficiency (types A/B/C) — a devastating neonatal epileptic encephalopathy phenotypically identical to isolated SUOX deficiency; MOCOS defects cause xanthinuria type II.
