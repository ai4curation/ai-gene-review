Histidine catabolism (histidine -> glutamate; HAL/UROC1/AMDHD1/FTCD)

Histidine catabolism degrades the essential amino acid L-histidine to L-glutamate in four steps, channelling the imidazole-ring one-carbon unit into folate metabolism at the final step. The pathway, most active in liver and skin, begins with histidine ammonia-lyase (HAL, histidase), which uses an autocatalytically-formed MIO prosthetic group to deaminate L-histidine to trans-urocanate plus ammonia. Urocanate hydratase (UROC1, urocanase) then adds water across the urocanate double bond (with a tightly bound NAD+ as an electrophilic cofactor) to give 4-imidazolone-5-propanoate. The zinc-dependent amidohydrolase imidazolonepropionase (AMDHD1) hydrolyses the imidazolone ring to N-formimidoyl-L-glutamate. Finally the bifunctional glutamate formimidoyltransferase-cyclodeaminase (FTCD) transfers the formimino one-carbon group to tetrahydrofolate — releasing L-glutamate and 5-formimino-THF — and then cyclodeaminates 5-formimino-THF to 5,10-methenyltetrahydrofolate, feeding a one-carbon unit into folate metabolism. Inherited defects cause characteristic aminoacidopathies: HAL — histidinemia (usually benign); UROC1 — urocanic aciduria; and FTCD — glutamate formiminotransferase deficiency, the second most common inborn error of folate metabolism, marked by urinary excretion of formiminoglutamate (FIGLU).
