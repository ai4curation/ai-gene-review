# MOGS removes terminal glucose from Glu3Man9GlcNAc2-CDH1
**Reactome ID:** [R-HSA-9932344](https://reactome.org/content/detail/R-HSA-9932344)
**Species:** Homo sapiens

## Summary

In a highly evolutionarily conserved process, after a core Glu3Man9GlcNAc2 glycan has been added to the target asparagine residues in the nascent polypeptide chain, the first glucose residue, attached via an alpha-1,2 linkage to the glycan's middle glucose residue, is rapidly removed by MOGS (also known as Glucosidase I), a monomeric enzyme that resides in the endoplasmic reticulum membrane (reviewed in Helenius and Aebi 2004). The removal of the first glucose residue prevents further association of the nascent peptide with the OST complex and enables further processing (reviewed in Helenius and Aebi 2004). While this processing step has not been specifically studied for CDH1 (E-Cadherin), and while the product of this step is probably present too briefly to be detected by mass spectrometry, the product of the subsequent processing step, catalyzed by Glucosidase II complex, for which Glu2Man9GlcNAc2 serves as a substrate, is detectable (Guo et al. 2017).
