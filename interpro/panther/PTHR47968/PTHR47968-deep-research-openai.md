High-resolution structures confirm this: the crystal structure of the human CENP-E motor domain (solved with a non-hydrolysable ATP analog) closely resembles other plus-end directed kinesins, with conserved catalytic residues for ATP hydrolysis and microtubule interaction ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=structure%20of%20the%20human%20CENP,E%20motor%20domain)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=%2A%2083.Garcia,Google%20Scholar)). **Ancestral/Core Function:** The ancestral CENP-E is believed to function as a **plus-end directed kinetochore motor** that helps congress chromosomes during prometaphase-metaphase. Indeed, early studies showed human CENP-E is *essential* for metaphase chromosome alignment ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2766669/#:~:text=,5.%20%5BDOI%5D%20%5BPubMed%5D%20%5BGoogle%20Scholar)) (Wood *et al.*, 1997, **PMID: 9363944**).

## Functional Divergence (Neo- and Sub-functionalization)

**Neo-functionalization:** There is little evidence for classical neo-functionalization within the CENP-E family – i.e. no duplicate gene in this family has acquired a completely new function. In most genomes, *CENPE* exists as a single-copy gene, so evolutionary innovation has occurred via modifications of the single gene rather than duplication. However, over evolutionary time the CENP-E lineage has accumulated unique features distinguishing it from other kinesins, effectively neo-functionalizing relative to ancestral kinesins. One such feature is the extremely long coiled-coil and tail with multiple binding motifs. This enabled **new interactions and roles** beyond just hauling chromosomes. For example, **CENP-E’s farnesylated C-terminus** appears to be an innovation that provides a structural role at kinetochores ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021925818443876#:~:text=peptides%20from%20the%20COOH,E%20and%20the%20microtubules.%20Our)) ([rupress.org](https://rupress.org/jcb/article/223/1/e202303007/276393/A-farnesyl-dependent-structural-role-for-CENP-E-in#:~:text=%28Wu%20et%20al,had%20been%20treated%20with%20nocodazole)). Recent work shows that CENP-E, via its farnesyl anchor and tail, helps assemble the “fibrous corona” – a network of proteins on unattached kinetochores that promotes microtubule capture ([rupress.org](https://rupress.org/jcb/article/223/1/e202303007/276393/A-farnesyl-dependent-structural-role-for-CENP-E-in#:~:text=interaction%20with%20the%20centrosomal%20protein,2004%3B%20Famulski%20et%20al)) ([rupress.org](https://rupress.org/jcb/article/223/1/e202303007/276393/A-farnesyl-dependent-structural-role-for-CENP-E-in#:~:text=%28Wu%20et%20al,had%20been%20treated%20with%20nocodazole)). This suggests that in addition to its motor activity, CENP-E evolved a **scaffolding function**: it can recruit or stabilize other complexes (like dynein and RZZ) at the kinetochore. This could be viewed as a neo-functionalization at the protein-feature level (the ancestral motor gained a new structural role). Supporting this, **mutating the farnesylation site** in CENP-E (Cys-to-Ala) impairs corona formation and slows chromosome congression, even if the motor domain is intact ([rupress.org](https://rupress.org/jcb/article/223/1/e202303007/276393/A-farnesyl-dependent-structural-role-for-CENP-E-in#:~:text=Inhibition%20of%20CENP,and)) ([rupress.org](https://rupress.org/jcb/article/223/1/e202303007/276393/A-farnesyl-dependent-structural-role-for-CENP-E-in#:~:text=F%20and%20G%29,E)). Notably, inhibiting CENP-E’s motor activity (with small-molecule inhibitors) does *not* prevent corona-based microtubule nucleation ([rupress.org](https://rupress.org/jcb/article/223/1/e202303007/276393/A-farnesyl-dependent-structural-role-for-CENP-E-in#:~:text=match%20at%20L1084%20nucleation%20requires,and)), indicating the motor’s presence (not movement) is what scaffolds other components. Thus, one could argue that CENP-E has a dual function: an **active transport motor** and a **passive structural tether** – the latter being a derived function not seen in simpler kinesins. This dual role is an evolutionary adaptation ensuring both **chromosome movement** and **checkpoint silencing/corona disassembly** happen efficiently once microtubules attach ([www.nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-023-41091-2#:~:text=phosphorylate%20CENP,essential%20for%20accurate%20chromosome%20segregation)).

There are no known subfamilies that catalyze opposite reactions (e.g. no CENP-E that removes microtubules or works as a depolymerase – those functions are performed by other kinesin families like kinesin-13/MCAK). All CENP-E family members move toward microtubule plus-ends, rather than any reversing polarity. Interestingly, CENP-E does contribute to microtubule dynamics in a supportive way: studies indicate **CENP-E can promote microtubule growth at kinetochores** – by holding on to microtubule tips, CENP-E helps stabilize a straight protofilament conformation, encouraging polymerization ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=Biol.%20340%2C%201107%E2%80%931116%2010.1016%2Fj.jmb.2004.05.053%20,R)). It is not a new *biochemical* activity per se (still related to binding microtubule ends), but it shows how the family’s function broadened: not only move chromosomes, but also modulate microtubule dynamics in their vicinity ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=Biol.%20340%2C%201107%E2%80%931116%2010.1016%2Fj.jmb.2004.05.053%20,R)).

**Sub-functionalization:** Since gene duplications of CENP-E are rare, classical sub-functionalization (splitting the original tasks between two daughter genes) did not occur in this family. In the intact protein these sub-functions are unified, but experiments can uncouple them. For instance, a CENP-E construct lacking the motor (tail alone) can still localize to kinetochores and bind BubR1 ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2132809/#:~:text=Kinetochores%20of%20unaligned%20chromosome%20exhibited,E%20and%20hBUBR1%20can)), whereas a motor-only fragment can still walk on microtubules ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=structure%20of%20the%20human%20CENP,E%20motor%20domain)). In evolution, there was no separate gene taking over one part of CENP-E’s job – both “sub-functions” remain tethered in one polypeptide. In some species, however, certain aspects might be less pronounced: e.g., if an organism’s CENP-E lacks the CAAX box, it might rely more on other corona proteins for kinetochore anchoring (this could be the case in organisms that lost farnesylation of CENP-E). This would be an example of lineage-specific sub-functionalization *via loss of a feature*, not via gene duplication. 

**Branch Lengths and Divergence:** Phylogenetic analyses of the kinesin-7/CENP-E family typically show **short branch lengths among vertebrate CENP-Es**, indicating strong purifying selection (little divergence) in these essential proteins. Some longer branches appear for more distant eukaryotes – for example, fungal genomes generally lack an obvious CENP-E homolog, but certain protists or plants with quick evolutionary rates might have distant CENP-E-like sequences. If present, those divergent sequences (long branches) might indicate adaptations, but functional data are scant. Overall, **no branch of the CENP-E family has diverged to perform an entirely different function**. The correlation between branch length and function is thus not dramatic within this family: longer evolutionary distance mostly correlates with differences in regulatory sequences or minor domain architecture changes, rather than a different enzymatic activity. In summary, the CENP-E family shows deep conservation of function, with evolutionary changes tuning the protein’s regulatory and interaction properties more so than its fundamental activity.

## GO Annotation Accuracy

**Propagated Annotations:** The GO annotations for the CENP-E family are typically broad terms like *“microtubule motor activity” (GO:0003777)*, *“plus-end directed microtubule motor activity” (GO:0008578)*, *“kinetochore” (GO:0000776) localization*, *“mitotic spindle checkpoint” (GO:0071174)*, and *“chromosome segregation” (GO:0007059)*. These stem from the well-established role of the ancestral protein in mitosis. By and large, **these annotations are appropriate for all known family members**. Every CENP-E ortholog studied localizes to kinetochores and binds microtubules, so cellular component terms like “kinetochore” and “spindle microtubule” apply universally. Molecular function terms describing ATP-dependent microtubule motor activity are likewise valid for all (no family member lacks the motor domain or has an opposite polarity). Biological process terms such as “mitotic chromosome alignment” or “spindle assembly checkpoint” are also broadly correct: CENP-E helps align chromosomes and is required for the checkpoint to be satisfied (or in its absence, the checkpoint remains active due to unattached chromosomes) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39266203/#:~:text=Chromosome%20congression%20and%20alignment%20are,and%20deletion%20lead%20to%20the)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39266203/#:~:text=cycle%20arrest%20in%20vivo,assembly%20checkpoint%20during%20cell%20division)). Notably, GO annotations from experimental evidence in one species (e.g. human) have been propagated to others (inferred by homology), and this is largely justified given the functional conservation.

**Potential Annotation Issues:** We should examine if any GO terms might **not** apply to certain members or if any important functions are missing. Since no subfamily has a distinct opposite function, there are no GO terms that are outright wrong for a subset. However, some **nuance isn’t captured** by existing annotations. For example, **CENP-E’s role in microtubule dynamics** (stabilizing plus-ends, promoting polymerization as shown in ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=Biol.%20340%2C%201107%E2%80%931116%2010.1016%2Fj.jmb.2004.05.053%20,R))) is not explicitly covered by current GO terms. A term like “positive regulation of microtubule polymerization” could be considered given evidence (Curr Biol 2010, Sardar *et al.*) that CENP-E motors can encourage tubulin addition at kinetochore microtubule tips (PMID: 20817529). Similarly, the recent finding that **CENP-E contributes to kinetochore-derived microtubule nucleation** (helping form new microtubules from kinetochores in early spindle assembly) ([rupress.org](https://rupress.org/jcb/article/223/1/e202303007/276393/A-farnesyl-dependent-structural-role-for-CENP-E-in#:~:text=%28Wu%20et%20al,had%20been%20treated%20with%20nocodazole)) suggests a new annotation could be relevant (e.g. “kinetochore microtubule nucleation”). This function was shown by Wu *et al.* 2024: kinetochore corona expansion by CENP-E facilitates microtubule nucleation for spindle assembly (PMID: 37934467). Currently, a CENP-E protein might not carry a GO term for “microtubule nucleation” or “centrosome organization”, yet knockout studies indicate **spindle pole organization defects** when CENP-E is lost ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39266203/#:~:text=gene%20editing%20method%20and%20high,of%20dividing%20lymphocytes%20and%20cell)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39266203/#:~:text=dispersion%20of%20spindle%20poles%2C%20the,spindle%20assembly%20checkpoint%20during%20cell)). For instance, Chen *et al.* (2023) found CENP-E<sup>–/–</sup> cells show *multipolar spindles* and disorganized centrosomes, implying CENP-E indirectly supports focused spindle poles (perhaps by ensuring proper pulling forces on centrosomes) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39266203/#:~:text=gene%20editing%20method%20and%20high,of%20dividing%20lymphocytes%20and%20cell)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39266203/#:~:text=dispersion%20of%20spindle%20poles%2C%20the,spindle%20assembly%20checkpoint%20during%20cell)). GO annotations might not currently include “centrosome organization” for CENP-E, but this new evidence suggests they might.

Another point relates to checkpoint signaling: CENP-E is annotated with terms like “spindle assembly checkpoint” (often in the context of positive or negative regulation). The literature shows that **CENP-E both activates and silences the checkpoint in different contexts**: it is required to sustain the checkpoint when chromosomes are unaligned, but once it attaches microtubules, it aids in checkpoint silencing by recruiting BubR1 and aligning the chromosome ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2132809/#:~:text=Kinetochores%20of%20unaligned%20chromosome%20exhibited,E%20and%20hBUBR1%20can)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2132809/#:~:text=hybrid%20screen%2C%20the%20kinetochore%20proteins,Li%20and%20Benezra%2C%201997%3B%20Taylor)). The GO term propagation might list CENP-E as involved in **checkpoint activation**, whereas its ultimate role is in checkpoint satisfaction (silencing) – these are two sides of the same coin in mitosis. Practically, CENP-E’s presence at an unattached kinetochore is needed for the checkpoint kinase BubR1 to generate the “wait” signal (so in that sense CENP-E is part of checkpoint signaling) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2132809/#:~:text=hybrid%20screen%2C%20the%20kinetochore%20proteins,Li%20and%20Benezra%2C%201997%3B%20Taylor)). Once microtubules bind, CENP-E and BubR1 together signal that the kinetochore is properly attached, turning off the checkpoint (CENP-E can literally mask BubR1’s kinase domain when under tension, according to some models). The **propagated GO annotations should reflect CENP-E’s role in the checkpoint accurately** – likely as *“mitotic checkpoint signaling”* rather than simplistically “checkpoint activation” or “inactivation” alone. Currently, no subfamily needs to be excluded from these terms, but it’s important GO notes the context (mitosis).

**Subfamily-Specific Annotations:** Since there are no distinct subfamilies in PTHR47968, we don’t have cases where one clade needs a different annotation. All orthologs studied (human, mouse, fly, zebrafish, etc.) localize to kinetochores and move chromosomes, so the GO terms apply uniformly. However, if any species’ CENP-E has reduced or altered behavior, curators should be mindful. For instance, if a hypothetical distant ortholog lacked the BubR1-binding motif, it might not be involved in checkpoint signaling – but no such example is confirmed. We can say with confidence that **no subset of this family has an opposite function that would conflict with the GO annotations**. One precaution: automated annotations sometimes assign CENP-E the term “protein phosphatase 1 binding” (because of an alias PPP1R61). This is not well-supported experimentally and appears to be a mis-annotation; no evidence indicates CENP-E directly binds PP1, and PPP1R61 was an old name based on low-throughput binding screens. Such GO terms should be reviewed; they likely do not hold for most family members and could be removed unless validated.

In summary, the GO annotations propagated to the CENP-E family are largely accurate and cover the central aspects of its function (motor activity, kinetochore localization, chromosome segregation). **We recommend adding** or refining some annotations to capture newly discovered roles (e.g. “kinetochore fiber maturation”, “spindle pole organization”) based on recent studies. Conversely, no GO term needs to be categorically removed for a subset of this family – the functional consistency is high. Curators should ensure annotations like “mitotic checkpoint” are kept, as CENP-E is indeed a core component of that process in all organisms examined (PMID: 14532006, 12934010). The **accuracy of GO for this family is high**, reflecting the tight conservation of function.

## Key Literature

- **Discovery and Core Function:** *Wood et al., 1997 (Cell)* – First demonstrated that CENP-E is a plus-end directed kinetochore motor essential for metaphase chromosome alignment ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2766669/#:~:text=,5.%20%5BDOI%5D%20%5BPubMed%5D%20%5BGoogle%20Scholar)) (PMID: 9363944). *Schaar et al., 1997 (J. Cell Biol.)* similarly showed CENP-E is required at kinetochores for chromosome alignment (PMID: 9396751). These foundational studies established the family’s ancestral role.

- **Kinetochore Interactions:** *Chan et al., 1998 (J. Cell Biol.)* – Mapped the kinetochore-binding domain of CENP-E, showing direct interactions with CENP-F and BubR1 ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2132809/#:~:text=Kinetochores%20of%20unaligned%20chromosome%20exhibited,E%20and%20hBUBR1%20can)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2132809/#:~:text=hybrid%20screen%2C%20the%20kinetochore%20proteins,Li%20and%20Benezra%2C%201997%3B%20Taylor)) (PMID: 9763420). This paper linked the motor to checkpoint control proteins. *Yao et al., 2000 (Nat. Cell Biol.)* – Showed CENP-E forms a physical link between microtubule attachment and the spindle checkpoint (binding BubR1), implicating CENP-E in checkpoint silencing (PMID: 10934468).

- **Mitotic Checkpoint Role:** *Abrieu et al., 2000 (Cell)* – Demonstrated that CENP-E is an essential component of the mitotic checkpoint in *Xenopus* extracts ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2766669/#:~:text=,2.%20%5BDOI%5D%20%5BPubMed%5D%20%5BGoogle%20Scholar)). Removing CENP-E abrogated the checkpoint signal, leading to premature anaphase (PMID: 11030625). *Weaver et al., 2003 (J. Cell Biol.)* – Using mouse cells, showed CENP-E is critical to prevent aneuploidy; heterozygous loss leads to increased chromosome loss, highlighting its checkpoint role (PMID: 12975356).

- **Motor Properties:** *Garcia-Saez et al., 2004 (J. Mol. Biol.)* – Solved the crystal structure of the human CENP-E motor domain ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=%2A%2083.Garcia,Google%20Scholar)), revealing it closely matches structures of other kinesins (PMID: 15288791). *Yardimci et al., 2008 (PNAS)* – Provided single-molecule evidence that CENP-E is a **processive** motor that can walk long distances along microtubules without detaching ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=,Google%20Scholar)) (PMID: 18443146). *Espeut et al., 2008 (Mol. Cell)* – Showed **phosphorylation relieves CENP-E autoinhibition**, identifying CDK1 and MPS1 as kinases that activate CENP-E by unfolding its conformation (PMID: 18342598).

- **Functional Nuances:** *Sardar et al., 2010 (Curr. Biol.)* – Discovered that active CENP-E motors can promote microtubule plus-end elongation at kinetochores ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=Biol.%20340%2C%201107%E2%80%931116%2010.1016%2Fj.jmb.2004.05.053%20,R)), a novel insight into its role in stabilizing kinetochore fibers (PMID: 20817529). *Maffini et al., 2009 (Curr. Biol.)* – Showed that CENP-E recruits CLASP proteins to kinetochores in a motor-independent manner to enhance kinetochore microtubule dynamics (PMID: 19733075). These studies underscore CENP-E’s multifaceted role in managing kinetochore-microtubule interactions.

- **Disease/Applied Research:** *Parrish et al., 2007 (PNAS)* and *Dumontet et al., 2010 (Clin. Cancer Res.)* – Developed **GSK-923295**, a potent small-molecule inhibitor of CENP-E. They demonstrated that GSK-923295 induces mitotic arrest and has broad antitumor activity in preclinical models ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4007900/#:~:text=broad%20antitumor%20activity%20in%20vivo,currently%20in%20human%20clinical%20trials)). A Phase I clinical trial (2007–2012, NCT00504790) tested this inhibitor in advanced cancers, marking the translation of CENP-E research into a therapeutic context. These works indicate the real-world potential of targeting CENP-E in oncology (PMID: 18483209).

- **Recent Advances (2020–2024):** *Craske, 2020 (Essays Biochem.)* – A contemporary review titled “Leaving no-one behind: how CENP-E facilitates chromosome alignment,” summarizing CENP-E’s mechanism and recent findings ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=independent%20manner,E%20motor%20activity)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475649/#:~:text=structure%20of%20the%20human%20CENP,E%20motor%20domain)) (PMID: 32347304). *Chen et al., 2023 (Cell Prolif.)* – Applied CRISPR knockout in human cells to reveal that CENP-E loss causes spindle pole fragmentation and severe genome instability, extending CENP-E’s known functions to centrosome organization ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39266203/#:~:text=gene%20editing%20method%20and%20high,of%20dividing%20lymphocytes%20and%20cell)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39266203/#:~:text=dispersion%20of%20spindle%20poles%2C%20the,spindle%20assembly%20checkpoint%20during%20cell)) (PMID: 39266203). *Wu et al., 2024 (J. Cell Biol.)* – Showed that **farnesylation of CENP-E is critical for expanding the fibrous corona** and promoting kinetochore microtubule nucleation; CENP-E has a structural role in assembling checkpoint complexes, independent of its motor activity ([rupress.org](https://rupress.org/jcb/article/223/1/e202303007/276393/A-farnesyl-dependent-structural-role-for-CENP-E-in#:~:text=%28Wu%20et%20al,had%20been%20treated%20with%20nocodazole)) ([rupress.org](https://rupress.org/jcb/article/223/1/e202303007/276393/A-farnesyl-dependent-structural-role-for-CENP-E-in#:~:text=Inhibition%20of%20CENP,and)) (PMID: 37934467). *Eibes et al., 2023 (Nat. Commun.)* – Demonstrated that **Aurora A and B kinases phosphorylate CENP-E to activate it**, thereby controlling the disassembly of the kinetochore corona at the proper time ([www.nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-023-41091-2#:~:text=phosphorylate%20CENP,essential%20for%20accurate%20chromosome%20segregation)) (PMID: 37687494). This study elaborated how CENP-E’s activity is finely tuned by cell-cycle kinases during mitosis. Finally, structural biologists Shibuya *et al.*, 2023 (FEBS Lett) solved a new high-resolution structure of CENP-E’s motor with ATP analog (PMID: 36823439), which could aid in rational drug design targeting the motor domain.

Each of these key publications has contributed to a comprehensive picture of the CENP-E family: from structural biology and biochemistry of the motor, to cell biological function in chromosome segregation, to regulatory mechanisms and potential for therapeutic targeting. The convergence of evidence from these works reinforces that **CENP-E is a vital, conserved motor protein** whose proper function is indispensable for accurate cell division.
